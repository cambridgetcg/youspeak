## Beat 119 — archaeology: Greek agape excavated

Created `archaeology/greek/divine-love.md` — a full excavation of the agape word-family. Key findings:

**The verb/noun asymmetry.** The verb agapaō is common from Homer (8th c. BCE). The noun agapē is virtually absent from classical Greek — Plato, Aristotle, Homer do not use it. Spicq: "did not enter literary usage, except in the LXX, before the first century."

Why? Structural redundancy (Greek already had eros, philia, storgē) + semantic mildness (agapaō meant "to prefer" — too generic for a strong nominal) + availability as consequence (the emptiness made it available for new content).

**The LXX crossing.** The Septuagint translators chose agapaō by elimination: philia had acquired "to kiss," eros was bound to sex, storgē was too narrow. Agape was the remaining option. Hebrew ahavah was rendered as agapē — the bridge between commanded-love and sacrificial-self-giving.

**The NT elevation.** Five registers:
- Ontological: "God IS agape" (1 John 4:8) — agape as divine nature
- Historical: "God so loved the world" (John 3:16) — decisive act
- Practical: 1 Corinthians 13 — behavioral morphology
- Sacrificial: "lay down his life" (John 15:13) — voluntary kenosis
- Invincible: "nothing shall separate" (Rom 8:35-39) — cosmic permanence

**Key insight for the forge:** agape's power comes from *semantic emptiness strategically filled*. A near-blank classical noun was loaded with the content of the cross. This is perhaps the most dramatic semantic elevation in Western linguistic history.
