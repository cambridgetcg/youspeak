# Machi, Kultrun, and Weichan: The Specialist, the Instrument, and the Struggle

_If admapu is the order and ngillatun is the enactment, the machi is the person who holds the
axis. The machi is not a priest who represents the community to the divine — the machi is the
person who travels the vertical axis between worlds, returning with what the community needs to
survive. The kultrun drum is not an instrument the machi plays — it is the map the machi reads._

---

## I. The Machi: Shaman-Priestess of the People

### The Role

**Machi** (the word carries no distinct etymology-etymology beyond its specialized usage; it
designates the office): the sacred specialist of the Mapuche community whose primary functions
include:

1. **Healing** (*perimontun*: vision-diagnosis; the machi identifies the relational rupture that
   has manifested as illness and works to restore the broken relation)
2. **Ceremonial leadership** (the machi leads or co-leads ngillatun and other communal ceremonies;
   her ascent of the rewe is the ritual's cosmological centerpiece)
3. **Cosmic maintenance** (the machi's ongoing relationship with the ngen-spirits and the pillanes
   keeps the territory's relational web intact between ceremonies)
4. **Prophecy and interpretation** (*perimontun*: vision/revelation; the machi receives
   communications from wenu mapu about threats to the community — disease, war, environmental
   disruption)
5. **Conflict mediation** (in some contexts the machi mediates community disputes by accessing
   ancestral guidance)

### The Calling

A machi does not choose the role. The calling comes **from outside** — typically through illness,
recurring dreams, visitations by spirit-forces, or the death of a family member who had been machi
(the role frequently passes within family lines, but cannot be simply inherited — the spirits must
confirm the successor).

The calling is often experienced as crisis: the person falls ill, has disturbing visions, begins
behaving in socially disruptive ways. The community recognizes this as *machi illness* — the
spirits pressing for acknowledgment. If the person resists the calling, the illness intensifies.
Acceptance of the role — undertaken in the *machiluwün* (initiation ceremony) — resolves the
crisis by completing the relationship the spirits are demanding.

**Structural parallel**: This pattern — calling as crisis that only acceptance resolves — appears
in the cathedral in:
- Igbo *dibia* calling: the oracle-deity possesses the initiate through illness
- Hebrew prophetic calling: Jeremiah's reluctance, Isaiah's burning coal, Jonah's flight
- Yoruba initiation: the orisha "mounts" the devotee in disruption before training
- Christian mystic initiation (Teresa of Ávila, dark night of the soul as the form of the calling)

The Mapuche machi calling is not idiosyncratic — it is an instance of the cross-tradition pattern
that **the sacred specialist is chosen from outside the ordinary social order by a force that
exceeds the specialist's will**.

### The Gender Theology of the Machi Role

This is where careful excavation is required. The machi role has often been described in
ethnographic literature as simply "female" — most machi practitioners today are women. But
Ana Mariella Bacigalupo's detailed field research reveals a more complex gender structure:

**The role is gendered feminine regardless of the practitioner's biological sex**. Male machi
(wentru machi) have historically existed and continue to exist. When a biological male takes the
machi role, the role itself feminizes him — he dresses in feminine clothing during ceremony,
adopts feminine social roles in ritual contexts, and is understood to have a feminine spiritual
identity in the context of his machi function.

**The reason**: The machi's access to wenu mapu requires feminine receptivity. The Mapuche
cosmological grammar positions the vertical axis — the communication between below and above —
as requiring a receiver. The masculine principle acts (wentru) in the horizontal plane of the
community; the feminine principle receives (domo) from the vertical plane of the sacred. The machi,
regardless of biological sex, must adopt the receiving posture. This is not subordination — it is
structural: the feminine capacity for reception *is* the access to the sacred.

**Ngünechen's structure illuminates this**: The supreme being contains both masculine and feminine
as complementary. The machi enacts the feminine-receptive aspect in order to receive from the
masculine-active-principle of wenu mapu. This is not a simple gender binary — it is a **structural
theology of complementarity** in which the gendering of the machi role is functionally required
by the cosmological grammar.

**Comparison**:
- Siberian shamanism: some traditions require male practitioners to adopt feminine identities
  (the "transformed shamans" documented in Chukchi tradition) — a parallel structure
- Yoruba priesthood: certain orisha (Oshun, Yemoja) are served by male priests who adopt
  feminine postures in possession — analogous structural logic
- Hopi kachina dancers: exclusively male performers who sometimes embody female kachinas —
  the role exceeds the gender of the performer

### The Machiluwün: Initiation

The machi initiation ceremony (machiluwün) is the formal completion of the calling:

1. **The rewe consecration**: A new rewe (notched ritual tree) is planted for the new machi.
   The rewe is not the machi's personal property — it is the axis of her community's
   communication with wenu mapu. It is planted at her home but belongs to the relational community.

2. **The first ascent**: The new machi climbs the rewe in an altered state (induced through
   drumming, sustained wakefulness, and sometimes herbal preparations). The ascent is not symbolic
   — in Mapuche understanding, the machi actually travels the vertical axis to wenu mapu, receives
   her *newen* (spiritual power/force), and returns with it to begin her work.

3. **The name-receiving**: The machi receives her machi-name from the spirits. This is distinct
   from her personal name — it is the name of her function, the name of her relationship to the
   forces she will work with.

4. **The community confirmation**: The surrounding community witnesses and confirms the initiation.
   The machi is not self-appointed — she is confirmed by the community, the spirits, and the
   ceremony simultaneously.

### The Machi's Newen: Force/Power

**Newen**: spiritual force or power. The machi's newen is what enables her work — it is received
through initiation, maintained through practice and right living, and can be depleted or corrupted.

Newen is not inherent personal power — it is **received force** that the machi holds in trust
for the community. This is structurally important: the machi's power is not hers to use as she
chooses. It is the community's power held in a specialist's body for the community's benefit.

**Kalku**: the dark mirror of the machi. A kalku is a person who holds newen but uses it for
harm — through envy, payment, or malice. The machi's primary diagnostic work often involves
determining whether illness is caused by natural-relational disruption or by kalku-work (harmful
spiritual action by an enemy or jealous party). The machi and kalku are in structural tension:
same access to the spiritual axis, opposite orientations.

This is the **ambivalence of sacred power** pattern — found across the cathedral:
- Yoruba àṣẹ: the same force that heals and creates can harm and destroy
- Igbo chi: powerful chi can be the ground of greatness or the source of hubris
- Mande nyama: the force in every being that, if released incorrectly, causes harm
- Baltic/Lithuanian: the power of the machi parallels the *burtininkas* (sorcerer) and
  *ragana* (witch) who hold similar access to the spirit world with ambiguous orientation

## II. The Kultrun: The Drum as Cosmological Map

### The Instrument

**Kultrun** (also kultrung, cultrún): the sacred drum of the machi. Physically, it is a
shallow half-sphere (like an inverted bowl) of wood, covered with horse or sheep skin, typically
30–40 cm in diameter. A single drumstick is used — the machi holds the drum in one hand and
strikes with the other, producing the sustained rhythmic pulse that underlies all machi work and
ngillatun ceremony.

But the kultrun is not primarily a musical instrument. It is a **cosmological object**.

### The Cosmogram on the Drum

The skin of the kultrun is painted with the **Mapuche cosmogram** — a cross-within-circle
design that maps the cosmos:

```
         North (pikun mapu)
              │
West ─────────┼───────── East (puel mapu — sacred direction)
(ngulumapu)   │          (toward the sun)
              │
         South (willi mapu)
```

The circle (wenu) contains the cross (the four directions of nag mapu). Inside the quadrants,
various elements are painted by tradition and by what the machi's newen directs:

- **Sun** (antu): the primary divine light; Mapuche prayer orients east
- **Moon** (küyen): cycles, women's power, agricultural timing
- **Stars** (*wangülen*): guidance, navigation, time
- **The rewe**: the axis represented within the map
- **Animals and plants sacred to the machi's particular power**

Each kultrun is unique — the painting reflects the specific newen and relational network of that
machi. Two machi do not have identical kultruns. The drum is a **map of that machi's cosmological
position** — her specific relationship to the forces she works with.

### The Drum as World

When the machi plays the kultrun during ceremony, she is not producing music that accompanies
the sacred — she is **enacting the cosmological structure** that enables contact with wenu mapu.
The sustained rhythm is the heartbeat-vibration of the cosmos as the machi understands it. When
the drum speaks, the cosmos speaks through the drum.

This is structurally related to but distinct from:
- Sámi noaidi's drum (also a cosmogram — the Sámi drum maps the spirit world in ways that
  guided the noaidi's trance journey; excavated S042 Finnish/Sámi)
- Tibetan singing bowl: resonance as the medium of cosmic contact
- Aboriginal didgeridoo: sustained drone as the voice of the Dreaming

The kultrun is closest to the **Sámi noaidi drum** — both are cosmographic instruments that map
the spirit world AND are the means of navigating it. The machi reads the drum during divination
by observing which objects placed on the drum-skin move toward which painted zone when the drum
is struck.

**Divination by kultrun**: During diagnostic work, the machi places small objects (seeds, stones,
tokens) on the kultrun's painted surface and strikes it. The direction the objects move toward
indicates the zone of the cosmogram relevant to the illness — which relational domain has been
disrupted, which ngen or pillan is involved, which direction healing must come from.

The drum thus functions simultaneously as:
1. **Cosmological map** (painting represents the structure of the cosmos)
2. **Divination instrument** (objects moving on the map diagnose the problem)
3. **Rhythmic medium** (sustained drumming enables the machi's trance-ascent)
4. **Relational document** (unique to each machi — her newen painted in pigment)

**This is the most functionally dense ritual object in the archaeological catalog**: the kultrun
does more distinct things simultaneously than any other sacred object examined.

### Newen and the Drum's Life

The kultrun is alive. It has newen — spiritual force — which increases through use. When a machi
dies, her kultrun must be carefully handled: buried with her, returned to the territory, or
passed to a successor through specific protocols. An abandoned kultrun without proper closure
retains dangerous spiritual charge.

The drum's newen is not merely associated with the machi — it is the concentrated residue of all
the ceremonies it has participated in, all the healing work it has witnessed, all the contact with
wenu mapu it has mediated. A very old kultrun is understood to be very powerful, very much alive.

## III. Weichan: The Sacred Struggle

### The Term

**Weichan** (also wichan): from the verb *weichan* (to fight, to struggle, to wage war). But the
Mapuche understanding of weichan is not simply military conflict — it is the **sacred struggle to
maintain identity, territory, and cosmic order against forces of disruption**.

In Mapuche theological understanding, the Spanish conquest, the Chilean state's Pacification of
Araucanía, and the ongoing dispossession are not merely political events. They are instances of
the same cosmological threat: forces that seek to break the relationship between the people (che),
their land (mapu), their ancestors (pillanes), and their order (admapu).

Weichan in this sense is the necessary response to the threat of cosmic dissolution.

### Historical Weichan

The Mapuche fought the Spanish from 1536 onward. The Arauco War — one of the longest continuous
armed conflicts in the Americas — produced the distinctive figure of the **toqui**: the war-chief
appointed specifically for the duration of conflict. (Normally Mapuche governance is through
lonko — community headmen — with no permanent political center. The toqui is the temporary
centralization of authority that crisis requires.)

Famous toquis:
- **Lautaro** (c. 1534–1557): the young warrior who served as stable-boy for Spanish Governor
  Valdivia, learned Spanish cavalry tactics, escaped, and organized the first effective Mapuche
  guerrilla campaign using Iberian horses against their former owners. He died at 23 but had
  inflicted the most significant early defeats on the Spanish in the region.
- **Caupolicán** (d. 1558): elected toqui through the famous ordeal of holding a log on his
  shoulders for three days — demonstrating the physical endurance that the role required.
- **Pelantaro** (late 16th-early 17th c.): led the Mapuche uprising of 1598, which destroyed
  seven Spanish cities and expelled Spanish settlements south of the Biobío River for decades.

The Treaty of Quilín (1641) formalized Spanish recognition of Mapuche sovereignty south of the
Biobío — the only formal treaty between the Spanish Crown and an indigenous nation acknowledging
their sovereignty. It was not fully honored, but its existence is a theological-political datum:
the Mapuche were recognized as a treaty-partner, not a conquered people.

### Contemporary Weichan

The Chilean state's Pacification of Araucanía (1861–1883) — what the Mapuche call the Occupation
— dispossessed Mapuche communities of approximately 90% of their traditional territory through
military force and land grants to Chilean and European settlers.

Contemporary weichan includes:
- Land recovery occupations (*recuperaciones*) of ancestral territory
- Court cases under both Chilean and international indigenous rights law
- Community-language revitalization
- Prosecution under the 1984 Anti-Terrorism Law (Ley Antiterrorista) — legislation originally
  designed for the Pinochet dictatorship's political opponents, now used against Mapuche activists

Weichan in its contemporary form encompasses all of these. The machi's role in contemporary
weichan is not only ceremonial — machi have been imprisoned, have had their sacred materials
confiscated by police, and have gone on hunger strike. In 2021–2022, machi Celestino Córdova was
imprisoned for over a year, during which time his rewe — the axis of his community's relationship
with wenu mapu — deteriorated from lack of care, constituting a cosmological emergency for his
community as well as a human rights violation.

**The theological structure of weichan**: resistance to colonial displacement is not merely
political — it is the maintenance of cosmic order against forces of chaos. This is the same
structure found in:
- Haudenosaunee resistance to treaty violations (the Great Law as the framework against
  which colonial disruption is measured)
- Lakota resistance post-Little Bighorn (Black Elk's charge: restore the Sacred Hoop)
- Kikongo Kimpa Vita's Antonian movement (the claimed recovery of the true Kongo cosmos
  against Portuguese colonial disruption)

**Weichan is the Mapuche instance of the broad cross-tradition pattern: resistance-as-cosmic-
maintenance**. The struggle is not merely to survive as a people — it is to keep the world in
right order against the forces that would dissolve it.

## IV. The Rewe: The Cosmic Axis as Political Object

The **rewe** (ceremonial notched tree/ladder, the axis of the world in Mapuche cosmology) is
planted at the machi's home and at ngillatun ceremony grounds. It is the physical point at which
wenu mapu and nag mapu connect.

In contemporary contexts, the rewe has become a **political symbol** as well as a cosmological
one. When the Chilean state confiscates or destroys a rewe — as has happened in police raids on
Mapuche communities — it is not merely destroying property. In Mapuche understanding, it is
severing the connection between the community and the sacred above, interrupting the machi's
ability to mediate healing and ceremony, and attacking the cosmic infrastructure of the people.

**The foye / canelo tree** (*Drimys winteri*): The traditional species for rewe construction.
The canelo has white flowers and is considered sacred — a connection to purity and the feminine
principle. Mapuche women carrying canelo branches have been used as peace signals. The sight of
canelo marks sacred space.

In land-rights protests, Mapuche activists have carried canelo branches as a deliberate
theological-political signal: this is sacred action, not mere politics. The branch says: we are
not merely citizens claiming property rights — we are a people maintaining cosmic order.

## V. The Cosmology of Illness and Healing

### Illness as Relational Disorder

In Mapuche theology, illness (*kutran*) is primarily understood as the manifestation of a broken
relation — between the ill person and:
- A ngen-spirit whose domain was violated
- A pillan-ancestor demanding acknowledgment
- A kalku who has directed harmful spiritual force
- An internal imbalance in the ill person's own newen

This is not the same as psychosomatic illness (the Western category that closest approximates it).
The Mapuche understanding is ontological, not psychological: the relational breach IS the illness,
not its cause. When the relation is restored, the illness has no basis.

### The Machitun: Healing Ceremony

The machi's healing ceremony (*machitun*) is the intensive version of her work:

1. **Diagnosis through perimontun (vision)**: The machi enters altered consciousness through
   drumming and may use herbal preparations (particularly *foye*, *poleo*, *canelo* — plants with
   spiritual as well as medicinal properties). She receives a vision of the disrupted relation.

2. **Spirit combat**: If kalku-work is involved, the machi's newen confronts the attacking force.
   This is not metaphorical — the Mapuche understand this as actual contact between spiritual
   forces, with the community present to support the machi's strength.

3. **Restoration**: The ceremony enacts the restoration of the severed relation — through prayer
   to the relevant ngen or pillan, through offering, through the machi's newen-work.

4. **The community as medicine**: The presence of the community around the ill person is itself
   therapeutic in Mapuche understanding. Healing is not administered to an isolated patient — the
   community gathers, drums, prays, and its collective newen supports the restoration.

**The community-as-medicine structure** is significant for YOUSPEAK: the idea that healing
requires the gathered community (not just the specialist's skill) appears also in:
- Igbo *dibia* healing: the healer works within the community's presence
- African Ubuntu healing contexts: wellness as communal restoration
- Early Christian healing in community (the elders gather; the community prays)
- Navajo ceremony: the hataalii sings, but the family and community are present and their
  presence is structurally required

## VIII. Summary: What the Mapuche Excavation Contributes to the Cathedral

**New theological grammar discovered**:

1. **Quaternary-complementary divine** (Ngünechen): the supreme being as elder/young ×
   masculine/feminine — a structure not found elsewhere in the catalog in this exact form; more
   precisely differentiated than Trinity, more unified than divine couple, more structured than
   pantheon.

2. **Law-as-the-form-of-the-territory** (admapu): ancestral law as inherent in the land
   itself, not merely in human tradition — the most territorial instance of law-as-theology in
   the catalog.

3. **The axis-holder specialist** (machi): distinct from priest, prophet, healer — the person
   who travels the vertical axis between worlds for the community; the gendering of this role
   (functionally feminine regardless of biological sex) as cosmologically required rather than
   culturally contingent.

4. **The cosmographic instrument** (kultrun): the sacred object that simultaneously maps and
   navigates the cosmos — the most functionally dense ritual object in the catalog.

5. **Resistance-as-cosmic-maintenance** (weichan): the struggle to maintain identity and territory
   as the maintenance of cosmic order — the Mapuche contribution to what is now a four-tradition
   convergence.
