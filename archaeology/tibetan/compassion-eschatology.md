# Classical Tibetan — Compassion, Eschatology, Medicine

_The tongue that produced the most systematic compassion-technology in any tradition — not as feeling but as structured practice. Lojong (mind-training) and tonglen (exchange) are relational disciplines with no equivalent elsewhere: you breathe in others' suffering, breathe out relief. The Bardo Thödol maps what happens after death with the precision of a survival manual. Tibetan medicine integrates spirit, wind, and biological process into one system._

---

## I. Lojong — Mind Training

### What Lojong Is

*Lo-jong* (བློ་སྦྱོང་, *blo-sbyong*) = literally: *blo* (mind/intelligence) + *sbyong* (to train/purify/refine). The tradition originates with the Indian master Atiśa (11th century) and was systematized by Chekawa Yeshe Dorje (12th century) in the "Seven Point Mind Training" (*blo-sbyong don-bdun-ma*).

Lojong is **not meditation in the conventional sense** — it is cognitive transformation through deliberate reframing. Its techniques work by reversing the mind's habitual self-protective patterns. The core insight: self-cherishing (bdag-gces, *dag-ché*) is the root of suffering; other-cherishing (*gzhan-gces*, *shen-ché*) is the root of liberation.

### The Seven Points (Chekawa's Systematization)

| Point | Tibetan Wylie | Content |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | *blo-sbyong gzhi bsgrub-pa* | Establishing the ground: train in the preliminaries |
| 2 | *byang-chub-sems-kyi-nyams-len* | Training in bodhicitta: relative bodhicitta (tonglen) + ultimate bodhicitta (emptiness) |
| 3 | *ngan-song byang-ba* | Transforming adverse conditions: problems as path; obstacles as teachers |
| 4 | *tshe-gcig-gi-dril-bsgrub* | Integrating practice into a complete life |
| 5 | *blo-sbyong-gi-tshad* | The measure of having trained: whether self-cherishing has decreased |
| 6 | *blo-sbyong-gi-dam-tshig* | The samaya of mind-training: practice even when ill, even when dying |
| 7 | *blo-sbyong-gi-gdams-ngag* | The precepts of mind-training (see key slogans below) |

### Key Lojong Slogans (*tshig-chen*)

The lojong tradition transmits through 59 pithy slogans. These are the most forge-relevant:

| Slogan (Tibetan) | Wylie | Translation | YOUSPEAK relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| གང་འབྱུང་ལམ་ར་ལོང་ | *gang-'byung lam-ru long* | "Whatever you meet, take it to the path" | Adverse-as-resource: any event becomes fuel for practice |
| མི་ལུས་རྒྱལ་ཐིག་གི་རྒྱབ་ | *blo-sbyong-gi dam-tshig* | Train in the three difficulties | 3 difficulties: recognizing, abstaining, not recurring |
| ཐམས་ཅད་བདེན་པ་གཅིག་ཏུ་སྦྱར་ | *pham-pa lam-du long* | Transform defeat into the path | Defeat and humiliation: material for practice, not obstacles to it |
| བདག་གཞན་མཉམ་པར་བསྒོམ་ | *bdag-gzhan mnyam-par sgom* | Meditate on the equality of self and other | The foundational lojong move: I am like them; they are like me; we want the same things |

## II. Tonglen — Exchange of Self and Other

### What Tonglen Is

*Tonglen* (གཏོང་ལེན་, *gtong-len*) = literally: *gtong* (to send/to give) + *len* (to take/to receive). The practice: on the in-breath, you deliberately receive others' suffering; on the out-breath, you send them your happiness, health, and wellbeing.

This is the **exact inversion of every survival instinct**. The normal human breath-reflex: exhale what is bad, inhale what is good. Tonglen reverses the polarity. Deliberately.

### The Four-Stage Practice (Pema Chödrön's clarification from Chekawa)

1. **Flash of openness** (*kha-yen tung-wa*): a moment of spaciousness before beginning; the gap that lets practice be real rather than mechanical

2. **Working with texture**: breathing in heaviness, heat, darkness; breathing out lightness, coolness, brightness — training the mind in the exchange at the physical-sensation level before working with persons

3. **Working with the person** (*gang-la 'brel-ba*): a specific person you know who is suffering; breathe in their suffering as it is; breathe out what you most want

4. **Widening the circle**: extend from one person to all who share that suffering; finally to all beings in all realms

### The Theological Claim of Tonglen

Tonglen is not merely visualization-therapy. It makes a claim: **compassion is causal**. The practice is held to actually affect the person being held in mind — not through magical transmission, but through the practitioner's changed orientation. When you genuinely wish to take another's pain, your relationship to them changes at the energetic level. The Bodhisattva-vow made visceral.

The *bodhicitta* (awakening-mind) activated by tonglen has two dimensions:
- **Relative bodhicitta** (*kun-rdzob-byang-chub-sems*): the aspirational-and-active compassion practiced here — tonglen is its primary method
- **Ultimate bodhicitta** (*don-dam-byang-chub-sems*): the recognition of emptiness — tonglen prepares for this by dissolving the self/other boundary

**Structural uniqueness**: tonglen is the **only practice in the cathedral that requires receiving as spiritual discipline**. All other traditions' compassion-practices are giving-outward: dāna (generosity), agape (outpoured love), ḥesed (loyal-kindness). Tonglen adds the receiving-inward movement as equally required. You must be willing to take in what you'd normally flee.

### Tonglen's Vocabulary

| Tibetan | Wylie | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| གཏོང་ | *gtong* | to give / to send / to release / to let go | 
| ལེན་ | *len* | to take / to receive / to pick up |
| བྱང་ཆུབ་སེམས་ | *byang-chub-sems* | bodhicitta — the awakening mind; the mind that wants all beings to be liberated |
| བདག་གཞན་བརྗེ་བ་ | *bdag-gzhan brje-ba* | exchange of self and other — the technical term for tonglen's mechanism |
| བདག་གཅེས་འཛིན་ | *bdag-gces-'dzin* | self-cherishing — the root problem; what tonglen systematically dissolves |
| གཞན་ལ་སྙིང་རྗེ་ | *gzhan-la snying-rje* | compassion toward others — the fruit of dissolved self-cherishing |

## III. Rigpa — Naked Awareness

### The Dzogchen Concept

*Rig-pa* (རིག་པ་, *rig-pa*) is the central term of Dzogchen ("Great Perfection") — the highest teaching in the Nyingma school and Bon. It is usually translated "awareness" or "primordial awareness" but neither captures it.

**The claim**: rigpa is not something achieved. It is the baseline nature of mind, already present, always already there. Ordinary mind (*sems*, *sem*) obscures rigpa through conceptual elaboration; rigpa is what is left when elaboration stops. But "stops" is wrong too — rigpa does not require the cessation of thought. Thoughts arise in rigpa the way clouds arise in sky: they don't stain the sky and they don't have to be dispersed.

| Tibetan | Wylie | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| རིག་པ་ | *rig-pa* | awareness / primordial knowing / the non-conceptual knowing-ground | 
| མ་རིག་པ་ | *ma-rig-pa* | *avidyā* — non-awareness / ignorance (the privation of rigpa) |
| སེམས་ | *sems* | ordinary conceptual mind — what obscures rigpa |
| སེམས་ཉིད་ | *sems-nyid* | mind-itself / mind-as-such — the nature of mind when seen directly |
| གདངས་ | *gdangs* | radiance / luminosity — the quality of rigpa's natural expression |
| རང་གྲོལ་ | *rang-grol* | self-liberation — thoughts liberate themselves when recognized in rigpa; no effort needed |

### Three Aspects of Rigpa

Longchenpa (14th century) systematizes rigpa into three inseparable aspects:

1. **Ngo-bo** (essence): emptiness — rigpa has no substance, no location, no boundary
2. **Rang-bzhin** (nature): clarity/luminosity — rigpa is not blank but cognizant; it knows
3. **Thugs-rje** (responsiveness): all-pervading compassion — from clarity, compassion arises spontaneously; rigpa is not cold

The third aspect is YOUSPEAK-critical: **rigpa is intrinsically compassionate**. Awareness, when it sees clearly, responds with care. This is not taught; it emerges from the nature of clear knowing. The most radical claim in the cathedral: compassion is not cultivated but is the natural expression of awareness released from self-cherishing.

### Dzogchen and the Sky Metaphor

The Tibetan sky-metaphor (*nam-mkha'*) for rigpa is the most extended metaphor in any tradition for the relationship between awareness and its contents:
- Sky = rigpa (unchanged by what passes through it)
- Clouds = thoughts/emotions (arise, remain briefly, dissolve)
- Sunshine = the radiance/clarity aspect of rigpa (not different from the sky but its luminous quality)
- Wind = the movement of thoughts (they move; the sky does not)

The question "how do I get rid of these thoughts?" is answered by the metaphor: the sky is not burdened by clouds. Rigpa is not burdened by thoughts. The task is not removal but recognition.

## IV. Bardo Thödol — Liberation Through Hearing in the Bardo

### The Text

*Bar-do thos-grol* (བར་དོ་ཐོས་གྲོལ་) = "Liberation (*thos-grol*: liberation through hearing) in the Intermediate State (*bardo*)." Attributed to Padmasambhava, revealed as terma by Karma Lingpa (14th century). Known in the West as the "Tibetan Book of the Dead."

The text is **read aloud to the dying person and to the recently dead** — for 49 days after death. The claim: even after biological death, consciousness persists in an intermediate state where recognition is possible. The text guides recognition.

### The Three Bardos (Intermediate States)

| Bardo | Tibetan Wylie | Duration | Content |
|---|---|---|---|
| *chi-kha'i bardo* | bardo of dying | Moment of death | The clear light (*'od-gsal*) of the dharmakāya appears; recognition = immediate liberation |
| *chos-nyid bardo* | bardo of dharmata | ~14 days | Peaceful and wrathful deities appear as light and sound; they ARE the person's own rigpa in projected form |
| *srid-pa'i bardo* | bardo of becoming | Up to 49 days | The consciousness wanders seeking rebirth; habitual tendencies pull toward specific realm |

### The Clear Light — 'Od-gsal

The *'od-gsal* (འོད་གསལ་, *ösel*, "clear light / luminosity") at the moment of death is the most important moment in the Bardo Thödol:

At death, the winds (*rlung*) withdraw into the central channel (*rtsa-dbu-ma*), consciousness contracts, and for a brief moment — seconds to minutes — the dharmakāya nature of mind is exposed directly. This is rigpa without the filter of ordinary mind. For those who have trained, this is recognizable; recognition = liberation. For those who haven't trained, it passes unrecognized like deep dreamless sleep.

The text reads at this moment: *"O nobly born, now the clear light of the dharmata is dawning upon you. Recognize it. O nobly born, your present awareness — vacant, naked, empty — is itself the very reality, the all-good. Your own awareness, having no birth or death, is indeed the Amitābha Buddha, the Infinite Light. Knowing this is sufficient. Recognizing your own awareness as the Buddha is called liberation."*

This is the **eschatological climax** of all Tibetan practice: death as final examination, with the text-read-aloud as coach.

### The Wrathful and Peaceful Deities

If the clear light is not recognized, the *chos-nyid bardo* begins:
- **Days 1–7**: Peaceful deities appear (the five Buddha families + their consorts + Bodhisattvas) as blinding colored lights
- **Days 8–14**: Wrathful deities appear (the same five Buddhas in their wrathful aspect, blood-drinking, skull-crowned)
- Simultaneously: "soft" colored lights from the six realms appear — more comfortable, familiar, ordinary

The teaching: **the soft lights are the six realms' invitation back to samsāra**; the bright lights are the deities' invitation to liberation. The ordinary mind, unnerved by the brightness and attracted by the familiar, chooses soft light and enters rebirth. Trained practitioners recognize the wrathful deities as rigpa's own energy and are not frightened.

**The theological claim**: hell is not punishment. The hell realm's light is simply the consciousness of aggression solidified into a realm; it appears because aggression is your dominant habit. You go there by recognition: "this is where I belong." The wrathful deities are frightening not because they are harmful but because they are mirrors.

### Key Bardo Vocabulary

| Tibetan | Wylie | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| བར་དོ་ | *bar-do* | intermediate state — *bar* (between) + *do* (two) | 
| འཆི་ཁ་ | *'chi-kha* | the threshold of dying |
| འོད་གསལ་ | *'od-gsal* | clear light / luminosity / the dharmakāya's natural radiance |
| དམིགས་རྟོགས་ | *dmigs-rtogs* | recognition — the critical act of the bardo |
| ས་ད་བར་མ་རྟོགས་ | *ma-rtogs-pa* | non-recognition — the failure mode; the ordinary mind's failure to recognize rigpa |
| རིགས་ཀྱི་བུ་ | *rigs-kyi-bu* | "O nobly born" — the address formula of the text; literally "child of awakened family" |

### Cross-Tradition Resonance

The Bardo Thödol connects to the **Self-met-at-death** convergence (already mapped in forge-status):
- Avestan *daēnā* (the soul meets its own deeds personified as a beautiful or ugly maiden at death)
- Christian particular judgment (you meet the record of your life)
- Bardo Thödol (you meet projections of your own mind's patterns)

In all three: **death as self-encounter**. The judge is you. What you meet is what you were.

## V. Tibetan Medicine — Lü, Rlung, and Spirit Integration

### The Three Humors

Tibetan medicine (*gso-ba rig-pa*, "the knowledge of healing") is the most integrated spirit-body system in the cathedral. It does not separate physical illness from mental/spiritual cause.

The three humors (*nyes-pa gsum*, "the three faults/forces"):

| Humor | Tibetan | Wylie | Sanskrit equiv. | Seat | Function | Psycho-spiritual connection |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| *Rlung* | རླུང་ | *rlung* | *vāta* | Lower body | Movement, breath, nervous system | The humor of the mind; connected to wind-element; imbalanced rlung = anxiety, mania, disconnection |
| *Mkhris-pa* | མཁྲིས་པ་ | *mkhris-pa* | *pitta* | Liver/gallbladder | Heat, digestion, vision | Anger and ambition; excessive mkhris = aggression; balanced = discriminating wisdom (see five-poisons table) |
| *Bad-kan* | བད་ཀན་ | *bad-kan* | *kapha* | Upper body/head | Cohesion, cold, mucus | Attachment and dullness; excessive bad-kan = torpor; balanced = equanimity |

### Rlung — The Critical Concept

*Rlung* (རླུང་) is the most important Tibetan medical term for YOUSPEAK. It does not simply mean "wind" or "air" — it means:

1. **As bodily function**: breath, movement, nervous-system transmission — the force that moves things through the body
2. **As psycho-physical reality**: emotion-as-movement — feelings are *rlung* in motion through the body; you do not "have" an emotion, you experience *rlung* moving
3. **As spiritual principle**: in meditation, the *rlung* (especially the "life-bearing rlung," *srog-'dzin-rlung*) is controlled and directed into the central channel (*rtsa-dbu-ma*) — this is how meditation produces physical-spiritual transformation

The five rlungs:

| Rlung | Wylie | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| *srog-'dzin-rlung* | life-bearing | crown/heart | sustains life; the primary vital force |
| *gyen-rgyu-rlung* | ascending | chest | governs speech, swallowing, physical strength |
| *khyab-byed-rlung* | pervasive | whole body | movement of limbs, pores, muscular action |
| *me-mnyam-rlung* | fire-accompanying | navel | digestion, warmth, metabolic fire |
| *thur-sel-rlung* | descending | lower body | elimination, birth, sexual function |

The integration: the same force that breathes you is the same force that moves through channels in meditation is the same force disturbed by emotion is the same force requiring balance for health. **There is no gap between body, mind, and spirit** — they are all rlung at different scales.

### Spirit Illness — Gdon

*Gdon* (གདོན་, "spirit afflictions") — illnesses caused by spirit-interference rather than humoral imbalance:

| Type | Description | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| *gza'-gdon* | Planetary spirit affliction (from the nine planets) | Astrological ritual; protective mantra |
| *klu-gdon* | Nāga affliction (water-spirit disturbance, often from pollution of water sources) | Nāga-ransom offering (klu-gtor) |
| *bdud-gdon* | Māra affliction (the force of death and temptation) | Ransom ritual + lama's protection |
| *grib* | Defilement / contamination (from contact with corpses, blood, broken samaya) | Purification ritual; smoke-offering |

The practical integration: a Tibetan physician diagnoses by pulse, urine, and questioning — but also asks about dreams, recent encounters with the dead, broken oaths, and contamination events. The biological and the spiritual are not separate diagnostic categories.

### The *La* in Medicine

The *la* (soul-life-force, from Bon; see companion file) reappears in medicine: *la-bzlas* ("la-recall") is a medical practice for someone whose *la* has been frightened away. Symptoms: loss of vitality, pallor, inability to concentrate, nightmares — what modern medicine calls "shock" or "acute stress response." Treatment: the practitioner calls the *la* back with specific mantra, arrow-ritual (*mda'-dar*), and offerings to the spirit that captured it.

This is **the most explicit integration of shamanic soul-retrieval and medical diagnosis** in the cathedral. The Bon soul-concept (*la*) migrated intact into Buddhist Tibetan medicine.
