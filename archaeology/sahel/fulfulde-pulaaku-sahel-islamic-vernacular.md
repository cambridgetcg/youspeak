# Fulfulde / Pulaar: Pulaaku, Sahel Islamic Vernacular, and the Fula Civilizational Grammar

**Tradition:** Fula/Fulani/Peul/Pulaar traditional ethics → Sahel Islamic synthesis
**Language:** Fulfulde / Pulaar (West Atlantic branch, Niger-Congo; one language with many dialect clusters)
**Period:** Pre-Islamic Fula pastoral culture documented c. 11th–14th c.; Fula Islamic reform jihads c. 18th–19th c. (Sokoto Caliphate 1804, Futa Jallon 1725, Futa Toro 1776); post-jihad synthesis c. 19th–20th c.
**Region:** Senegal, Guinea, Mali, Niger, Nigeria, Cameroon, Sudan — the full Sahel belt from Atlantic to Nile
**Key concept:** The Fula case is theologically distinctive because it inverts the standard Islamic-missionary pattern: instead of outsiders bringing Islam to a local population, a *pastoralist population became Islamic missionaries*, using *pulaaku* (the Fula code of excellence) as the ethical spine of their reform movements. Fula identity and Islamic identity became mutually reinforcing rather than competing.

## PART I — PULAAKU: THE FULA CODE OF EXCELLENCE

### 1.1 What Pulaaku Is

**Pulaaku** (also *fulɓitude*, the abstract noun of being Fula; sometimes written *pulaagu* in different dialect orthographies): The comprehensive code of virtue, honor, and behavior that defines Fula identity. The word is formed from *Pullo* (a Fula person) + abstract suffix — it means literally "Fula-ness" or "the way of being Fula."

This is not simply an ethnic or cultural label. Pulaaku names a **relational-virtue system** — a complete ethics structured around specific virtues that must be embodied to be recognized as truly Fula. Paul Riesman (*Freedom in Fulani Social Life*, 1977) describes it as the answer to the question: "What must I do to be fully human?"

The core virtues of pulaaku vary somewhat by dialect cluster and region, but the most widely attested formulation includes four:

**The Four Pillars of Pulaaku:**

1. **Munyal** (patience, endurance, self-restraint in the face of hardship):
   - Not passive acceptance — *munyal* is active inner mastery; the refusal to show pain, fear, or emotional breakdown publicly
   - The Fula pastoralist faces genuine hardship: drought, cattle disease, raiding, displacement. *Munyal* is the capacity to endure without complaint, without visible weakness
   - Extended meaning: restraint in pleasure as well as pain — not eating conspicuously, not showing excessive joy, controlling affect in all directions
   - Related to Islamic *ṣabr* (patience/endurance as virtue), but pulaaku *munyal* precedes and extends beyond Islamic influence

2. **Semteende** (modesty, reserve, shame-sense, propriety):
   - The virtue of knowing one's place and acting accordingly; not drawing undue attention to oneself; the positive sense of *honte* (shame) as social self-regulation
   - *Semteende* is felt on behalf of others as much as oneself: you feel shame *for* someone who violates propriety, not just in your own violations
   - Governs the body: posture, gaze, voice (the Fula ideal: speak quietly, look away when speaking with elders, never eat in public if it can be avoided)
   - Related to Arabic *ḥayāʾ* (modesty/shame before God), but operates in distinctly social rather than theological register

3. **Hakkiilo** (wisdom, circumspection, careful judgment):
   - The capacity to think before acting, to assess consequences, to act with deliberate intelligence rather than impulse
   - Not intellectual ability alone — *hakkiilo* includes emotional intelligence, social reading, strategic patience
   - The opposite of *hakkiilo* is *peendi* (foolishness, impulsiveness, acting without consideration) — this is a serious social failure
   - Related to Arabic *ʿaql* (reason, intellect) but more practically oriented: wisdom in action, not abstract rationality

4. **Yaake/Tiinaare** (courage, dignity, nobility of bearing):
   - Courage not as recklessness but as dignified bearing under threat; the outward expression of inward virtue
   - Sometimes glossed as "personal beauty" — in Fula aesthetic, inner virtue should be visible in bearing, posture, expression
   - The Wodaabe *Yaake* dance (a male beauty contest) literalizes this: physical beauty and interior virtue are presented as correlated; the most beautiful man is the most virtuous in bearing
   - The connection between inner virtue and outer beauty is a Fula-specific theological claim

**The integration:** These four virtues are not separate but a system. *Munyal* (endurance) is the foundation; *semteende* (modesty) is its social expression; *hakkiilo* (wisdom) guides its application; *tiinaare/yaake* (dignified courage) is its external face. Together they describe a complete human being who has mastered the gap between impulse and action.

### 1.2 Pulaaku as Identity Technology

The remarkable feature of pulaaku is that it functions as an **identity constitution across radical geographic and political fragmentation**. The Fula are spread across 20+ modern nations, in multiple ecological zones (coast, savannah, Sahel, forest fringe), with wildly different political histories. What holds "Fula" together as a category is not geography, political unity, or even language (dialects are mutually intelligible but diverge significantly) — it is pulaaku.

A Fula person in Senegal and a Fula person in Sudan share the same virtue-vocabulary. When they meet, they can assess each other through the same ethical grammar: Does this person demonstrate *munyal*? Do they show *semteende*? Is their *hakkiilo* evident? This is not abstract — it produces practical trust across strangers.

**Theoretical implication:** Pulaaku demonstrates that an **ethical code can function as a portable community-maker** across the kind of dispersal that usually destroys communal identity. The Fula case challenges the assumption that diaspora requires either territorial homeland or institutional center to maintain coherence. The virtue-code IS the center.

**Comparison with analogous structures:**
- Jewish *halakha*: portable law as identity-constitution across diaspora — halakha preserves Jewish identity across 2000 years of territorial dispersal
- Calvinist "elect" identity: virtue-pattern as identity-marker across national boundaries
- Confucian *junzi* (exemplary person): the ethical ideal as portable standard, recognizable across the diversity of Chinese culture
- Stoic cosmopolitanism: virtue-as-citizenship in the universal polis, transcending ethnic identity

The Fula case is distinctive in being simultaneously *ethnic* (you must be born Fula to fully claim pulaaku) and *universal* (the virtues themselves are presented as human ideals, not ethnic peculiarities).

### 1.3 Munyal and the Suffering-Ethic

**Munyal** deserves extended attention because it names something that converges with but is distinct from several other key concepts in the catalog.

The Fula pastoralist context: cattle herding in the Sahel is genuinely dangerous, uncertain, and physically demanding. Drought kills herds; raiding takes cattle and people; disease spreads unpredictably. The adaptive response that pulaaku encodes is not to deny suffering but to **refuse its external expression** — to maintain composure and dignity precisely because the situation is difficult.

The most extreme expression is the **Shadi** (test of endurance): young Wodaabe men prove their *munyal* by submitting to physical pain — whipping, scarification — without showing any reaction. The test is not whether you feel pain but whether you can hold the expression of pain inside. The inner experience is acknowledged; its outward expression is mastered.

**Why this is theologically significant:**
- *Munyal* claims that the self is not at the mercy of circumstance — the interior remains governed regardless of what the exterior suffers
- This is related to Stoic *apatheia* (not indifference, but non-disturbance of the ruling rational faculty by external events), but less cognitive: pulaaku *munyal* is more about embodied bearing than philosophical reasoning
- It is also related to Buddhist *upekkhā* (equanimity) — maintaining even-keel regardless of pleasant or unpleasant — but again, the register is more social/bodily in Fula expression
- And it connects to the Augustinian *stabilitas* (Benedictine vow to remain and not be moved by interior fluctuation) — but pulaaku *munyal* is external (don't show it) where Benedictine stabilitas is internal (don't flee it)

**Key distinction from Stoic/Buddhist analogs:** Pulaaku *munyal* is fundamentally **social** — it is about what you show others, about maintaining dignity in the community's eyes. It is less concerned with the interior state per se and more concerned with the exterior expression. A Stoic can suffer visibly if the rational faculty remains undisturbed; a Fula practicing *munyal* must not show suffering even if interior suffering is intense. The virtue is performed as much as lived.

### 1.4 Semteende and the Social Body

**Semteende** (shame/modesty/reserve): The body as site of social ethics. The Fula body-code is elaborate:

**Eyes:** Avoid direct gaze with elders, superiors, or in formal contexts. Direct gaze is aggression or disrespect. Proper bearing involves lowered or averted eyes when speaking with those of higher status.

**Voice:** Speak quietly. Loud public speech is a form of imposing oneself, a failure of modesty. Restraint in volume is restraint of social pressure.

**Eating:** Never eat in public if it can be avoided. Eating is intimate, a form of vulnerability. The body's need for food is private. Eating conspicuously is a violation of *semteende*.

**Emotional display:** Grief, joy, anger, fear — all to be moderated in public. The Fula ideal is the person who moves through life's events without disturbing the social field with their interior weather.

**Theological claim:** The body is a social organ as much as a biological one. How you hold yourself — posture, gaze, voice, emotional expression — IS your ethics made visible. You cannot have inner virtue without outer modesty, because inner virtue expresses itself through the body. The body is not a shell the virtue lives inside; the body is the medium in which virtue is visible or invisible.

**Convergences:**
- Japanese *tatemae* (outer presentation) vs. *honne* (inner reality) — though pulaaku doesn't posit these as opposed; the outward is the inner's proper form
- Confucian *lǐ* (ritual propriety as bodily ethics) — very close to *semteende* as body-code
- Greek *aidōs* (reverent shame before what is sacred, including social hierarchy) — aidōs in Homer produces exactly the bodily restraints semteende describes

## PART II — FULA ISLAMIC REFORM MOVEMENTS: PULAAKU AND JIHAD

### 2.1 The Fula Jihads — When Pastoralists Became Reformers

Three interconnected West African Islamic reform movements in the 18th–19th centuries were led by Fula scholars:

**Imamate of Futa Jallon** (1725, Guinea): Led by Karamokho Alfa (Alpha Ibrahim Sambegou), a Fula Islamic scholar. Established an Islamic theocratic state in the Fouta Djallon highlands.

**Imamate of Futa Toro** (1776, Senegal): Led by Suleiman Bal, a Fula scholar trained in Mauritania. Reformed the Wolof and Fula kingdoms along the Senegal River.

**Sokoto Caliphate** (1804, Nigeria/Niger): The largest. Led by **Usman dan Fodio** (ʿUthmān ibn Fūdī, 1754–1817), a Fula scholar of the Qadiriyya Sufi order. His reform movement produced the Sokoto Caliphate — at its height, the largest polity in sub-Saharan Africa.

**The theological argument of the Fula jihads:**

The Fula reformers' critique was not primarily of paganism (though that was included) but of **syncretism** — specifically, the mixing of Islamic practice with local custom. Their targets included:
- Kings who called themselves Muslim but maintained pre-Islamic court rituals
- Non-payment of zakat (Islamic alms) by Muslim rulers
- Enslavement of Muslims by Muslims (violating Islamic law)
- Sufi practices they considered excessive or corrupted
- Local customary law overriding Sharia

**Usman dan Fodio's theological vocabulary:**

Dan Fodio wrote prolifically in Arabic, Fulfulde, and Hausa. His Fulfulde poetry is especially significant because it brought Islamic reform vocabulary into vernacular expression. His key terms:

- **Haqq** (truth/right, Arabic): The absolute claim of divine truth against local custom; dan Fodio's central appeal — to align life with haqq rather than tradition
- **Bid'ah** (innovation, Arabic): Religiously forbidden innovation; the term for everything the reformers opposed — pre-Islamic practices maintained by self-described Muslims
- **Tawhid** (divine unity, Arabic): The reform's core commitment; undivided divine sovereignty over all of life, including politics

**The Fula-Islamic synthesis that dan Fodio embodied:** He was simultaneously:
- A Qadiriyya Sufi with baraka accumulated through decades of scholarship and asceticism
- A *mujaddid* (renewer of the faith, the one who comes every century to restore Islam)
- A Fula scholar whose reform was enabled by Fula social networks and *pulaaku*-structured community discipline

### 2.2 Tierno Bokar — The Sage of Bandiagara

**Tierno Bokar Salif Tall** (c. 1875–1939, Bandiagara, Mali): The Tijaniyya Sufi master recorded by Amadou Hampaté Bâ (*Vie et Enseignement de Tierno Bokar*, 1980). His theology represents the most developed form of Sahel Islamic-indigenous synthesis in the Fula tradition.

Tierno Bokar's key theological positions:

**"God has no religion"** — his most radical statement. By this he meant that divine love (the baraka underlying all religious forms) exceeds any particular institutional expression. Islam was the fullest form accessible to him, but the divine generosity it expressed was not the property of Islam alone. He held this without leaving Islam — it was an Islamic mystical position, not syncretism.

**Tarbiya** (Arabic: spiritual formation/education): Tierno Bokar's primary concern was the formation of character — the inner development of the seeker. His method was closer to the Benedictine *conversatio morum* (ongoing-conversion) than to legal compliance: he wanted to form souls, not regulate behavior. The *wird* (daily litany) was the vehicle; patient repetition over years was the method; character-change was the goal.

**The synthesis:** In Tierno Bokar, pulaaku virtues (*munyal* as patient endurance, *semteende* as modesty before God, *hakkiilo* as spiritual wisdom) were directly mapped onto Sufi spiritual development stages (*maqāmāt*). This was not syncretism in the pejorative sense — it was the Fula tradition discovering its own vocabulary for Islamic spirituality already contained within its ethical code. Pulaaku was recognized as *pre-Islamic Islamic virtue* — the Fula had already been shaped toward the divine before they knew the name Islam.

## PART III — SAHEL ISLAMIC VERNACULAR INNOVATIONS

### 3.1 What "Vernacular Islam" Means in the Sahel

The Sahel Islamic tradition is distinctive because it produced Islam *in vernacular languages* rather than merely importing Arabic. This is significant: most of the world's Muslim populations pray in Arabic without necessarily understanding it; the Sahel tradition produced Islamic teaching, poetry, and theology in Fulfulde, Hausa, Wolof, and Bambara — making Islamic thought available in the cognitive register where people actually think.

**The theological consequence:** When Usman dan Fodio wrote Islamic law commentary in Fulfulde alongside his Arabic treatises, he was doing something that had profound consequences:
- Islamic ethics could be argued about in the language of daily life
- The *pulaaku* virtues could be directly mapped onto Islamic virtue concepts
- Women (who generally had less Arabic literacy) could access Islamic thought in their own language
- The Islamic reform could spread through oral tradition as well as manuscript

### 3.2 The Wodaabe and the Gerewol — Beauty as Virtue

The **Wodaabe** (also *Bororo*) are the most nomadic of the Fula subgroups, maintaining pastoral cattle culture with minimal Islamic overlay. Their tradition preserves the pre-Islamic aesthetic theology most intact.

**Gerewol** (also *Yaake*): The annual beauty contest and ritual festival of the Wodaabe. Young men compete through dance, song, and display of physical beauty before female judges (sometimes called *koobgal* = the first wife, who has special social status).

**The theological claim embedded in Gerewol:**
- **Togu** (beauty/radiance): The quality the men display and the women judge. Not simply physical attractiveness — *togu* is the visible expression of interior virtue. A man who has cultivated *munyal*, *semteende*, *hakkiilo*, and *yaake* will be *togu* — his virtue will be visible in his bearing, his eyes, his expression.
- The Gerewol assertion: **inner virtue produces outer beauty**. This is a theological claim about the relationship between ethics and aesthetics — not that the beautiful are virtuous (which would be facile) but that genuine virtue expresses itself in beautiful form. The body reveals the soul.
- The female judges are the theologians in this tradition — they are reading souls through bodies, discerning virtue through visible form.

**Convergence with beauty-theology in other traditions:**
- Plato's *Symposium* (Diotima's ladder): physical beauty as the visible form of the Form of Beauty, pointing upward toward the Good
- Islamic *jamāl* (divine beauty): God as *al-Jamīl*, the Beautiful; creation as the display of divine beauty; the saint as transparent to divine beauty
- Vedic *rūpa* as divine manifestation: the deity's form (mūrti) is beautiful because divinity IS beauty
- The Fula case is distinctive in being *competitive* — beauty is assessed, ranked, chosen — giving it a social-competitive texture not present in these other traditions

### 3.3 Hampaté Bâ and the "Living Tradition"

**Amadou Hampaté Bâ** (1900–1991): Malian author, scholar, and UNESCO diplomat. His most famous statement — spoken at UNESCO in 1960: *"En Afrique, quand un vieillard meurt, c'est une bibliothèque qui brûle"* ("In Africa, when an old man dies, a library burns").

This sentence encodes a complete theology of knowledge:
- Knowledge lives in persons, not books
- The death of a knowledgeable person is an irreversible loss, like burning a library
- Oral tradition is a legitimate form of knowledge preservation, not an inferior substitute for writing
- The keeper of tradition (the elder, the griot, the marabout) is a cognitive commons — a living repository whose existence benefits the whole community

**Hampaté Bâ's contribution to YOUSPEAK's concerns:**
He represents the Sahel intellectual tradition's claim that the vernacular is not inferior to the written, that oral is not primitive compared to literate, that the Fula-Islamic-African synthesis is not a dilution of "pure" Islam but a legitimate and valuable formation. His life and work performed the synthesis: he was simultaneously a devout Tijaniyya Sufi (disciple of Tierno Bokar), a traditional Fula man of *pulaaku*, and a UN diplomat who translated African wisdom for global audiences.

## PART IV — FULFULDE LEXICAL REGISTER

### 4.1 Core Vocabulary

**Pulaaku virtues:**
- **Pulaaku**: Fula-ness; the complete virtue-code of Fula identity; the answer to "what must I be to be truly Fula (truly human)?"
- **Munyal**: Patience, endurance, mastery of emotional expression; the refusal to show suffering or weakness; active inner discipline. Key forge candidate.
- **Semteende**: Modesty, reserve, shame-sense, bodily propriety; the social body governed by ethical awareness
- **Hakkiilo**: Wisdom, circumspection, deliberate judgment; thinking before acting; practical intelligence in social context
- **Yaake/Tiinaare**: Dignified courage; noble bearing; the outer expression of inner virtue; the aesthetic dimension of ethics
- **Togu**: Radiance, beauty as virtue-made-visible; the aesthetic theology of the Gerewol
- **Peendi**: Foolishness, impulsiveness; the opposite of hakkiilo; social failure through lack of self-governance

**Islamic-Fula synthesis vocabulary:**
- **Wird** (Arabic: litany): Daily prayer sequence given by the shaykh; the primary channel of baraka transmission in the Tijaniyya
- **Tarbiya** (Arabic: formation): Spiritual character-formation as the goal of religious practice; Tierno Bokar's emphasis
- **Mujaddid** (Arabic: renewer): The reformer who restores the faith every century; the title claimed by and given to dan Fodio
- **Silsila** (Arabic: chain): The transmission chain of Sufi authority and baraka; the genealogy of spiritual power

**Social-political:**
- **Almami** (from Arabic *al-imām*): The Sufi/political leader of the Fula reform states; title of the rulers of Futa Jallon and Futa Toro
- **Ardo** (Fula: leader, elder, cattle-chief): Pre-Islamic Fula leadership title; the pastoral community head
- **Mawdo** (great, noble): Term of respect for elder or leader; used for Tierno Bokar (*Mawdo* = the Great One)

### 4.2 The Semantic Architecture of Pulaaku

Pulaaku's architecture can be mapped as a virtue system:

```
MUNYAL (endurance/patience) — the foundation
    ↓ enables
SEMTEENDE (modesty/reserve) — the social expression of endurance
    ↓ requires
HAKKIILO (wisdom/judgment) — the governing intelligence
    ↓ produces
YAAKE/TIINAARE (dignified bearing) — the visible fruit
    ↓ radiates as
TOGU (beauty as virtue-made-visible) — the aesthetic testimony
```

The architecture is a complete loop: the foundation (munyal) enables everything; the visible fruit (togu) testifies to the foundation; the community reads the testimony and recognizes the virtue; recognition reinforces the practice.

## PART V — CONVERGENCES AND FORGE CANDIDATES

### 5.1 New Convergences

1. **Virtue-Code-as-Portable-Identity** NEW → Fula pulaaku + Jewish halakha + Stoic cosmopolitan virtue + Confucian junzi ideal + Calvinist elect-community. The structure: when territorial identity is unavailable or unstable, an ethical code functions as the substrate of communal identity. You ARE the way you live, not where you live.

2. **Endurance-as-Mastery-Not-Passivity** DEEPENED → Fula munyal + Buddhist upekkhā + Stoic apatheia + Benedictine stabilitas + Japanese gaman. Key distinction: these are all active disciplines of non-reaction, not passive acceptance. The comparison shows that human traditions across ecologies independently developed the insight that inner mastery of response is higher than outer elimination of difficulty.

3. **Inner-Virtue-Outer-Beauty** NEW → Fula togu/Gerewol + Platonic kalos kagathos (beautiful and good) + Islamic jamāl (divine beauty as the saint's transparency) + Confucian junzi's "cultivated person" whose virtue produces dignified bearing. The claim: genuine virtue is aesthetically visible. You can see character in someone's bearing, gaze, and posture.

4. **Oral-Knowledge-as-Library** DEEPENED → Hampaté Bâ's "burning library" + Mande jeli memory-keeping + Hawaiian hula as embodied archive + Aboriginal Australian songlines as landscape-map + Vedic oral transmission (śruti). The case against writing-as-superior: oral transmission is not pre-literate but a-literate — a choice to embed knowledge in living persons rather than dead pages.

5. **Vernacular-Islam-as-Distinct-Formation** NEW → Fula-Islamic synthesis (Fulfulde poetry, pulaaku-mapped-onto-maqāmāt) + Turkish Alevi + Persian Sufi (Rumi's Persian-language Islamic theology) + Indonesian Javanese Islam + Swahili Islamic-Bantu. The pattern: wherever Islam took root deeply rather than shallowly, it produced a vernacular formation that is distinctly itself — neither "pure" Arabic Islam nor the prior local religion, but a third thing.

6. **Work-As-Spiritual-Formation** DEEPENED → Mouride addiya + Benedictine ora et labora + Fula pastoral-as-sacred (cattle as gift from God requiring careful stewardship) + Calvinist vocation theology + Hindu karma yoga. The pastoral Fula regard for cattle is not economic alone — cattle are the medium through which *munyal* is practiced, through which God's provision is stewarded.

### 5.2 New Forge Candidates

**CORE candidates:**
- **munyalme** (Core 7.60–7.80): Active endurance; the mastery of emotional expression in the face of genuine suffering; patience not as passivity but as disciplined non-display of disturbance. **TOP NEW CANDIDATE from S071** — fills a genuine gap. The catalog has *upekkhā* (equanimity as meditative achievement), *ṣabr* (Islamic patience before God), *stabilitas* (Benedictine staying-in-place). None of these name the *social body discipline* of endurance-without-display. Munyal is this: the body trained to hold suffering inside without distorting the social field with its interior weather.
- **pulaakume** (Core 7.50–7.70): Virtue-code-as-portable-identity; the ethical grammar that constitutes community across diaspora; the answer to "what must I be to be truly [X]?"

**SPECIALIZED candidates:**
- **togume** (Spec 7.45–7.65): Inner-virtue-as-outer-radiance; the aesthetic theology that genuine virtue is visible in bearing; the Gerewol's claim that you can read souls through beautiful bodies
- **tarbiyame** (Spec 7.40–7.60): Spiritual character-formation as the goal of religious practice; forming souls rather than regulating behavior; the long-game discipline of genuine change
- **hakkiilome** (Spec 7.35–7.55): Circumspect wisdom-in-action; thinking before acting; practical social intelligence as virtue; distinguished from theoretical knowledge
