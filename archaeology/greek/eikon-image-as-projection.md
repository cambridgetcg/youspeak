# Greek — Eikōn: Image-as-Participatory-Projection

_The Greek term that named the most-developed theology of visual projection in any tradition: the icon as image that participates in its prototype, not merely as representation._

---

## Etymology of εἰκών (eikōn)

Greek εἰκών (*eikōn*): image, likeness, portrait, statue. Root in εἴκω (eikō) — "to be like, to resemble." Cognate with Latin *icon*; source of English *icon*, *iconic*, *iconography*, *iconoclasm*.

The semantic family:
- *eikōn* — image, likeness (noun)
- *eikonikos* — pertaining to images (adjective)
- *eikōnographia* — image-writing, the discipline of icon-creation
- *eikōnoklastēs* — image-breaker (the iconoclasts)
- *eikōnolatria* — image-worship (the iconoclast's accusation)
- *eikōnodoulia* — image-honor (Theodore the Studite's careful distinction: not worship of image but honor passing through image to prototype)

The Greek term covers more than mere visual-resemblance; from its earliest classical uses, *eikōn* names the image that bears-something-of the original.

---

## Genesis 1:26-27 LXX — the foundational text

The Septuagint translation of Genesis 1:26-27:

> *καὶ εἶπεν ὁ θεός, Ποιήσωμεν ἄνθρωπον κατ' εἰκόνα ἡμετέραν καὶ καθ' ὁμοίωσιν...*
>
> "And God said, Let us make humanity *according to our eikōn (image)* and *kath' homoiōsin* (according to our likeness)..."

The pair *eikōn + homoiōsis* establishes the framework for all subsequent Christian image-theology:
- Eikōn — the image (the irrevocable structural likeness)
- Homoiōsis — the likeness (the ethical-spiritual conformity to be developed)

Irenaeus (*Against Heresies* V) distinguished: humans always retain the *eikōn* (the structural image) but must grow into the *homoiōsis* (the ethical likeness). The eikōn-doctrine: every human is, structurally, an image bearing the trace of the divine.

---

## Colossians 1:15 — Christ as eikōn

Paul writes that Christ is:

> *εἰκὼν τοῦ θεοῦ τοῦ ἀοράτου, πρωτότοκος πάσης κτίσεως*
>
> "*The image (eikōn) of the invisible God, the firstborn of all creation.*"

Christ is named as the eikōn of the invisible God. This is the highest-grade use of eikōn — the divine-incarnate as the perfect image of the divine-transcendent. The image-doctrine reaches its theological apex: the invisible reaches visibility through eikōn.

Hebrews 1:3 echoes: Christ is the *charakter tēs hypostaseōs autou* (the exact-impression of his substance). Charakter is the engraver's-mark — the image that participates by exact stamp from its prototype.

## Patristic icon-theology

The patristic East developed image-theology systematically:

**Pre-iconoclastic** (3rd–6th c.):
- Origen and Athanasius use eikōn for Christ and for humanity
- Basil the Great's famous formula: "The honor paid to the image (eikōn) passes through to the prototype" (*De Spiritu Sancto* 18). This becomes the foundational defense of icon-veneration.

**Iconoclastic controversy** (730–787 and 815–843):
- Emperor Leo III's iconoclasm; subsequent destruction of icons
- Defenders: John of Damascus (*Three Treatises on the Divine Images*); Theodore the Studite (*Antirrhetici*)
- Resolution at Nicaea II (787; the Seventh Ecumenical Council): icon-veneration is canonical; icons are honored, not worshipped; veneration passes through to prototype

**Doctrinal articulation** (Theodore the Studite, *Antirrhetici* III):
- An icon is not the prototype; it is *of* the prototype
- The icon's matter (wood, paint, gold) is not divine; the icon's depicted-form participates by likeness in the prototype
- Iconography requires fidelity to canonical patterns (the prototype's depicted-features are received from the tradition)
- Veneration of icons is licit because Christ truly assumed circumscribable form (took on morphē-doulou); his form can be depicted because it was real

The key doctrinal move: **the icon participates in its prototype rather than merely represents it**. This is the patristic East's contribution to image-theology and the basis for the cathedral's eikonme doctrine.

## Modern Orthodox icon-theology

The 20th-century Orthodox renaissance developed icon-theology further:

**Leonid Ouspensky** (*Theology of the Icon*; *The Meaning of Icons* with Vladimir Lossky): the icon is a witness to the incarnation; iconography is theological discipline; the icon's reverse-perspective (where geometric lines converge toward the viewer rather than away) is a deliberate theological-aesthetic choice that signals the icon's reaching-toward-the-viewer rather than the viewer's reaching-into-pictorial-depth.

**Paul Evdokimov** (*The Art of the Icon*): the icon as eschatological-visual; the saint-as-depicted is shown in their glorified state; the icon participates in the eschaton.

**John Meyendorff** + **Alexander Schmemann**: icon-theology as integral to liturgical-theology; the icons within the Divine Liturgy are not separate aesthetic objects but co-projections in the multi-modal synthesis.

The Orthodox icon-tradition is the deepest single-medium theology of visual projection in any tradition. The cathedral receives it primarily.

## Cross-tradition resonance — image-as-participatory-projection

Every developed tradition has its eikonme-equivalent. The convergence (shared with morphame's convergence at `convergences/multi-modal-projection-of-meaning.md`):

- **Catholic religious imagery** — particularly Marian iconography (the Madonna types: Hodegetria, Eleousa, Orans, etc.), Sacred Heart imagery, the saints' iconography. Less doctrinally-developed than Orthodox but structurally similar.

- **Buddhist Buddha-rūpa** — the sculpted/painted Buddha-form is made specifically to enable practice. Tibetan thangka tradition develops detailed iconography (specific deities have specific colors, mudras, ornaments, attributes); the thangka is consecrated through a *rab nas* ritual that "installs" the deity in the image. The Buddha-image participates in the Buddha; misdirected veneration was a controversy in Buddhist history similar to Christian iconoclasm.

- **Hindu murti** — the murti is the form of the deity for *darśan* (sacred-seeing). The *prāṇa-pratiṣṭhā* ritual installs the deity's prāṇa (life-breath) in the murti. The murti is not merely sculpted bronze; it participates in the deity's presence. The Vaiṣṇava and Śaiva traditions have detailed murti-theology paralleling Orthodox icon-theology.

- **Tibetan thangka** — see Buddhist above; specifically detailed iconographic canons (the *Kalachakra* mandala, the wrathful and peaceful deities, etc.).

- **Yoruba sacred sculpture** — the bronzes, beadwork, ritual masks, ibeji-twin figures: each carries the orisha's specific iconography. The sculpture is consecrated and participates in the orisha's presence.

- **Japanese mandala and Buddha-imagery** — the *taimitsu* (Tendai esoteric) and *Shingon* iconographic traditions; specific Buddhas and Bodhisattvas with specific iconographic signatures; mandala-painting as cosmic-projection.

- **Egyptian sacred imagery** — the *medu-netjer* hieroglyphs themselves are images (the hieroglyph is iconic in classical sense — the symbol bears resemblance to its referent). The temple-walls' carved imagery participates in the divine action depicted.

- **Maya glyphic-imagistic synthesis** — the Maya glyphs integrate logographic-syllabic writing with iconographic representation; the king's portrait is also his name; the king IS depicted-in-image as participation in his royal identity.

- **Calligraphic-iconographic Islamic traditions** — though aniconic in theology, Islamic calligraphy treats the divine Names visually with extraordinary care; specific calligraphic styles (Kufic, Naskh, Diwani) carry theological weight.

The pattern is universal across traditions that have allowed images. Even traditions that restrict imagery (Reformed Christianity, much of Islam) have parallel doctrines about word-as-image or absence-as-meaningful-form. The image-as-participatory-projection is universally recognized where it is allowed.

## The semantic core for eikonme

What eikōn carries that nothing else does:

- **Participatory image**: the image is not mere representation; it participates in its prototype. This is patristic icon-theology's distinctive contribution; the Greek term carries it.
- **Likeness-bearing**: the image bears something of the original (*charakter* — the engraver's mark); the projection is structural, not decorative.
- **Canonical form**: iconography requires fidelity to received form; the prototype's depicted-features are received from the tradition.
- **Veneration-licit**: the image can be honored; honor passes through to prototype (Basil's formula). The image is theologically active.
- **Cosmic scope**: humanity is created kat' eikona theou; Christ is eikōn theou; the eikōn-doctrine reaches from anthropology through Christology.

Adding Sumerian *me*:

- The participatory image is *me*-grade: the form is received from the tradition (icon-canons); the depicted-prototype is received from the realm; the capacity to make and to venerate icons is bestowed
- The icon-as-completed-work is the cosmic-gift of the divine prototype's reaching through visible form to the beholder

**eikonme** = the image understood as received-ordinance participatory-projection: visual morphame specialized to the image-medium; what Orthodox iconography, Buddhist Buddha-rūpa, Hindu murti, Tibetan thangka, Yoruba sacred sculpture, and parallel image-traditions all are structurally.
