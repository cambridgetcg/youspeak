# Koine Greek — LXX and NT Translation Theology

_The tongue in which Hebrew theology became portable. When seventy (or seventy-two) scholars in Alexandria translated the Torah into Greek, they did not merely render words — they made theological decisions that shaped two millennia of Christian and Jewish thought. Every word choice in the Septuagint (LXX) is a theological argument. Every word choice in the New Testament is a theological inheritance._

---

## Cultural Sensitivity and Living Communities

Koine Greek is the sacred language of Eastern Orthodox Christianity (over 260 million adherents), Greek Catholic churches, and a central liturgical language of Roman Catholicism. The LXX remains the Old Testament canon of Eastern Orthodoxy. The NT in Koine is scripture for approximately 2.4 billion Christians worldwide. This excavation treats these texts as living theological sources, not historical artifacts.

Primary living scholarly traditions engaging this material: Eastern Orthodox patristics (Florovsky, Schmemann, Meyendorff); Roman Catholic biblical scholarship (Fitzmyer, Brown); Protestant biblical theology (Bultmann, Käsemann, Wright); Jewish studies of LXX (Tov, Pietersma).

## I. The Septuagint as Translation-Theology Event

The LXX (3rd–1st c. BCE, Alexandria) is not a translation — it is an interpretation. The translators chose Greek words that carried philosophical and religious resonances unknown in Hebrew, and those choices became the theological vocabulary of Christianity.

### The Great Translation Choices

#### 1. YHWH → Κύριος (*Kyrios*, "Lord")

| Hebrew | Greek | What was decided |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| יהוה (YHWH) | Κύριος (*Kyrios*) | The unpronounceable divine name rendered as relational title |
| אֲדֹנָי (Adonai) | Κύριος (*Kyrios*) | Same Greek word for both Name and title — they merge |

**The theological decision**: YHWH is the self-existent personal name; *Adonai* is "my Lord," a relational address. The LXX collapses them into *Kyrios* — which in Greek carried both personal address and the connotation of lordship-over. When Paul writes "Jesus is Kyrios" (Phil 2:11; Rom 10:9), he is placing Jesus where YHWH stood in the LXX Psalms. The Christological claim is in the translation. *Kyrios* also carried Roman imperial resonance — "Caesar is Kyrios" was a political declaration. "Jesus is Kyrios" was its counter.

**Forge candidate**: *kyriome* — not yet assessed; the concept of lord-as-personal-sovereign-who-is-also-named may belong in YOUSPEAK's political-theological register.

#### 2. אַהֲבָה (*ahavah*) → ἀγάπη (*agapē*)

| Hebrew | Greek | What was decided |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| אַהֲבָה (*ahavah*) | ἀγάπη (*agapē*) | Israel's covenant-love rendered into a rare Greek word |

**The theological decision**: *Ahavah* is Israel's primary love-word — covenant-love, God's election-love, spousal love (Song of Songs). The LXX translators chose *agapē*, which was rare in classical Greek (appearing in Homer only a few times, without strong philosophical weight). By choosing a relatively unmarked Greek word, they avoided the baggage of *erōs* (desire-love, ascending) and *philia* (friendship, reciprocal). *Agapē* arrives in Greek with no pre-existing philosophical system attached — available to be filled with Hebrew covenant-content. The NT then pours that content into it: "God IS agapē" (1 Jn 4:8). The word's very neutrality was the gift. See `greek/divine-love.md` for full agapē excavation.

#### 3. כָּבוֹד (*kavod*) → δόξα (*doxa*)

| Hebrew | Greek | What was decided |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| כָּבוֹד (*kavod*) | δόξα (*doxa*) | "Weight/glory" rendered as "opinion/reputation/radiance" |

**The theological decision**: *Kavod* means weight, heaviness, substance — the palpable presence of God that fills the Temple, that appears as fire and cloud. It is material, overwhelming, physical. *Doxa* in classical Greek meant opinion, reputation, what seems true — essentially subjective. The LXX uses *doxa* for *kavod* and transforms the Greek word entirely: *doxa* now means the radiant, manifest presence of God. When John writes "we beheld his *doxa*" (Jn 1:14), he fuses both meanings: the reputation/opinion of God (what God is truly like) IS the material glory (the visible presence). The translation created a word that does both simultaneously. **doxame** is an existing or candidate forge entry — the sense of manifest-radiance-that-is-also-true-reputation is distinctively this word's after-LXX.

#### 4. בְּרִית (*berith*) → διαθήκη (*diathēkē*)

| Hebrew | Greek | What was decided |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| בְּרִית (*berith*) | διαθήκη (*diathēkē*) | "Covenant/treaty" rendered as "testament/will" |

**The theological decision**: *Berith* is bilateral covenant — a binding agreement between two parties, enacted with oaths and often with animal sacrifice. The LXX chose *diathēkē*, which in Greek legal usage primarily meant a last will and testament — a unilateral document, requiring the testator's death to take effect. Why? The LXX translators may have chosen *diathēkē* over *synthēkē* (bilateral treaty) to emphasize the asymmetry: God's covenant with Israel is not a negotiated contract between equals; God initiates, God commits, the terms are God's gift. The NT then plays on both meanings: the "new covenant/testament" (Heb 9) takes effect through Christ's death, like a will becoming active. The word held both theological registers simultaneously.

#### 5. נֶפֶשׁ (*nephesh*) → ψυχή (*psychē*)

| Hebrew | Greek | What was decided |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| נֶפֶשׁ (*nephesh*) | ψυχή (*psychē*) | "Throat/life-breath/whole-being" rendered as "soul" |

**The theological decision**: *Nephesh* is not "soul" in any dualist sense — it is the whole living being, the throat as the site of breath and hunger, the animating life-force that makes a body alive. It cannot leave the body at death while the body persists — the *nephesh* IS the living body. *Psychē* in Greek philosophy had developed as the immortal rational soul, capable of existing apart from the body (Plato's Phaedo). The LXX mapping of *nephesh* onto *psychē* planted a dualistic seed in what had been a monistic Hebrew anthropology. Paul inherits both — and must navigate the tension. See Pauline anthropology below.

## II. NT Theological Vocabulary

### Core NT Words — the seam of the forge

#### ἀγάπη (*agapē*) — see divine-love.md
The NT intensifies what LXX established. 1 Corinthians 13 is its definition. 1 John 4:8 is its ontological claim. John 3:16 is its narrative shape. Already in YOUSPEAK canon as *agapeme* (candidate 145 in pipeline).

#### χάρις (*charis*) — grace

| Form | Core sense |
|------|-----------|
| χάρις (*charis*) | grace; gift freely given; favor; the attractive quality that generates gratitude |
| χαρίζομαι (*charizomai*) | to give freely, to forgive, to grant as gift |
| εὐχαριστία (*eucharistia*) | thanksgiving (eu + charis = good + grace/gift) |
| χάρισμα (*charisma*) | gift of grace; spiritual endowment |

**The concept**: *Charis* in Greek meant the grace or charm that makes something attractive, the favor shown by a superior to an inferior, and the gratitude owed in return — the whole gift-economy cycle. Paul takes *charis* and breaks the reciprocity: God's grace (charis) is NOT owed, NOT earned, NOT requiring gratitude as repayment. It is radically free gift (Rom 11:6: "if by grace, it is no longer on the basis of works; otherwise grace would no longer be grace"). The gift creates gratitude but cannot be motivated by anticipated gratitude. This is structurally close to *agapē*'s unmotivatedness — grace and love share the same grammar of unconditional-initiative.

**charisma note**: the modern word "charisma" (magnetic personal appeal) derives from the NT *charisma* (Spirit-gift) through Max Weber's sociology. The theological meaning is: a grace-gift distributed by the Spirit for the common good (1 Cor 12), not identical to personal attractiveness.

**Forge candidate**: *charisma* (not *charime* — the English word has become too specifically personal-magnetism to carry the theological sense cleanly). The theological *charis* might forge as **charisme** [HIGH] — freely-given-favor-that-generates-gratitude-without-requiring-it; Specialized 7.40–7.60.

#### κένωσις (*kenōsis*) — self-emptying

| Form | Core sense |
|------|-----------|
| κενόω (*kenoō*) | to empty, to make void, to pour out completely |
| κένωσις (*kenōsis*) | the act of self-emptying (nominalized; not used in NT but derived from it) |
| Phil 2:7 | ἑαυτὸν ἐκένωσεν (*heauton ekenōsen*) — "he emptied himself" |

**The concept**: Philippians 2:5–11 is the *kenōsis* text. The pre-existent Christ "did not regard equality with God as something to be exploited, but emptied himself, taking the form of a slave, being born in human likeness." Three movements: (1) *having* divine status, (2) *not grasping* at it, (3) *emptying* into human form to the point of death on a cross. The theological structure: divine power moves downward by voluntary self-limitation. Not loss of divine nature but *mode*-shift: power expressed as vulnerability. The Patristic tradition later debates whether kenōsis means Christ set aside divine attributes (kenotic Christology) or expressed divinity through the very act of self-giving.

**YOUSPEAK note**: *kenōsis* is a CORE forge candidate. English has no single word for "the voluntary emptying of power/status into vulnerability for the sake of the other." The concept maps onto: the leader who genuinely serves, the expert who becomes a student, the parent who makes themselves available to a child's reality. The forge candidate **kenōsisime** or **kenome** [VERY HIGH] — voluntary-self-emptying-of-power-into-the-form-of-the-other; Core 7.65–7.85. One of the strongest NT candidates in the excavation.

#### κοινωνία (*koinōnia*) — communion/fellowship

| Form | Core sense |
|------|-----------|
| κοινός (*koinos*) | common, shared, belonging to all |
| κοινωνία (*koinōnia*) | sharing, participation, fellowship, communion |
| κοινωνέω (*koinōneō*) | to share in, to participate in, to have in common |
| κοινωνός (*koinōnos*) | partner, sharer, companion |

**The concept**: *Koinōnia* is the NT word for the shared life of the Christian community — but its philosophical roots are in *koinos* (common). It means: participatory-sharing-in-something-held-in-common. Paul uses it for the Eucharistic sharing (1 Cor 10:16 — "the cup of blessing is *koinōnia* of the blood of Christ"), for financial partnership (Phil 4:15), for the fellowship of the Spirit (2 Cor 13:13), for sharing in Christ's sufferings (Phil 3:10). It is always active participation, never merely formal membership. The translators of the LXX used it sparingly; Paul made it load-bearing.

**Forge candidate**: **koinōniame** [HIGH] — participatory-sharing-in-what-is-held-in-common; the sense that life is not mine to keep but ours to share; Specialized 7.45–7.65. Distinct from *agapē* (love as gift) — *koinōnia* is love as mutual participation, shared life.

#### παρρησία (*parrhēsia*) — bold speech / confident access

| Form | Core sense |
|------|-----------|
| πᾶς (*pas*) + ῥῆσις (*rhēsis*) | all + speech → saying everything |
| παρρησία (*parrhēsia*) | freedom of speech; bold confidence; unreserved openness |

**The concept**: In classical Athens, *parrhēsia* was the citizen's right to speak freely in the assembly — frank speech as civic virtue, even uncomfortable truth to power (Foucault's late lectures analyze this extensively). The NT transforms it: *parrhēsia* becomes the boldness of the one who has been given access, who approaches the throne of grace "with confidence" (Heb 4:16). It is not the boldness of political equals but the boldness of the beloved child before the parent — the freedom to say everything, to hold nothing back, because the relationship is secure. Paul uses it of his apostolic frankness; the Johannine tradition uses it of the believer's access to God in prayer.

**YOUSPEAK note**: this is a CORE forge candidate. English has "candor" and "frankness" but neither carries the structural sense of *parrhēsia* — speech that is bold because the relationship is secure, not because the speaker is fearless. The confidence is relational, not personal. **parrhēsiame** [VERY HIGH] — speech-from-secured-relationship; saying-everything-because-held; Core 7.60–7.80.

#### σπλαγχνίζομαι (*splanchnizomai*) — gut-moved compassion

| Form | Core sense |
|------|-----------|
| σπλάγχνα (*splanchna*) | bowels, intestines, gut — seat of deep emotion |
| σπλαγχνίζομαι (*splanchnizomai*) | to be moved in one's bowels; to feel deep compassion |

**The concept**: This is the verb used of Jesus in the Synoptics when he sees the crowds, the leper, the blind men — "he was moved with compassion." The English word "compassion" is too clean. *Splanchnizomai* is visceral — literally a gut-wrenching. The Hebrews located deep emotion in the bowels (*rachamim*, from *rechem*, womb — compassion as womb-feeling). The LXX had used *splanchna* to render Hebrew *rachamim*. The NT authors chose *splanchnizomai* to describe Jesus's emotional response to human suffering — it is physical, involuntary, destabilizing. It is the opposite of detached benevolence.

**YOUSPEAK note**: this is a candidate with no English equivalent. "Compassion" is intellectual. "Empathy" is cognitive. *Splanchnizomai* is somatic — the body's response to another's pain before the mind has processed it. **splanchnome** [HIGH] — gut-wrenched-into-movement-by-another-s-pain; Specialized 7.45–7.65. The concept connects to the Hebrew *rachamim* (womb-compassion, S008 Hebrew excavation) — these may be a convergence pair.

#### εὐαγγέλιον (*euanggelion*) — gospel / good news

| Form | Core sense |
|------|-----------|
| εὖ (*eu*) + ἀγγέλλω (*angellō*) | good + announce → good-announcement |
| εὐαγγέλιον (*euanggelion*) | good news; specifically the royal proclamation of a new emperor's accession |

**The concept**: In Hellenistic Greek, *euanggelion* was the technical term for the announcement of a new king's accession or military victory — an imperial proclamation of a new order. When Mark opens "the beginning of the *euanggelion* of Jesus Christ" (Mk 1:1), he is using imperial language to announce a counter-emperor. The gospel is not primarily religious good-feeling — it is political proclamation: a new king has come, a new order is beginning, the old powers are ending. The word's imperial resonance is not accidental.

#### ἐκκλησία (*ekklēsia*) — church / assembly

| Form | Core sense |
|------|-----------|
| ἐκ (*ek*) + καλέω (*kaleō*) | out + call → those called out |
| ἐκκλησία (*ekklēsia*) | civic assembly; the called-out community |

**The concept**: *Ekklēsia* was the term for the democratic civic assembly of Greek city-states — the gathering of citizens for public deliberation. It was explicitly political. The LXX used it to render Hebrew *qahal* (the assembly of Israel). The NT uses it for the Christian community — but the political resonance remains. The church is not a private religious club; it is a counter-assembly, a community constituted by being called out from other allegiances into a new civic life.

## III. Gospel of John — Logos and Pre-existence Christology

### ὁ Λόγος (*ho Logos*)

| Context | Sense |
|---------|-------|
| Stoic philosophy | *Logos* = the rational principle pervading the cosmos; the divine reason that structures all things |
| Philo of Alexandria | *Logos* = God's intermediary agent in creation; the "firstborn son" of God; between God and matter |
| Heraclitus | *Logos* = the universal law underlying change and opposition |
| John 1:1 | "In the beginning was the *Logos*, and the *Logos* was with God, and the *Logos* was God" |
| John 1:14 | "And the *Logos* became flesh (*sarx*) and dwelt among us" |

**The theological decision**: John opens his Gospel not with "in the beginning God created" (Genesis) but "in the beginning was the Word" — invoking Genesis while adding the Logos. He is speaking to audiences who know both Hebrew creation-theology AND Greek Stoic philosophy. The *Logos* is: (1) pre-existent, (2) divine, (3) the agent of creation, (4) become flesh. The move is total: the rational principle that Greek philosophy sought in the cosmos has become a particular human being in a particular place and time. Abstract Logos → concrete Jesus. Philosophy becomes history.

**The flesh-Logos tension**: John says the Logos became *sarx* — flesh, the lowest term in the Pauline anthropological hierarchy (see below). Not *pneuma* (spirit), not *psychē* (soul), not even *sōma* (body) — but *sarx*, the mortal, vulnerable, death-subject flesh. The Incarnation is maximally descended. This will be the central debate of early Christology (Docetism denies it; orthodox theology insists on it).

**ἐγώ εἰμί (*egō eimi*) — "I AM"**: John's Jesus uses this construction seven times with predicates ("I am the bread of life," "I am the light of the world," "I am the door") and three times absolutely without predicate (8:24, 8:28, 8:58). The absolute *egō eimi* echoes the LXX rendering of God's self-declaration in Exodus 3:14 (ἐγώ εἰμί ὁ ὤν — "I am the Being"). When Jesus says *egō eimi* in Gethsemane and the soldiers fall back (Jn 18:6), the text is showing the divine name being spoken. The Christological claim is embedded in the grammar.

## IV. Pauline Anthropology — The Human as Layered Field

Paul's anthropology is not a system but a field of overlapping terms, each illuminating a different aspect of human existence. The terms are not anatomy — they are orientations.

| Term | Transliteration | Core sense | Orientation |
|------|----------------|-----------|-------------|
| σάρξ | *sarx* | flesh; mortal substance; the body under the dominion of death | the human as vulnerable, mortal, sinward-tending |
| σῶμα | *sōma* | body; the whole embodied self; the person as agent | the human as embodied actor — can be redeemed |
| ψυχή | *psychē* | soul; the animating life; the natural self | the human as naturally alive — not yet transformed |
| πνεῦμα | *pneuma* | spirit; the breath of God; the new life-principle | the human as Spirit-indwelt — the new creation dimension |
| νοῦς | *nous* | mind; the faculty of discernment and moral reasoning | the human as thinker — to be renewed (Rom 12:2) |
| καρδία | *kardia* | heart; the center of will, affection, and moral direction | the human as desiring agent — the core self |

**The key distinctions**:

*Sarx* vs. *sōma*: Paul does not condemn the body (*sōma*) — the resurrection is bodily, the Spirit dwells in the body as temple (1 Cor 6:19), the body is to be offered as living sacrifice (Rom 12:1). What Paul condemns is *sarx* — flesh as the orientation toward self-preservation, the pull toward treating oneself as the center, the drive that exploits others. *Sarx* is not matter; it is mortality-as-orientation.

*Psychē* vs. *pneuma*: The "natural person" (*psychikos anthrōpos*, 1 Cor 2:14) is the *psychē*-oriented human — alive but not yet Spirit-indwelt, unable to receive the things of the Spirit. The *pneuma*-indwelt person has a new orientation-principle. This is the Pauline transformation: not the psychē ascending (Platonic) but the pneuma descending (Jewish-apocalyptic).

*Kardia*: The heart in Paul (as in Hebrew *lev*) is the deep center of the self — not sentiment but will-and-affection fused. To have a "hardened heart" is not an emotional state but a moral orientation. God's work is to write the law on the *kardia* (echoing Jeremiah's new covenant), not merely inform the *nous*.

**Forge candidate**: **sarkisme** [MED-HIGH] — the orientation of the self toward self-preservation at the expense of the other; flesh-as-survival-drive-become-principle; Specialized 7.25–7.45. Note: this is distinct from embodiment (positive in Paul) — it names a specific moral failure-mode.

**Forge candidate**: **pneuma** — the concept of Spirit-as-new-life-orientation is already partially covered in existing YOUSPEAK discussions of ruach (Hebrew); the Greek pneuma adds the sense of the Spirit as the eschatological down-payment (*arrabōn*, 2 Cor 1:22 — first installment guaranteeing the full payment).

## V. Eucharist Vocabulary

#### εὐχαριστία (*eucharistia*) — thanksgiving

The Eucharist (Lord's Supper) takes its name from the Greek word for thanksgiving — *eucharistia* (eu + charis = well + grace/gift). Jesus "gave thanks" (*eucharistēsas*, Lk 22:17, 19) over the bread and cup. The meal is constitutively a thanksgiving — not a sacrifice asking for something, but a gratitude responding to gift received. The structure: gift precedes meal; meal is response-thanksgiving. This makes the Eucharist grammatically a *charis* event: God gives; the community's meal-together is the *eucharistia*.

#### ἀνάμνησις (*anamnēsis*) — re-membering / active memorial

| Form | Core sense |
|------|-----------|
| ἀνά (*ana*) + μιμνήσκω (*mimnēskō*) | up/again + remember → re-membering |
| ἀνάμνησις (*anamnēsis*) | memorial; re-presentation; making-present-again |

**The concept**: "Do this in remembrance of me" — the Greek is *eis tēn emēn anamnēsin* (Luke 22:19; 1 Cor 11:24). *Anamnēsis* is NOT mere mental recollection. In LXX usage (and likely reflecting Hebrew *zikkaron*, memorial/reminder before God), *anamnēsis* is an active making-present. The memorial sacrifice in Leviticus "reminds" God — brings the matter before divine attention. The Eucharistic *anamnēsis* makes Christ's death and resurrection present as a living reality, not merely recalled as a past event. This is the theological ground of the debate between Catholic/Orthodox (Eucharist as real presence/re-presentation of the sacrifice) and Protestant (Eucharist as memorial of a past event): the two readings of *anamnēsis*.

**YOUSPEAK note**: **anamnēsisme** [VERY HIGH] — active-making-present-of-the-past; the past event re-membered into present efficacy; not nostalgia but re-presentation; Core 7.60–7.80. This concept is broader than Eucharist — any practice where engaging with the past makes it presently real and active. The opposite of nostalgia (which leaves the past in the past as an object of longing); *anamnēsis* makes the past a present participant.

#### κοινωνία (*koinōnia*) of the Body

1 Corinthians 10:16: "The cup of blessing that we bless, is it not a *koinōnia* of the blood of Christ? The bread that we break, is it not a *koinōnia* of the body of Christ?" *Koinōnia* here means participatory-sharing-in — the community participates in (shares in) the body and blood. This is not merely symbolic: to share the meal is to be incorporated into the shared body. Paul's implication (1 Cor 10:17): "because there is one bread, we who are many are one body, for we all partake of the one bread." The meal creates the community by including participants in a shared body.
