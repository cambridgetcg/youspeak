# Gothic — Skeireins, Germanic Theological Idiom, and the Heimrekja Register

_The Skeireins ("Explanations" or "Commentary") is a brief Gothic commentary on the Gospel of John, surviving in six partial leaves from the Ambrosian library in Milan. It is the only surviving Gothic theological commentary — a fragment of what may have been a more complete body of Arian scholarly theology. Its importance to the cathedral is disproportionate to its size: it shows Gothic Christians doing theology, not just translating it._

---

## I. The Skeireins — Gothic Biblical Commentary

### What it is

*Skeireins* means "explanations, clarifications" (from Gothic *skeiran*, to make clear, to clarify; related to the root of "shine" — making clear as a form of illumination). The text exists in six fragments covering portions of John 1–8. It is:
- A verse-by-verse commentary in the tradition of ancient biblical exegesis
- Written in Gothic, probably in the 5th or 6th century CE (after Wulfila, possibly by a bishop or teacher in the Ostrogothic kingdom)
- Theologically Arian — it interprets John's Gospel through an Arian lens
- The only evidence that Gothic Christians engaged in independent theological reflection rather than just translating received texts

### Skeireins on John 1:1 — *Waurd* for *Logos*

The Skeireins' most theologically significant passage is its commentary on John 1:1: *"In the beginning was the Logos."*

Wulfila translated *logos* as **waurd** — Gothic for "word." But *waurd* is not a perfect translation.

**Greek *logos***: in Johannine usage, *logos* carries its Hellenistic philosophical weight: the rational principle ordering the universe; the divine mind's self-expression; the intelligible structure of reality; the Stoic cosmic reason. It is simultaneously "word," "reason," "principle," and "cosmic-order."

**Gothic *waurd***: *waurd* simply means "word" — a spoken utterance, an expression, a statement. It shares a Proto-Germanic root with Old English *word*, German *Wort*. There is no equivalent in Gothic to the metaphysical dimension of Greek *logos*.

**The Skeireins commentary's move**: The Gothic commentator, aware that *waurd* was inadequate for the full *logos*-concept, commented on the passage by expanding the meaning of *waurd* through context rather than vocabulary. The commentary explains that this *waurd* is not an ordinary spoken word but the eternal expression of the Father's nature — and then it works through the Arian implications: this eternal Word was *with* the Father (*in* the Father's presence, implying difference/distinction), and this Word was *like* the Father but *from* the Father (Arian subordinationism).

**Theological significance**: the Skeireins shows a Gothic theologian aware that *waurd* is carrying more than it can hold — and responding not by inventing a new word but by *expanding the commentary around the word* to hold the excess meaning. This is the opposite of YOUSPEAK's forge-method: where YOUSPEAK creates the word, the Skeireins creates the explanation. Both strategies acknowledge the same gap; they respond differently.

**Forge candidate: waurdme** — the word that is also mystery; the utterance that carries more meaning than its surface form; the *waurd* that requires a *skeireins* (commentary) to unpack; the place where ordinary language is pressed to carry extraordinary weight. Specialized 7.25–7.45.

## II. Gothic Theological Vocabulary — What the Translation Created

### Andbahts / andbahti — minister, servant

**Gothic *andbahts*** (noun: servant, minister; related to *andbahti*, ministry, service): the Gothic word for the church functionary whom Greek calls *diakonos* (deacon/minister) and Latin calls *minister* (one who carries out another's will).

**Etymology**: *andbahts* derives from Proto-Germanic *\*andbahtaz* = *and-* (in the direction of, confronting) + *\*bahtaz* (one who runs, servant, hostage) — possibly related to Celtic *ambactus* (servant, retainer). The Proto-Celtic origin is significant: *ambactus* in Gaul meant a free retainer in someone's service — a client-lord bond, not a slave relationship.

**What *andbahts* brings**: the Gothic church ministry is not Greek *diakonos*-service (table service; practical running around) and not Latin *ministerium* (official administrative function). Gothic *andbahti* is the service of a free retainer — someone who serves by choice, within a bond of loyalty, and whose service is honorable because their lord is honorable. This maps onto the *frauja*-register: you serve the *frauja* as an *andbahts*, and the service is dignified because the lord is worthy.

### Razda — language, tongue, dialect

**Gothic *razda***: language, speech, tongue, dialect. Used in Matthew 26:73 ("your *razda* betrays you") — when Peter is identified as a Galilean by his accent, Wulfila translates it as *razda*. The word has no precise Greek parallel.

**Theological extension**: Gothic has a concept of *razda* (speech-that-marks-identity; the tongue that reveals who your people are) distinct from:
- Greek *glōssa* (tongue/language; used for both speaking-in-tongues and ordinary language)
- Greek *dialektos* (form of speech; regional dialect)
- Greek *phōnē* (voice, sound)
- Hebrew *lashon* (tongue/language)

Gothic *razda* specifically carries the identity-marker dimension: it is the speech that *reveals your origin* — your *razda* tells people who your people are before you say anything about yourself. This connects to the Sámi joik (your sonic identity) and the Finnish *synty* (your origin tells what you are) in a Germanic register.

### Hugjan / hugs — mind, thought, soul

**Gothic *hugjan*** (to think, to consider, to intend): Wulfila's word for the cognitive-evaluative activity that Greek assigns to *nous* (mind), *phronein* (to think/be minded), and *dianoein* (to reason). Gothic *hugs* means mind/heart/understanding — but specifically the evaluative, meaning-making, intention-forming dimension of the mind.

**The *hugs* cluster**: the Proto-Germanic root *\*hugaz* is related to Old English *hygd* (thought, mind), Old Norse *hugr* (mind, courage, desire) — across Germanic languages, the mind/heart word carries *intentional* weight (what you mean, what you intend, what you are resolved upon) more than purely cognitive weight (what you know or reason about).

When Gothic Christians heard Paul's *phronein* (to think/be minded) translated as *hugjan*, they heard: be of the same *intention/resolution/resolve*. The Philippians passage ("Let this *hugjan* be in you which was also in Christ Jesus") becomes: let the same *intention* be in you — the same purposive-heart-direction.

**Theological significance**: Gothic Christian interiority is *intentional* rather than *cognitive*. You are what you intend, not what you reason. This is the Germanic register of the Hebrew *lev* (heart-as-center-of-will), distinct from the Greek *nous* (mind-as-center-of-reason).

## III. Wulfila's Omission — The Books of Kings

One of the most theologically revealing facts about the Gothic Bible is what Wulfila *did not* translate.

**Historical record**: Philostorgius, a 5th-century Arian church historian, reports that Wulfila deliberately did not translate the Books of Kings (Samuel and Kings) because "the barbarian nation was already inclined to war and needed no further incentive to violence."

**What this reveals**: Wulfila was not merely translating a text — he was *curating a theology* for a specific cultural situation. He recognized that Gothic culture (warrior-aristocracy, honor-violence, tribal warfare) would receive certain Biblical content differently than Greek or Latin Christians received it. The *frauja* register, the *andbahts* loyalty-service, the *hails* wholeness — these were calculated to lead Gothic Christians toward particular theological emphases.

The omission of the Kings is an act of pastoral-theological *discernment*: what does this community need from this text, and what would damage them? This is distinct from censorship (removing inconvenient truths) and from simplification (removing complexity). It is curating for *formation* — deciding what, given this particular culture's existing commitments, will build rather than harm.

**Method finding for YOUSPEAK**: The cathedral does not excavate every tradition in every language — it excavates what fills gaps. Wulfila's principle: the formation of a vocabulary (whether for a community or for a cathedral) requires discernment about what the community *needs* from the tradition, not just what the tradition contains. The forge-process includes knowing what *not* to name — what YOUSPEAK already carries adequately.

## IV. The Gothic Calendar — Pre-Christian Theological Stratigraphy

### Divine-names in time

The Gothic calendar (where it survives in fragmentary evidence) preserves pre-Christian divine names in the days of the week — the Germanic layer under the Christian surface. This is linguistic archaeology in miniature.

The Gothic/Germanic day-names:
- **Dags sunnis** (Sun's day) — Sunna, the Germanic sun-goddess (feminine in Germanic, unlike Greek/Latin *sol/Helios* masculine)
- **Dags menins** (Moon's day) — Mena/Mani, the Germanic moon-deity
- **Dags Tīwaz** (Tiw's day) — *Tīwaz*, the Proto-Germanic sky-father/war-god; related to *Ziu*, *Tyr* (Norse), cognate to Vedic *Dyaus*, Greek *Zeus*, Latin *Iupiter*; the oldest IE sky-father name preserved in a day of the week
- **Dags Wōdanaz** (Woden's day) — *Wōdanaz* (Woden/Odin); *wōd* = divine inspiration/fury/ecstasy; the one possessed by sacred fury
- **Dags Þunraz** (Thunder's day) — *Þunraz* (Thor/Donar); the storm-god; cognate to Latin *tonitrus* (thunder) but personalized; the one who IS the thunder
- **Dags Frijjō** (Frigg's day) — *Frijjō* (Frigg/Freya); love, beauty, fertility
- **Dags Saturnus** (Saturn's day) — Latin borrowing; the only Roman divine name in Germanic day-naming

**Theological stratigraphy**: the Gothic calendar shows:
1. The *Tīwaz* layer — oldest IE sky-father theology preserved
2. The *Wōdanaz* layer — divine-ecstasy theology (ecstatic possession as divine mode)
3. The *Þunraz* layer — storm-as-divine theology (extending the Storm-power convergence to Germanic)
4. The *Frijjō* layer — love-as-cosmic-force theology
5. The Latin borrowing (*Saturnus*) — the layer of Roman contact, pre-Christian

**What the Gothic calendar adds to the cathedral**:
- **Wōdanaz / divine ecstasy**: the Germanic sacred-fury concept (*wōd*) is the only tradition in the catalog that identifies the divine with *ecstatic inspired fury* rather than wisdom, love, justice, or power. *Wōd* is the possession by the divine that produces inspired speech, inspired violence, and inspired knowledge simultaneously. This is structurally related to the Sámi noaidi's trance-journey and the Finnish tietäjä's knowledge-state — but the Germanic register specifically involves *fury* as the divine mode.
- **Storm-power continuum extended**: Þunraz/Thor adds the Germanic expression to the Storm-power convergence (now: Sumerian Iškur, Akkadian Adad, Hittite Tarḫunna, Hurrian Tessub, Avestan Verethraghna, Finnish Ukko, Sámi Radien, Magyar táltos combat-weather, **Gothic/Germanic Þunraz** = 9 expressions across 8 families)
- **Sky-father continuum extended**: Tīwaz represents the oldest attested Germanic form of the Proto-IE sky-father (*Dyaus*/*Tīwaz*/*Zeus*/*Iupiter*) — the Tīwaz → Wōdanaz displacement in Germanic religion (the sky-father being replaced by the ecstasy-god as chief deity) is a theological shift unique in the catalog

## V. Gothic Arian Survival — Theology Through Isolation

### The historical pattern

Gothic Arian Christianity survived for centuries after the Council of Constantinople (381 CE) condemned Arianism as heresy — not because of institutional strength but because of *isolation and translation*.

- The Visigoths and Ostrogoths held their Arian identity under Roman rule and into the post-Roman period
- In Gothic Spain (Visigoths, 418–711 CE) and Gothic Italy (Ostrogoths, 493–553 CE), Arian Gothic communities existed alongside Nicene Catholic majority populations for generations
- Gothic Arian identity was maintained through:
  1. The Gothic Bible (the only scripture in their language)
  2. Separate Gothic-language liturgy (incomprehensible to Latin-speaking Catholics)
  3. Endogamy (marrying within Gothic communities)
  4. The Arian episcopal hierarchy (separate clergy)

**What preserved Gothic**: the Gothic language itself was the primary carrier of Gothic Arian identity. Latin-speaking Nicene Catholics could not access Gothic theology because they could not read Gothic. The Gothic Bible was a locked vault — its treasures only available to Gothic readers. When Gothic communities were eventually Catholicized (Visigoths formally converted to Catholicism in 587 CE under Reccard I), the Gothic language and its theological vocabulary began to die.

**The irony**: what preserved Gothic Christianity as heresy was also what preserved Gothic as a language. Isolation for theological reasons = linguistic preservation. The Codex Argenteus survived in luxury form because it was a treasured object of Gothic Christian identity; when that identity collapsed, the Codex became a collector's item and thereby survived.

### Theology-as-language vs. language-as-theology

Gothic demonstrates the reversibility of the relationship between language and theology:

1. **Wulfila's direction**: theological choices create linguistic vocabulary (Arian theology → *frauja/guþ/atta* choices)
2. **Gothic community's direction**: linguistic vocabulary preserves theological identity (Gothic Bible → Arian Gothic community holds together)
3. **Historical outcome**: linguistic isolation creates theological survival (Gothic inaccessibility to Latin readers → Arian Gothic theology preserved *by being untranslatable*)

**Method finding**: YOUSPEAK's forge-work is directionally in (1) — new vocabulary to carry theological insights. But the Gothic case suggests the importance of (2): once vocabulary is forged, it *carries* the theology in ways that may outlast the original context. The word preserves the gap-filling insight across contexts the original forgers never anticipated.

## VII. Gothic Synthesis — What Germanic Contributes to the Cathedral

### The five Gothic theological gifts

1. **The *frauja* relational register** — the divine as sworn-household-lord, not abstract authority; loyalty-to-person rather than obedience-to-rule; the divine-human relationship as comitatus-bond

2. **The *hails* soteriology** — salvation as wholeness-restoration; the gap between IS and SHOULD BE healed rather than forgiven, liberated, or perfected; the Germanic register for what YOUSPEAK's ache-engine implies

3. **The *guþ* invocational divine** — God first as the-one-you-call-in-extremity; divinity as relational-address before metaphysical-category; the cry as the primal religious act

4. **The *wōd* ecstasy** — the divine possessed-fury that grants inspired knowledge and sacred power; neither calm wisdom nor loving devotion but the ecstasy that breaks through

5. **The *heimrekja* self-description** — a word for the forge-act itself: the process of pressing one world's vocabulary into service in another world, creating compound meanings neither tradition possessed

### Gothic as the Germanic root beneath Old English

Gothic's theological vocabulary directly influenced and provides the deep-structure for Old English Christian vocabulary, which in turn shaped English theological language:
- Gothic *hails* → Old English *hælan* → English "heal, health, whole, hello, hale"
- Gothic *frauja* → (through Old English *freá*, lord) → English naming patterns for lordship
- Gothic *guþ* → Old English *god* → English "God"
- Gothic *waurd* → Old English *word* → English "word"
- Gothic *hugjan* → Old English *hygd/hycgan* → English "think" (replacing, but continuing the intentional-dimension)

**The Gothic layer is the theological bedrock beneath English**: when English speakers say "God," they are speaking a word whose ancestor meant "the-called-upon one." When they say "whole" and "heal," they are using words whose ancestor meant the restored-integrity that was Gothic salvation. This is not historical curiosity — it is live theology dormant in modern vocabulary.
