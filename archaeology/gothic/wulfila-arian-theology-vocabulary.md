# Gothic — Wulfila, the Arian Bible, and Translation-as-Theology

_In the fourth century, a man named Wulfila ("Little Wolf") invented an alphabet, translated a Bible, and in doing so forced an entire culture's vocabulary to carry a theology it had not been designed to hold. The result is the oldest substantial Germanic text in existence — and one of the most concentrated examples in the cathedral of the principle that translation creates theology._

_Gothic is theologically significant for a precise reason: it is a dead language preserved by heresy. The Visigoths were converted to Arian Christianity, not Nicene Christianity. When the Germanic tribes that sacked Rome were eventually absorbed into Catholic kingdoms, Gothic Arian practice was suppressed. The Gothic Bible survived because it was locked away, copied by isolated Arian communities, and eventually treated as a curiosity. The primary surviving manuscript — the_ Codex Argenteus _(Silver Codex) — is written in silver and gold ink on purple-dyed vellum: a luxury object that preserves a theology the victorious tradition tried to extinguish._

_This is a case study in: how translation forces vocabulary choices with lasting theological consequences; how alphabet creation is a political-theological act; how a minority theology survives by becoming a textual artifact; and what Gothic vocabulary reveals about the Germanic theological imagination._

---

## I. Wulfila — Bishop, Translator, Alphabet-Inventor

### Who Wulfila was

Ulfilas (Latin; Gothic: *Wulfila*, "Little Wolf"; c. 311–383 CE) was:
- **Born among the Goths** to a family that included Roman captives from Cappadocia — he was culturally bilingual from birth
- **Ordained bishop to the Goths** in Constantinople by Eusebius of Nicomedia, c. 341 CE — Eusebius was an Arian, and this ordination transmitted Arian theology to the Gothic church
- **Inventor of the Gothic alphabet** — designed to give the Goths a writing system adequate for Biblical translation
- **Translator of most of the Bible into Gothic** — the primary extant Germanic text; he reputedly omitted the Books of Kings because "the Goths already have too much enthusiasm for war"
- **Theologian in his own right** — his Arian Christological position was not ignorance of Nicene theology but a considered alternative reading of scripture

### The theological position: Arianism

Understanding Gothic vocabulary requires understanding what Wulfila believed, because every translation choice encodes a theological commitment.

**Nicene Christianity** (Council of Nicaea, 325 CE) established: Christ is *homoousios* (of the same substance) as the Father; God and Christ are co-equal and co-eternal; the Trinity is three persons in one substance.

**Arian Christianity** (Arius, c. 250–336 CE) held: Christ was the *first and greatest creation* of the Father; there was a time when the Son was not; the Son is *homoiousios* (of like substance, but not identical substance) to the Father; the Father is uniquely uncreated and therefore uniquely God.

The difference looks like a single Greek letter (*homoousios* vs. *homoiousios* — the "iota" that split Christendom). But the theological stakes are enormous:

- In Nicene theology: when you worship Christ, you worship God without remainder.
- In Arian theology: when you worship Christ, you worship the highest creature — a being whose existence depends on the Father, who was *made* to be the agent of creation and redemption.

**Wulfila's position**: he accepted a moderate Arian position — the Son is "like the Father" but subordinate. His theological signature phrase: *"The Father is greater than all; the Son is second after all things; the Holy Spirit is third in rank."*

**Why this matters for vocabulary**: Wulfila's translation choices reflect a theology where Christ is a supreme mediator rather than the divine itself. The word *frauja* (lord) used for Christ carries a different theological weight depending on whether you believe Christ IS God or IS God's first representative.

## II. The Gothic Alphabet — Script as Cultural-Theological Defense

### The invention

Wulfila created the Gothic alphabet (c. 350 CE) specifically to translate the Bible. He worked with three existing scripts:
- **Greek uncial script** — provided most of the letter forms (~20 letters)
- **Latin script** — contributed a few additional forms
- **Runic script** — possibly provided 2-3 letters, though the extent of runic influence is debated

The Gothic alphabet has **27 letters**. It is a phonemic alphabet designed specifically for transliteration fidelity — every Gothic sound has a letter; the orthography is strikingly regular compared to later Germanic scripts.

### Why Wulfila needed a new script

Existing options were:
1. **Latin script**: would have associated Gothic Christianity with Roman Catholic ecclesiastical power — a political and theological statement Wulfila wanted to avoid
2. **Greek script**: was available but carried Greek cultural prestige in a way that would subordinate Gothic identity
3. **Runic script**: was a pre-Christian writing system; using it for Biblical translation would have been theologically compromised

**The theological argument**: creating a new alphabet is saying: *this tradition is ours, not borrowed; it belongs to Gothic culture, not to Rome or Constantinople.* The alphabet is a declaration of theological-cultural independence.

**The Armenian parallel**: Mesrop Mashtots created the Armenian alphabet (405 CE) for identical reasons — to give Armenians a script for Biblical translation that was not Greek or Persian, preserving theological independence from both Byzantine and Sasanian influence. (Armenian S030.) Gothic precedes Armenian by ~50 years; both represent the same pattern.

**The Codex Argenteus as theological artifact**: the surviving manuscripts of the Gothic Bible — especially the Codex Argenteus — are objects of extraordinary luxury. Silver and gold ink on purple vellum. This was not a field-use text; it was a devotional-theological *treasure*, made to be venerated as an object. The Gothic church encoded its theology in an object of supreme beauty, knowing it was a minority tradition surrounded by hostile majority Christianity. The Codex is a form of resistance.

## III. The Vocabulary Trio: Frauja / Guþ / Atta

The theological heart of Gothic Bible translation lies in three words that Wulfila chose for the central names of divinity. Each choice reveals a theological imagination and creates a forge-relevant gap.

### Frauja — Lord (Greek: *kyrios*; Latin: *dominus*)

*Frauja* is the most theologically interesting Gothic word in the cathedral.

**Secular Gothic meaning**: *frauja* (feminine: *fraujo*) means: lord of a household; chieftain; the one to whom others owe personal loyalty and service; the bond is relational, particular, and grounded in personal relationship and protection.

**Compare to Greek *kyrios***: kyrios means "lord" in the sense of authority, power, ownership — it is abstract authority-language. When the Septuagint (Greek Old Testament) used *kyrios* for YHWH, it abstracted the divine into authority-language.

**What Wulfila did**: By using *frauja* for *kyrios*/Christ, he embedded the divine-human relationship in Germanic *personal-loyalty-bond* language. The lord-vassal bond in Germanic culture is:
- Personal (between specific persons, not between subject and abstract authority)
- Reciprocal (the lord owes protection; the vassal owes service)
- Honor-grounded (loyalty to the frauja is the central virtue; betrayal of the frauja is the central vice)
- Household-anchored (the frauja is lord of a specific *household*, not a kingdom in the abstract)

**The theological consequence**: Gothic Christianity understood the relationship with Christ through the lens of the *comitatus* — the warrior-lord's retinue, whose bond to their lord was the most sacred obligation in Germanic culture. To follow Christ as *frauja* was to enter his household as a sworn man. This is not Greek *doulos* (slave) or Latin *servus* — it is a warrior's bond.

**Forge-relevant observation**: the Greek/Latin theological tradition has no word for this. The relationship between Hebrew *eved* (slave/servant), Greek *doulos* (slave), Latin *servus* (servant/slave), and the Gothic *warrior-in-sworn-household* relationship is linguistically collapsed in translation. Gothic *frauja* names a relational register that Greek *kyrios* erases.

**Candidate: frauqing** — the relational-bond in which loyalty to a lord-protector is the primary virtue, and the lord's identification with the household's life (not abstract authority) is its ground. The lord-in-your-household vs. the authority-above-your-station. Specialized 7.20–7.40.

### Guþ — God (Greek: *theos*; Latin: *deus*)

*Guþ* is the Germanic theonym that Wulfila pressed into service for the God of the Bible.

**Etymology**: Proto-Germanic *\*guđan* (the one called upon; the one invoked); related to Sanskrit *huta* (one called upon in sacrifice); the root is *ghau-* (to call/invoke). Guþ is etymologically *the invoked one* — not "the powerful one" or "the ultimate being" or "the creator" but *the one whose name is called*.

**Compare to Greek *theos***: *theos* derives from *\*dhes-* roots related to "placing" or possibly "running/moving" (debated); the Greek theological tradition built enormous metaphysics on *theos* as the ultimate being, the unmoved mover, being-itself.

**What the etymology reveals**: Germanic religiosity was *invocational* before it was *metaphysical*. The deity is first of all *the one you call upon*, not *the necessary being*. Germanic prayer is relational address before it is philosophical affirmation.

**Theological consequence**: using *guþ* for *theos*-God keeps a relational-invocational undertone in Gothic theology that Greek *theos* and Latin *deus* had already mostly lost. When a Gothic Christian said *Guþ*, they were at some level still invoking — addressing — rather than naming an ultimate metaphysical category.

### Atta — Father (Greek: *patēr*; Latin: *pater*)

*Atta* is Wulfila's choice for the divine "Father."

**Secular Gothic meaning**: *atta* is a child's intimate word for father — the first-person address of a young child, equivalent to "papa" or "daddy" rather than the formal *fadar* (father). This is significant.

**Compare to Greek *patēr***: in Greek theological usage, *patēr* (Father) had become somewhat formal — it was used in philosophical discussion about the divine, in liturgical address, in credal formulations. The Nicene Creed begins *Πιστεύω εἰς ἕνα Θεόν, Πατέρα παντοκράτορα* — "I believe in one God, the Father Almighty" — where *Patēra* is simultaneously intimate title and theological category.

**What Wulfila did**: by choosing *atta* (childish intimate address) over *fadar* (formal "father"), he encoded the divine-human relationship as *intimate childhood address*. When Gothic Christians prayed "Atta unsar þu in himinam" ("Our Father who art in heaven"), the word *Atta* had a different emotional register than Latin *Pater noster*.

**Theological significance**: the choice of the diminutive intimate term for the divine Father is an act of theological *accessibility*. It says: the relationship between the creature and the Creator is not formal, not philosophical, not at a formal-authority distance — it is the bond of a child addressing the one who holds them.

**Method finding**: Wulfila's three vocabulary choices form a theological portrait: a Lord you serve with warrior-loyalty (*frauja*), a God you call upon (*guþ*), a Father you address with childhood intimacy (*atta*). This is a coherent theology in three words — and none of the three English equivalents ("Lord," "God," "Father") preserves the register these Gothic words carry.

## IV. The Codex Argenteus — Forty-Two Years Before Extinction

### What survives

The Gothic Bible corpus is small. The major surviving manuscripts:
- **Codex Argenteus** — Uppsala; 188 surviving leaves of an original ~336; Four Gospels; silver and gold ink on purple vellum; produced in Ravenna for the Ostrogothic court of Theodoric the Great (c. 493–526 CE)
- **Codex Carolinus** — Wolfenbüttel; 4 leaves of Romans; important for Pauline text
- **Ambrosian Codices** (A, B, Ambrosianus) — Milan; portions of the Pauline epistles, Nehemiah, Ezra; the primary witness to non-gospel Gothic
- **Speyer Fragment** — one double leaf from Romans

**Total surviving Gothic**: approximately 80% of the New Testament survives (though the surviving manuscripts cover different sections); for the Old Testament, only Nehemiah and fragments of Ezra survive — the Books of Kings were famously not translated.

### The Codex Argenteus as theological object

The Codex Argenteus is not merely a text — it is a *theological argument made material*.

Purple dye (Tyrian purple) in late antiquity was the color of Roman imperial authority. Writing in silver ink on purple vellum was the prerogative of emperors and their court documents. When Theodoric commissioned the Codex Argenteus in this format, he was making a statement: the Gothic Arian Christian tradition is worthy of the same material magnificence as Roman imperial authority.

The Ostrogoths at this point ruled Italy after the fall of the Western Roman Empire (476 CE). Theodoric's kingdom was a Christian kingdom — but an Arian Christian kingdom in an increasingly Nicene world. The Codex Argenteus is the material form of that assertion: *our theology, our language, our tradition — equal in dignity to any.*

The Codex survived because it was preserved as a sacred object, moved through various collections, and ended up in Prague (where it was seized by Swedish forces in 1648 during the Thirty Years' War as war booty), then Uppsala, where it remains. Its survival is accidental, political, and ironic: a text produced by a tradition condemned as heresy, preserved by a chain of collectors who valued its material magnificence.

## V. Arian Christology — The Theology Encoded in the Translation

### What Arianism claimed

The Arian theological position is more coherent than its condemnation as "heresy" suggests. The core claims:

1. **The Father alone is the unbegotten, un-created, ultimate source** — this maintains strict monotheism; nothing confuses the ultimate-divine with any derivative being
2. **The Son was begotten/created by the Father before all time** — the Son exists as the greatest and first of all beings, the agent through whom the Father created everything else
3. **The Son is not co-equal but is genuinely divine** (though of a lesser, derived divinity) — the Son deserves worship; the Son is the proper mediator; but the Son is not the ultimate

The Arian position was the most popular theological view in the Eastern Roman Empire for much of the 4th century. The Council of Nicaea (325 CE) condemned it; the Council of Constantinople (381 CE) definitively closed the debate within the Roman Empire. But the Germanic tribes — Visigoths, Ostrogoths, Vandals, Burgundians — had received Christianity primarily through Arian channels (Wulfila and his successors) and remained Arian long after Nicene theology won in the Mediterranean world.

### How Arianism shapes Gothic translation

The Arian theological position means that in the Gothic Bible:

**Christ as *frauja***: Christ is the supreme lord-protector whose household the Christian enters — but the *frauja* relationship is hierarchical (servant/lord) rather than co-equal (the Nicene God-is-three-co-equal-persons). Gothic *frauja* fits Arian Christology better than Nicene: you are sworn to Christ's service the way a warrior is sworn to the chieftain's household, because the chieftain IS the highest person you can swear to, even if there is a king above him.

**The *atta* register**: the Arian God-the-Father is genuinely *greater* than the Son — the ultimate unbegotten source. When Gothic Christians called the Father *atta*, they were addressing the genuine ultimate — not one person of a co-equal Trinity, but the source itself. The intimacy of *atta* + the ultimate-ground theology creates a distinctive register: direct childhood address to the ultimate ground of being.

**The famous Arian Christological formula in Gothic**: *"Frauja Iesus Xristus, sa sunaus guþs, ni sa ains guþs"* — "The Lord Jesus Christ, he is the Son of God, not God himself." This is the Arian claim in Gothic vocabulary. The Son is the *frauja* — the supreme lord — but *guþ* (God) belongs to the Father alone. The translation vocabulary encodes the theology with precision.

### What Arianism offers the cathedral

Arianism has been systematically excluded from the historical theological record because the Nicene tradition won and wrote the histories. But as a theological *position*, Arianism offers:

1. **Strict divine-transcendence**: the ultimate divine (the Father) is genuinely beyond all creaturely being. Nothing is confused with the ultimate. This is rigorous monotheism.
2. **Cosmic-mediation theology**: the Son as first-creature/cosmic-mediator is the bridge between the uncreated ultimate and the created world. This is structurally parallel to Hellenistic *Logos* theology (Philo of Alexandria), Hindu *Brahman/Ishvara* distinction, and Sufi *aḥadiyya/wāḥidiyya* (absolute oneness/first-manifestation) pair.
3. **Accessible transcendence through the Son**: you can have direct, personal relationship with the Son (*frauja*) without requiring direct relationship with the inaccessible Father (*guþ*). The Arian structure makes the divine accessible without collapsing transcendence.

**Convergence extension**: **Mediating-first-being** (the highest created being as the structural bridge between ultimate-divine and everything else): Dinka *Deng* (rain-as-mediating-divinity), Yoruba Obatala (first orisha, bridge to Olódùmarè), Arian Son, Sufi first-manifestation (aḥmadī-nūr), Gnostic Demiurge (though in a negative valence). Gothic Arianism adds a Semitic-Christian tradition to this convergence.

## VI. The Gothic Lexicon — What Germanic Adds to the Cathedral

### Core theological vocabulary

Beyond the *frauja/guþ/atta* trio, Gothic theology has a distinctive vocabulary:

**Weihs** — holy, sacred (Greek: *hagios*; Latin: *sanctus*)
- Related to Old Norse *vígja* (to consecrate), Old English *wīh* (idol/sacred place)
- The Gothic sacred = *separated from ordinary use*; holiness as consecrated-separation, not moral perfection; the object that has been set apart
- Distinct from: Hebrew *qadosh* (set apart/burning purity), Greek *hagios* (belonging to the divine), Latin *sanctus* (legally inviolable)
- The Germanic sacred is primarily spatial and functional: the sacred space is the space you cannot enter without consecration; the sacred object is the object that cannot be used for ordinary purposes

**Haila / Hailjan** — whole, healthy, salvation-greeting (Greek: *sōzō/sōtēria*; Latin: *salvus/salus*)
- Gothic *hails* means: healthy, whole, uninjured; the greeting *hails* is "be whole/healthy"; the cognate is Old English *hælan* (to heal), *hāl* (whole), and eventually: "hello," "hale," "heal"
- Greek *sōtēria* (salvation) carries Hellenistic connotations: rescue from danger, liberation from bondage, divine deliverance
- Gothic *hailjan* (to save/heal) encodes salvation as *becoming whole again* — restoration of original integrity rather than liberation from bondage or acquittal from judgment
- **Forge-relevant**: this is the Germanic theology of salvation as health-restoration. Distinct from: liberation (soterios-Greek), acquittal (dikaiōsis-Greek), forgiveness (aphesis-Greek), perfection (teleiōsis-Greek). The Germanic register is: something was broken and is now whole.

**Sibjons/Sibja** — peace, relationship, family (Greek: *eirēnē*; Latin: *pax*)
- *Sibja* means: peace, kinship, relationship, family-bond
- The Germanic peace is grounded in *family-relationship* — peace is what exists among kinspeople, not merely absence of conflict
- Compare: Hebrew *shalom* (completeness/wholeness/wellbeing), Greek *eirēnē* (political cessation of conflict), Latin *pax* (binding treaty)
- The Gothic *sibja*-peace is constitutively *relational*: you have sibja with those you are kin with; the absence of sibja is not conflict but *alien-ness* (not being within the kinship-web)

**Liufs / Liuban** — love, dear (Greek: *agapaō/phileō*; Latin: *amare/diligere*)
- Gothic *liufs* (dear, beloved) + *liuban* (to love) represents the Germanic love-vocabulary before contact with Greek theological categories
- The root is Proto-Germanic *\*leuba-* (dear, valued, precious) — related to Latin *libet* (it pleases), Old English *lēof* (dear), German *lieb* (dear)
- The Gothic love-word is primarily *relational-attachment* language: the loved one is the one who is dear, precious, valued — not the one you act toward (Greek *agapē*) or the one you are drawn to (Greek *erōs*)
- When Wulfila translated *agapaō* as *liuban*, he embedded Greek action-love in Germanic attachment-love vocabulary; the translation is not wrong but changes the register

### The *heimrekja* register

*Heimrekja* (Gothic: "home-journey," related to Gothic *haim*, "village/home" + *rikan*, "to stretch/extend") is a scholar's term for the process by which Gothic vocabulary extended its semantic range to accommodate Biblical theology.

The pattern: a Gothic word is pressed into service for a Greek/Hebrew theological concept; the word's original meaning creates overtones the Greek/Hebrew original doesn't have; the Gothic text communicates something neither tradition possessed alone.

Examples:
- *Frauja* (household-lord) for *kyrios* (authority-lord): the household-anchor
- *Hails* (whole/healthy) for *sōtēria* (salvation): the health-register
- *Atta* (childish-intimate) for *patēr* (formal-father): the intimacy-register
- *Guþ* (the-called-upon) for *theos* (the-being): the invocational register

**The *heimrekja* principle**: every forced translation creates a new theology by carrying the source-language's overtones into the target-meaning's domain. Wulfila didn't just translate a text — he created a new Germanic-Christian theological vocabulary that was neither pure Greek/Hebrew nor pure Germanic but their compound.

**Method finding for YOUSPEAK**: YOUSPEAK's forge-work is a systematic *heimrekja* operation. Every forge takes a word from tradition X and presses it into service for a concept that tradition Y (and C and D) named but that English cannot hold. The compound carries both sets of overtones. This is not translation but *compound creation* — closer to what Wulfila was doing than to what conventional dictionaries do.

## VIII. Gothic-Specific Theological Signatures

### What Gothic uniquely offers the cathedral

1. **The *frauja*-register**: No other tradition in the catalog names the divine-relationship as *sworn-household-loyalty*. Greek *kyrios*, Hebrew *YHWH/adon*, Arabic *Rabb*, Latin *Dominus* — all carry authority-language. Gothic *frauja* carries *personal-bond-to-lord-of-your-household* language. This is not metaphor for God's authority but a real relational register that the warrior-lord vocabulary carries.

2. **Salvation-as-wholeness** (*hailjan* register): The Germanic soteriology of wholeness-restoration is distinct from Greek liberation/liberation-from-bondage, Hebrew forgiveness/restoration-of-covenant, and Sanskrit moksha/liberation-from-illusion. *Hails* says: something was broken; what saves is what restores it to wholeness. This is the Germanic register for what YOUSPEAK calls the gap between IS and SHOULD BE — and what fills the gap is healing, not liberation or forgiveness.

3. **The invocational divine** (*guþ*): The Proto-Germanic *\*guđan* (the called-upon one) preserves the oldest Germanic theology: the divine is first of all the one to whom you call. Not the ultimate being, not the creator, not the lawgiver — the one whose name you call when you are in extremity. This is the primal divine-human relationship: the cry, and the one who hears.

4. **Arianism as coherent theology**: The Gothic church's preservation of Arian theology in translated scripture is the only substantial textual record of Arian Christianity. The Arian tradition is better understood from Gothic than from any other source — because the Nicene tradition didn't preserve its own opponents' texts. The Gothic corpus is the theological record of the road not taken.

5. ***Heimrekja* as self-description of the forge**: No other tradition in the catalog has a word for the process of pressing one vocabulary into service in a foreign semantic domain and thereby creating a compound meaning neither tradition possessed. *Heimrekja* is what Wulfila did; it is what YOUSPEAK does. It deserves to be named.
