## 1. Overview and Scholarly Landscape

The Cherokee people — who call themselves **Aniyvwiya** (ᎠᏂᏴᏫᏯ, "the Real People" or "the Principal People") — are among the largest Indigenous nations of North America. Their traditional homeland spans the southern Appalachian Mountains: present-day western North Carolina, eastern Tennessee, northern Georgia, and adjacent areas. The Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians holds ancestral land at Qualla Boundary, North Carolina; the Cherokee Nation and United Keetoowah Band are headquartered in Oklahoma, descendants of the Forced Removal of 1838-39 ("The Trail of Tears," Nunna daul Tsuny — "the trail where they cried").

Current tribal enrollment exceeds 400,000 across the three federally recognized bands. Cherokee is a Southern Iroquoian language — distinct from the main Iroquoian cluster in New York; the Southern branch separated thousands of years ago and moved south. The language survives primarily among elderly speakers in North Carolina and Oklahoma, with revitalization programs ongoing.

**The Linguistic Structure**

Cherokee (Tsalagi Gawonihisdi, ᏣᎳᎩ ᎦᏬᏂᎯᏍᏗ) is polysynthetic and verb-heavy — single words can encode complex events with multiple participants. The language uses a system of eleven vowels (seven oral, four nasal), four tones (high, falling, low, rising), and grammatical prefixes encoding speaker-hearer relationships, directionality, and animacy. Crucially for YOUSPEAK: Cherokee encodes whether an action moves toward or away from the speaker (directional prefixes), whether the subject is animate or inanimate, and whether plural entities are acting separately or together. These grammatical distinctions encode theological commitments about relationship and agency.

**The Griaule Problem Does Not Exist Here**

Unlike the Dogon tradition (subject of a devastating fieldwork critique), Cherokee religious knowledge is well-attested through multiple independent channels:

1. **Mooney's collection (1891)**: James Mooney's "Sacred Formulas of the Cherokees" (BAEB 7:301-397) — directly obtained from Cherokee medicine men who wrote their formulas in the syllabary and gave Mooney access after careful relationship-building. The formulas are original documents.

2. **Kilpatrick corpus**: Jack and Anna Kilpatrick collected hundreds of additional medicinal and ceremonial manuscripts in the 1960s, published as *Friends of Thunder* (1964) and *Walk in Your Soul: Love Incantations of the Oklahoma Cherokees* (1965).

3. **Ethnobotanical records**: Mooney and Olbrechts' *The Swimmer Manuscript: Cherokee Sacred Formulas and Medicinal Prescriptions* (1932) provides a comprehensive account from A'yûñinï ("Swimmer"), one of the most important Cherokee medicine-man informants.

4. **Living tradition**: Unlike many extinct-language traditions, Cherokee ceremony continues. The Eastern Band maintains the Sacred Fire, conducts the Green Corn Ceremony annually, and has active didahnvwisgi practitioners today.

**Primary Scholars**

- **James Mooney** (1861-1921): First systematic collector of Cherokee ceremonial literature. Deeply respected by informants; his "Myths of the Cherokee" (BAEB 19, 1900) and "Sacred Formulas" remain primary sources.
- **Raymond Fogelson** (1933-2011): Definitive anthropologist of Cherokee culture; extensive work on Booger Dance, witchcraft beliefs, and Cherokee social structure.
- **Jack and Anna Kilpatrick**: Mid-20th century collectors who expanded the formulaic-prayer corpus significantly.
- **Duane Champagne**: Sociological analysis of Cherokee political theology and constitutional structure.
- **Clyde Ellis, Luke Lassiter, Gary Dunham**: Contemporary editors of Native American religious studies including Cherokee sources.
- **Willard Walker and James Sarbaugh**: Historical analysis of the syllabary adoption.

## 2. The Three Worlds — Cherokee Cosmology

Cherokee cosmology centers on **three realms** in dynamic relationship:

### The Upper World (Galunlati / ᎦᎷᏛ)

Galunlati is the sky-world — the realm of order, pattern, perfection, and fire. It is the source of the normative: what things should be, how relationships should properly function. The word derives from *galun-* (sky, above) with a locative suffix; it is both a place and a quality of being.

The Upper World is associated with:
- **Red** and **white** (triumph, purity, harmony)
- **East** and **South** (success, peace)
- **Fire** — specifically the sacred council fire maintained by the Thunders
- Regularity: the Sun's path, the seasons, the ceremonial calendar
- The Thunderers (Ani-Hyuntikwalaski — "Those Who Live Above") — divine beings who bring rain and control weather

### This World (Elohi / ᎢᏯᎳᏫ or Elohino)

Elohi is the earth-world — the human realm of impermanent, always-contested balance. The Great Island floats on primordial water, suspended by cords attached to the vault of the Upper World. When the cords age and break, the island will sink again into the water.

This World is neither purely good nor purely evil but a field of tension between the forces of the Upper and Under Worlds. Human ceremonial life is the practice of maintaining or restoring the proper balance — tilting toward the Upper World's order while acknowledging the Under World's chaotic vitality.

Elohi is associated with:
- **Green and brown** — the colors of vegetation, of this-world life
- All four directions simultaneously — the cardinal cross pattern that appears throughout Cherokee ceremonial art
- The seven clans and the council system — the human attempt to replicate Upper World order

### The Under World (Elati / the River Below)

The Under World lies beneath the surface of Elohi, associated with underground rivers, the aquatic realm, and the forces of disruption, transformation, and chaos. It is not simply evil — it is the necessary counterforce to Upper World order. Certain beings (the Uktena serpent, the Tlanuwa thunderbird, the Dakwa giant fish) inhabit the boundary zones.

The Under World is associated with:
- **Blue and black** (trouble, death)
- **West** and **North** (decline, cold, danger)
- Water, caves, underground rivers
- The color-direction-spirit system that structures formulaic prayer

**The Cosmic Cross**

The four directions form a sacred cross (the "medicine wheel" concept, though that specific term is more associated with Plains traditions). East=red=success; South=white=peace; West=black=death; North=blue=defeat. Cherokee formulaic prayers typically invoke this directional-color grid, placing the prayer's need in relation to each pole. Healing prayers call on Red and White (East and South). Harmful conjuring summons Blue and Black. Most ceremonies involve navigating all four.

## 3. Unetlanv — The Great Apportioner

**Unetlanv** (ᎤᏁᎳᏅᎯ, sometimes written Yowa) is the supreme principle in Cherokee theology. The name is typically translated "The Apportioner" or "The Great One" or "God" in missionary literature — but the etymology reveals more.

*Unetlanv* derives from *u-net-lanv*:
- *u-* (third person) — what belongs to or comes from a third party
- *net-* from the root for "to apportion, to give each their proper share, to allot"
- *-lanv* (a nominalizing element indicating an agent or force)

Unetlanv is the One Who Portions Out — the cosmic allocator who assigns each being its proper place, function, and measure. The concept parallels Akkadian **šīmtu** (fate-as-decree) and Avestan **xvarnah** (divine portion/destiny-light), but where those are decrees issued by a supreme god, Unetlanv IS the apportioning itself — a cosmic principle more than an anthropomorphic deity.

**Yowa**

The most sacred name for this supreme force is **Yowa** (sometimes spelled Yehowa in syllabary-influenced transliterations), which certain scholars have traced to the syllabic combination of three sacred names — but this etymology is contested and possibly influenced by later Christian missionary contact. What is clear: Yowa is treated as a name too sacred for ordinary use, analogous to the Jewish treatment of YHWH.

**Asgaya Galunlati — "The Man Above"**

A distinct (or related) being is Asgaya Galunlati ("The Man Above" or "Man Who Dwells in the Sky"). In some accounts, Asgaya Galunlati is the agent-form of Unetlanv — the personal face of the cosmic principle. In post-contact accounts, Asgaya Galunlati became heavily identified with Christ, making it difficult to recover the pre-contact theology precisely.

What is recoverable: Asgaya Galunlati is associated with:
- Thunder and lightning (he throws lightning bolts)
- The maintenance of cosmic order
- Sending help to humans in distress when properly petitioned
- The fire that connects humans to the sky-world

**The Thunder Brothers**

The **Ani-Hyuntikwalaski** ("the Thunder People" or "Those Who Live Above") are a class of divine beings associated with weather, rain, and cosmic order. They are not identical with Unetlanv but serve under or embody aspects of the Upper World's order. The Thunderers are generally beneficent toward humans — bringers of life-giving rain, controllers of drought. Their opposition to the Uktena (water serpent) and other Under World forces is an ongoing cosmic drama.

**Little People (Yunwi Tsunsdi / ᏧᏁᏓ ᏧᏁᏟᏗᏍᏓ)**

A particularly important class of beings in Cherokee cosmology: the **Little People** (Yunwi Tsunsdi, "little persons"), small human-like beings who inhabit caves, cliffs, and waterfall areas. They are not tricksters or mischief-makers but sometimes helpers who can teach humans ceremonies or rescue lost children. They demand respect; those who encounter them should not speak of the experience for seven days. The ethical demand encoded in their existence: beneficial spiritual encounters require restraint, silence, and respect.

## 4. Selu and Kanati — Origin Narrative and Agricultural Theology

The Selu-Kanati narrative is the foundational Cherokee origin story — more immediately present in daily life than abstract cosmology, because it governs the relationships between humans and the fundamental sources of life: corn and game.

### Selu — The Corn Mother

**Selu** (ᏎᎷ) means "corn" in Cherokee — both the woman and the plant share the name. She is the first woman and the divine source of agricultural sustenance.

**The Narrative:**

Kanati and Selu live with their son (the Wild Boy). One day, Kanati finds a second boy — formed from the blood of game washed from Selu's basket. He takes this second boy into the family. The two boys grow up together, curious about their parents' secrets.

They follow Kanati to a large log where he removes a boulder and releases game animals when he needs meat. The boys discover this and release all the animals — scattering them across the world. This is why hunting is now difficult: the animals no longer come from a single, maintained source but must be pursued across the whole of the earth. The act simultaneously explains scarcity and implicitly demands ceremonial responsibility.

The boys then watch their mother:

She goes into the storehouse and shakes corn from her body — rubbing her side produces corn, rubbing her belly produces beans. The boys, watching through a crack, are horrified. They conclude she is a witch (because the food comes from her body, not from the field).

They tell Kanati what they saw. He says: "If she is producing food that way, you have spoiled everything. She will die now."

Selu knows she will be killed. She tells the boys what to do when she is dead: "Clear a large field. Drag my body in a circle around it seven times. Watch through the night and by morning, corn will have grown."

The boys clear the field but do not drag her body the full seven circles — they cut it short. Therefore corn does not grow everywhere but only in certain places, and must be planted and tended carefully. The seven-circle complete version would have given corn universally and abundantly; the boys' impatience created agricultural scarcity and the need for human cultivation.

**Theological Content:**

1. **The body of the divine is the source of food.** Selu's body produces corn literally — she IS corn. This is participatory theology: the divine does not merely create food from outside, but is constituted by it. The Eucharistic parallel ("this is my body") is not accidental to comparative religion.

2. **Sacrifice and transformation.** Selu must die for corn to exist in the world. Her death is not punishment but transformation — she passes from concentrated, embodied form into distributed, agricultural form. The corn that grows is not made FROM her but IS her, in a new form.

3. **Ritual protocols its own necessity.** The seven-circle instruction is not arbitrary but precise — the boys' failure to complete it introduces agricultural scarcity. Ceremony isn't supplementary to natural order; it IS natural order, properly observed. Cutting the rite short corrupts the outcome.

4. **Female creative power.** The most important original act of creation — establishing the food supply — is female, embodied, and violently received. The agricultural theology is embedded in a narrative about the discomfort with female bodily power.

### Kanati — The Lucky Hunter

**Kanati** (ᎧᎾᏘ) means "lucky hunter" or "provider" — the name encodes fortunate hunting, the ability to reliably provide game. He is the first man.

Where Selu embodies agricultural abundance (the reliable, the domestic, the cyclically renewed), Kanati embodies hunting success (the unpredictable, the wild, the once-dispersed). Together they represent the dual economy of Cherokee subsistence: corn (female, predictable, cultivated) and game (male, uncertain, pursued).

**The Wild Boys**

The two sons — the human-born Wild Boy and the boy-formed-from-blood — become important cultural heroes after their parents' deaths. They travel, have adventures, encounter spirits and monsters, and eventually follow Kanati to the West, where he went to hunt after the release of the animals. The Wild Boys figure in numerous supplementary narratives.

### Comparative Notes

The Selu narrative sits within a wider North American pattern of **Corn Mother myths** — found in varying forms across the Eastern Woodlands, the Southwest, and portions of the Plains:

- Algonquian/Ojibwe: Mondawmin ("corn") born from a wrestling match with a young man
- Abenaki: Glooskap stories involving corn's origin
- Iroquoian: Three Sisters (corn, beans, squash) narratives
- Pawnee: Corn Mother similar in structure to Selu

The Cherokee version is notable for its emphasis on the boys' *betrayal* — the cosmic disruption comes from within the family, from misunderstanding female creative power as witchcraft. This gives it a sharper ethical edge than Algonquian versions.

## 5. The Seven Clans and Social-Cosmological Structure

Cherokee society is organized around seven matrilineal clans (all members of the same clan are considered siblings):

| Clan Name (English) | Cherokee Name | Meaning | Ceremonial Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wolf | Aniwaya (ᎠᏂᏩᏯ) | "Wolf people" | Largest, most politically powerful |
| Deer | Anikawi (ᎠᏂᎧᏫ) | "Deer people" | Ceremonial runners, keepers of hunting lore |
| Bird | Anitsisqua (ᎠᏂᏥᏍᏆ) | "Bird people" | Messengers, associated with Upper World |
| Paint | Aniwodi (ᎠᏂᏬᏗ) | "Paint people" | Medicine people, healers (strong didahnvwisgi tradition) |
| Long Hair | Anigilohi (ᎠᏂᎩᎶᎯ) | "Long hair people" | Peacekeepers; diplomats; some accounts: those of foreign or mixed descent |
| Blue | Anisahoni (ᎠᏂᏌᎯᏂ) | "Blue people" / "Blue Holly clan" | Keepers of children; medicine for children |
| Wild Potato | Anigatogewi (ᎠᏂᎦᏙᎨᏫ) | "Groundhog mother's children" | Gatherers, associated with earth |

The number **seven** is sacred throughout Cherokee ceremony:
- Seven clans
- Seven directions (four cardinal + up + down + center)
- Seven-day ceremonial cycles
- Seven circles in Selu's death instruction
- Seven successive degrees in some healing formulas
- Seven Cherokee sacred plants (varies by source)

The council system places each clan's representative around the central fire — a spatial-political-cosmological arrangement that maps the social order onto the cosmic cross. The townhouse (council house) is both a meeting place and a sacred geometry.

## 6. The Green Corn Ceremony — Selu's Festival

The **Green Corn Ceremony** (Selu's Festival; *Busk* from Muskogee *poskita*, "to fast") is the most important ceremony in the Cherokee ritual year. It takes place in late summer when the first corn is ripe.

**Structure and Elements:**

*The Forbidden Period*: In the days before the ceremony, new corn cannot be eaten. The prohibition is absolute. The corn belongs first to Selu; humans receive access to it through the ceremony, not before.

*Purification*: All community members fast, bathe ritually, and abstain from activities that would create pollution (contact with death, sexual activity in some traditions, certain foods).

*The Extinction of Fire*: All household fires are extinguished. The community enters a state of cosmological zero — the fire that connects home to Upper World is intentionally ended.

*The New Fire*: The sacred fire is kindled by friction (drilling, not spark) by the fire-keeper. This new fire is lit from the first friction, without earlier fire as source — it comes directly from the principle of fire, not from an inherited flame. The new fire connects directly to the Upper World's fire; it has not accumulated pollution.

*Distribution*: The new fire is distributed from the central sacred fire to every household fire in the community. All hearths are re-lit from the single sacred source.

*The Feast*: The new corn is eaten for the first time that year — only now, with Selu properly honored and the community properly prepared.

*Forgiveness*: The Green Corn Ceremony is traditionally a time of communal forgiveness. Wrongs of the past year — except murder — are forgiven. Grudges are released. Community bonds are renewed.

**Theological Meaning:**

The ceremony enacts the Selu myth annually. Selu died so that corn could exist in the world; the ceremony acknowledges this debt and renews the covenant. The new fire parallels the new corn: both are primordial gifts received fresh, without accumulated history.

The forgiveness function is theologically significant: communal renewal requires clearing the relational slate. The ceremony maintains community the way Selu's death maintains agriculture — through a sacrificial reset.

**Living Tradition:**

The Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians continues to conduct the Green Corn Ceremony on Qualla Boundary. It is not a reconstructed or revived practice but a continuous tradition, though individual elements have varied. YOUSPEAK must treat this as a living ceremony with active custodianship, not an artifact.

## 7. The Booger Dance (Anikdatsi or similar; various sources)

The **Booger Dance** is one of the most distinctive and most analytically interesting ceremonies in Cherokee tradition. Raymond Fogelson's work (*Cherokee Boogers*, 1961; expanded in various publications) is the definitive scholarly account.

**What It Is:**

In winter evenings, masked performers (4-12 in number) interrupt a regular social dance. They wear grotesque masks — carved wooden faces representing distorted, frightening, or foreign features. They enter "from outside" — from the cold, from the wilderness, from the direction associated with danger.

The masks represent **boogers**: beings from outside the social/cosmic order who have entered the human realm. They represent:
- Disease beings
- Enemy warriors (historically)
- Dangerous strangers (including, post-contact, European colonizers)
- Chaotic forces threatening to disrupt social harmony

The performers behave badly: they demand women for sex (in crude, exaggerated terms), they stumble and fall, they refuse to cooperate, they are clumsy, frightening, and ridiculous simultaneously.

The host community must accept them — they cannot simply be expelled. Instead:
1. The community asks the Boogers their names — they give absurd, often sexually explicit names
2. The Boogers are asked to dance — their dance is clumsy, chaotic, exaggerated
3. Eventually, the Boogers are asked to leave, or they leave of their own accord

After they leave, the regular social dances resume.

**Theological and Psychological Function:**

Fogelson's analysis identifies multiple functions:

1. **Cosmological containment**: The Booger Dance acknowledges that disruptive forces exist and must be encountered — but it contains them within ceremonial form. By giving the dangerous forces a structured role, it prevents them from erupting uncontrolled.

2. **Catharsis**: The exaggerated awkwardness and sexual crudeness are funny. The community laughs — laughter at the threatening as a method of its diminishment. This is not naive denial but deliberate ritual reduction.

3. **Historical trauma processing**: After European contact, Booger masks increasingly represented Europeans — complete with distorted features caricaturing colonial intruders. The ceremony became a vehicle for processing colonial disruption. The most dangerous force in Cherokee history was given a mask, given a clumsy dance, and made laughable.

4. **Social boundary maintenance**: By dramatizing what is outside the social order, the Booger Dance clarifies what is inside it. The distinction between the orderly social dance (inside) and the Booger's chaos (outside) reinforces community norms.

**Connection to Cherokee Cosmology:**

The Booger Dance maps onto the three-worlds structure. The Boogers arrive from the Under World or from beyond the social-cosmic boundary (West, North, the direction of decline). The community represents This World's order. The ceremony is a micro-drama of the cosmic tension: Upper World order (the regular dance) interrupted by Under World chaos (the Boogers), resolved through structured encounter rather than avoidance.

This is a more sophisticated response to the problem of evil than either denial or dualistic separation. The Boogers are not defeated; they are processed, laughed at, and sent away — with the understanding that they will return.

## 8. Sacred Fire Theology

**Atsila Galunkwoti** — the Ancient/Honored Fire — is the sacred council fire maintained in the townhouse. It is the most concrete form of the connection between Elohi (This World) and Galunlati (the Upper World).

The sacred fire is:
- **Perpetual**: It should never go out. Letting the sacred fire die is a severe community failure.
- **Pure**: Certain actions or presences (death, menstruation in some traditions, contact with enemies) would pollute the fire and require renewal.
- **Cosmic**: The fire on the altar IS a form of the Sun, the original fire of the Upper World. It is not a symbol of that fire but an instance of it.

The seven-clan seating around the council fire maps the social cosmos: each clan in its proper directional position, the fire at center, the community's relationship to the cosmos made architectural.

**Fire-as-Presence theology**: Cherokee sacred fire theology is structurally parallel to:
- Zoroastrian Atash Bahrām (fire as divine son, actual presence not symbol)
- Vedic Agni (fire as divine messenger/priest, carrying sacrifice to gods)
- Ancient Israelite temple flame (eternal fire of the altar)

The commonality: fire as the most-physical form of divine presence available in the world, requiring maintenance, vulnerable to pollution, renewed by proper ceremony.

### Priority 1: **duyuktame** (Core candidate, 7.75–7.95)

*Cherokee duyukta* (ᏛᏳᎧ, "the right way" / balanced-action-in-right-relation) + Sumerian *me*.

**Duyukta** is the Cherokee ethical principle meaning "the right way" or "walking in the correct path" — a word that names the quality of action that is simultaneously righteous, balanced, and in proper relation to the cosmos. It is not merely "right" in a moral sense (that would be one-dimensional) nor merely "balanced" (that would be neutral) but the fusion: *the path that is right because it maintains cosmic balance*.

Semantic core: right-action-as-cosmic-balance. The action that sustains the three-world equilibrium. The ethical life as constitutive of cosmic order.

- Algonquian Seven Grandfathers (balanced virtue as cosmic maintenance)

This cluster is one of the cathedral's most important unforged convergences. The concept: *the cosmos depends on human right-action for its maintenance.* Not merely "be good" but "your goodness is cosmologically constitutive." The cosmos is not self-sustaining; it requires the cooperation of beings who walk the right way.

*duyuktame* is positioned as the Cherokee-donor entry for this cluster, with *Hózhǫ́*-me or similar as the Navajo entry when that excavation is conducted.

### Existing Convergence Deepened: **Vital-Force-As-Cosmic-Given**

Cherokee does not have a word structurally parallel to *nyama* (Mande vital force) — but the Cherokee cosmological concept of *tohi* (peace-health-wellbeing as cosmic state) and the directional energy system (red/white/blue/black as cosmic forces) is a parallel: the idea that the cosmos is permeated by directional force-qualities that can be invoked, manipulated, and aligned.

This is less specific than nyama but points toward a broader vital-force-as-directional-available-energy pattern.

### Existing Convergence Deepened: **Body-As-Divine-Gift-Of-Food**

Selu connects to:
- Christian Eucharist theology
- Aztec Corn God narratives
- Mesoamerican bloodletting-as-feeding-the-gods (the inverse: human bodies feeding divine powers vs. divine bodies feeding humans)
- *Danaqing* (gift-giving-as-felt-bond) — but Selu's gift is more extreme: not a gift OF the divine but a gift that IS the divine, transformed.

A potential convergence file: **Divine-Body-As-Food** — tracking how different traditions encode the theological claim that eating food is eating the divine.
