## 1. The Didahnvwisgi — Medicine People

The **didahnvwisgi** (ᏗᏓᏁᏝᏍᎩ) are the specialist practitioners of Cherokee spiritual-medical knowledge. The name is typically glossed as "those who work with medicine" but more precisely derives from:
- *di-* (plural marker)
- *da-* (reflexive or causative element)
- *-nv-* (related to killing/affecting)
- *-wisgi* (agent suffix: "ones who")

A common translation is "they kill witches" or "they can destroy harmful conjurations" — the name encodes the dual nature of the practitioner. The didahnvwisgi does not merely heal; they operate in the same register as those who cause harm. They must understand malevolent magic precisely because their work is to counter it.

**Two Types of Practitioners**

Cherokee medical-spiritual practice distinguished between (though the distinction was not always sharp in practice):

**Alidahi** (ᎠᎸᏓᎯ) — herbalist-healers, practitioners of plant medicine. The *alidahi* works with botanical knowledge: identification of plants, preparation, dosage, interaction. This is a vast and systematized body of knowledge. The Mooney-Olbrechts *Swimmer Manuscript* (1932) is partly an account of this tradition — A'yûñinï's formulaic text included both the prayer to be spoken over a remedy and the specific plant remedy itself. The two components were always paired.

**Didahnvwisgi proper** — spiritual diagnosticians and conjurers. Their work focuses on the spiritual cause of illness, harm, or misfortune rather than its physical symptoms. A condition caused by witch-conjuring requires a didahnvwisgi to counter the conjuration; herbs alone will not address the root.

In practice, the most respected practitioners combined both types of knowledge — knowing the plants AND the prayers that activated them.

**The Training and Transmission**

Didahnvwisgi knowledge was transmitted through apprenticeship, often within families, and increasingly (after the syllabary) through written manuscripts. A practitioner would spend years learning from an elder — memorizing formulas, learning plant identification, understanding the ceremonial protocols that surrounded each use.

The training was not merely technical but experiential: the practitioner had to develop the capacity to enter the altered states of awareness that effective spiritual work required, to build relationships with the spirit-powers addressed in the formulas, and to accumulate the personal spiritual authority (*tohi*) necessary for the work to take effect.

**The Manuscripts**

After the syllabary's adoption, didahnvwisgi began writing down their formulas. This was both preservation and privatization: a formula written in the syllabary was encoded in a medium that most community members could read, but the *full* formula — including the specific protocols, the timing, the accompanying physical actions — was often not written down completely. The written text preserved the core verbal element while the practical knowledge remained oral.

Mooney received access to these manuscripts through careful relationship-building. His primary informant A'yûñinï ("Swimmer," 1835-1899) gave him manuscripts representing decades of practice. The Kilpatricks in the 1960s collected hundreds more from Oklahoma Cherokee practitioners.

## 2. Structure of a Cherokee Formula

The formulaic prayers collected by Mooney and the Kilpatricks follow a recognizable structure — not absolutely rigid, but with consistent elements:

### Element 1: Address to the Helper-Spirit

Every formula begins by identifying and addressing the spirit-power being petitioned. This is not abstract — it names a specific being by their characteristics, their dwelling place, their power:

> "You, the Red Man, you live in the East. You are at the Seventh Heaven."

> "You, the White Raven, you are the Pathfinder."

> "Listen! You who live in the Sunland, Red Dog!"

The spirit-beings are consistently associated with the directional-color system:
- Red beings → East → associated with success, power, triumph
- White beings → South → associated with peace, harmony, healing
- Blue beings → North → associated with trouble, counter-magic against enemies
- Black beings → West → associated with death, final resolution

The color-direction of the spirit invoked signals the formula's purpose. A formula against disease invokes Red or White helpers; a formula to defeat an enemy invokes Blue; a death-conjuration invokes Black.

### Element 2: Identification of the Problem

The formula states what needs to be addressed — often with elaborate metaphorical language that encodes the spiritual nature of the condition:

> "The intruder has come. The Raven Mocker has approached. The path has been made black."

Illness, misfortune, and enemy harm are understood as spiritual intrusions — beings or forces that have entered the patient's life-space inappropriately. The formula first names the intrusion precisely; only by naming it correctly can it be addressed.

This is **naming-as-power theology**: the formula works because it correctly identifies the nature of what it addresses. An incorrect identification (wrong spirit name, wrong directional association) would render the formula ineffective or dangerous. The practitioner's knowledge is precisely the knowledge of what-to-call-things.

### Element 3: Statement of Desired Outcome

The formula then states what the practitioner requests — sometimes in the present tense, as if it were already accomplished:

> "The intruder will be destroyed."
> "The relief comes now."
> "The path is now white."

The present-tense accomplishment is theologically significant: the formula is not petition (asking that something happen) but declaration (speaking the accomplished spiritual reality). The didahnvwisgi speaks as one who has authority in the spiritual realm, not as a supplicant.

This parallels the speech-theology across the cathedral:
- Akkadian *awātu* (the divine word that constitutes reality by naming it)
- Hebrew *davar* (word-as-event, not word-as-symbol)
- Aramaic *memra* (divine speech as standing presence)
- Dogon *giri so → so dayi* (surface speech up to the utterance that changes reality)

### Element 4: Repetition and Ritual Action

Most formulas specify:
- **The number of repetitions** (typically 4 or 7 — both sacred numbers in Cherokee)
- **Accompanying physical actions** (blowing on the patient, application of the plant medicine, specific gestures)
- **Timing** (dawn, sunset, specific phases of the moon or ceremonial calendar)

The repetition structure is not accidental. It reflects the Cherokee understanding that spiritual reality is not changed by a single declaration but by sustained spiritual pressure. The fourth or seventh repetition completes the work — the formula's effect accumulates across the series.

### Element 5: Closing / Seal

Many formulas end with a sealing phrase that confirms the spiritual action is complete:

> "It is finished."
> "Your life has been returned."
> "The path is now clear."

## 3. Selected Formula Types (from Mooney's Collection)

Mooney organized the Sacred Formulas into categories. The categories reveal what domains Cherokee spiritual practice covers:

### Disease Formulas

The largest category. Covers specific conditions: rheumatism (conceived as caused by the spirits of game animals the hunter failed to properly propitiate), fever, wounds, deterioration of sight and hearing, various pains attributed to specific spirit intrusions.

A formula for rheumatism (Mooney's translation, formula addressed to "the Little Man of the Woodlands"):
> "Listen! Ha! In the Sunland you repose, O Red Dog, O now you have swiftly drawn near to hearken. O great ada'wehi, you never fail in anything. O, appear and show your countenance! Now then we two have met...
>
> Instantly you have rendered it [the disease spirit] impotent. You have placed it [the disease] at rest under the earth. Your relief never fails... Relief has come."

**Theological features:**
- The practitioner addresses the spirit as an equal or superior — not in abasement but in relationship
- The spirit is flattered: "you never fail in anything" — this is not flattery in the manipulative sense but acknowledgment of the spirit's actual power, which is a prerequisite for it acting
- The outcome is stated in accomplished-present: "you have rendered it impotent" — the formula speaks the deed into being

### Love Formulas (Walk in Your Soul category — Kilpatrick)

A distinct category that operates in the relational register. The Kilpatricks' *Walk in Your Soul* (1965) collected hundreds of love-magic formulas — prayers to create, maintain, or sever emotional bonds.

These formulas address the "soul" (*adela*, the animating spiritual principle) of the target:

> "You are to say [target's name], 'I am thinking about you.' Your soul comes right to the very center of my soul, never to turn away. I take your soul."

The metaphysics: the soul of the person being addressed can be accessed and moved toward the speaker by formulaic speech. This is not coercive magic in the Western witch-trial sense but a technology of spiritual intimacy — using the speech-power that didahnvwisgi possess to draw souls into proper relationship.

The love formulas are controversial in contemporary Cherokee communities, as some are understood as harmful (removing someone's spiritual autonomy). The Kilpatricks published them over the objections of some community members who felt the knowledge should remain restricted.

**Theological features:**
- The soul (*adela*) is understood as moveable and accessible to speech
- The formula works on the spiritual dimension of the person, not their visible behavior
- The practitioner speaks "your soul comes right to the center of my soul" — the union sought is spiritual-soul fusion, not merely behavioral attachment

### Hunting Formulas

Prayers addressed to the game animals — specifically to their spiritual presences or the spirit masters of their species — seeking to draw animals toward the hunter and propitiate their spirits so that illness (from failing to honor the animals killed) does not result.

This category reveals: Cherokee hunting is ritually framed as a relationship between equals, not as human dominance over animals. The hunter addresses the deer's spirit, acknowledges the taking of life, and offers spiritual relationship in return. The formula is a covenant-prayer.

**Connection to Kanati theology**: Kanati, the Lucky Hunter, is the archetype this practice invokes. The formula asks to have Kanati's luck — to be, in this hunt, temporarily the one to whom game comes as it once came to the first man.

### Counter-Conjuration Formulas

Formulas designed to reverse harmful magic sent by a witch (whether a human practicing destructive conjuration or a spirit acting against the patient). These formulas identify the source of the harm and its path back to the sender:

> "The blue Raven Mocker has come to put the blue tracks in the road. But I now cause the road to be made straight again. The road is now white. The covering is white, the white fire is burning."

**Theological features:**
- Color-substitution: the blue (harmful) is replaced by white (healing)
- The road metaphor: the life-path (*path* is a pervasive Cherokee metaphor for the proper unfolding of a life, as in *duyukta* — "the right way") has been contaminated by harmful magic; the formula restores it
- White fire: the sacred fire of the Upper World is invoked to purify what blue (the North, decline, trouble) has contaminated

### Death Formulas

The most restricted and rarely shared category. Formulas for dealing with death: speeding someone's death (offensive conjuration), protecting against death-conjuration, accompanying a person through the dying process, preventing the *adela* (soul) from remaining near the living in a harmful way.

The existence of death formulas reveals the fullness of Cherokee spiritual cosmology: the same powers that heal can harm; the same knowledge that defends can attack. The didahnvwisgi is not a purely benevolent figure but a specialist in a morally complex domain. The ethical constraints come from community relationships and apprenticeship training, not from the formulas themselves.

## 4. Raven Mocker — The Witch-Type

The **Raven Mocker** (Kâ'lanû Ahkyeli'ÿski — "the Raven that Mocks") is the most feared being in Cherokee spiritual tradition — a witch-type that steals life from the dying.

The Raven Mocker is invisible or appears as a human being. It flies through the night with fire trailing behind it (the visible streak of fire is the sign of its approach). It attacks the seriously ill, adding its stolen years to its own lifespan.

The Raven Mocker cannot be perceived by ordinary people but can be seen by the didahnvwisgi, who has the spiritual vision to identify it. The counter-formula against the Raven Mocker is among the most important in a practitioner's repertoire.

**Theological function**: The Raven Mocker is not a personified evil but a theological category — the being that feeds on *shortened life*. It embodies the principle that another person's suffering can become someone else's gain. The Raven Mocker is the anti-covenant, the anti-duyukta: instead of right-action maintaining cosmic balance, the Raven Mocker distorts balance by stealing what belongs to another.

The counter-formula against Raven Mocker is essentially a claim for proper apportionment: "this person's life belongs to them; it is not yours; Unetlanv the Apportioner has not allocated it to you; return to the West where you belong."

## 5. The Directional-Color System as Theological Grammar

The directional-color system pervades Cherokee formulaic prayer so thoroughly that it functions as a **theological grammar** — a systematic structure that organizes all spiritual action.

| Color | Direction | Association |
|---|---|---|
| Red | East | Success, power, triumph, strength |
| White | South | Peace, happiness, purity, healing |
| Black | West | Death, defeat, final reckoning |
| Blue | North | Trouble, failure, difficulty, counter-magic |

This grid appears throughout the formulas:
- Healing invokes Red and White helpers
- Counter-magic against enemies invokes Blue
- Death-dealing invokes Black
- Most formulas navigate all four: starting from the patient's need (typically a White or Red goal), working against an intrusive force (Blue or Black source)

**The number 4 and 7**: Both appear constantly. 4 = the four directions, the four colors, the fundamental cosmic structure. 7 = the seven clans, the seven heavens, the seven sacred plants, the seven circles of Selu's instruction. Formulas often specify four repetitions (for structural completeness) or seven (for full, complete spiritual effect).

**The "seven heavens" concept**: Multiple formulas address beings in "the seventh heaven" — implying a layered sky cosmology with seven levels of increasing spiritual density. The highest beings inhabit the seventh heaven. A formula reaching to the seventh heaven is a formula reaching to the fullest available spiritual resource.

## 6. The Swimmer Manuscript — Primary Source

**A'yûñinï** ("Swimmer," 1835-1899) was one of the most important Cherokee medicine men of the late 19th century. He had accumulated, over his lifetime, a collection of manuscripts — his own and others' — written in the syllabary. When Mooney came to collect Cherokee traditions in the 1880s-1890s, A'yûñinï eventually shared these manuscripts and provided extensive interpretation.

The manuscripts contained:
- Several hundred formulaic prayers across all the major categories
- Medical prescriptions paired with each formula (the specific plant to be used, how prepared, when applied)
- Commentary on the spiritual context of specific formulas
- Some account of where each formula came from — its genealogy within the transmission chain

The *Swimmer Manuscript* (published posthumously by Mooney and Olbrechts, 1932) is named for A'yûñinï. It is the single most comprehensive document of Cherokee medical-spiritual practice.

**What the manuscript reveals about the syllabary-religion relationship:**

A'yûñinï had been writing and reading Cherokee in the syllabary for decades before Mooney encountered him. The syllabary was not an external technology imposed on Cherokee tradition; it had become the primary medium of Cherokee sacred knowledge preservation within one generation of its adoption. The manuscripts Mooney received were not oral traditions recently transcribed — they were accumulated, revised, transmitted documents with their own textual history.

This means: within two generations of the syllabary's invention, Cherokee sacred knowledge had a textual tradition.

## 7. YOUSPEAK Resonances

### Speech-as-Spiritual-Technology

The formulaic prayer corpus is the most developed case study in the cathedral of **speech-as-technology** — the use of precise, structured utterance to accomplish spiritual work. The formula is not a petition (hoping something happens) but a tool (doing something).

This parallels:
- Vedic *mantra* (sacred speech as cosmological action)
- Avestan *manthra* (same root as mantra — the word that contains truth's power)
- Egyptian *heka* (magic-as-speech, already excavated)
- Aramaic *dibbura* (the divine utterance that constitutes)
- Dogon *so dayi* (the fourth level of speech that changes what is)

The Cherokee contribution is distinctive: the formula is not divine speech but human speech addressed to spirits, and its efficacy depends on the practitioner's accumulated spiritual authority (*tohi*) and precise knowledge of the directional-color theology. It is a technology of relational spiritual action.

### Naming as Causation

The identification element of Cherokee formulas — the precise naming of the spirit-being addressed, the precise naming of the problem — reflects the broader naming-as-ontological-act convergence:

> "By correctly naming you, I can address you. By correctly naming the harm, I can oppose it."

This connects to: Basque *izena duen gutzia omen da* ("everything that has a name exists"), Akkadian *awātu* (constitutive divine word), Aboriginal songlines (naming the country summons it), Hebrew *davar* (word-as-event).

The Cherokee version is specifically **diagnostic naming**: the practitioner's knowledge is primarily the knowledge of names. They know what to call things in the spiritual world; that knowledge is the power.

### The Soul-as-Moveable (Adela Theology)

The love formulas' treatment of the soul (*adela*) as accessible and moveable by speech is a distinctive theological element:

> "Your soul comes right to the center of my soul."

The *adela* (animating soul-principle) is not fixed in the body but can be addressed, moved, drawn, and affected by properly spoken words. This is a relational-soul theology — the soul is constituted through its relationships, which can be reinforced or disturbed by spiritual action.

This parallels Algonquian *manidoo* (personhood-as-spirit not confined to individual body) and Bantu *ubuntu* (personhood-constituted-through-relation) — though the Cherokee version emphasizes the *vulnerability* of the soul to external spiritual action more than those traditions do.

### Duyukta as Right-Path Theology

The formulaic prayer corpus consistently returns to the metaphor of the *path* (the life-unfolding as a directional journey). Healing is restoring the path to white/clear; harm is making the path blue/black; counter-magic is returning the path to its proper color.

*Duyukta* — "the right way" — is the path-metaphor's underlying theology: the life lived in right relation to cosmic order is the life that walks the white/red path. The formulas are technologies of path-restoration.

## 8. Convergence Additions

### Right-Action-As-Cosmic-Maintenance (deepened)

The formulaic prayer corpus adds a dimension to this convergence: right-action (duyukta) is not merely an ethical ideal but a *recoverable state*. When the path is made black or blue by harmful forces, it can be restored. The cosmos is not deterministic; spiritual action can correct deviations.

This gives duyukta a dynamic character that ma'at (Egyptian) and asha (Avestan) share: these are not merely present-states to be maintained but conditions that can be lost and recovered through right practice.

### Speech-As-Spiritual-Technology (new/deepened)

Cherokee formulaic prayer strengthens this convergence (alongside Vedic mantra, Avestan manthra, Akkadian awātu, Aboriginal songlines). The Cherokee contribution is:
- The most thoroughly documented corpus of speech-as-tool in the cathedral (hundreds of specific formulas)
- The clearest expression of the *diagnostic* dimension: the formula works because it names correctly
- The most explicit connection between speech and *soul-movement* (the love formulas)

### Naming-As-Ontological-Act (deepened)

The "identify the spirit by their name and characteristics" element of every formula adds to the naming convergence. Cherokee adds: **the effectiveness of spiritual action depends on correct naming**. This is different from Basque (naming creates existence), Akkadian (naming constitutes), or Aboriginal (naming summons the country) — it is naming as the *prerequisite for relationship*. You cannot address what you cannot name.
