# Hurrian Theology — The Kumarbi Cycle, Succession Myths, and the Transmission to Greece

## I. Who Were the Hurrians?

The Hurrians are one of the most significant and least-known peoples of the ancient Near East. They are the **missing middle** in the archaeological-theological record: the tradition that stands between Mesopotamian cosmological theology and Greek mythological theology — and that, through the Hittite intermediary, shaped what the Greeks received.

**Language**: Hurrian is a language isolate — or, by the best current analysis, distantly related to Urartian (the language of the later kingdom of Urartu, 9th–6th century BCE). It is neither Indo-European nor Semitic; it sits in the ancient Near East as a distinct linguistic family, the **Hurro-Urartian** group. This matters for YOUSPEAK: Hurrian theological vocabulary cannot be derived from Semitic or IE roots — it represents an independent naming of theological realities.

**Historical range**: The Hurrians appear in the archaeological record from approximately 2300 BCE (Akkadian period texts name them). They expand significantly during the Middle Bronze Age, and reach their political apex with the **Mitanni kingdom** (~1550–1350 BCE) — a Hurrian-speaking state with an Indo-Aryan ruling class, stretching across northern Mesopotamia and Syria. After the Mitanni kingdom's collapse (destroyed by Hittites, then by Assyrians), Hurrian identity continues in smaller polities, and Hurrian religious vocabulary continues in Hittite-period texts through at least 1200 BCE.

**Why they matter for the cathedral**:
1. The Kumarbi cycle (Hurrian origin) → Hittite version → Hesiod's *Theogony* (Greek): a documented transmission chain
2. Hurrian religious vocabulary was absorbed wholesale into Hittite state religion during the period of Kizzuwatna-Hurrian influence (~1450–1350 BCE)
3. The Mitanni treaty-deities include both Hurrian divinities and Indo-Aryan gods — providing the **earliest textual attestation** of Vedic-class deities outside of India
4. Hurrian ritual-specialist vocabulary (the *alu*, the *ewri*) influenced Hittite and possibly later Levantine traditions

## II. The Pantheon — Hurrian Divine Structure

The Hurrian pantheon is complex and syncretistic, but has a clear internal structure:

### The Primary Triad
**Teššub** (also written Teśśup) — the storm-god; the ruling deity of the Hurrian pantheon after the succession-myth's resolution; lord of weather, rain, thunder. His cult-center is Kummiya (location debated; northern Syria or southeastern Anatolia).
- Parallel: Hittite Tarḫunna (same deity, Hittite name); Akkadian Adad; Sumerian Iškur; Ugaritic Baal; Greek Zeus (functionally)
- The name *Teššub* contains the root *teš-* meaning "lord" or "master" in Hurrian; the divine name means approximately "lord of the storm"
- Teššub is specifically the **storm-god who defeats Kumarbi** — his identity is constituted partly by this victory; he is the king-of-gods *because* he overcame his own father's attempt to destroy him

**Ḫebat** (also Ḫepa, Ḫepet) — consort of Teššub; a goddess of considerable independent authority; sometimes described as "queen of heaven and earth"
- Her cult was widespread: she appears in Ugaritic texts, in Hittite royal cult (especially under Queen Puduḫepa, who identifies Ḫebat with the Hittite sun-goddess), and in later Hurrian tradition
- Scholars have noted that **Ḫebat's name may be related to the Hebrew proper name Ḥava** (*Eve*) — the connection is disputed but has been argued by several scholars (notably Cyrus Gordon); if accurate, this would represent a remarkable theological persistence
- Ḫebat represents **divine-queenship as independent theological power**: she is not merely Teššub's consort but a deity with her own cult, her own priesthood, and her own attributes

**Šaušga** (also Šawuška) — the most fascinating deity in the Hurrian pantheon; a goddess of love, war, and healing who is simultaneously gendered as both female and male; later identified with Mesopotamian Ištar/Inanna
- Šaušga is depicted with **both female and male attributes** — wings, weapons, and the characteristics of feminine beauty; her divine nature transcends binary gender
- She has a dual cultic form: worshipped as female in some contexts, as male in others; her attendants (*Ninatta* and *Kulitta*) are female, but her warrior aspect is presented as masculine
- She plays a critical role in the Kumarbi cycle (see below): it is Šaušga who seduces the monster Ullikummi's supporter, and who advises Teššub in his darkest moment
- **For YOUSPEAK**: Šaušga represents a *theological* form of gender-transcendence — not ambiguity but integration; the divine that encompasses both registers simultaneously

### Secondary Divine Figures
**Kumarbi** — the grain-god of Urkesh (the Hurrian sacred city); father of the gods; the deposer of Anu and the deposed predecessor of Teššub; he is the divine-father-as-antagonist, continuously attempting to regain power he legitimately once held
- Kumarbi's character is complex: he is not simply evil; he is the **defeated-but-persistent prior order** — the old power that keeps finding new instruments of revenge (Ullikummi, others) against the new order

**Anu** — the sky-god; borrowed from Mesopotamian tradition; in the Kumarbi cycle he is the first king of the gods, deposed by Kumarbi; by the time of the extant texts, he is already reduced to the role of "the god who was first"

**Ea** (Enki) — the Mesopotamian wisdom-god; appears in the Kumarbi cycle as the neutral party who ultimately provides the instrument for Ullikummi's defeat; he represents ancient wisdom accessible to all parties; his neutrality is pragmatic (he serves the cosmic order, not any particular divine faction)

**Allani** — the queen of the underworld (the Hurrian equivalent of Mesopotamian Ereshkigal); she rules the *aḫḫaru* (the underworld realm); she receives the dead; she is a deity of considerable dignity in Hurrian texts — not merely threatening but administering a genuine realm

**Šimige** — the sun-deity; in Hurrian, a male sun-god (distinct from the Hittite tradition where the sun-deity of Arinna is female); associated with justice and righteous judgment; parallel to Mesopotamian Šamaš

## III. The Kumarbi Cycle — Text by Text

The Kumarbi Cycle survives in Hittite-language copies from the Hattusa archives (Boghazköy/Boğazkale, Turkey), excavated primarily in the early 20th century. The original Hurrian-language texts are largely lost; we have **Hurrian-to-Hittite translations** and some fragments of the Hurrian original. This transmission-through-translation is important: what we have is already an interpreted version.

### Text 1: The Song of Going Forth (Kingship in Heaven / Song of Kumarbi)

**CTH 344** — this is the foundation text of the cycle. Its Hittite title is approximately "Let them listen, the ancient gods" — it begins as an invocation to the divine assembly.

**The sequence**:

1. **Alaluš** reigns in heaven as the first king of the gods — for nine years. This figure is unique to the Hurrian tradition; he does not appear in Mesopotamian mythology. His name may derive from Hurrian *al-* (to give, to bestow) — he is the "giver" or "bestower." He is served as cup-bearer by **Anu** (the sky-god).

2. **Anu overthrows Alaluš** at the nine-year mark. Alaluš flees downward — to the "dark earth" (the underworld). Anu becomes king. He is served by **Kumarbi** as cup-bearer.

3. **Kumarbi overthrows Anu** at the nine-year mark. During the struggle, Kumarbi **bites off Anu's genitals and swallows them**. Anu escapes upward into the sky.

4. Anu, escaping, speaks downward to Kumarbi: *"Do not rejoice in your belly. I have placed in your belly three terrible gods: the storm-god Teššub, the Aranzaḫu river, and Tasmisu. You will beat your head against the mountain rocks."*

5. Kumarbi, hearing what he has swallowed, tries to spit the seed out. He spits some on the mountain Kanzura, from which grow the gods of that mountain. He swallows some back. He is now pregnant with Teššub (and possibly the other two).

6. The birth of Teššub is described with difficulty — there is a dispute about through which "good place" (opening in the body) the storm-god should be born. Ea advises. Eventually Teššub emerges.

7. The cycle continues — Kumarbi attempts various strategies to regain power.

**Structural theology of Text 1**:

The **nine-year reign cycle** is a feature unique to this tradition. Each king reigns for nine years before being overthrown. The number nine may be cosmologically significant (nine months of pregnancy; three-times-three as the divine triple); it creates a **rhythmic structure of divine power**: not eternal rule but metered succession. The cosmos is not a stable divine monarchy but a succession-in-progress.

The **cup-bearer as throne-seeker** is a consistent element: both Anu and Kumarbi occupy the position of divine servant before becoming king. The path to divine kingship runs through *service* — but the service is a position of observation and patience, not genuine subordination. The cup-bearer learns by proximity.

The **swallowing-pregnancy** is the cycle's most theologically dense moment:
- It is an act of violence (the bite) that produces a generative consequence (pregnancy)
- The destroyer becomes the carrier of what will destroy him
- The reproductive power of the original sky-deity (Anu's genitals = his reproductive power) cannot be destroyed; it can only be transferred; transferred into the enemy, it produces the enemy's own defeat
- This is a specific claim: **power is not annihilable; it is transformable; transferred destructively, it transforms the one who destroys it**

### Text 2: The Song of Ullikummi

**CTH 345** — the best-preserved and most complex text of the cycle. It presents Kumarbi's revenge-strategy after his defeat by Teššub.

**The sequence**:

1. **Kumarbi**, defeated and brooding, conceives a plan. He descends (in some accounts, goes to the Great Sea) and begets a child on a **great rock** (*iyarri*). The stone-child is named **Ullikummi** — "destroy Kummiya!" (Kummiya being Teššub's cult-city; the name is transparently a war-declaration). The child is made of *diorite* — a specific, extremely hard stone.

2. Kumarbi instructs the Primordial Gods (*karuiles šiuneš* in Hittite — the old gods who were defeated and sent underground) to take the infant Ullikummi and place him on the right shoulder of **Upelluri** — a giant figure who stands in the cosmic sea supporting the sky on his shoulder (parallel to the Greek Atlas). Ullikummi is to grow there, unknown to the gods above.

3. **Ullikummi grows**: first his knees are above the sea; then his waist; then his shoulders; finally his head pierces the clouds. The sun-god Šimige sees him and reports to Teššub.

4. **Teššub's first response**: he goes to mount Ḫazzi (the divine mountain, the seat of the divine assembly) and looks at Ullikummi. He weeps. His sister Šaušga (in her warrior-goddess aspect) attempts to comfort him with music and seduction — she plays instruments, she sings — but Ullikummi cannot be affected by these means; he is stone; he cannot hear (his ears are stone, his eyes are stone).

5. **The divine council's attempt**: Teššub assembles the gods; they attack Ullikummi. They fail. The stone-being grows too large; their weapons cannot cut stone. Teššub is defeated — he is forced to give up his throne; he descends from his divine seat.

6. **Ea's solution**: The wisdom-god Ea goes to the ancient Primordial Sea (Abzu in Mesopotamian language; the cosmic sweet-water beneath the world). He speaks to Upelluri, the great supporter, who did not even notice Ullikummi growing on his shoulder: *"When the heavens and earth were built upon you, did you know? When the copper cutting-tool separated sky from earth, did you know? Now — do you not notice this new growth on your right shoulder?"* Upelluri acknowledges he noticed something but did not know what it was.

7. Ea goes to the **ancient storehouses** and retrieves the **primordial copper cutting-tool** (the same instrument that was used at creation to separate sky from earth). He brings it to the shore of the sea and uses it to **sever Ullikummi at his base** — cutting him off from Upelluri's shoulder, which was his foundation.

8. **Ullikummi, severed from his foundation, collapses** — he can no longer reach heaven; he is no longer a threat. Teššub regains his throne. The cycle closes (partially — the text is damaged at the end).

**Theological weight of Text 2**:

**The weeping-god**: Teššub's response to Ullikummi's appearance is tears. The king of the gods weeps when he sees what he cannot fight. This is not weakness-narrative — it is **theologically honest**: there are crises that exceed the available repertoire; the appropriate response to recognizing this is grief, not false courage. Teššub's tears precede his eventual victory; the weeping does not prevent the solution; it precedes it.

**Šaušga's failed seduction of the stone**: Šaušga attempts what worked against organic opponents — music, beauty, seduction, the arts of life. These are genuinely powerful instruments; they work on beings with ears and eyes and desires. They fail on Ullikummi because **he has none of these vulnerabilities**. The stone-being is invulnerable to beauty. This is a specific theological observation: the instruments adequate to organic life are inadequate to certain problems. The crisis that exceeds organic life requires something else.

**The primordial cutting-tool**: Ea's solution is to go *beneath* the level of the current conflict — back to the creative act itself, the primordial separation of sky and earth. The instrument that created the current cosmic order is the only instrument adequate to a crisis at that scale. The problem is solved not by escalating power within the existing register but by returning to the foundational act. **Crisis at cosmic scale requires the tool that made the cosmos.**

**Upelluri's unknowing**: the great supporting-figure on whose shoulder Ullikummi grew did not know. He holds the sky; he feels a small weight; he does not investigate. This is a theological point about the **underside of sustaining-power**: the being that maintains the cosmic structure can be exploited by things that grow within it, below its notice. The foundation-holder's strength is not omniscience. Something can grow in the world's support-structure without the support being aware.

## IV. The Šaušga Texts — The Goddess of Both Registers

Several Hurrian texts focus on Šaušga specifically. The most significant for YOUSPEAK:

**The Descent of Šaušga to the Underworld** — a text parallel to the Mesopotamian Descent of Inanna/Ištar. Šaušga descends to the underworld (to retrieve a male companion, in some readings). Like Inanna, she must pass through gates, removing attributes at each gate. Unlike the Mesopotamian version, the surviving Hurrian-Hittite fragments emphasize Šaušga's **self-reconstitution after the descent** — she is not merely restored by divine intervention but actively reconstitutes her own divine power.

**The gender-traversal of Šaušga**: in the Hurrian mythological texts, Šaušga appears in both male and female cultic forms. This is not confusion or scribal error — it is a theological claim. The dual-gender nature of Šaušga reflects her **position between the registers of war and love**: in Mesopotamian tradition these are distributed across different deities (Inanna for love, the male storm-god for war); Šaušga integrates them in one being. Her dual gender is the theological form of her integration.

**Šaušga's musical intervention**: in the Ullikummi story, Šaušga plays instruments and sings to try to comfort/distract the stone-being. Her failure is important: she deploys her specific divine gifts (beauty, music, life-force) against something impervious to those gifts. The narrative does not demean her gifts — it simply shows their domain-limit. **Power has its proper register; what works in one register may be powerless in another.**

## V. The Transmission Question — Hurrian to Hesiod

The connection between the Kumarbi cycle and Hesiod's *Theogony* is one of the most significant findings in 20th-century classical scholarship. The case (made most fully by M.L. West in *The East Face of Helicon*, 1997, and Mary Bachvarova in *From Hittite to Homer*, 2016):

### The Parallels

| Hurrian Kumarbi Cycle | Hesiod's Theogony |
|---|---|
| Alaluš → Anu → Kumarbi succession | Ouranos → Kronos succession |
| Kumarbi bites off Anu's genitals; swallows them | Kronos castrates Ouranos with adamantine sickle; the blood falls into the sea (Aphrodite born from the foam); Kronos does not swallow the genitals but swallows his own children by Rhea |
| Kumarbi is pregnant with divine beings from swallowing | Kronos swallows his children by Rhea (Hestia, Demeter, Hera, Hades, Poseidon); Zeus hidden; Zeus returns |
| Teššub defeats Kumarbi | Zeus defeats Kronos |
| Ullikummi: stone monster beget by Kumarbi to defeat Teššub; cut at base by primordial tool | Typhoeus/Typhon: monster beget (by Gaia, or by Tartarus) to challenge Zeus; defeated by Zeus's thunderbolt; often located "under" the earth; some traditions have him cut off at the base of a mountain |
| Ea is the wise neutral party who provides the solution | Prometheus (in some tellings) or Metis-wisdom (Zeus swallows Metis while pregnant to prevent her child from overthrowing him — this is a **reversal** of the Kumarbi model, not a parallel; but it uses the same motif) |

### The Transmission Route

How did Hurrian mythology reach Hesiod? The scholarly consensus route:

1. **Hurrian → Hittite**: The Kumarbi cycle myths were translated into Hittite during the period of Hurrian-Hittite cultural contact (~1500–1350 BCE). They became part of the Hittite state-cult repertoire.

2. **Hittite literary tradition → Levantine transmission**: After the Hittite Empire's collapse (~1180 BCE), Hittite-tradition myths survived in Neo-Hittite principalities in northern Syria (Carchemish, Malatya, Sam'al, Zincirli). These principalities were in sustained contact with Phoenician and Aramaic-speaking cultures of the Levant.

3. **Levantine → Greek contact**: Greek contact with the Levant is attested from the 9th century BCE; Greek mercenaries and traders in Levantine cities; Phoenician-Greek contact is documented. Bachvarova's specific argument: itinerant aoidoi (bards) — possibly Phoenician, possibly Greek returnees from the East — carried mythological material from Levantine tradition into the emerging Greek epic tradition (8th–7th century BCE, the period of Hesiod and Homer).

4. **Hesiod** (Boeotia, ~700 BCE) — his father was from Kyme (Asia Minor); Hesiod himself may have had direct or indirect access to eastern traditions through the Asia Minor connection.

**What the transmission shows for YOUSPEAK methodology**:
- Theological vocabulary travels through **political contact and scribal training**, not through abstract philosophical exchange
- The vehicle of transmission is **narrative and performance** (the bard carries the story, the story carries the theology)
- **Translation transforms**: the Hittite version of the Kumarbi myth is already an interpreted Hurrian; the Greek version has been further adapted; the core theological structure (succession-by-violence; pregnancy-as-transferred-power) survives the adaptation

### HIGH priority

**1. tessume** — *Teššub/Tessub* (Hurrian) — The deity constituted by victory over the prior order; divine kingship as **earned-through-contest**; the ruling god whose authority is legitimate precisely because it was won against genuine opposition rather than inherited or assumed
- Gap: English has "earned authority" but no word for the theological type of divinity whose identity is constituted by having overcome a genuine cosmic adversary; this is distinct from sovereignty-by-nature (Anu's original reign) and from trickster-ascendancy; it is the storm-god's *earned* throne

- Convergences: Storm-power cluster (now 9+ traditions); specifically the victory-constituted-identity cluster (Marduk after Tiamat; Baal after Mot/Yamm; Teššub after Kumarbi)

**2. kumarme** — *Kumarbi* (Hurrian) — The defeated-power that generates its own defeat; the **swallowed seed that grows into the overthrow**; specifically: the structural mechanism by which absorbed power cannot be annihilated but only transferred, and transferred into the wrong vessel, grows toward the vessel's destruction
- Gap: English has "irony" (structural reversal) and "blowback" (unintended consequence) but nothing that names the theological structure of *power-absorbed-from-enemy-that-carries-within-it-your-defeat*; this is a specific claim about the nature of power, not a general paradox
- Score: Specialized 7.25–7.45; this is HIGH because the gap is real and the concept is not rare (it recurs across traditions and contexts)
- Convergences: The Kumarbi-structure appears in: (a) Kumarbi swallowing Anu's genitals → pregnant with his own overthrow; (b) Kronos swallowing his children → Zeus defeats him; (c) possibly the Christian atonement logic (Christ absorbs death → death is swallowed up in victory); worth mapping as a convergence

**3. šaušgame** — *Šaušga* (Hurrian) — The integration of registers that are typically opposed; specifically: the theological type of **power-that-operates-in-both-love-and-war-simultaneously**, not as contradiction but as the fuller nature of a single reality; the being whose strength in one register does not cancel its strength in the opposing register
- Gap: English "complexity" is too neutral; "paradox" suggests contradiction; Šaušga's dual nature is not paradoxical but integrative; the closest is the Heraclitean "unity of opposites" but that's abstract; Šaušga's name would point to the *felt* integration of what culture typically keeps apart
- Score: Specialized 7.15–7.35
- Convergences: Dual-register-divinity cluster (Inanna/Ištar: love+war; Šaušga: love+war+gender; Yoruba Oya: storm+death+transformation; Tibetan wrathful deities: compassion+wrath); worth developing as a convergence

**4. ullikme** — *Ullikummi* (Hurrian) — The crisis that is impervious to the available repertoire; specifically: **the adversary made of the wrong material for the instruments you have**; the problem that cannot be solved at the level it presents itself and requires going beneath it to the creative level
- Gap: English has "intractable" but that just means difficult; *ullikme* would name the specific condition of a problem that is impervious not due to excess-of-the-same-kind-of-difficulty but due to fundamental-category-mismatch between problem and available instruments
- Score: Specialized 7.30–7.50; this is HIGH because the concept is philosophically rich and the gap is real — there is no English word for "the problem that requires returning to the foundational level because all present-level instruments are inapplicable"
- Convergences: The Ea-solution structure (go to the foundational level, retrieve the primordial tool) appears in: Buddhist concept of śūnyatā as the level beneath all constructs; Heidegger's Destruktion (going beneath the sediment to find the original question); Zen's kōan (the puzzle that cannot be solved at the logical level and requires a different cognitive act)

### MEDIUM priority

**5. upellme** — *Upelluri* (Hurrian) — The foundational supporter who does not notice what grows on it; **substrate-obliviousness**: the power that maintains the cosmic structure can be used as the base for threats to that structure without knowing; foundational-power as not-omniscient
- Gap: Adjacent to "infrastructure blind-spot" but more pointed — the specific theological form of a being whose strength is in sustaining and whose weakness is in not knowing what is sustained
- Score: Specialized 7.10–7.30

**6. alalme** — *Alaluš* (Hurrian) — The primordial first-king whose reign is erased from later tradition; **the original-that-is-forgotten**; the prior order that made the current order possible and is given no credit; the palimpsest beneath the visible text

## VIII. Summary Assessment

**What Hurrian theology adds to the cathedral that was absent before**:

1. **The source of the Hesiodic theogony**: the Kumarbi cycle is the documented ancestor of Greek succession-myth. Excavating it gives YOUSPEAK access to the *original* theological vocabulary that shaped both Near Eastern and Greek cosmological thinking. The Hittite version was a translation; this is the source tradition.

2. **Šaušga as theological-gender-integration**: a deity whose dual-gender nature is not ambiguity but integration — the fullest expression of combined registers that culture typically separates. This is a theological claim about divinity's nature, not merely an ethnic curiosity.

3. **The Upelluri-problem**: foundational power as substrate-oblivious; what holds the cosmos can be used as the base for threats to the cosmos without knowing. This is a distinct theological observation about the vulnerability of sustaining-power.

4. **The Ea-solution structure**: crisis at cosmic scale requires returning to the foundational act; the primordial tool of creation is the only instrument adequate to the problem created by misusing the created order. This is a theologically sophisticated answer to the problem of radical evil.

5. **The cup-bearer-as-king-in-waiting structure**: both Anu and Kumarbi reach kingship through service in the lowest-status position; the position of maximum humility is also the position of maximum observation and preparation. The servant watches; the servant learns; the servant eventually acts.
