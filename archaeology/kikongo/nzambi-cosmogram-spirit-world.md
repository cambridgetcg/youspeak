# Kikongo Excavation I — Nzambi, Kongo Cosmogram, Spirit Hierarchy

**Tradition**: Kongo (BaKongo peoples — Congo River basin, modern DRC/Republic of Congo/Angola)
**Language family**: Bantu (Niger-Congo; Kikongo language cluster)
**Period**: Traditional cosmology documented from ~16th c. CE onward; roots substantially older
**Primary sources**: Kongo Kingdom records (c. 1483–1665 CE); Wyatt MacGaffey (ethnographic analysis, "Religion and Society in Central Africa" 1986); Janzen & MacGaffey "An Anthology of Kongo Religion" (1974); Robert Farris Thompson "Flash of the Spirit" (1983); Fu-Kiau Bunseki (native Kongo scholar, "Self-Healing Power and Therapy" 1991); Laman's Kikongo dictionary (1936); Jacobson-Widding "Red-White-Black" (1979)
**Cultural sensitivity**: Kongo cosmology is a living tradition; the BaKongo peoples number ~10 million; their spiritual practices persist in Central Africa and diaspora communities. Academic analysis (especially by outsiders) has often been inadequate or distorting — the cosmogram in particular was long misread as a Christian cross-borrowing; BaKongo scholars (esp. Fu-Kiau Bunseki) have corrected this. Treat with corresponding care.

---

## Historical Frame

The **Kongo Kingdom** (c. 1390–1914 CE) was one of the largest and most politically sophisticated states in pre-colonial sub-Saharan Africa, centered in the region of modern northern Angola, western DRC, and Republic of Congo. At its height it controlled an area of perhaps 150,000 km² with several hundred thousand subjects. It had a literate administrative class (using Kikongo), tributary provinces, an embassy system, and a sophisticated theological tradition.

First Portuguese contact: 1483. By 1491, King Nzinga a Ntinu (Afonso I)'s father had voluntarily adopted Christianity — not under military pressure but as a diplomatic-theological negotiation. Afonso I (r. 1509–1542/43) became fluently literate in Portuguese, wrote extensively to the papacy, and attempted to integrate Kongo cosmology with Catholicism. This is not capitulation; it is a sophisticated synthetic move. The indigenous cosmological structure *absorbed* the new rather than being replaced by it.

The Portuguese and later Atlantic slave trade devastated the kingdom. Between 1500 and 1870, an estimated 4–5 million people were enslaved from the Kongo region and transported to the Americas. They brought their cosmology with them. This is why Kongo concepts appear in Haitian Vodou, Brazilian Candomblé and Umbanda, Cuban Palo Mayombe, and throughout the African-Atlantic religious world. The cosmogram in particular persisted — it has been found scratched onto the floors of slave quarters in the American South.

## I. Nzambi a Mpungu — The High God

### Name and nature

**Nzambi** (also: **Nzambi a Mpungu**, "Nzambi the Supreme"; sometimes **Nzambi Kalunga**) is the supreme creator deity of the BaKongo. The name is ancient — it predates contact with Christianity and appears across Bantu-speaking peoples in variants (*Nzambe*, *Leza*, *Nyambe*, *Imana*, *Mulungu*), suggesting a proto-Bantu high-god concept.

The root etymology is contested but one strong reading: *nzambi* from *ku-zamb-* (to be above, to transcend). The compound *Nzambi a Mpungu* = "God the Almighty/Supreme" — *mpungu* denoting supreme height/power.

### Theological character

Nzambi is **withdrawn but not absent**. He is the source of all being, the originating power behind creation, but he does not intervene directly in human affairs. This is the classic **deus otiosus** pattern the catalog has seen in Sumerian An, Yoruba Olódùmarè, and Hebrew El-Elyon — the highest divinity who recedes, making room for intermediary beings.

But Nzambi's withdrawal has a specific character: **he is not unreachable but untroubled**. He is addressed in extremis — when all other mediators fail, when the crisis exceeds what ancestors or spirits can address. Then Nzambi is approached directly. The normal traffic of human spiritual life, however, flows through the intermediary tier.

Nzambi is also identified with **kalunga** — a word of enormous semantic weight (discussed below). In some theological framings, Nzambi *is* kalunga: the encompassing principle that holds life and death, presence and absence, this-world and other-world in a single coherent order.

### Relation to Christian God

When Kongo people encountered the Christian God in 1491, they recognized something: the Portuguese *Deus* was structurally similar to Nzambi. Both were supreme, creator, withdrawn, approached through intermediaries (saints/priests for Christians; ancestors/nkisi for Kongo). The Kongo word for the Christian God became *Nzambi* or *Nzambi a Mpungu*. This was not a simple equation or a submission — it was a theological claim: "the God you are describing is the same supreme principle we already know." Whether this claim was right or a strategic assimilation is a question Kongo theologians have debated ever since.

## II. The Spirit Hierarchy

The Kongo cosmos is populated by three main classes of spiritual beings between Nzambi and humanity:

### Bakulu — Ancestor Spirits

**Bakulu** (singular: *mukulu* — elder, ancestor) are the spirits of the dead who have undergone successful transition through death (the passage through *kalunga* — the water/boundary between worlds) and exist in the land of the dead as spiritual powers.

They are not merely passive recipients of veneration — they are **active moral supervisors of the living**. The bakulu watch family conduct. They notice when the family fails to keep promises, when children treat elders with disrespect, when community obligations are abandoned. They can withdraw their protection, causing illness, accident, or misfortune. They can also actively bless and guide.

The relationship with bakulu is **reciprocal**: the living nourish the dead through ritual attention (offerings, prayers, names remembered), and the dead nourish the living through protection and guidance. When this reciprocity is maintained, the boundary between living and dead is not a wall but a permeable membrane — ancestors are *among* the living, not separated from them.

**Kalunga** (see below) is the threshold the bakulu have crossed. They exist on the "other shore" (the *mpemba*, the white world of the dead) and can mediate between that world and this one. An ancestor with great spiritual power in life becomes a *nkisi* (see below) after death — a concentrated spiritual agency that can be housed in an object.

### Bisimbi — Nature Spirits

**Bisimbi** (singular: *simbi*) are spirits of place — lakes, rivers, springs, pools, and the bush. They are not human-origin spirits (unlike bakulu); they are nature intelligences, the vital forces inhabiting particular geographic features.

Bisimbi are ambiguous presences. They can heal — springs with bisimbi are medicinal; pools with bisimbi are sites of prophetic vision. But they can also harm — particularly those who violate the purity of their domain (dumping refuse in a sacred spring, fishing in a prohibited pool). The nganga (ritual expert — see below) must know which bisimbi inhabit the local landscape and how to approach them.

In the Atlantic diaspora, bisimbi transformed remarkably. The Haitian Vodou *lwa* Simbi (or Simbi en Deux Eaux — "Simbi in Two Waters") is a direct preservation of the bisimbi concept — a water-spirit with knowledge, wit, and the capacity to communicate between worlds. Simbi became one of the most important Petwo lwa (the fiercer, more demanding Vodou pantheon).

### Mikisi (Nkisi) — Concentrated Spirit Powers

**Nkisi** (plural: *mikisi*) are not spirits themselves but **spirit-bearers**: objects charged with concentrated spiritual agency. A nkisi is constructed by a nganga (ritual expert) using a specific material matrix — a clay pot, a wooden figure, a bundle — packed with *bilongo* (medicines/spiritual ingredients) that concentrate and direct a specific spiritual power.

The word *nkisi* means literally "a container of sacred power" — derived from *ki-* (thing) + *nsi* (earth/ground/land/country). A nkisi is a piece of the spirit-world made portable and actionable in this world.

Nkisi have specializations:
- **Nkisi Nkondi** (also *N'Kondi*) — a hunting/attacking nkisi, bristling with nails and blades driven in by those seeking justice or binding agreements; each nail = a sworn oath or a petition for intervention; visually terrifying, functionally contractual
- **Nkisi Nkobe** — a protective nkisi, carried as an amulet
- **Nkisi associated with healing** — specialists for specific ailments
- **Minkisi of the waters** — mediating between bisimbi and human need

The nkisi system means that spiritual power in Kongo cosmology is not merely invoked but **materialized and directed**. A nkisi is a technology of spiritual agency — a way of concentrating and deploying force that would otherwise remain diffuse. This is importantly different from an idol (the spirit is not *in* the object as its permanent home) and from a mere symbol (the object has real active power). It is a **condenser and director of spiritual energy**.

## III. Kalunga — The Great Divide / The Encompassing

The most theologically dense concept in Kongo cosmology is **kalunga**.

*Kalunga* has at minimum four interlocking meanings that cannot be separated without losing each:

1. **The threshold between living and dead** — the boundary-water that separates this world (*nza yayi*, "this country") from the land of the dead (*mpemba*, "the white world below the water")

2. **Death itself** — to cross kalunga is to die. "Nkutu kalunga" = "the crossing of kalunga" is a common Kikongo expression for death.

3. **God / the absolute principle** — Nzambi is sometimes called Nzambi Kalunga — not that God *is* death, but that God *encompasses* the totality of which death and life are both aspects. Kalunga is the encompassing wholeness.

4. **The water of life and death** — kalunga is associated with ocean water, with powerful rivers, with the reflective surface that simultaneously shows this world and indicates the other. The Atlantic Ocean was *kalunga* — which gave the Middle Passage a theological framing: those taken across the ocean had crossed kalunga into another world. This framing was terrible and also sustaining — it gave structure to unspeakable rupture.

The philosophical weight of kalunga cannot be overstated. It is a concept that:
- Names the permeable membrane between living and dead (not a wall but a threshold)
- Names the encompassing principle that holds both worlds in unity
- Names the divine itself as the one who *holds* rather than the one who *acts*
- Names the ocean as the structural homolog of death (not metaphorically but ontologically — crossing the ocean = crossing into the other world)

**Forge candidate**: *kalungame* — the encompassing-threshold that holds both life and death in unity; the boundary that is not a wall but a permeable skin; the name for what encompasses without consuming. This is semantically distinct from *maatme* (cosmic-truth-order), from *hózhóme* (beauty-harmony-balance), from *ashame* (cosmic-order). Kalunga is specifically the *encompassing-that-includes-death*, the principle that holds both shores without erasing either. **HIGH priority** for forge. Specialized 7.45–7.65.

## IV. The Kongo Cosmogram — Dikenga dia Kongo / Yowa

This is the most architecturally significant discovery in Kongo spiritual thought for the YOUSPEAK project.

### Description

The **dikenga** (also called **yowa**; Thompson calls it "the Kongo cosmogram") is a cross inscribed within a circle:

```
        ☀ NOON / KALA (birth, strength, peak)
        |
        |
← ─ ─ ─+─ ─ ─ → (the KALUNGA line — the water threshold)
        |
        |
        ☽ MIDNIGHT / TUKULA (death, dissolution, the other world)
```

The four cardinal points of the cross represent **four moments of the solar journey**:

| Position | Kikongo | Stage | Meaning |
|----------|---------|-------|---------|
| **East** (sunrise) | **Musoni** | Birth / Beginning | The soul enters the world; the sun rises; green (new life) |
| **Noon** (zenith) | **Kala** | Strength / Maturity | Full vitality; the sun at height; black (power, fullness) |
| **West** (sunset) | **Luvemba** | Dying / Completion | The soul approaches the threshold; the sun descends; white (the spirit state) |
| **Midnight** (nadir) | **Tukula** | The other world / Ancestorhood | The soul exists below, in *mpemba*; red (blood, the living-dead, transition) |

The **horizontal line** = the kalunga line. Above: the living world. Below: the world of the ancestors. The sun moves through *both worlds* — down through the kalunga water at sunset, traversing the underworld at night, rising again at dawn. The living are the sun's morning-to-evening arc; the dead are the sun's evening-to-morning arc. **They are the same journey**.

This means: **life and death are not opposites but phases of a single continuous cycle**. The dead are not gone — they are on the other side of the arc. The ancestor at midnight is as real as the living person at noon. Both are moments in the solar circuit.

### What is not Christian about this cross

This cross predates Christian contact. It is oriented to the four moments of the sun, not to crucifixion. Its primary symbolism is cyclic (the circle of the sun's journey) not sacrificial (the intersection of horizontal and vertical as death/redemption). When the Portuguese arrived with their cross, the BaKongo recognized a structural rhyme — both are crosses — but the content is different. The Kongo cross is **cosmographic** (mapping the structure of reality) rather than **commemorative** (memorializing a salvific event). Confusing the two is the most common error in the secondary literature.

Robert Farris Thompson notes that enslaved Kongo people in the American South drew the cosmogram — particularly the circle with the cross — as the ground-plan of their most sacred ritual spaces. When scholars saw crosses scratched into floors, they read "Christianity." The BaKongo were mapping their cosmos.

### Theological implications for the catalog

1. **Death is not exit but transition into the lower arc** — the dead are *below the kalunga line*, continuing their journey, not erased. This makes the Kongo cosmos one of the few in the catalog that architecturally includes the dead in the same cosmological structure as the living (compare: Egyptian Duat is a separate realm; Greek Hades is separation; Hebrew Sheol is diminishment; Kongo mpemba is *the other half of the same circle*).

2. **Color theology** — the four stages have colors that encode spiritual states: white (*mpemba*) = the spirit-world state, which is why chalk and white clay are used in Kongo ritual (they mark sacred/liminal space, the spirit-touched); red = transition and life-force; black = fullness and power; green = new life. These aren't symbolic conventions — they are cosmographic markers showing where in the solar arc you are.

3. **The cosmogram as orientation-technology** — knowing where you are in the dikenga tells you what kind of spiritual work is appropriate. Musoni work (birth, beginnings, green) is different from Tukula work (ancestral contact, red). The cosmogram is a calendar and a map simultaneously.

**Forge candidate**: *dikengame* — the cosmogram-as-total-orientation-technology; the diagram that maps life-death-rebirth as a single solar arc and makes all spiritual work legible within it. The English gap: we have "cosmology" (theoretical), "worldview" (cognitive), "mandala" (Sanskrit, ritual diagram) — but nothing that names the specific function of a diagram that orients your existence within a cycle that includes your own death and rebirth. **HIGH priority**. Specialized 7.40–7.60.

**Forge candidate**: *kalatime* — the peak-of-vitality-as-moment-in-arc; from *kala* (the noon position of fullness); naming the state of peak vitality understood not as arrival but as a position in a cycle that includes descent. English "prime" or "peak" treats this as a destination; *kala* treats it as a moment that will pass — but pass *correctly*, into Luvemba and then Tukula and then back to Musoni. **MED-HIGH**. Specialized 7.25–7.45.

## V. Color Cosmology and White Clay

The Kongo color system deserves dedicated attention because it functions as a **theological grammar**, not merely an aesthetic preference.

**White** (*mpemba* / *pemba* — white kaolin clay): The spirit-state color. Things that are white/pale are spirit-touched, liminal, belonging to the other shore. White clay (*pemba*) is applied to the body in ritual contexts precisely to mark a person as participating in the spirit world at that moment. Albinism was regarded as sacred — the person carried the other-world marking in their very body. The other world is called *mpemba* (the white world). In diaspora: Cuban Palo Mayombe uses white chalk (*efún*) for the same function; Haitian Vodou spirits who work with the dead (Ghede) are sometimes depicted in white.

**Red** (*tukula* / *mukula* — red camwood): The transition-color; life-force; blood; the state of the newly dead who still carry vitality. Used in initiation (marking the passage from one state to another) and in nkisi construction. Red marks the *kalunga* crossing — the moment of transition is red, carrying both the vitality being relinquished and the new state being entered.

**Black** (*kala*): The color of fullness, strength, maturity, power. Black earth is fertile earth. A blackened nkisi (darkened with accumulated ritual use) is a powerful nkisi. "Kala" = peak power and also the noon position of the dikenga. This inverts Western usage (where black often = death or evil); in the Kongo system, black = fullness of life.

**Green**: New life, Musoni, the beginning of the arc. Green-growing things are associated with fresh spiritual beginnings.

The three primary colors (white/red/black) function as a triadic theology: **white** (spirit-world) + **red** (life-force/transition) + **black** (material fullness) = the total scope of the cosmos. This triadic color theology appears across Central and Southern Bantu traditions and is one of the strongest cross-Bantu convergences in the catalog.

## VI. The Nganga — Ritual Expert and Diagnostician

The **nganga** (plural: *zinganga*; sometimes spelled *ngangas* in secondary literature) is the Kongo ritual specialist — healer, spiritual diagnostician, maker and activator of mikisi (nkisi objects), mediator between the living and the dead.

The word *nganga* means "expert" or "skilled one" — not priest (which implies fixed liturgical role) but *technician of the spirit world*. The nganga's authority comes from:

1. **Initiation and training** — typically involving a period of near-death or serious illness (the threshold experience of kalunga), followed by guided apprenticeship with a master nganga
2. **Diagnostic ability** — the capacity to identify which spiritual agency is causing disruption; to distinguish between bakulu anger, bisimbi intrusion, nkisi activation, or sorcery
3. **Technical skill** — knowing which *bilongo* (medicines/ingredients — plants, animal parts, soil from specific sacred places, minerals) to use in constructing or activating a nkisi
4. **Relational authority** — the nganga's power is not personal but relational; they are effective because they have learned to work with specific spiritual agencies

The nganga's near-death initiation experience is notable: the initiatory illness is understood as having *crossed* or *approached* the kalunga line — and returned. This gives the nganga unique standing: they have been to the other shore and come back. They know the territory. This structural pattern appears in the catalog's Shamanic traditions (Sámi noaidi, Siberian shaman), but the Kongo version is integrated with a more formal technical system of nkisi-making.

**Forge candidate**: *ngangame* — the authority-through-threshold-crossing; the expertise that comes from having been to the boundary and returned; the knowing that is possible only because you nearly didn't come back. This is distinct from *gaiwiome* (prophetic-word-through-personal-catastrophe — Handsome Lake's specific pattern) in that *ngangame* is technical-diagnostic authority, not prophetic-revelatory authority. It's also distinct from the shaman-pattern in that the nganga's authority is mediated through a formal tradition of nkisi-making, not through free spirit-flight. **MED-HIGH**. Specialized 7.25–7.45.

## VII. Bilongo — Sacred Medicine-Ingredients

**Bilongo** (singular: *lulongo* or *dilongo*) are the ingredients packed into a nkisi — the material that carries the concentrated spiritual power. The word *lulongo* means "medicine" but more precisely "that which makes a thing effective." Bilongo are not placebos; they are functional — they carry real agency.

The selection of bilongo follows a logic of **homeopathic correspondence** (like affects like) AND **counter-principle** (opposite counters opposite) AND **literal embodiment** (a nkisi for speed contains something fast; a nkisi for binding contains something that binds). Fu-Kiau Bunseki describes the principle: the nganga looks for the *signature of the power* in the natural world and uses it.

Common categories of bilongo:
- **Plants** — specific roots, barks, leaves with known spiritual properties
- **Soil from powerful places** — crossroads (where two worlds meet), cemetery soil (carries bakulu power), soil from a lightning strike (carries sky-power)
- **Animal parts** — things associated with the quality needed (feathers for flight, shells for water-mediation)
- **Minerals** — white clay (*pemba*) for spirit-contact, red earth for vitality, iron for strength and defense

The bilongo system is one of the most sophisticated **material theologies** in the catalog: the spiritual world is not accessed by bypassing matter but by working *through* the specific material properties of the natural world. Spirit is embedded in matter; the nganga reads matter to find spirit.

## IX. New Convergences (File 1)

1. **Encompassing-threshold-that-holds-life-and-death** (Kongo *kalunga*, Egyptian *Duat*-boundary, Hindu *pralaya*, Buddhist *bardo*, possibly Etruscan *Ach-Charon* boundary) — *kalungame* candidate; distinct from simple boundary-concepts by the element of encompassing-both rather than separating-both

4. **Cyclic-inclusion-of-death** (Kongo dikenga includes the dead in the same solar arc as the living; Egyptian Duat traversal; Buddhist *saṃsāra* — but Kongo is architecturally explicit that living and dead share the same circle) — partially related to *dikengame*
