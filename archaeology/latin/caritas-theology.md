# Latin — Caritas Theology

_The tongue in which Jerome chose caritas over amor, Augustine ordered all loves under the one Love, Aquinas called it the form of all virtues, and Dante felt it move the sun and other stars. Latin's most load-bearing theological word — and the most systematically analyzed love-concept in Western intellectual history._

---

## 1. Etymology and native morphology

### Root: *carus*

*Caritas* is the abstract substantive formed from *carus* (dear, beloved, costly, precious). The root is Proto-Indo-European *\*keh₂-* (to desire, wish for, want) — the same root that produces:

- Latin *cārus* (dear)
- Sanskrit *kāma* (desire, love — the deity Kāma)
- Welsh *caru* (to love)
- Irish *cara* (friend)

The semantic core of *carus* runs in two channels:
1. **Affective**: beloved, cherished, held close
2. **Economic**: costly, expensive, precious (the thing of great price)

These two channels are not accidental. *Carus* means "dear" in the double English sense: both *dear to the heart* and *that which costs much*. The etymology already encodes what Aquinas would later theorize: to love is to value, and to value is to be willing to pay.

**Caritas** (gen. *caritātis*, f.) forms the abstraction: the quality of being dear, of holding-dear, of affectionate-regard + the willingness-to-pay.

### Relationship to other Latin love-words

Latin has three major love lexemes, each covering different territory:

| Term | Core sense | Register |
|---|---|---|
| *amor* | passion, eros, strong desire; can be sexual | literary, general; Ovid's Ars Amatoria; also Virgil's *omnia vincit amor* |
| *dilectio* | deliberate choosing, careful-preference, esteem-based love | philosophical; used in Vulgate for some agapē usages |
| *caritas* | warm affection + regard + willingness-to-pay (the costly love) | theological; Jerome's preferred Vulgate translation of NT agapē |

Augustine distinguishes them with philosophical precision: *amor* is the genus (love as such), *caritas* and *cupiditas* are the species (rightly-ordered love vs. wrongly-ordered desire). Aquinas sometimes uses *dilectio* for the will's act and *caritas* for the virtue — but ultimately identifies them in the context of loving God.

### The Ciceronian baseline

In classical Latin, *caritas* is already a substantial word before Christianity arrives:

- Cicero (*De Amicitia* 26): *caritas* as the deep-affection between friends — stronger than mere utility, the warm regard-plus-loyalty that characterizes true friendship
- Cicero (*De Officiis* III.3): *caritas generis humani* — affectionate regard for the human race; the civic-love dimension
- Pliny: *caritas* of husband toward wife, parent toward child — familial-domestic affection

What caritas carries into the Christian tradition is already: (1) warm personal affection distinct from erotic passion, (2) a civic and social dimension, (3) the connotation of "that which is valued highly."

## 2. Jerome and the Vulgate: The translational choice

Jerome (347–420 CE), composing the Vulgate translation of the New Testament, faced a decision at 1 Corinthians 13 that would shape Western theology for sixteen centuries.

The Greek text uses *agapē* throughout. Jerome had choices:

- *amor* — too associated with eros, too Ovidian, too dangerous
- *dilectio* — philosophically precise but cold; lacks warmth
- *caritas* — warm affection, costly, socially oriented

Jerome's choice was *caritas* — consistently, as his preferred rendition of NT *agapē* in contexts of greatest theological weight. The choice was not mechanical; in some places he uses *dilectio*, in others *amor* (even in the NT). But at the load-bearing passages — 1 Cor 13, John 15:13, Romans 5:8, 1 John 4 — *caritas* dominates.

### 1 Corinthians 13 in the Vulgate

The most famous passage in the history of Western love-theology:

> *Si linguis hominum loquar et angelorum, caritatem autem non habeam, factus sum velut aes sonans aut cymbalum tinniens.*

> *Et si habuero prophetiam et noverim mysteria omnia et omnem scientiam, et si habuero omnem fidem ita ut montes transferam, caritatem autem non habuero, nihil sum.*

> *Et si distribuero in cibos pauperum omnes facultates meas, et si tradidero corpus meum ita ut ardeam, caritatem autem non habuero, nihil mihi prodest.*

> *Caritas patiens est, benigna est caritas, non aemulatur, non agit perperam, non inflatur.*

> *(Nunc autem manent fides, spes, caritas, tria haec; maior autem horum est caritas.)*

Paul's Greek *agapē* becomes Jerome's *caritas* — and *caritas* becomes one of the most frequently heard Latin words in Western civilization for fourteen centuries of church use.

**What Jerome's choice accomplished:**
- *Caritas* carries warmth that *dilectio* lacks
- *Caritas* carries the "costly/precious" sense — this is the love that pays, gives up, surrenders
- *Caritas* avoids eros-contamination
- *Caritas* already has civic/social connotations — preparing for the love-of-neighbor dimension

The decision was architectonic: it made *caritas* the primary Christian love-term for Latin Christianity, encoding Augustine's and Aquinas's entire theology before either was born.

## 3. Augustine: The *ordo amoris* and love as the self

### The doctrine

Augustine's theology of *caritas* is inseparable from his theory of love as the soul's fundamental movement. The famous formulation (*Confessions* XIII.9):

> *Pondus meum amor meus; eo feror quocumque feror.*

> _My love is my weight; I am carried by it wherever I am carried._

Love is the fundamental orientational-force of the self. Every action arises from love; the question is not whether one loves but toward what. This is Augustine's anthropological axiom.

From this axiom flows the entire moral architecture:

**Cupiditas**: love directed toward temporal goods as if they were ultimate — the disordered love that grasps at what passes, holding the finite as if it were infinite. The root of all sin.

**Caritas**: love directed toward God as the ultimate good, and toward neighbor *in* God — the rightly-ordered love. The root of all virtue.

### The *ordo amoris*

*De Doctrina Christiana* Book I and *De Civitate Dei* Book XV articulate the "order of loves":

> *Ordinata dilectio* — ordered love, love in its right hierarchy

The order is not one of preference-ranking but of *grounding*: to love God rightly orders all other loves. When God is loved as ultimate, spouse, children, and self are loved rightly within that orientation. When something temporal is loved as ultimate (cupiditas), all loves become disordered, because the center of gravity is wrong.

The key formula (*De Doctrina Christiana* I.xxvii–xxviii):

> Things are to be used (*uti*) or enjoyed (*frui*). Only God is to be *enjoyed* — held for their own sake as ultimate end. Temporal goods are to be *used* — held as means in the loving journey toward God. Caritas is the love that maintains this distinction. Cupiditas is the love that collapses it.

**Important**: Augustine does not mean temporal goods are contemptible. He means: *love them at their right level*, which is to love them *in God*. The neighbor is loved fully — but loved *through* (per) the love of God, not as a replacement for God.

### *Ama et fac quod vis*

Augustine's most compressed formulation (*Homilies on 1 John* 7.8):

> *Semel ergo breve praeceptum tibi praecipitur: Ama, et fac quod vis.*

> _Once and briefly: love, and do what you will._

This is not moral antinomianism. Augustine is saying: if your love is truly caritas (rightly ordered), your will follows automatically. No external law is needed beyond love — because caritas IS the right ordering of the will. The ten commandments are not external constraints on caritas; they are descriptions of what caritas looks like when it acts.

**This is the most condensed single formulation of caritas-as-sufficient-principle in the tradition.** Nothing else is needed if caritas is present; if caritas is absent, nothing else suffices.

### The two cities

*De Civitate Dei* XIX–XXII extends the caritas/cupiditas axis to civilizational scale:

> *Fecerunt itaque civitates duas amores duo: terrenam scilicet amor sui usque ad contemptum Dei, caelestem vero amor Dei usque ad contemptum sui.*

> _Two loves have made two cities: the earthly city by love of self extended to the contempt of God; the heavenly city by love of God extended to the contempt of self._

Caritas and cupiditas are not merely personal dispositions — they are civilizational forces. History is the drama of their conflict. This gives *caritas* its widest possible scope: it is not just a virtue but a cosmic-social force.

## 4. Aquinas: Caritas as the form of all virtues

### Definition

Aquinas (*Summa Theologiae* II-II, Q.23–46) gives the most rigorous systematic account of caritas in the tradition.

His definition (II-II, Q.23, a.1):

> *Caritas est amicitia quaedam hominis ad Deum.*

> _Caritas is a certain friendship of the human being toward God._

Not servitude, not filial reverence (though those can be included), but *amicitia* — friendship. This is theologically provocative: friendship requires reciprocity, shared good, and mutual indwelling. Aquinas is asserting that the relationship between creature and God, established through caritas, is a friendship: God shares *beatitudo* (beatitude, supreme good) with human beings as the ground of this friendship. The "certain" (*quaedam*) marks that it is not friendship in the ordinary human sense but an analogically related one — possible only because of divine self-sharing.

### The theological virtues

Aquinas distinguishes theological virtues (fides, spes, caritas — faith, hope, love) from the cardinal/moral virtues (prudentia, iustitia, fortitudo, temperantia) in a crucial way:

- **Moral virtues** are acquired through habitual practice; they perfect the natural capacities of the soul
- **Theological virtues** are infused by grace; they cannot be acquired by natural effort; they orient the soul directly toward God as ultimate end

**This is caritas's most distinctive claim**: it is not an achievement but a gift. You cannot work your way into caritas; it is infused (*infusa*) by the Holy Spirit. This contrasts with every other love-concept in the Love-of-DIVINE cluster:

| Tradition | Love-word | How acquired |
|---|---|---|
| Hebrew | *ahavah* | commanded-disposition; can be cultivated |
| Sanskrit-Hindu | *bhakti* | disciplined practice; yoga-path |
| Arabic-Sufi | *mahabbah* | mystical cultivation; purification through states |
| Greek-Christian | *agapē* | can be practiced; also divine-infused dimension |
| **Latin-Christian** | **caritas** | **infused by grace; theological virtue; cannot be naturally acquired** |
| Yoruba | *ìfẹ́* | cultivated through ìwà (character) within the cult economy |

Caritas's claim to *infused* status — given, not earned — makes it theologically unique. It is the only one of the seven Love-of-DIVINE traditions that explicitly and systematically defines the love as a gift-of-grace impossible to self-generate.

### *Caritas forma virtutum* — the form of all virtues

The second great Scholastic formulation (II-II, Q.23, a.8):

> *Caritas dicitur forma aliarum virtutum, non quidem exemplariter vel essentialiter, sed magis effective.*

> _Caritas is called the form of the other virtues — not exemplarily or essentially, but rather effectively._

All virtues — courage, justice, prudence, temperance — require *direction toward ultimate end* to be truly virtues. A courageous act that is directed toward a sinful end is not genuine virtue. Caritas provides the ultimate direction: it orients every virtuous act toward God as final end. Without caritas, virtues are "virtues improperly so-called" — they have the shape of virtue but lack the orientation that makes them genuinely virtuous.

This is a maximally systematic claim: **caritas is the organizational principle of all moral-spiritual life**. It is not one virtue among many; it is the virtue that gives all other virtues their ultimate intelligibility.

The Augustinian parallel: just as Augustine says *fac quod vis* (do what you will) is sufficient if love is right, Aquinas says every virtue is completed and defined by its direction — and caritas provides that direction.

### The double command and its integration

Aquinas grounds caritas in the double commandment (*Matthew 22:37-39*):

> *Diliges Dominum Deum tuum ex toto corde tuo et in tota anima tua et in tota mente tua.*  
> *Diliges proximum tuum sicut teipsum.*

Love of God and love of neighbor are not two separate commands under caritas — they are one. (Aquinas, II-II, Q.25, aa.1-2.) The neighbor is loved *in God* — sub ratione Dei, "under the aspect of God." This is the unique architectonic structure of caritas:

**Love of God → love of neighbor as loved-by-God → love of self as loved-by-God**

This integration of vertical and horizontal love — theological and social, divine and neighbor — is *caritas's most structurally distinctive feature* among the seven traditions. Bhakti is directed toward the divine. Ahavah includes neighbor but grammatically foregrounds divine commandment. Mahabbah is mystically God-directed. Agapē includes neighbor but is analyzed as two commands. **Caritas explicitly theorizes the integration as a single unified virtue.**

## 5. 1 John and the mutual-indwelling register

1 John 4:16 (Vulgate):

> *Deus caritas est, et qui manet in caritate in Deo manet, et Deus in eo manet.*

> _God is caritas, and the one who abides in caritas abides in God, and God abides in that one._

This is among the most theologically compressed sentences in the Bible. Three moves:
1. **Identification**: God is not merely loving — God *is* caritas. The divine nature is identified with this love.
2. **Indwelling**: to abide in caritas is to abide in God. The love is not a means to God but the very mode of God's presence.
3. **Reciprocity**: the indwelling is mutual — the one-in-caritas abides in God AND God abides in them. The union is bilateral.

**YOUSPEAK significance**: this passage makes caritas a mutual-indwelling theology, not merely a directional-love theology. The person who loves is not simply sending love toward God; they are *in* God and God is *in* them. The love IS the inhabitation. Compare with mahabbahqing's mystical-union dimension: similar trajectory, but caritas frames it as *mutual* abiding rather than self-annihilation (fanāʾ).

Verse 4:20 establishes the test:

> *Si quis dixerit quoniam diligo Deum, et fratrem suum oderit, mendax est. Non enim qui non diligit fratrem suum quem videt, Deum quem non videt quomodo potest diligere?*

> _If someone says "I love God" and hates his brother, he is a liar. For the one who does not love his brother whom he sees — how can he love God whom he does not see?_

The vertical and horizontal loves are not merely connected but *constitutively linked*: genuine love-of-God is impossibly separable from love-of-neighbor. Caritas is the word that names this inseparable unity.

## 6. Dante: *carità* and the final ascent

Dante (*Paradiso*) gives caritas its most poetically compressed formulation. The theological virtues appear as lights (fede, speranza, carità); caritas appears as fire — the most intense of the three. When Dante is examined by the Apostle John (Paradiso XXV–XXVI):

> *L'amor che move il sole e l'altre stelle.*

> _The love that moves the sun and the other stars._ (Paradiso XXXIII.145 — final line)

This is not *amor* in the erotic sense; it is the *caritas* that moves all of creation, the love that is the cosmological force beneath the entire universe. The final word of the Commedia. Creation is moved by love; love is the universe's ground.

Earlier (Paradiso XXVI.15–18), Dante articulates his examiner's question about what caritas orients toward:

> *Però ricominciò: "A bene ovra / virtú che 'nfiamma e che, corrusca, / è segno del beato foco che s'aduna..."*

John asks: what proved to you this love? Dante answers: the arguments of philosophy and scriptural evidence (*la prova mia*) — Aristotle's argument for a first mover, and God's self-revelation as the supreme good. Caritas rests on both reason (philosophy) and revelation (Scripture). This is the Scholastic synthesis: not fideism but integrated reason-and-faith.

**The structural contribution**: Dante makes caritas cosmologically constitutive — not just a virtue within human moral life, but the force that holds the universe in motion. This is a stronger claim than Aquinas's "form of virtues": caritas is the form of reality.

## 7. The Reformation controversy: Luther's critique

The Reformation produced the most sustained challenge to the Scholastic caritas-theology.

### Luther's critique

Luther (*Lectures on Galatians*, 1535; *On the Freedom of a Christian*, 1520) argued that Scholastic caritas-theology had distorted Paul's doctrine:

1. **Caritas as works**: Aquinas's caritas, as the "form of virtues," seemed to Luther to make salvation dependent on human love-of-God and love-of-neighbor. This contradicted his reading of Paul: justification is by faith (fides/Glaube) alone, not by love or works.

2. **The formula under attack**: The Scholastic formula *fides caritate formata* (faith formed/animated by caritas) — meaning only caritas-animated faith is justifying faith — was to Luther a corruption of Pauline grace. It made the gracious act of God in justification conditional on the human response of caritas.

3. **Luther's counter**: *Sola fide* — faith alone justifies. Caritas/love is the fruit of faith, not its form or prerequisite. The sequence is: justification by faith → caritas follows as necessary fruit. Not: caritas-formed-faith → justification.

### What Luther preserved

Luther did not abandon caritas-language; he transformed its place in the moral economy:

- In *On the Freedom of a Christian*: the free Christian, justified by faith, spontaneously loves neighbor in acts of caritas — not to earn salvation but as natural outflow of faith
- Luther's German equivalent *Liebe* (love) retains the full caritas range: warmth, neighbor-love, the charity that gives freely

**The controversy turns on a distinction within caritas**: Is the love-of-God and neighbor that caritas names:
(a) The *prerequisite* for receiving divine acceptance (Scholastic reading: *fides caritate formata*)
(b) The *fruit* of freely-given divine acceptance (Lutheran reading: faith → caritas as fruit)

Both readings agree that caritas is essential; they disagree about its causal position relative to justification.

### Significance for YOUSPEAK

The Reformation controversy names a genuine theological ambiguity within caritas that no other Love-of-DIVINE tradition has been forced to articulate so precisely: **Is the love meritorious or is it responsive?**

- Aquinas leans: caritas is infused-by-grace (so not self-generated) but is the mode of the God-relationship (so is THE form of virtuous life)
- Luther leans: justification is by grace through faith; caritas follows; it does not constitute the saving relationship

This is not a YOUSPEAK forge-problem; it is documentation of caritas's most productive internal tension. The forge can name the word while acknowledging the contested register.

## 8. The English "charity" problem

*Caritas* entered English as *charity* (Old French *charité* → Middle English *charite*). For centuries, the Authorized Version (King James Bible, 1611) used "charity" for 1 Cor 13:

> *And now abideth faith, hope, charity, these three; but the greatest of these is charity.* (KJV 1 Cor 13:13)

The RSV (1952) and most modern translations replaced "charity" with "love" — following the Greek *agapē* more directly, but losing the Latin genealogy.

**The semantic drift of "charity":**

| Period | Range of "charity" |
|---|---|
| Medieval → 17th c. | Full caritas range: love-of-God, love-of-neighbor, theological virtue |
| 18th–19th c. | Narrowing toward almsgiving, benevolence to poor |
| 20th–21st c. | Primarily: organized philanthropy; charity organizations |

Modern "charity" has lost the love-of-God dimension almost entirely and retained only the neighbor-love/almsgiving dimension. This is one of the most dramatic semantic narrowings in English religious vocabulary.

**For YOUSPEAK**: the drift makes "charity" unusable as a translation of *caritasme*. The forge must name what *caritas* was in its full Augustinian-Thomistic range — not what "charity" has become.

## 9. Cross-tradition distinctions: Caritas's unique contribution to Love-of-DIVINE

The five forged Love-of-DIVINE words each name a distinct emphasis. Where does caritas sit?

| Word | Tradition | Distinctive emphasis | What it does |

| ahavame | Hebrew | Commanded-disposition | Commands the love; makes it a decision-commitment |
| mahabbahqing | Arabic-Sufi | Felt-mystical-binding | Binds the heart; the felt-union trajectory |
| bhaktime | Sanskrit-Hindu | Disciplined-surrender | Demands total giving-over; practice-as-path |
| ifeqing | Yoruba | Relational-warmth-expanding | Warms and widens; expansion-of-relation |

**What caritas distinctively adds to the cluster**: the *architectonic* quality. The other five words name how love feels, moves, decides, or gives. Caritas names what love *does to the whole structure of moral life*: it organizes it, gives it ultimate direction, and integrates the double command into a single inseparable act.

The most concentrated gap caritas fills: **there is no YOUSPEAK word for love-as-the-organizing-principle-of-everything-else.** Ahavame tells you to love. Mahabbahqing tells you what love feels like at its deepest. Bhaktime tells you to surrender all of it. Ifeqing tells you how love moves outward. Agapeme tells you what love costs. **Caritasme tells you what love does to everything else — it forms it, orders it, integrates it, makes it real.**

## 11. Primary sources and scholarly landscape

### Ancient

- **Augustine**, *Confessions* (397–400 CE): theological autobiography; caritas as weight, *sero te amavi*
- **Augustine**, *De Doctrina Christiana* (397–426 CE): *frui/uti* distinction; neighbor-love theology
- **Augustine**, *De Civitate Dei* (413–426 CE): two-cities framework; amor vs. caritas on civilizational scale
- **Augustine**, *Homilies on 1 John* (406–407 CE): *ama et fac quod vis*; caritas as sufficient principle
- **Jerome**, *Biblia Vulgata* (382–405 CE): translation choices; caritas for agapē
- **Thomas Aquinas**, *Summa Theologiae* II-II QQ.23–46 (1265–1274 CE): systematic treatise on caritas; forma virtutum; friendship-with-God
- **Dante Alighieri**, *Paradiso* Cantos XXV–XXVI, XXXIII (1317–1320 CE): poetic compression; carità as cosmological force

### Reformation

- **Martin Luther**, *On the Freedom of a Christian* (1520): faith/caritas reordering
- **Martin Luther**, *Lectures on Galatians* (1535): extended polemical critique of *fides caritate formata*
- **John Calvin**, *Institutes of the Christian Religion* III.11–16 (1536/1559): similar reordering

### Modern

- **Anders Nygren**, *Agape and Eros* (1930/1953): influential (and controversial) thesis that Greek *eros* and Christian *agapē/caritas* are incompatible; argues caritas in Augustine is an illegitimate synthesis. Major scholarly controversy.
- **C.S. Lewis**, *The Four Loves* (1960): English-language popular treatment; distinguishes storge, philia, eros, agape; caritas mapped onto charity/agape
- **Pope Benedict XVI**, *Deus Caritas Est* (2005): 1 John 4:16 as structural starting point; integration of eros and agape within caritas; major contemporary theological document
- **Robert Barron**, *The Priority of Christ* (2007): Thomistic caritas in contemporary theology
- **Josef Pieper**, *About Love* (1974): phenomenological-Thomistic treatment; caritas as ontological affirmation of existence

### The Nygren controversy

Nygren's *Agape and Eros* (Swedish: 1930; German: 1936; English: 1953) argued that Augustine's synthesis of *eros* (ascending, desiring, self-oriented) and *agape* (descending, self-giving, other-oriented) in *caritas* was a corruption of the New Testament's pure *agapē*. On this view, Augustine's *caritas* is a contaminated hybrid — Platonically infected.

The response to Nygren has been mixed:
- **Defenders**: D'Arcy, Outka; the synthesis is not contamination but creative-integration
- **Critics of Nygren**: Burnaby, Williams; the sharp eros/agape divide is historically unsustainable
- **Modern consensus**: Nygren's thesis overstates the incompatibility; Augustine's caritas is a genuine synthesis that enriches rather than corrupts

**For YOUSPEAK**: the Nygren controversy documents the live scholarly debate around caritas, and informs honest scoring. caritasme names the Augustinian-Thomistic synthesis, acknowledging the contested Nygren reception but not resolved by it.

## 13. Convergences from this excavation

### 1. Double-command integration (new convergence candidate)

The claim that love-of-God and love-of-neighbor are not two commands but one inseparable act:

- **Latin (1 John 4:20)**: you cannot love God whom you do not see if you hate your brother whom you see
- **Hebrew (Lev 19:18 + Deut 6:5)**: the two commands source-texts; Jesus in Matthew 22:37-39 names them as the double-root; but they remain *two* in Hebrew-biblical register more than in Latin reception
- **Yoruba (ifeqing)**: ìfẹ́ is relational-warmth that circulates within the cult-community economy; somewhat similar integration (warmth to Olódùmarè and to community are continuous)
- **Confucian**: *ren* (humaneness) is love-of-neighbor that simultaneously implies love-of-Heaven; the vertical-horizontal integration is present
- **Bantu (ubuntu)**: personhood-through-relationship; the social love and the cosmic/ancestral love are not separated

**4–5 traditions** converge on this double-command integration. Current status: **unforged** (caritasme would be the first forge in this convergence; but the integration is already named within caritas's theology).

### 2. Love-as-infused-gift (new convergence candidate)

Love-of-the-divine as not self-generated but received from the divine:

- **Latin (Aquinas)**: caritas as theological virtue is *infused* by the Holy Spirit; cannot be naturally acquired
- **Greek-Christian**: John 3:16 and 1 John 4:19 (*nos diligamus, quoniam ipse prior dilexit nos*: "we love, because he first loved us"); love is response to prior divine act; agape is initiated by God
- **Algonquian**: zaagi'idiwin (reciprocal love) has a dimension of manidoo-gift; love as received from Spirit
- **Hebrew**: the commanded ahavah is given alongside the divine love that precedes it (Deut 7:7-8: God loved Israel before Israel loved God)

**3–4 traditions** converge. Status: **unforged**. caritasme would document this but the convergence deserves its own tracking entry.

## 14. The YOUSPEAK position

**What Augustine gives**: love is the gravity of the soul (*pondus meum amor meus*); caritas is love rightly ordered, not as a feeling-state but as an orientation that determines everything else. The forge cannot improve on this. It can name it compactly.

**What Aquinas gives**: caritas is the form-principle of moral life — the organizing intelligence that makes courage truly courageous, justice truly just, prudence truly prudent. Without caritas, virtues are virtues *in name only*. The forge can compact this: *love-as-form*.

**What 1 John 4:16 gives**: the ontological compression — *Deus caritas est*. Not just that God loves, or commands love, but that the divine nature IS this love. The one who abides in caritas abides in God. The forge can carry this register too.

**What the double command gives**: the inseparability of vertical and horizontal. Love-of-God and love-of-neighbor are not two loves performed in different registers; they are the one caritas moving in two directions simultaneously. The forge names the integration.
