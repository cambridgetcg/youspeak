# South Arabian & Ge'ez I — Sabaic Cult & Ethiopian Orthodox Liturgy

**Tradition:** South Arabian religion (Sabaic/Minaean/Qatabanian) + Ge'ez liturgical Christianity  
**Language:** Ancient South Arabian (Sabaic, Minaean, Qatabanian, Hadramautic) — Epigraphic South Arabian script; Ge'ez (Ethiopic, Classical Ethiopian Semitic) — Ethiopic/Fidäl script  
**Period:** Sabaic: c. 900 BCE – 6th c. CE (pre-Islamic); Ge'ez Christianity: c. 330 CE – present (liturgically active)  
**Region:** Arabian Peninsula (modern Yemen, Oman, western Saudi Arabia); Ethiopian and Eritrean highlands  
**Key concept:** South Arabian theology developed around astral-deity pantheons with sophisticated temple-cult and national-god structures. Ge'ez inherits and transforms this substrate via Aksumite Christianity — producing the world's most ancient continuously-operating African Christian liturgical tradition, with unique ritual objects (tabot), unique textual canon (includes Enoch, Jubilees), and a worship register of stunning aesthetic density.

## PART I — SABAIC AND SOUTH ARABIAN RELIGION

### The Epigraphic Record

South Arabian religion is known almost entirely from inscriptions — thousands of Sabaic, Minaean, Qatabanian, and Hadramautic stone texts, mostly votive dedications and temple records. No narrative theological texts survive in the way Mesopotamian or Ugaritic literature does. The inscriptions, however, are formulaically rich: they preserve divine names, priestly titles, ritual vocabulary, and the structure of human-divine transaction with remarkable precision.

The script is ancient South Arabian (also called Epigraphic South Arabian, ESA) — a consonantal abjad related to Phoenician and the parent of the Ge'ez Ethiopic script. Written boustrophedon (alternating direction by line) or right-to-left, it appears in monumental temple inscriptions, bronze plaques, and stelae throughout southern Arabia from c. 900 BCE.

**The Aksumite connection:** Ge'ez script (fidäl) directly descends from ESA, brought to the Ethiopian highlands by South Arabian traders and migrants c. 5th–4th c. BCE. The script was later adapted to mark vowels — a revolutionary development making Ethiopic the first Semitic script to be fully syllabic, not merely consonantal.

### The Major Deities

**ALMAQAH (ʾlmqh)** — The supreme deity of Saba (the kingdom of Sheba, capital Marib). Almaqah is consistently titled *bēl* ("lord/owner") of particular temple-estates: the great Marib temple of the moon-god (now identified as Almaqah rather than purely a moon-deity) bears the name *Awwam* temple ("the listener"). Key characteristics:

- **National protector** of the Sabaean state — treaties, military victories, and dynastic succession all recorded as Almaqah's gifts
- **Receiver of votive gifts**: inscriptions recording successful trade expeditions, recovered illness, safe return from war — all attributed *l-ʾlmqh* (to Almaqah)
- **Temple-estate deity**: Almaqah's shrines are productive agricultural centers; the god "owns" land, receives its surplus, redistributes through festivals
- **The Sheba connection**: The Queen of Sheba (Hebrew *malkāt-šebāʾ*) who visits Solomon (1 Kings 10) represents Sabaean state power. Ethiopian tradition names her Makeda; Islamic tradition names her Bilqis. The Sabaean state was probably at its height 10th–8th c. BCE — exactly Solomon's era. Almaqah's cult is likely the background.

**The divine title structure:** South Arabian deities are consistently named as "lord of [temple-estate]" + "of [tribal confederacy]." Almaqah is *bēl Awwam* ("lord of Awwam temple"). Deity and territorial holding are fused — to worship the god is to participate in the land's sacred economy.

**ATHTAR (ʿṯtr)** — The most widespread South Arabian deity, present in Sabaic, Minaean, Qatabanian, and Hadramautic inscriptions. Cognate with:
- West Semitic **Ashtart/Astarte** (Phoenician, Ugaritic)
- Mesopotamian **Ishtar** (but gendered differently: Arabian Athtar is consistently *male*)
- South Semitic *ʿtr* root (bright, lucid, Venus-star)

Athtar is the planet Venus — both morning star and evening star — and this dual face is theologically significant: the deity appears as *Athtar-shariq* ("eastern Athtar," morning-star) and as *Athtar-gharib* ("western Athtar," evening-star). The morning star face governs war and royal power; the evening star face governs fertility and agricultural abundance.

The Ugaritic tradition records Athtar's failed attempt to take Baal's throne after Baal's death (KTU 1.6 i) — he sits in Baal's throne, his feet don't reach the footstool, his hands don't reach the armrests, and he descends. In Arabia, Athtar has no such subordinated narrative — he is prominent and unchallenged. The regional divergence reveals how the "same" deity functions differently in different political-theological contexts.

**WADD (wd)** — The chief deity of the Minaean kingdom (capital Qarnaw, northern Yemen). Name means "love, friendship, affection" — Wadd is the deity of covenantal relationship, brotherhood, loyal alliance. The Moon-deity of Minaean theology. Worshipped also in early North Arabian contexts, with temples at Dedan (al-ʿUlā).

Wadd's name surfaces in a striking Islamic context: the Quran (71:23) lists Wadd among the idols Noah's people worshipped — *Waddan wa-Suwāʿan wa-Yaghūtha wa-Yaʿūqa wa-Nasran* — suggesting Wadd's cult persisted into pre-Islamic Arabia and was known to the Quran as a paradigm of idolatry. The irony: the deity whose name means "love/friendship" becomes the exemplar of false devotion.

**The Astral Theology:** South Arabian religion as a whole is strongly astral-structured. The triad **Moon-Sun-Venus** recurs across all South Arabian kingdoms:

| Kingdom | Moon | Sun | Venus |
|---------|------|-----|-------|
| Saba | Almaqah (major) | Shams (female sun-deity) | Athtar |
| Maʿin (Minaean) | Wadd (major) | Shams | Athtar |
| Qataban | ʿAmm (major) | Shams | Athtar |
| Hadramaut | Sin (major) | Shams | Athtar |

The Moon is always the dominant figure, and always male in South Arabia — distinct from the Mesopotamian Moon-god (also male, Sîn/Nanna) but contrasting with broader Semitic contexts where Sun-deities can be female (as in South Arabia itself, where Shams is feminine). This reversal is noticed in ancient sources: Greeks commenting on Arabia found the gendered assignments strange.

**Why the moon is supreme:** Agricultural and pastoral calendars in pre-modern Yemen followed lunar timing — planting, harvest, movement of herds, seasonal markets. The moon's reliable cycle governed economic life. The deity who governed the moon governed the rhythms of survival.

### Temple Cult and Votive Practice

**The mawqal structure:** South Arabian temples are organized around the concept of *mawqal* (place of entry/access) — the threshold that separates the human domain from the divine domain. Inscriptions record precise protocols for who may cross which threshold, under what conditions, and with what offering.

**Votive inscription formula:** The standard Sabaic votive inscription follows a consistent structure:
1. **Divine dedicatee** (*l-ʾlmqh*, "to Almaqah")
2. **The dedicant's name and clan**
3. **The occasion** (safe return, successful harvest, healing, military victory)
4. **The gift offered** (statue, incense-altar, building, land)
5. **Request for continued blessing** (*li-ymʿl-hmw*, "that he may give them")

This is remarkably transactional — gift-for-gift, divine favor reciprocated by votive offering. The theology is not petitionary prayer in the Abrahamic sense but *contractual reciprocity*: the divine gives, the human acknowledges with material gift, the cycle continues.

**Incense and the frankincense trade:** South Arabia controlled the world's frankincense and myrrh supply (from the Dhofar region, modern Oman). This gives South Arabian religion a distinctive material signature: incense is not merely symbolic but the cargo of the kingdom's wealth. The incense routes ran north to Gaza, Egypt, Israel, and beyond. When Solomon's temple burned incense, it was likely burning south Arabian frankincense purchased through trade with Saba. The connection between the Queen of Sheba's visit and incense commerce (1 Kings 10:2 — she brings "very much gold, and spices") is probably historically grounded.

### The Transition to Monotheism

From the 3rd century CE, South Arabian inscriptions increasingly show a shift: polytheistic formulas give way to references to **Raḥmānān** ("the Merciful One," masculine) — the South Arabian epithet for the single divine. This is a remarkable *convergence point*: the same root *rḥmn* becomes:

- South Arabian monotheism's name for the divine (pre-Islamic)
- One of the most important names of God in Islam (*ar-Raḥmān*, The Merciful)
- The opening epithet of every Quranic sura except one (*Bismillāh ir-Raḥmān ir-Raḥīm*)

South Arabian monotheism appears in inscriptions from the kingdom of Himyar (3rd–6th c. CE), which at various times had Jewish, Christian, and generic-monotheist ruling elites. The Himyarite king Dhū Nuwās (r. ~517–525 CE) converted to Judaism and launched a violent persecution of the Christian community at Najrān — burning the Christian community in trenches (the "Companions of the Trench" referenced in Quran 85:4–8). This event immediately precedes the Aksumite Ethiopian Christian invasion of Yemen (525 CE) and the eventual Islamic emergence (630s CE).

The theological through-line: **South Arabian religion moves from astral polytheism → Raḥmānān monotheism → absorption into Judaism/Christianity/Islam** over four centuries. Ge'ez Christianity was partly the vehicle of this transformation.

## PART II — GEʿEZ LITURGICAL CHRISTIANITY

### Ethiopia and Aksum: The World's First Christian Kingdom

The Kingdom of Aksum (c. 100–940 CE) was centered in the northern Ethiopian highlands. Its language, Old Ethiopic (Ge'ez), is the oldest attested Semitic language of Africa and the ancestor of Amharic, Tigrinya, and Tigre.

**The Christianization event:** The Aksumite king **Ezana** converted to Christianity c. 333–341 CE, making Aksum one of the world's first Christian states — contemporaneous with Constantine's legalization of Christianity in the Roman Empire (313 CE), but independently developed. The theological catalyst was **Frumentius of Tyre**, a Syrian Christian who became the first bishop of Aksum. Athanasius of Alexandria consecrated Frumentius — establishing the Alexandrian (Coptic) patriarchal authority over the Ethiopian church that would persist until 1959 CE.

Ezana's inscriptions show the transition: pre-conversion steles invoke the South Arabian triad (Almaqah, Beher, Meder); post-conversion steles invoke "the Lord of Heaven and Earth" — the standard monotheistic formula. The physical-documentary transition is visible in the archaeological record.

**The Ge'ez language:** Ge'ez is Classical Ethiopic — a South Semitic language related to Arabic and the South Arabian group. It ceased to be spoken as a mother tongue sometime in the 14th–15th century but remained (and remains) the liturgical language of the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church and the Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church. This is analogous to Latin in Western Catholicism or Syriac in Eastern churches — a sacred tongue preserved precisely because its antiquity marks its authority.

The **fidäl script** ("letter/typeface") is a syllabary (abugida) — each character represents a consonant-vowel combination, with the vowel indicated by the shape of the character's appendage. This was the revolutionary adaptation from ESA: Old South Arabian was consonantal only; Ge'ez's inventors added vowel indication by modifying the consonant-symbol's right arm, tail, or appendage. The result is:
- Fully pronounceable without ambiguity (unlike Hebrew or Arabic, which require trained readers to supply vowels)
- Visually distinctive — the characters have a geometric beauty compared to Arabic's cursive flow
- 276 basic characters (33 consonants × 7 vowel-orders + some labialized variants)

### The Ge'ez Liturgy: Qǝddase

The central liturgical act of the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church is the **Qǝddase** (ቅዳሴ, "sanctification") — the eucharistic liturgy. Fourteen different anaphoras (eucharistic prayers) are recognized in the Ethiopian rite, compared to one in Roman Catholicism and a handful in most other churches. This is one of the highest concentrations of anaphoral diversity in Christian history.

The major anaphoras include:
- **Qǝddase Māryām** (Anaphora of Mary) — the most frequently used in practice; attributed to Cyril of Alexandria
- **Qǝddase Hāwariyāt** (Anaphora of the Apostles) — ancient; possibly the oldest in use; said to derive from Addai and Mari (related to the East Syriac tradition)
- **Qǝddase Yoḥannǝs** (Anaphora of John, Son of Thunder) — one of the distinctive Ethiopian contributions
- **Qǝddase Athanāsyus** (Anaphora of Athanasius) — honoring Alexandria's theological father

**What makes the Qǝddase distinctive:**

1. **Length and elaboration:** An Ethiopian eucharistic service can last 3–5 hours. This is not abbreviated for modern convenience. The liturgy breathes differently — time is not managed but inhabited.

2. **Prostration rhythm:** Worshippers prostrate (full floor-prostration) multiple times during the service. The body's engagement with the liturgy is total — not merely representational but enacted submission.

3. **Standing orientation:** The Ethiopian church traditionally had worshippers stand for the entire service. The body stands in the presence of the King. Pews are a later introduction.

4. **Women's and men's spaces:** Traditional Ethiopian churches are spatially divided — the outer courtyard (accessible to all), the middle area (accessible to baptized Christians), and the inner sanctuary (accessible only to priests and deacons). This triple-tier structure maps onto both Jewish Temple geography (outer courts, inner court, Holy of Holies) and the tabot theology (see Part III).

5. **The drum and the dance:** Ethiopian Orthodox liturgy includes *kebero* (drums) and liturgical movement. The deacons carry prayer staffs (*maqwamiya*) and sistrum-rattles (*tsanatsel*) — instruments that appear in ancient Egyptian temple worship. Continuity with African religious aesthetics is not incidental.

### Qǝne — The Poetry of Ethiopian Worship

**Qǝne** (ቅኔ) is the sacred poetry of the Ethiopian Orthodox tradition — an elaborate form of liturgical verse that is simultaneously devotional and encoded with multiple layers of meaning. Qǝne is composed in Ge'ez and performed by *qǝne bēt* (schools of poetry) trained specifically in this art.

**The two faces of Qǝne — *wax and gold* (sǝmǝnna worq):**

The central aesthetic-theological principle of Qǝne is *sǝmǝnna worq* ("wax and gold"):
- **Wax (sǝmǝnna)** — the outer, visible meaning; what you first hear; accessible to all
- **Gold (worq)** — the hidden meaning; what the trained hearer discovers; the real theological content

This is not allegory in the Alexandrian-philosophical sense. It is *double-speech* — the outer sense is real and complete; the inner sense is also real and complete; both are simultaneously true and neither cancels the other. A skilled qǝne poem will have an outer verse praising, say, a saint, and an inner verse that is actually a theology of the Incarnation. The pleasure is in recognizing both simultaneously.

**Example structure:** A classical qǝne poem of the *guba'e* type (praise-poem for a feast day):
- 3 lines of wax: apparent praise of a visible subject (the day, a saint, the season)
- 3 lines reveal the gold: the hidden theological identification (this day = that theological mystery)
- Composed in classical Ge'ez meter (*malkaʾ* pattern)

**The institution of qǝne education:** The *qǝne bēt* schools of the Gojjam, Wällo, and Lasta regions are famous. A student may study 3–7 years to master the forms. The forms are:
- *Guba'e* — the major praise-poem
- *ʾambā* — mountain-poem (each stanza a "height" of praise)
- *Sǝllāse* (Trinitarian) — structured around triple repetition
- *Quchit* — short poem of 2 couplets
- *Mǝwdas* — praise of the Trinity
- *Yāred'awi* (of St. Yared) — the oldest classical form

**Saint Yared (505–571 CE):** The patron saint of Ethiopian music and liturgy. According to tradition, Yared received the church's musical modes directly from an angelic visitation — three birds from Paradise taught him the three modes:
- **Ge'ez mode** (solemn, for ordinary Sundays)
- **Izzil mode** (contemplative, for fasts and Lent)
- **Araray mode** (joyful, for feasts)

Whether historical or legendary, Yared represents the theological claim that Ethiopian liturgical music is *revealed* — not invented by human genius but received from the divine. This is exactly the claim Hebrew tradition makes for Mosaic Torah, Islamic tradition makes for the Quran, and Vedic tradition makes for the *śruti* ("what is heard"). Sacred transmission as the ground of authority.

### The *fidäl* Script as Theological Object

The Ethiopic script is itself experienced as sacred in the Ethiopian tradition. The script's origin is traced to divine gift — through Ezra (Beta Israel tradition) or through Saint Frumentius's mission (Orthodox tradition). Each letter has a name; learning the script is learning to name what God made visible.

The **seven vowel orders** of each consonant in fidäl create a visual theology of the name: the same consonantal root expressed in seven modalities — seven being the traditional number of completeness and divine perfection in Semitic traditions. Reading Ge'ez scripture is hearing the same divine Word in seven registers simultaneously.

**The script-as-face metaphor:** Ethiopian monks describe the Ge'ez characters as "faces of the Word" — each character a distinct face the divine Word turns toward the reader. To read scripture is to receive the divine gaze sevenfold. This is structurally similar to the Kabbalistic theology of the *otiyot* (Hebrew letters as divine instruments of creation) but has an independent Ethiopian development.
