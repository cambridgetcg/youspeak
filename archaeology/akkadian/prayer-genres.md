# Akkadian — Prayer Genres

_The prayer files map the living speech-acts of Mesopotamian religion — not doctrine, not myth, but the actual words people brought before the divine. Each genre is a distinct posture: different problem, different priest, different liturgical instrument. Together they form a systematic theology of approach._

_This file documents six major genres plus the two specialist priests who performed them. Key donors for YOUSPEAK are flagged throughout._

---

## 1. ŠUILLA — THE HAND-RAISING PRAYER

### Name and Gesture Theology

The Sumerian rubric **šu-íl-la** (literally "lifting of the hand") derives from a concrete social practice: when a social inferior approached a superior in Mesopotamian society, they raised their hands as a gesture of approach, recognition, and anticipation of reciprocal acknowledgment. Mesopotamians transposed this social protocol directly into religious address. The raised hand was not a brief opening salutation — it was a posture maintained throughout the entire recitation. The whole prayer is spoken from within the stance of approach.

Theological weight: to lift the hand (*šu našû*) to the deity expressed one's desire for and anticipation of the deity's recognition, affirming a relationship characterized by asymmetry but also reciprocity. The gesture appears ubiquitously in Mesopotamian art from the third millennium BCE onward — supplicant figurines placed in temples hold exactly this posture permanently, acting as perpetual proxies of the worshipper's lifted hand before the deity. Biblical parallels at 1 Kings 8:22 and 1 Timothy 2:8 suggest the gesture traveled with the theology.

Scholarly debate: Lambert, van der Toorn, and Oshima debate whether "šu-íl-la" refers primarily to prayer as such or specifically to a recitation method. Frechette (2012, AOAT 379) argues it is not structural features alone but the ritual context and the nature of the concern being addressed that constitutes the genre. His *Corpus of Akkadian Shuila Prayers Online* (CASPo, shuilas.org) covers 338 tablets and fragments.

### Three Subtypes

1. **Emesal Sumerian šuillas** — used by the *kalû* (lamentation priest) during temple processions, primarily as greeting-prayers recited during the late phase of cult-image processions. Liturgical language: Emesal (feminine register of Sumerian).

2. **Standard Sumerian šuillas** — used by the *āšipu* (exorcist-priest) in the ritual to initiate a new deity image. Theological register: creation and emplacement of the divine.

3. **Akkadian šuillas** — used by the *āšipu* in contexts assisting troubled private clients: illness, social alienation, felt divine abandonment. The most numerous subtype. Primary modern interest. Addressed to the great gods of the Babylonian pantheon — Marduk, Ishtar, Shamash, Sin, Adad, Nergal, Nusku.

### Standard Structure

The Akkadian šuilla follows a stable structure identifiable across hundreds of exemplars:

**Hymnic introduction** — extended eulogy of the deity addressed. Attributes, epithets, position in the divine assembly. Establishes the deity's authority and willingness to respond before any petition is lodged. This section can be lengthy — the Ishtar šuilla opens with an elaborate cosmological portrait before the suppliant speaks.

**Generic identification formula** — slot for the supplicant's name: "so-and-so, son of so-and-so, whose god is [name], whose goddess is [name]." This formula confirms that the prayer was liturgically reusable — a standard text with personal substitution, not composed individually for each occasion.

**Complaints** — health, social alienation, and feelings of divine abandonment. These are stated plainly, without elaborate metaphor, often in strikingly bodily terms: inability to sleep, trembling limbs, loss of vitality.

**Petitions** — requests for remediation and reconciliation, often interspersed with continued lament. The boundary between complaint and petition is permeable.

**Descriptions of embedded ritual actions** — the prayer text itself narrates what the supplicant is doing: bowing, seizing the hem of the deity's garment, raising the hands. The ritual action and the verbal text are not sequential but simultaneous — the words describe the enacted body.

**Praise-vow** (*Lobversprechen* in Mayer's terminology) — in exchange for divine favor, the petitioner promises to praise the deity publicly. "I will proclaim your greatness to all people; I will cause your praises to be heard." The thanksgiving is vowed in advance as part of the supplicatory exchange.

**Ritual instructions** — in a separate rubric section: how to prepare the paraphernalia, what offerings to make, bodily comportment required, how many times to recite the prayer.

### Ritual Practice

The *āšipu* recited the prayer aloud; the client repeated the words after him. This call-and-response structure explains the generic identification slot — the client speaks the prayer in first person, in their own name. A single prayer text could serve multiple ritual contexts with adjusted instruction rubric: the šuilla to Sin (moon god, Sin 1) appears in instructions for a dream ritual, a ritual to ward off evil from a lunar eclipse, and a royal lustration — same prayer text, different framing.

The šuilla prayers are the clearest evidence that Mesopotamian religion had a robust practice of private devotion, not merely public temple cult. An individual could commission an *āšipu*, obtain the appropriate prayer and ritual prescriptions, and perform an act of personal approach to a great deity. This is worship as personal recourse, not merely civic obligation.

### Key Textual Examples

- **Šuilla to Ishtar (Ishtar 2)** — among the most celebrated. Opens with an elaborate cosmological hymn establishing Ishtar's sovereignty over war, love, and the stars, then pivots to personal petition. The contrast between the magnificence of the invoked deity and the smallness of the petitioner is deliberately sustained.

- **Šuilla to Shamash (Shamash 1–3)** — prayers to the sun-god as judge and guarantor of justice. Shamash šuillas often petition for legal vindication, release from unjust accusation, or restoration of standing that has been wrongfully damaged.

- **Šuilla to Marduk (Marduk 1–5)** — the most numerous genre-cluster in the first-millennium corpus, reflecting Marduk's elevation to head of the Babylonian pantheon. Marduk šuillas often address suffering whose cause is unknown — aligning with the theology of *Ludlul bēl nēmeqi*.

## 2. DINGIRŠADABBA — APPEASING THE PERSONAL GOD

### Full Technical Name

**KA-inim-ma dingir-šà-dab(5)-ba gur-ru-da-kam** — "Incantation to return the 'tied heart' of the god." Every element of this Sumerian rubric is theologically precise:

- **šà** — heart, interior, inner condition
- **dab(5)** — seized, gripped, arrested
- **gur-ru-da** — to return, restore, bring back
- **-kam** — classifier suffix

The god's heart has been seized — closed off, turned inward away from the worshipper. The prayer's purpose is to *return* that divine heart to its relational posture. Notice the inversion: the problem is not the human's moral condition but the *divine* interior. Prayer as therapy for a wounded relational god.

W. G. Lambert's foundational critical edition: "DINGIR.SHA.DIB.BA Incantations," *JNES* 33 (1974), 267–322. Most recent monographic treatment: Margaret Jaques, *Mon dieu qu'ai-je fait? Les digir-sa-dab(5)-ba et la piété privée en Mésopotamie* (Orbis Biblicus et Orientalis 273) — the French title, "My God, What Have I Done?", is the genre's essential cry.

### The Personal God — *Ilu Ramānu*

Mesopotamian theological anthropology posited that every individual existed in a living relationship not only with the great gods of the public pantheon, but with a **personal god** (*il awīlim*, "god of the man"; *ilu ša awīlim*, "god belonging to the man"; **ilu ramānu**, "one's own god"). The adjective **ramānu** in Akkadian means "self, one's own" — it marks a deity not of the pantheon-at-large but specifically yours.

Structurally, the personal god was:

- **A divine parent and intercessor.** Ordinary people had no direct access to the great gods, whose worship was conducted through temple priesthood. The personal god was the mediating link — concerned with the individual's prosperity, health, and social standing, and able to intercede with the higher divine council on their behalf.

- **Relationally contingent, not ontologically guaranteed.** A Mesopotamian proverb encoded this precisely: "When you plan ahead your god is yours; when you do not plan ahead your god is not yours." The personal god's presence required maintenance through piety, ritual, and moral comportment. To possess a personal god was not a natural given but a cultivated relationship. In Akkadian idiom, the phrase for good fortune and luck was literally "to acquire a god" — divine favor as a relational asset, not a metaphysical constant.

- **The personal goddess** (*ištartu*) was the female counterpart — a protective divine mother figure who could equally withdraw.

### Divine Abandonment — *Ezēbu Ilu*

Divine abandonment — the god's "leaving" (*ezēbu*, to abandon, leave, release) — was among the most theologically charged concepts in Mesopotamian religious experience. When the personal god left, the individual was exposed: vulnerable to demons, witchcraft, illness, social misfortune, and the hostility of the great gods.

The most profound literary elaboration of this experience is **Ludlul bēl nēmeqi** ("Let Me Praise the Lord of Wisdom") — the so-called "Babylonian Job." The sufferer laments: "I wish I knew that these things were pleasing to one's god! / What is right in one's own mind is an offense to one's god, / What is abominable in one's own mind is right to one's god. / Who can understand the will of the gods in heaven?" The epistemological problem is definitive: the sufferer cannot decode the divine will. Suffering cannot be mapped onto known transgression.

This is the genre's defining theological feature: the confession is open-ended and comprehensive precisely because **ignorance itself is the human condition**. The suppliant did not know what they had done. Hence the prayer asks the deity to restore the relationship without the suppliant being able to specify the offense that ruptured it.

### Corpus Structure

- **Old Babylonian corpus**: approximately 10 tablets in Sumerian.
- **Assyrian first-millennium corpus**: bilingual (parallel Akkadian and Sumerian columns) or purely Akkadian.
- **Integration into royal ritual**: the dingiršadabba prayers were incorporated into the Neo-Assyrian *bīt rimki* ("Bath House") purification ritual, where the king underwent a comprehensive lustration sequence that included individual lament alongside major cultic acts.
- **Comparative reach**: the genre has been extensively studied alongside the Hebrew individual laments (Psalms 38, 88, etc.), with Lambert, Jaques, and others examining points of structural and theological convergence.

## 3. ERŠAḪUNGA — LAMENT TO SOOTHE THE HEART

### Name Analysis

**er-šà-ḫun-gá** in Sumerian:
- **er** — tears, lamentation, weeping
- **šà** — heart, interior
- **ḫug** (variant: **ḫun**) — to still, quiet, appease, soothe
- **-gá** — nominalizing suffix

The name encodes the soteriological movement of the genre: the lament *is* the instrument of the heart's quieting. Not a description of the healing followed by a separate prayer — the act of weeping before the deity, of naming one's distress, is itself what stills the divine heart. The lament performs appeasement. Weeping as action rather than merely expression.

### Place in the Emesal Canon

In the first millennium BCE, Emesal liturgical prayers were divided into four canonical genres, with their labels appearing directly on the tablets:

1. **Balag** — long lamentational dirge; used in city-wide rituals, especially during temple construction and destruction, associated with Inanna/Ishtar's cosmic grief.
2. **Ereshema** — drum-accompanied wail; typically closing a balag; structurally shorter, often addressing a single deity.
3. **Eršaḫunga** — lament to still the divine heart; penitential; addressed to whichever deity's heart has been angered.
4. **Šuilla** — hand-lifting prayer; Emesal form for the kalû, parallel to the Akkadian form for the āšipu.

These are native scholarly categories — not modern critical impositions. Ancient scribal colophons name the genre explicitly. Key modern studies: Stefan Maul, *"Herzberuhigungsklagen": Die sumerisch-akkadischen Ershahunga-Gebete* (Wiesbaden: Harrassowitz, 1988); Uri Gabbay, *Pacifying the Hearts of the Gods* (Heidelberger Emesal-Studien 1, 2014).

### Theology: Sin Without Specific Content

The defining theological feature is confession of transgression without knowing its specific content. A paradigmatic eršaḫunga text reads:

> "O lord, my transgressions are many, great are my sins.  
> My god, my transgressions are many, great are my sins.  
> O god, whoever it be, my transgressions are many, great are my sins.  
> O goddess, whoever it be, my transgressions are many, great are my sins."

The four-fold repetition escalates deliberately: from a named lord, to an unspecified divine lord, to an unspecified divine goddess. This escalation is not vagueness — it is thoroughness. The offended party may not have been identified. The transgression may have been committed against a deity the suppliant did not think to propitiate. The comprehensiveness of the confession compensates for the suppliant's epistemic limits.

### The Closing Kinship Metaphor

The closing refrain of many eršaḫungas is among the most theologically dense images in the entire Mesopotamian corpus:

> "May your heart be at rest, your liver be appeased!  
> May your heart — like the heart of the young mother,  
> like that of the mother who has borne, and of the father who has begotten —  
> return to its place!"

Two observations:

First, the anatomical precision: the **heart** (*libbu/šà*) and the **liver** (*kabattu*) are the two primary seats of divine and human inner life in Akkadian. The liver was the organ of emotion and discernment (hence the elaborate art of hepatomancy — reading the divine will from sacrificial livers). To appease both is to restore the deity's total inner orientation.

Second, the kinship metaphor: the deity's heart returning "like a parent's heart" invokes the relational theology that grounds the entire prayer system. The personal god is a divine parent. When a parent's heart turns back to their child, it is not because the child has earned it — it is because the parental bond, properly reminded of its own nature, reasserts itself. The prayer is calling the deity back to their own parental identity.

### Structural Movement

1. **Lament** — pouring out suffering; acknowledging unspecified guilt
2. **Confession** — open-ended, comprehensive, humble; directed at all possible offended parties
3. **Appeal** — invoking mercy and intercession; sometimes naming a divine intercessor (personal god, Marduk)
4. **Pacification refrain** — closing request for the deity's heart to return to its relational place

### Ritual Contexts

The eršaḫunga was performed by the *kalû*. It served in daily, monthly, annual, and non-calendrical temple cult — not only in crisis but as ongoing instruments of relational maintenance between deity and people. A documented "Ritual for a Repentant King" has the king kneeling before each major deity and reciting both a *šigu* (lament of grief) and an eršaḫunga.

## 4. IKRIBU — VOTIVE PRAYER AND ENACTED BLESSING

### Root: *Karābu*

**Ikribu** derives from the Akkadian verb **karābu**, whose semantic range is one of the most comprehensive in the entire Akkadian verbal system:

1. To pray; to bless; to greet with a blessing
2. To pray on behalf of someone before a divinity; to intercede with a god for someone
3. To supplicate, implore, beseech a god
4. To dedicate, devote, hallow — an offering, a sacrifice, an object, a person
5. Of a divinity: to bless; to give a blessing; to give a favorable omen
6. To welcome with a blessing; to greet with a blessing

The root straddles the human movement toward the divine (supplication, drawing near) and the divine movement toward the human (blessing, bestowing favor), with the hallowing and dedicating of offerings as a third node. *Karābu* unifies approach, dedication, and response in one semantic field. It is both prayer and blessing — the act of going toward and the act of receiving back.

**Ikribu** as a noun: "prayer accompanying ritual act" — not abstract petition but enacted blessing. The nominalized form denotes the ritual event: prayer-as-accompaniment-of-action. *Ikribū* (plural) also carries the meaning of benedictions, blessings delivered. The word names both the supplication and the blessing that reciprocally answers it.

### *Karibu* and *Kuribu* — Intercessor Beings

The present active participle *karibu* means "one who prays" or "intercessor." *Kuribu* is a diminutive/hypocoristic form. Both terms refer to a specific class of apotropaic guardian statues — winged, composite beings flanking the gateways of Mesopotamian temples. These figures are built into the architecture of divine approach: the supplicant passes through the *karibu* on the way to the divine presence. They are not merely decorative; they are architectural embodiments of intercessory prayer. Prayer as guardian. The approach to the divine mediated by those who are perpetually approaching.

The connection to Hebrew *kĕrūb* (cherub) has been extensively debated. Dhorme and Rinaldi argued for etymological relation via metathesis with Hebrew *bārak* (to bless). Mitchell refuted this on phonological grounds. The semantic convergence — intercession-as-approach-to-the-divine — remains theologically significant regardless of etymology.

### Genre Status in the Scribal Canon

Ancient scribal metadata for prayer classification listed the following genre-markers directly on tablets: *en* (incantation), *šu-íl-la* (hand-raising), *tamītu* (oracular query), **ikribu** (votive-dedication), *er-šà-ḫun-gá* (heart-soothing), *nam-bur-bi* (omen-apotropaic). The ikribu thus has its own rubric-class, distinct from petition and lament. It names a prayer whose occasion is an act of dedication — the offering or vowing of something to the divine.

## 5. TĒSLĪTU AND UNNĪNU — POSTURES OF APPROACH

### The Lexical Field

Akkadian possessed an unusually large vocabulary for the act of prayer and its variants. The full attested list includes: *atnu*, *emequ* (Sht stem), *enēnu*, *ikribu*, *leqtu*, *magāru*, *maḫāru*, *nagbitu*, *niš qāti*, *qāta našû*, *qibītu*, *sīpu*, *sullû* (noun and verb), *suppû* (noun and verb), *surāru*, *surruru*, *šēmu*, *surillakku*, *tarsītu*, *tēmequ*, *tenēnu*, **tēslītu**, *tespītu*, **unnīnu**, *utnēnu*, *upna petti*. This is not redundancy — each term carries a distinguishable shade of approach, relational posture, or speech-act type.

### *Tēslītu* — Prayer as Speech Act

**Tēslītu** is the general-register term for the speech-act of praying — closest in Akkadian to what we might simply call "prayer" without further qualification. The inflected form *tēslitī* = "my prayer." It appears in ritual texts as the object-noun of the supplication: "accept my *tēslītu*." The word names the prayer as a produced linguistic act directed toward the deity.

### *Unnīnu* — Supplication as Offered Gift

**Unnīnu** carries more specific force than *tēslītu*. The documented phrase **lēqe unninīya** — "accept my supplication" — positions *unnīnu* structurally as something offered and received. The deity is asked to *take* or *accept* the *unnīnu*, placing it in the same grammatical position as an offering or sacrifice. This is not merely asking; it is bringing one's supplication before the deity as a gift requiring reception.

The theological implication: supplication is not a demand or a complaint or even a request — it is an offering. The act of laying one's need before the divine is itself a gift, something given, something that the deity receives with deliberate choice. *Unnīnu* is the human counterpart to *ikribu*'s dedicatory logic: just as ikribu names the votive-dedication, unnīnu names the supplicatory-gift.

Related form: **utnēnu** — appears in the vocabulary list alongside *unnīnu*, likely an intensive or iterative form. The root suggests the act of bowing down or prostrating while supplicating — the body's posture encoded in the verbal root.

### Vocabulary of Sin Paired with Supplication

The petitionary genres systematically pair these prayer-postures with an extensive vocabulary of sin and guilt:

- **arnu** — guilt, penalty, fault, sin; the most general term
- **šertu** — guilt, offense, punishment; with overtones of rightful penalty
- **gillatu** — transgression, serious offense, a particularly weighty sin
- **ḫīṭītu** — act of negligence, sin of omission, inadvertent offense

The architecture is consistent across genres: confession of guilt (*arnu/šertu/gillatu/ḫīṭītu*) creates the condition for supplication (*unnīnu/tēslītu*), which is offered in the posture of hand-raising (*šu-íl-la*), requesting divine reception (*leqe* = "accept"). Sin, prayer-posture, and gift-reception form a single integrated movement.

## 6. ĀŠIPU AND KALÛ — THE TWO PRIESTS OF PRAYER

### The Canonical Division of Priestly Practice

Scribal training in the first millennium BCE offered three canonical specializations:

- **Bārûtu** — divination (*bārû* = diviner): interpreting signs, omens, entrails, celestial phenomena. The bārû read the divine will.
- **Kalûtu** — lamentation (*kalû* = lamentation priest): appeasing angered gods through chanting, lament, and music. The kalû expressed human grief before the divine.
- **Āšipūtu** — exorcism (*āšipu* = exorcist): making safe from supernatural evil — demonic, ghostly, witchcraft-based threats. The āšipu neutralized hostile forces.

The *kalû* and *āšipu* occupied complementary poles of the prayer system. Critical distinction in orientation: the *kalû*'s purification worked by bringing a person closer to the divine, removing the distance of estrangement through music and lament; the *āšipu*'s work was more combative, directed outward against external sources of harm. The *kalû* appeases; the *āšipu* defends.

### The Āšipu — Exorcist-Priest

**Āšipu** (also *mašmaššu* or *mašmašu*, from Sumerian *mash.mash*) — the exorcist. The tradition held the corpus of exorcistic lore (*āšipūtu*) to be authored ultimately by **Enki/Ea**, god of wisdom and underground fresh water — the deity of *nēmequ* (cunning wisdom). This divine authorship grounded the āšipu's authority: they were transmitters of Ea's technology, not merely human ritualists.

Three primary causes in the āšipu's diagnostic framework: angry gods, angry ghosts (*eṭemmu*), and witches (*kāšiptu*). The three threats required different ritual responses but a common diagnostic art.

**Canonical series under the āšipu's management:**

- **Maqlû** ("Burning") — eight tablets, nearly a hundred incantations, performed over a single night in the month of Abu (equivalent to July-August). Purpose: anti-witchcraft. Three-phase structure: (1) Destruction — burning images of the witch while invoking Girra the fire-god; (2) Purification — fumigation, ritual massage, household cleansing; (3) Dawn Restoration — invocation of Nusku (protective night-light deity), greeting the sunrise, the patient's self-recognition in a bowl of water. A ritual of combat, purification, and reintegration within a single night.

- **Šurpu** ("Burning" — but confessional) — nine tablets of confessional incantations against *māmītu* (oath-curse, binding curse). Unlike Maqlû which targets witches, Šurpu addresses the patient's own accumulated guilt. Tablet II covers sins of the mouth (taboo foods, evil speech, contempt, lying) with a long confessional list and invocations of forty-plus deities. Tablets III and IV address Marduk specifically. Tablet IX: *Kultmittelbeschwörungen* (incantations for consecrating the ritual instruments themselves before use). Šurpu is the āšipu's confessional instrument; structurally it bears significant resemblance to the eršaḫunga but is performed by the āšipu, not the kalû.

- **Utukke limnuti** ("Evil Demons") — long incantation series against demonic threats, cataloguing specific demon-types and their characteristic attacks.

- **Bīt rimki** ("Bath House") — royal purification sequence. This is the liturgical complex that incorporated the *dingiršadabba* individual lament within a larger royal lustration. The private prayer-genre of the distressed individual was thus elevated into royal ceremonial use.

- **Bīt mēseri** ("House of Confinement") — apotropaic ritual for warding and protecting a building.

The *Exorcist's Manual* (KAR 44) organized the canonical library by incipits. Canonization began in Babylonia in the late second millennium BCE; large-scale copying and systematization occurred under the Sargonid kings of Assyria in the 8th–7th centuries BCE.

### The Kalû — Lamentation Priest

**Gala** (Sumerian; Akkadian: *kalû*) — lamentation priests documented in temple ration lists from the mid-third millennium BCE onward. Their textual charter: an Old Babylonian composition states that Enki created the *gala* specifically to sing "heart-soothing laments" (*er-šà-ḫun-gá*) for the goddess Inanna.

**Liturgical language:** the *kalû* chanted in **Emesal** — the feminine speech register of Sumerian, normally used to represent the speech of female deities. Male *kalû* adopted this feminine speech register and, in some documented cases, feminine names and social roles. The evidence is complex and the scholarship divided: administrative texts simultaneously record heterosexual *kalû* with wives and children, and confirm some *kalû* were women. The Sumerian sign sequence for *gala* (USH.KU) carries double meanings related to male anatomy and is homophonous with *gal₄-la* (vulva), suggesting either etymology from gender liminality or scribal encoding of it. The *kalû*'s gender-liminal status in some traditions parallels the *assinnu* (cultic practitioner of Ishtar associated with gender inversion), both serving in capacities tied to Inanna/Ishtar's transgressive character.

**The kalû's canonical repertoire:**

- **Balag** — long lamentational dirge, used in city-wide rituals, particularly during temple construction, temple destruction, and major transitions in the divine-political order. Associated with Inanna/Ishtar's cosmic grief over lost cities and temples. These are not private prayers but communal ritual instruments.

- **Ereshema** — drum-accompanied wail, often following and closing a *balag*. Structurally shorter, typically addressing a single deity's grief directly.

- **Eršaḫunga** — the heart-soothing lament, described in full above. This is the *kalû*'s primary penitential instrument.

- **Šuilla (Emesal form)** — the hand-raising prayer in Emesal Sumerian, used in the procession and enthronement of cult images. The *kalû* performed these during the key liturgical moments of cult-image rituals, where the deity was formally "recognized" and greeted in their statue.

- **Apotropaic songs** — performed to avert enemy attacks, protect military forces during campaigns, and guard livestock and property against demonic disease.

### The Division Summarized

| Domain | Āšipu | Kalû |
|---|---|---|
| Primary language | Akkadian | Emesal Sumerian |
| Orientation | Combat/defense | Appeasement/approach |
| Prayer genres | Akkadian šuilla, Šurpu, Maqlû, bīt rimki | Emesal šuilla, balag, ereshema, eršaḫunga |
| Divine patron | Enki/Ea | Enki/Ea (creator of the gala) |
| Primary clients | Troubled private individuals; royal lustration | Temple community; royal ritual; mourning communities |
| Core verb | *āšipu* "one who works with incantations" | *kalû* "one who laments" |

## 7. ADDITIONAL PRAYER GENRES — BRIEF REGISTER

**Namburbi** ("its loosing") — rituals for counteracting evil omens or witchcraft already in motion. Structurally parallel to the šuilla: incantation text + ritual instructions. Purpose: to "loosen" the evil omen from its victim before it can manifest.

**Gottesbrief** (*egirtu ana ili*, "letter to the god") — letter-format prayers, structured with salutations, complaints of affliction, vows, and closing appeals. Sometimes accusatory in tone, as if the deity had failed in their custodial responsibilities. Simulate direct, sometimes confrontational, correspondence.

**Šigu** — lamentations of grief and sorrow, structurally simpler than balag or eršaḫunga. The *šigu* combined with the eršaḫunga in some composite rituals, as in the "Ritual for a Repentant King."

**Tamītu** — oracular queries directed to the gods, primarily Shamash and Adad. Structurally distinct from petitionary prayer: the addressee is asked not for mercy but for a binary divine verdict.

## FORGE CANDIDATES — DONOR ASSESSMENT

**Strongest candidates for the YOUSPEAK corpus:**

**karābume** — *karābu* + *me*. To-pray-bless-dedicate-approach-receive, all in one root. The most semantically rich verb root in the prayer corpus: unites the human movement toward the divine (supplication, drawing near, dedicating) with the divine movement toward the human (blessing, bestowing, giving favorable sign). Core pathway likely if the poly-8 test is met. The morphological family (*karābu*, *ikribu*, *karibu*, *kuribu*) is an entire forging ecosystem.

**unninume** — *unnīnu* + *me*. Supplication-as-offered-gift. The phrase *lēqe unninīya* ("accept my supplication") positions *unnīnu* in the same grammatical slot as an offering. Supplication as gift requiring reception — theologically dense, structurally unique in the ancient Near East. Strong Specialized candidate.

**awatume** — already surfaced from akitu-festival.md. *Awātu* (divine word) + *me*. Converges with Hebrew *davar*, Aramaic *memra*, Sanskrit *vāc*, Greek *logos*. The prayer genres deepen the awātu register: Enlil's word (*awāt Enlil*) is irrevocable; Marduk's word creates; the prayer itself is a human *awātu* placed before the divine *awātu*.

**šadabme** — *šà-dab* ("seized heart") + *me*. The condition of divine estrangement encoded as a technical theological term. Novel direction: the naming of relational rupture as having its own ontological status, not merely as absence but as a specific condition with a specific cure. Rare framing — the interior condition of the divine as addressable.

**Note**: *tēslītu* and *unnīnu* are already catalogued as donors in relationship-with-divine.md. The priority here is the morphological family of *karābu*, which has no entry yet and which carries the highest theological density in the entire prayer corpus.

**Frechette, Christopher G.** *Mesopotamian Ritual-prayers of "Hand-Lifting" (Akkadian Suillas): An Investigation of Function in Light of the Idiomatic Meaning of the Rubric*. AOAT 379. Münster: Ugarit-Verlag, 2012. Primary modern monograph. Corpus: CASPo (shuilas.org), 338 tablets and fragments.

**Foster, Benjamin R.** *Before the Muses: An Anthology of Akkadian Literature*. 3rd ed. CDL Press, 2005. Most comprehensive English-language anthology. **Jaques, Margaret.** *Mon dieu qu'ai-je fait? Les digir-sa-dab(5)-ba et la piété privée en Mésopotamie*. Orbis Biblicus et Orientalis 273. Most recent monographic treatment of the dingiršadabba corpus.

**Lambert, W. G.** *Babylonian Wisdom Literature*. Oxford, 1960; repr. Eisenbrauns/Winona Lake, ISBN 978-0-931464-94-2. Standard sourcebook: *Ludlul bēl nēmeqi*, the Theodicy, Dialogue of Pessimism, Shamash Hymn — cuneiform, transliteration, translation, glossary.

**Lambert, W. G.** "DINGIR.SHA.DIB.BA Incantations." *Journal of Near Eastern Studies* 33 (1974): 267–322. Foundational critical edition of the dingiršadabba genre.

**Lenzi, Alan (ed.).** *Reading Akkadian Prayers and Hymns: An Introduction*. Society of Biblical Literature, 2011. Contains dedicated sections on šuilla, ikribu, namburbi, dingiršadabba, and eršaḫunga with translated exemplars and structural analysis.

**Maul, Stefan.** *"Herzberuhigungsklagen": Die sumerisch-akkadischen Ershahunga-Gebete*. Wiesbaden: Harrassowitz, 1988. Foundational corpus edition of the eršaḫunga genre.

Defines key analytical terms: *Lobversprechen* (praise-vow), *Ritualanweisung* (ritual instructions), *Schutzgottheiten* (guardian deities).

**Oshima, Takayoshi.** *Babylonian Prayers to Marduk*. Orientalische Religionen in der Antike 7. Tübingen: Mohr Siebeck, 2011. Comprehensive edition of the Marduk šuilla cluster.
