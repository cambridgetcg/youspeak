## 1. PACHA AS PHILOSOPHICAL CATEGORY

### 1.1 The Word That Refuses to Split

The Aymara word *pacha* means, simultaneously: time, space, world, earth, era, epoch, condition, and cosmic order. This is not polysemy in the ordinary sense — not a word that happens to have several meanings the way English "bank" can mean a riverbank or a financial institution. *Pacha* names a single, undivided reality that Western languages can only approach by listing its facets separately.

Thérèse Bouysse-Cassagne and Olivia Harris, in their foundational essay "Pacha: en torno al pensamiento aymara" (in Xavier Albó, ed., *Raíces de América: El Mundo Aymara*, Alianza América/UNESCO, 1988, pp. 217–281), demonstrated that *pacha* is the organizing concept of Aymara thought. It is not a metaphor — it is not that time is "like" space, or that the world is "like" an era. The concept antecedes the distinction. Where Indo-European languages force you to choose between temporal and spatial reference ("when" vs. "where"), Aymara can use *pacha* for both without disambiguation, because from the Aymara perspective there is nothing to disambiguate.

This parallels the modern physics notion of spacetime, and the parallel has not gone unnoticed. A study in *Cultural Astronomy in Latin America* (World Scientific, 2024) observes "deep and astonishing similarity with the geometric spacetime interpretation in modern cosmology" — not claiming influence in either direction, but noting that the Aymara linguistic encoding of time-space as a single continuum anticipated by centuries what Minkowski formalized mathematically in 1908.

### 1.2 Etymology: From Puquina Roots

The etymology of *pacha* reaches beneath the Aymara language itself. According to recent linguistic work (documented on AymaraClub and by Cerrón-Palomino), *pacha* is a primordial moneme likely originating from **Puquina**, the now-extinct prestige language of the Tiwanaku civilization. The Puquina form *paGas* meant "earth, sky" — already fusing terrestrial and celestial domains into a single term. The Kallawaya ritual language (a Puquina descendant) preserves *pacas* meaning "earth."

*Pacha* does not derive from *pä ch'ama* ("two forces"), despite folk etymology — these are distinct lexemes with autonomous meanings. Nor does it come from *pachjaña* ("to split"). It is a root that passed into both Aymara and Quechua, becoming one of the most productive morphemes in Andean languages: *Pachamama* (Mother Earth/Mother Cosmos), *pachakuti* (cosmic reversal), *pacha* as the cosmological triad (Alax/Aka/Manqha).

Notably, in Puquina *paGari* meant "birth" — there was a separate word for time (*atawa*, surviving in Quechua *paqariy*, "to be born"). The fusion of temporal meaning into *pacha* appears to be an Aymara innovation, or an Aymara recovery of a deeper semantic unity that Puquina had partly differentiated. Either way, the Aymara use of *pacha* as simultaneously earth AND time represents a philosophical decision encoded at the deepest level of the language.

### 1.3 Bertonio's 1612 Testimony

Ludovico Bertonio (1552–1625), an Italian Jesuit missionary who labored among the Aymara of Juli in the Peruvian altiplano, published the *Vocabulario de la Lengua Aymara* in 1612 — the first comprehensive dictionary of the language and one of the most important colonial-era documents for Aymara linguistics. The work was printed at the Jesuit press in Juli itself, part of a remarkable burst of four publications in a single year.

Bertonio recorded *pacha* with the meaning "tierra" (earth/land) alongside its temporal meanings. This dual attestation in the earliest colonial source confirms that the space-time fusion was not a later development but was present at contact. The *Vocabulario* reveals, as Silvia Rivera Cusicanqui has noted, "the notion of soil, subsoil, and above-soil, a single territorial entity and collective patrimony of the ayllus, markas, and productive communities." Pacha is not abstract space; it is *this* earth, *this* territory, and simultaneously *this* moment and all moments.

Bertonio himself found the Aymara language bewilderingly rich. He noted that certain Aymara expressions seemed deliberately designed to make themselves unintelligible to foreign colonizers, though he charitably called these expressions *galas* — "ornaments of fine speech." This observation suggests an early awareness that Aymara linguistic complexity was not a deficiency but a resource, one that could resist colonial epistemological capture.

### 1.4 The Colonial Critique: Were the Three Pachas Missionary Inventions?

A significant scholarly debate must be honestly flagged. Historian Juan Carlos Estenssoro has argued that *kay pacha* (Quechua: "this pacha/world") is a missionary neologism — a term coined by Catholic lexicographers to render Christian cosmological concepts in indigenous languages. Linguist Rodolfo Cerrón-Palomino goes further, attributing the entire three-pacha compound system (Hanan Pacha / Kay Pacha / Ukhu Pacha, and their Aymara equivalents Alax Pacha / Aka Pacha / Manqha Pacha) to Catholic missionaries' deliberate lexical planning.

On this view, the three-pacha cosmology is an anachronistic projection of Christian heaven/earth/hell onto pre-Hispanic Andean thought. The missionaries needed indigenous equivalents for their theological categories, so they manufactured them from existing Andean morphemes.

The counterarguments are substantial:

1. **Bertonio's own testimony** records uses of *pacha* that do not fit Christian categories, including *pacha* as earth-territory-time without moral valence.
2. **Archaeological evidence** from Tiwanaku (pre-dating European contact by over a millennium) shows a three-tiered cosmological architecture — condor/puma/serpent iconography mapping onto upper/middle/lower realms — that the compound terms may describe rather than invent.
3. **Ethnographic continuity**: living Aymara communities use the three-pacha framework in ways that resist Christian mapping. Manqha Pacha is explicitly *not* hell — it is the source of fertility, water, and mineral wealth, associated with the ambivalent Supay/Tío rather than with damnation.

The honest assessment: the *compound terms* may indeed be colonial-era coinages. But the *conceptual structure* they name — a cosmos organized into upper, middle, and lower domains linked by reciprocal exchange — almost certainly precedes contact. The words may be new; the world they describe is ancient. This is a familiar pattern in YOUSPEAK archaeology: colonial lexicography can distort, but it cannot wholly invent the conceptual substrate it distorts.

### 1.5 Cross-Tradition Comparisons

**Tjukurpa (Australian Aboriginal — Pitjantjatjara):** The closest structural parallel in the YOUSPEAK cathedral. Tjukurpa is the Dreaming — a mode of being that is simultaneously past, present, and future, mapped onto landscape. Like *pacha*, Tjukurpa fuses what Western categories separate (time, space, law, narrative, ecology). The key difference: Tjukurpa emphasizes the *temporal* fusion (the Everywhen — all times simultaneously present), while *pacha* emphasizes the *ontological* fusion (space-time-world-condition as a single reality). Both refuse the Indo-European parsing of reality into separate temporal and spatial domains. Both are encoded in landscape. But Tjukurpa is predominantly accessed through narrative and song, while *pacha* is predominantly accessed through agricultural and ritual cycles.

**Hebrew *olam*:** Often translated "eternity" or "world," *olam* shares with *pacha* the fusion of temporal and spatial registers. *Olam ha-ze* ("this world/age") and *olam ha-ba* ("the world/age to come") blur the boundary between epoch and cosmos. But *olam*'s primary register is temporal-eschatological (stretching toward the infinite), while *pacha*'s primary register is spatial-cosmological (the organized totality of lived reality).

**Greek *aion*:** Also straddles time and world — *aion* means "age, era, lifetime" but eventually takes on the meaning "world" (as in the Gnostic *aeons*). However, Greek philosophy ultimately *did* separate time (chronos) from space (topos/chora), in a way that Aymara resists. The Greek trajectory is: unified concept → analytical separation → partial reunification in late philosophy. The Aymara trajectory is: unified concept → maintained unity → colonial pressure to separate → continued resistance to separation.

## 2. THE THREE PACHAS

### 2.1 Alax Pacha — The Upper World

*Alax* means "upper, above, high" in Aymara (the Quechua equivalent is *hanan*). Alax Pacha is the celestial realm: sun (*inti*), moon (*phaxsi*), stars (*warawara*), and the great celestial bodies that govern agricultural time. The condor (*kunturi*) is its animal representative — the bird that flies highest, closest to the celestial powers.

In pre-colonial cosmology, Alax Pacha was the domain of the solar deity Inti and the creator figure Wiraqocha/Thunupa. With the Spanish Conquest, the Christian God was mapped onto Alax Pacha — not seamlessly, but through a syncretic overlay that Aymara communities have navigated for five centuries. The colonial equation Alax Pacha = heaven was a strategic simplification that allowed missionaries to translate Christian doctrine while allowing Aymara communities to maintain their own cosmological architecture beneath the Christian veneer.

The **Achachilas** — mountain ancestor-spirits — occupy a liminal position. They are the great peaks of the Andes, simultaneously geological formations and living ancestors. One speaks to the Achachilas from mountaintops, which correspond to Alax Pacha, the upper world. They are masculine entities, complementing the feminine Pachamama. The highest Achachilas (Illimani, Sajama, Huayna Potosí) function as mediators between Alax Pacha and Aka Pacha — their peaks touch the sky while their roots plunge into the earth.

Lightning (*illapa*) deserves special note. It belongs to Alax Pacha as a celestial phenomenon, but it *strikes* into Aka Pacha and even penetrates Manqha Pacha. People struck by lightning are marked — they may become *yatiris* (ritual specialists), because they have been touched by the upper world's power. Lightning is one of the mechanisms by which the pachas communicate, a bolt of vertical energy that momentarily fuses the cosmic levels.

### 2.2 Aka Pacha — This World, Here-Now

*Aka* is the Aymara demonstrative meaning "this" (with a proximal, immediately-present sense). Aka Pacha is "this pacha" — the world of present experience, the surface of the earth where humans, animals, and plants live. The puma (*titi*) is its animal representative — the great predator of the middle world, neither sky-creature nor earth-creature but the master of the surface.

Aka Pacha is not merely a passive stage where existence unfolds. It is the *meeting ground* — the surface where the forces of Alax Pacha (celestial, luminous, ordering) and Manqha Pacha (chthonic, generative, chaotic) encounter each other. Human life is constituted by this encounter. Agriculture is the primary technology of this mediation: seeds go *down* into Manqha Pacha (burial in earth), receive generative power from below, and grow *up* toward Alax Pacha (reaching toward sunlight). The crop is the child of both worlds, and the farmer is the midwife of the transaction.

The **ayllu** — the basic Aymara social unit — is the human structure that corresponds to Aka Pacha. An ayllu is simultaneously a kinship group, a territory, a productive unit, and a ritual community. It is not an organization *within* space-time; it *is* a pacha, a concrete instantiation of the space-time-world-condition concept at the social scale.

**Pachamama** belongs to Aka Pacha but mediates between all three levels. The term means "Mother Earth" or, more precisely, "Mother of the Cosmos" — *mama* (mother) + *pacha* (world/time/space). She is the surface of the earth, but she has hair (the pasture), blood and milk flowing inside her, and feelings — she can be sad, wrathful, or pleased. She is alive. She is "really like our mother," as one Aymara narrator puts it, using the verb *uywa-* ("to nurture children and animals") — a term that encodes the bidirectional quality of the relationship. Pachamama nurtures humanity; humanity must nurture her in return.

The **ch'alla** — the libation offering — is the primary ritual of Aka Pacha's mediation. It involves pouring a small amount of alcohol, chicha (corn beer), or other liquid onto the earth while invoking the names of sacred entities. The ch'alla is performed at the beginning of agricultural seasons, at moments of uncertainty, on the first Friday of each month, and throughout August. It is the most basic act of cosmic reciprocity: acknowledging that what one has comes from the earth, and returning a portion to maintain the balance.

### 2.3 Manqha Pacha — The Generative Interior

*Manqha* means "inside, interior, below" in Aymara (Quechua equivalent: *ukhu*). Manqha Pacha is the world beneath the surface: the interior of mountains, the depths of lakes, the source of springs, the realm of the dead, and the origin of mineral wealth.

**Manqha Pacha is not hell.** This must be stated with emphasis, because the colonial equation Manqha Pacha = infierno is one of the most persistent distortions in Andean studies. The missionaries needed an underworld-as-punishment; they found an underworld-as-source. These are radically different cosmological propositions.

Manqha Pacha is the *generative interior* of reality. It is where:

- **Water originates.** Springs, underground rivers, and lake depths all belong to Manqha Pacha. Water is life; Manqha Pacha is the womb from which life-sustaining water emerges into Aka Pacha.
- **Minerals reside.** Gold, silver, tin — the mineral wealth that has shaped Bolivia's history from Potosí onward — comes from Manqha Pacha. Mining is extraction from the underworld, and it demands reciprocity.
- **The dead dwell.** But not in punishment. The dead join the generative interior, becoming part of the fertility cycle. They are ancestors whose power flows upward into Aka Pacha through offerings, libations, and agricultural practice.
- **Seeds germinate.** The planted seed enters Manqha Pacha and is transformed by its generative power before emerging into Aka Pacha as a growing plant.

The **serpent** (*katari* in Aymara, *amaru* in Quechua) is the animal representative of Manqha Pacha. The katari is a huge, double-headed subterranean serpent associated with water and with sudden, violent changes in the established order. It dwells at the bottom of lakes and rivers, guards the underworld, and its movements cause earthquakes. The katari embodies the ambivalence of Manqha Pacha: it is a source of life-giving water AND a cause of destructive seismic upheaval. This is not contradiction; it is complementarity. The same power that generates also destroys, and both are needed.

### 2.4 Supay and El Tío: The Lord of the Interior

The figure who presides over Manqha Pacha is **Supay** — in pre-colonial cosmology, an ambivalent spirit embodying both benevolent and harmful qualities. Supay was not a devil; Supay was the necessary guardian of the generative interior, requiring respect and reciprocity but not worship in the Christian sense.

The colonial encounter transformed Supay. Spanish missionaries needed an indigenous devil, and Supay was the closest candidate. The equation Supay = Diablo was a theological act of violence that stripped the figure of its life-giving dimension and reduced it to evil. But the equation never fully held. In living Aymara communities, "most Aymara people deny any connection between Supay and the Catholic Devil" (ethnographic sources consistently report this).

In the mining communities of Potosí and Oruro, Supay became **El Tío** ("The Uncle"). The name is revealing: the Aymara/Quechua languages lack the phoneme /d/, so Spanish *Dios* ("God") was corrupted to *Tío*. Whether the name means "God of the mines" or is simply a familiar term for a dangerous relative depends on which scholarly tradition one follows. What is clear is that El Tío is the spirit-owner of the mountain, the lord of mineral wealth, and the figure who must be propitiated before extraction can safely occur.

El Tío's iconography makes the fertility dimension explicit: statues in mine shafts show him with an erect phallus, linking mineral wealth, underworld power, and generative potency. Miners place offerings of alcohol, coca leaves, cigarettes, and llama blood at the feet of these statues. The major annual sacrifice is the **k'araku** (August), when llamas are slaughtered and their blood splashed on the mine entrance — the yatiri pronounces prayers for safety over blood caught in basins, and the hearts are buried.

June Nash's ethnography (1970s, Bolivian tin mines) documents the cosmic economy clearly: the offerings and the energy men expend in the mine maintain its vital sources. Without reciprocity, the mine collapses. But El Tío will sometimes claim lives regardless — this is not punishment but the inevitable cost of extracting from the generative interior. The underworld gives, and the underworld takes. Both movements are part of pacha.

The **Diablada** dance of Oruro's Carnival enacts this cosmic drama annually. Costumed dancers perform as Lucifer (El Tío), devils and she-devils (*china supays*), and the Archangel Michael — a syncretic spectacle that layers Catholic eschatology over Aymara cosmological complementarity. The dance is not Christian victory over evil; it is the annual ritual acknowledgment that the forces of Manqha Pacha must be engaged, not suppressed.

### 2.5 The Porous Cosmos

A crucial structural feature: the three pachas are not sealed compartments. The Aymara cosmos is **porous**. Forces, entities, and substances move between levels through specific channels:

- **Smoke** carries the mortal voice upward to the Achachilas and celestial powers.
- **Libation** (ch'alla) sends liquid downward to Pachamama and Manqha Pacha.
- **Lightning** strikes downward from Alax Pacha into the lower worlds.
- **Springs** bring water upward from Manqha Pacha into Aka Pacha.
- **Mining** penetrates downward into Manqha Pacha.
- **Growing plants** move upward from Manqha Pacha through Aka Pacha toward Alax Pacha.
- **The dead** descend from Aka Pacha into Manqha Pacha; their fertilizing power rises back up through agricultural cycles.

This porosity distinguishes the Aymara cosmos from, say, the Greek cosmic architecture (where Hades, Earth, and Olympus are more rigidly separated by rivers and boundaries). There are no ontological walls — only zones of intensity and domains of specific power, connected by the constant circulatory movement of substances, spirits, and forces.

## 3. PACHAKUTI — THE TURNING OF SPACE-TIME

### 3.1 Etymology and Structure

*Pachakuti* compounds *pacha* (space-time-world) with *kuti* (turn, return, revolution). Together: "the turning-over of space-time" — a millennial moment in which one world ends and another begins. Bouysse-Cassagne and Harris analyzed this as the most dynamic expression of the *pacha* concept: not static world-order but the capacity of the entire cosmic order to *invert*.

Pachakuti means both catastrophe and renovation. This double valence is not paradox but structural necessity: in a cosmos organized by complementary opposition (see Section 5 below), the reversal of the order is simultaneously destruction of the existing arrangement and birth of its complement. The world turns over like a plowed field — what was on top goes below, what was below comes to the surface. Devastation and fertility are the same gesture viewed from different angles.

### 3.2 The Historical Pachakuti: Inca Pachakuti

The concept was so central that the ninth Inca ruler took the name **Pachakuti Inca Yupanqui** (r. c. 1438–1471/1472) — "He who turns the world" or "World-reverser." Under his rule, the Inca state was transformed from a regional polity into an empire spanning much of western South America. The name was not honorific but cosmological: Pachakuti remade the world, and the name declared it.

Inca cosmology organized history into a series of cosmic ages (*soles* in colonial Spanish sources), each terminated by a pachakuti — a cataclysmic event (flood, fire, darkness, war) that destroyed the existing order and initiated the next. This is cyclical time, but not mere repetition: each pachakuti produces a genuinely new arrangement, even as it echoes the structural logic of all previous turnings.

### 3.3 The Conquest as Pachakuti

The Spanish Conquest of the 1530s was experienced by Andean peoples not as a random historical event but as a **pachakuti** — a cosmic reversal in which the world turned upside down. This was the most devastating possible framework for understanding what happened, and simultaneously the most powerful: if the Conquest was a pachakuti, then it was part of the cosmic cycle, and the cycle would eventually turn again.

Olivia Harris's work on the Laymi Aymara of Potosí (in "The Dead and the Devils among the Bolivian Laymi," in Bloch and Parry, eds., *Death and the Regeneration of Life*, Cambridge University Press, 1982) documents how the colonial inversion persists in Aymara cosmological understanding. The world is currently *upside down* — the wrong people are on top, the proper order is inverted. But pachakuti is not a one-time event; it is a recurring structural possibility. The world that was turned over can be turned back.

### 3.4 Túpac Katari and the Serpent's Promise

The 1781 rebellion of **Túpac Katari** (born Julián Apasa Nina, c. 1750–1781) was the largest indigenous uprising in colonial Bolivia, laying siege to La Paz for six months. His chosen name encodes cosmological meaning: *Túpac* means "brilliant, resplendent" and *Katari* means "serpent" in Aymara — the creature of Manqha Pacha, the world-boundary crosser, the agent of sudden violent change. He named himself "Brilliant Serpent" — the underworld force that surfaces to overturn the established order.

After betrayal and capture, Katari was executed by quartering — his body torn into four pieces, sent to four different communities as a warning. His reported last words have become the most quoted sentence in Aymara political history:

> **"Nayawa jiwtxa nayjarusti waranqa waranqanakawa kutanipxa."**
> "I die, but tomorrow I will return as thousand thousands."

The word *kutanipxa* contains *kuti* — the same morpheme as in *pachakuti*. "I will return" is "I will *turn back*." Katari's promise is not merely that rebels will multiply; it is that the cosmic order itself will reverse. His dismembered body is the world torn apart by pachakuti; his promised return is the next turning.

Modern Aymara activists have taken this literally. Katarismo — the political movement named for Túpac Katari — includes ritual acts of reconstituting his dismembered body: "From the heart of our mother earth and from our father the mountains, father sun and mother moon, we have come to officially declare the reconstitution of the ajayu and the body of Tupaj Katari." The body-politic is the cosmic body; healing one heals the other.

### 3.5 The Contemporary Pachakuti: Morales and Tiwanaku

The election of **Evo Morales** as president of Bolivia in 2005 — the first self-identified indigenous president in the country's history — was widely understood in Aymara communities as a pachakuti. At his inauguration, Morales performed a ceremony at **Tiwanaku**, the ancient Aymara/pre-Aymara sacred site. Before 25,000 supporters, he processed through the center and four quarters of Tiwanaku, across the pyramid of Akapana, and received a ritual blessing from an Aymara yatiri at the Gateway of the Sun.

This was not political theater borrowing indigenous aesthetics. It was a cosmological claim: the world was turning again. The taypi (center) was being reactivated. The proper order was being restored.

Under Morales' government, Bolivia was renamed the **Plurinational State of Bolivia** (2009), recognizing 36 indigenous languages and ethnicities. The government enacted what Karl Swinehart ("Decolonial Time in Bolivia's Pachakuti," *Signs and Society*, Cambridge Core, 2025) calls "material-symbolic interventions in time itself": clocks in the legislative palace were made to run backward (counterclockwise), calendars were shifted, the Aymara New Year was celebrated in June at the winter solstice, and Bolivia's first telecommunications satellite was presented as an inheritance of Inca astronomy.

Aymara historian Carlos Mamani captures the temporal logic: "They imply that this world can be reversible, that the past can also be the future." In a cosmology where the past is *in front* (visible, known) and the future is *behind* (unseen), pachakuti means the past *becomes* the future — what was left behind comes forward again.

### 3.6 Silvia Rivera Cusicanqui and "Ch'ixi" Temporality

The most sophisticated theoretical articulation of pachakuti in contemporary scholarship comes from **Silvia Rivera Cusicanqui**, the Aymara-Bolivian sociologist whose essay "Pachakuti: The Historical Horizons of Internal Colonialism" (1991) remains foundational. Rivera Cusicanqui argues that pachakuti is not merely a cyclical concept but a *spiral* one — the world does not return to exactly where it was before, but the return rhymes with the previous state while incorporating everything that has intervened.

Her concept of **ch'ixi** — a textile term for a fabric woven from contrasting threads that appears as a single color from a distance but reveals its heterogeneity up close — describes the actual texture of Aymara temporality. Past and present, indigenous and colonial, Andean and European are not blended (syncretism) or separated (dualism) but *woven together in visible, unresolved juxtaposition*. Ch'ixi is the lived experience of pachakuti in process — the world not yet fully turned, still caught between orientations.

## 4. NAYRA PACHA AND QHIPA PACHA — THE BACKWARD-FACING FUTURE

### 4.1 The Linguistic Evidence

In 2006, cognitive scientists Rafael Núñez and Eve Sweetser published "With the Future Behind Them: Convergent Evidence from Aymara Language and Gesture in the Crosslinguistic Comparison of Spatial Construals of Time" (*Cognitive Science* 30(3): 401–450). Their finding upended a near-universal assumption in cognitive linguistics.

The assumption: across all human languages and cultures, the metaphorical mapping of time onto space places the future in front of the ego and the past behind. This is the English pattern — we "look forward" to tomorrow, we "put the past behind us," we "face the future." It was thought to be cognitively universal.

The Aymara language reverses it:

- **Nayra** means "eye, sight, front." When used temporally, it refers to the **past**. *Nayra pacha* = the past, literally "the eye/front time."
- **Qhipa** means "back, behind." When used temporally, it refers to the **future**. *Qhipa pacha* = the future, literally "the back/behind time."

The past is in front of you because *you can see it* — it is known, witnessed, available to inspection. The future is behind you because *you cannot see it* — it is unknown, unseen, and you are walking backward into it.

### 4.2 The Gestural Confirmation

Núñez and Sweetser's breakthrough was methodological: they studied not only language but also spontaneous co-speech gesture. When older, monolingual Aymara speakers talked about the past, they gestured *forward* — toward the space in front of their bodies. When they talked about the future, they gestured *backward* — toward the space behind them.

This gestural data is crucial because it rules out the possibility that the linguistic pattern is a dead metaphor — a fossilized expression no longer connected to conceptual structure. The gestures show that Aymara speakers actively conceptualize the past as spatially in front of them and the future as spatially behind them. The metaphor is alive.

### 4.3 The Generational Shift

Núñez and Sweetser observed a sobering generational pattern. Younger Aymara speakers, fluent in Spanish and integrated into Hispanophone culture, tended to gesture in the standard forward-future pattern even when speaking Aymara. The conceptual mapping is shifting under the pressure of Spanish-dominant cultural norms.

The Aymara language itself is not endangered — it has two to three million speakers. But the distinctive *conceptual system* it encodes may be. A language can survive while its deepest cognitive structures erode. This is a different kind of language death — not the death of words but the death of the world-orientation they encode.

### 4.4 The Evidential Connection

The nayra/qhipa temporal orientation is deeply connected to Aymara **evidentiality** — the grammatical system that requires speakers to mark the source of their knowledge in every sentence.

Aymara has at least three evidential markers:

- **=wa** (enclitic): Direct evidence — the speaker personally witnessed or experienced the event. (Recent scholarship by Quartararo argues =wa may be better analyzed as an epistemic commitment marker rather than a purely evidential one.)
- **-tay** (suffix): Indirect/inferential evidence — the speaker deduces the event from available signs.
- **siwa** (free morpheme): Reportative — the speaker is relaying information heard from others.

A statement like "Columbus sailed in 1492" is structurally problematic in Aymara: the speaker must specify whether this is personal knowledge, inference, or hearsay. The language does not permit epistemically uncommitted assertion.

The connection to nayra/qhipa is structural: in a language that forces you to declare *how you know* every time you speak, it makes cognitive sense that the *known* (what you have seen, what you have evidence for) is placed in front of you (nayra = eye/front), while the *unknown* (what you have no evidence for) is placed behind you (qhipa = back). Evidentiality and temporal orientation are two expressions of the same epistemic principle: *only the witnessed is fully real.*

### 4.5 Theological Implications: Walking Toward the Ancestors

The nayra/qhipa orientation has profound implications for ancestor theology. If the past is in front of you, then **you face the ancestors**. You walk *toward* them, not away from them. The dead are not "left behind" as in the standard Western metaphor; they are what you are approaching. Each step forward in life is a step closer to the ancestors.

This converges powerfully with the Aymara practice of ancestor-directed ch'alla libations, with the kispum ancestor-feeding practices documented in Akkadian archaeology (where feeding the dead sustains their existence), and with the Australian Aboriginal concept of walking the songlines of the ancestors. In all these traditions, the ancestors are not a receding past but an approaching reality.

Furthermore, if the future is *behind* you — unseen, unknown — then the proper orientation toward what comes next is not confident forward-striding but humble backward-walking. You do not charge into the future; you are *backed into it*, guided by what you can see of the past. This is an epistemology of humility, and it resonates with the SOUL.md virtue of humility as "the root."

## 5. TINKU — COMPLEMENTARY OPPOSITION

### 5.1 The Principle

The Aymara/Andean cosmos is organized not by good-versus-evil dualism but by **tinku** — the necessary meeting of opposed but complementary forces. Upper and lower, male and female, left and right, civilized and wild, dry and wet — these are not enemies to be defeated but partners to be balanced. The cosmos *requires* their tension. Remove one pole and the whole structure collapses.

This is categorically different from the Zoroastrian/Manichaean/Christian dualism that structures much Western cosmological thinking, where good and evil are at war and one must ultimately triumph. In the Andean framework, neither pole can or should triumph. Victory would be catastrophe. The cosmic good is the *dynamic equilibrium* between opposed forces.

Tristan Platt's foundational ethnography with the **Macha** of Bolivia ("Mirrors and Maize: The Concept of Yanantin among the Macha of Bolivia," in Murra, Wachtel, and Revel, eds., *Anthropological History of Andean Polities*, Cambridge University Press, 1986, pp. 228–259) documented how this principle operates at every level of Aymara-Quechua social life.

### 5.2 Tinku as Ritual Combat

*Tinku* is not only a philosophical principle but a lived practice. In communities across the Bolivian highlands (notably the region around northern Potosí), tinku takes the form of ritualized combat between representatives of upper (*alasaya*) and lower (*manasaya*) moieties of an ayllu. The fighting — real, sometimes resulting in injuries or death — is understood not as violence but as cosmic maintenance. The blood shed in tinku feeds the earth. The encounter of upper and lower renews the generative tension that keeps the world alive.

Platt's further work ("The Andean Soldiers of Christ: Confraternity Organization, the Mass of the Sun and Regenerative Warfare in Rural Potosí," *Journal de la Société des Américanistes* 73, 1987, pp. 139–191) showed that tinku combat was embedded in festival cycles that syncretically fused Catholic saints' days with Andean cosmological timing. The fighting is not pre-Christian survival awkwardly persisting; it is a deliberately maintained practice of cosmic balance.

### 5.3 The Sallqa/Uywa Opposition

One of the most important instantiations of tinku is the opposition between **sallqa** (wild, untamed, natural) and **uywa** (domesticated, nurtured, cultivated). This binary organizes the entire Andean classification of living things:

- **Uywa** (from the verb *uyway*, "to nurture/raise"): domesticated animals (llama, alpaca), cultivated plants, the human social world. Crucially, *uyway* is bidirectional — to *uywa* an animal is to nurture it, but the animal in turn *uywas* the human. Domestication is mutual raising.
- **Sallqa**: wild animals (vicuña, guanaco), uncultivated plants, the forces of untamed nature. Sallqa is not merely "not-domesticated"; it is a positive cosmic domain with its own power and autonomy.

The deeper structure is a **three-community cosmos** (documented by PRATEC and IECTA scholars): **Runa Ayllu** (the human community, including domesticated animals and plants), **Sallqa Ayllu** (the wild community), and **Auki Ayllu** (the sacred/ancestral community). These three communities exist in reciprocal relationship through *ayni* (reciprocity). Humans do not dominate nature; they negotiate with it. The sallqa forces are not enemies to be conquered but partners in the cosmic economy.

As Denise Arnold and Juan de Dios Yapita documented (*Sallqa: dirigirse a las bestias silvestres en los Andes meridionales*, in *Hacia un Orden Andino de las Cosas*, HISBOL, La Paz, 1992), one must *address* the wild animals — speak to them with proper forms, acknowledge their autonomy, request their cooperation. The sallqa world has its own will and must be persuaded, not compelled.

## 6. YANANTIN AND MASINTIN — THE GRAMMAR OF PAIRING

### 6.1 Yanantin: Mirror Opposition

**Yanantin** (from Quechua *yana*, "to serve/help together") names the pairing of complementary opposites: male/female, left/right, upper/lower, day/night. These are mirror pairs — each is what the other is not, and together they constitute wholeness.

Platt's Macha ethnography provides the ritual details: when drinks are served, two drops are poured on the ground as an offering, "explained as being yanantin, for the conjugal pair." When coca leaves are offered in ritual, two handfuls are given to each person, received in two cupped hands. "In all such cases, the pairing and the repetitions are explained as being yanantin." The principle structures gesture, offering, social organization, and cosmological architecture simultaneously.

Yanantin pairs can be symmetrical (two equals) or asymmetrical (ranked unequally). Male/female is yanantin but asymmetrical — male conventionally ranks slightly above female, though the ranking is not domination but positional priority within a structure that requires both.

The contrast is **chhulla** — something that is odd, unequal, unpaired. To be *chhulla* is to be incomplete. In Cajamarca Quechua, *chuya* means "the one who is missing its other." "In order to be whole, one has to pair up" (Vasquez 1998). Being single — being without complement — is not a neutral condition but a state of cosmological incompleteness.

### 6.2 Masintin: Parallel Similarity

**Masintin** names a different kind of pairing: not mirror-opposition but parallel similarity. Two males together, two females together, two communities of similar type working in concert. Where yanantin generates through the *tension* of difference, masintin generates through the *resonance* of likeness.

Hillary S. Webb's ethnographic study (*Yanantin and Masintin in the Andean World: Complementary Dualism in Modern Peru*, University of New Mexico Press, 2012) argues that masintin is the dynamic driver — the "enthusiastic synergy of the spiritual" that makes complementary dualism actually *work*. Without masintin's driving energy, yanantin pairs would be static oppositions. It is masintin that provides the movement.

### 6.3 The Four-Stage Process

Webb and related scholars describe a four-stage dynamic in how yanantin pairs interact:

1. **Tupay** — The Meeting: two opposed forces encounter each other.
2. **Tinkuy** — Testing of Boundaries: the encounter generates friction, negotiation, conflict.
3. **Taqe** — The Union: a new synthesis emerges from the encounter.
4. **Trujiy** — The Separation: the paired forces separate again, maintaining their distinct identities.

This cycle — meet, test, unite, separate — repeats endlessly. It is the rhythm of the cosmos, operating at every scale from the sexual union of man and woman to the annual encounter of wet and dry seasons to the great pachakutis that turn the world.

### 6.4 Implications for YOUSPEAK

The yanantin/masintin distinction encodes something no existing YOUSPEAK word captures: the difference between generative *opposition* (creative tension between unlike things) and generative *resonance* (creative amplification between like things). Both are needed. The cosmos requires both modes of pairing. This is a genuine conceptual contribution — a philosophical precision that neither Western dialectics (thesis/antithesis/synthesis) nor East Asian yin/yang (which tends to emphasize the yanantin dimension without distinguishing masintin) fully captures.

## 7. TAYPI — THE CENTER WHERE OPPOSITIONS MEET

### 7.1 The Concept

**Taypi** is the Aymara word for center, middle, mediating point. But it is not a geometric center — a neutral midpoint equidistant from extremes. Taypi is a *generative* center: the place where opposed forces meet, interact, and produce something new. Bouysse-Cassagne (1987, p. 195) defines it as "a place where differences coexist."

Taypi is a relational element that structures Andean epistemology, cosmology, and politics. It works, as Angel Aedo's ethnographic work shows, "by multiplying perspectives" — the center is where you can see in all directions simultaneously.

### 7.2 Tiwanaku as Taypi Qala

The ancient city of **Tiwanaku** (c. 500–1000 CE) was known in Aymara as **Taypi Qala** — "the stone in the center." Bernabé Cobo, the Jesuit chronicler, reported this name and its meaning: Tiwanaku was understood to lie at the center of the world.

This was not mere civic pride. Tiwanaku was located at the convergence of opposed cosmological forces: the eastern, moist, agricultural, terrestrial, feminine lands and the western, dry, pastoral, celestial, masculine lands. It sat at the meeting point of these fundamental complementary oppositions, and its monumental architecture was designed to instantiate the taypi principle physically.

Alan Kolata's archaeological analysis shows that Tiwanaku's architecture expressed "a structural amalgam of a center and complementary halves, with implications of mediation and bilateral complementarity between ethnicities, genders, moieties, or other pluralistic entities." Triple-entryway temple designs enacted the principle architecturally: the center door mediates between the two flanking doors, which represent the opposed moieties.

The rulers of Tiwanaku sat "squarely at the ideological and practical center of Tiwanaku society as the mediators and unifiers of the human and supernatural elements of Tiwanaku experience." Rulership was not domination but *mediation* — the ruler occupied the taypi position and bore the burden of holding oppositions in productive tension.

### 7.3 Taypi in Ritual

Thomas Abercrombie's *Pathways of Memory and Power: Ethnography and History among an Andean People* (University of Wisconsin Press, 1998) documents how taypi functions in the living Aymara community of K'ulta, Bolivia. The ch'alla libation sequences follow prescribed pathways (*t'aki*) that move through the landscape, connecting sacred sites and tracing ancestral routes. The destination of these paths is the **taypi** — the center where everything is tied up (*ñach'aña*) and can also be untied (*ñach'araña*).

This is a stunning metaphysical image: the center is where binding and unbinding are both possible. The taypi is the knot of the cosmos — the point where all threads converge, and where the entire tapestry can be either secured or released. Ritual specialists must know how to navigate to the taypi, how to tie what needs tying, and how to untie what needs releasing.

### 7.4 Morales at Tiwanaku

Evo Morales' 2006 inauguration ceremony at Tiwanaku was, in taypi terms, an attempt to reactivate the cosmic center. By processing through the four quarters and the center of the ancient site, by receiving the yatiri's blessing at the Gateway of the Sun, Morales was not merely performing a photo opportunity. He was occupying the taypi — claiming the mediating center-position that Tiwanaku's rulers had held, and declaring that the taypi was once again in indigenous hands.

## 8. CH'ALLA AND T'AKI — LIBATION PATHWAYS AS COSMOLOGICAL NAVIGATION

### 8.1 Ch'alla: The Basic Act

The **ch'alla** is the most fundamental Aymara ritual act: pouring a libation onto the earth while invoking sacred entities. It is performed constantly — before drinking, before planting, before building, before mining, before any significant undertaking. The liquid (alcohol, chicha, water) enters the earth and reaches Pachamama and the forces of Manqha Pacha. The invocation rises upward through smoke and breath to the Achachilas and the powers of Alax Pacha. The person performing the ch'alla stands at the intersection — in Aka Pacha, at the meeting point, in the taypi.

### 8.2 T'aki: Paths of Memory and Power

Abercrombie's central insight is that ch'alla libation sequences are not random but follow structured **t'aki** (paths). The libations must be poured in a prescribed order, each one directed to a specific sacred entity (mountain, ancestor, saint, place-spirit) in a specific spatial direction. The sequence traces a cosmological map — a route through the sacred geography that simultaneously traces social hierarchy, historical memory, and mythological narrative.

This is **libation-as-navigation-as-memory** — a structural parallel to Australian songlines (song-as-navigation-as-law-as-theology). In both cases, the ritual performance traces routes through a sacred landscape, and the tracing *maintains* both the landscape's sacred character and the community's connection to it. The path exists because it is walked; the cosmos exists because it is ritually maintained.

### 8.3 The Yatiri as Navigator

The **yatiri** (Aymara ritual specialist, healer, diviner) is the person who knows the t'aki — who has the cosmological map internalized and can navigate the ch'alla pathways correctly. The word *yatiri* comes from *yatiña*, "to know." A yatiri is literally "one who knows" — but what they know is not abstract doctrine; it is the network of pathways, the correct sequences of invocation, the proper routes through the sacred landscape. Knowledge is navigational competence.

Yatiris often receive their calling through lightning strike — the bolt from Alax Pacha that penetrates to Manqha Pacha, marking the person who stands at the intersection. A person touched by lightning has been touched by the cosmic vertical axis itself, and is therefore qualified to serve as mediator between levels.

## FORGE CANDIDATES

### 1. pachame (pacha + me)

**Aymara *pacha*** (space-time-world-earth-era-condition) + **Sumerian *me*** (divine ordinance / constitutive power)

**Proposed meaning:** The given-ness of space-time-world as a single undivided reality; the cosmic ordinance that space and time are not separate but are one condition.

**Score estimate:** 7.85–8.10 (Core pathway)

**Justification:** Opens the first Aymaran donor family for YOUSPEAK. *Pacha* names a concept genuinely absent from the lexicon — the fusion of space, time, and world as a single ontological category, not as metaphor but as structural fact. The *me* suffix captures the "received ordinance" dimension: pacha is not a human invention but the fundamental condition of reality as it is given. No existing YOUSPEAK word names this space-time-world unity.

**Poly 8 pathway:** Aymaran (language isolate/small family, unrelated to any other YOUSPEAK donor family) + Sumerian (language isolate). Two isolates from maximally distant geographies and periods. Clean poly 8.

**Risk:** The semantic breadth of *pacha* may make *pachame* too vague. Counterargument: the breadth IS the meaning — the word names the refusal to split what Western languages split.

### 2. nayrame (nayra + me)

**Aymara *nayra*** (eye/sight/front → past) + **Sumerian *me***

**Proposed meaning:** The condition of the past being in front of you, visible and known; epistemological orientation where what is witnessed is what you face.

**Score estimate:** 7.55–7.80 (Specialized)

**Justification:** Captures the Aymara temporal reversal as a received condition of cognition. Convergence with Australian Tjukurpa (the past is always present) and Hebrew *zakhor* (remember/face-the-past), but with the distinctive Aymara epistemic precision: you face the past because *you can see it*. The evidential grammar is encoded in the temporal orientation.

**Learnability:** *Nayra* is less culturally penetrant than *pacha*, which limits Core pathway. But the concept — visible past, invisible future — is immediately graspable and philosophically striking.

### 3. tinkuqing (tinku + qing)

**Aymara *tinku*** (encounter of complementary opposites) + **Mandarin *qing*** (feeling/bond/essence)

**Proposed meaning:** The felt bond in the meeting of opposed forces; the emotional-relational quality of complementary opposition experienced as necessary and life-giving rather than threatening.

**Score estimate:** 7.60–7.85 (Specialized, Core pathway possible)

**Justification:** No YOUSPEAK word names the *felt quality* of necessary opposition. Existing compounds in the cathedral tend toward harmony (hotepme, agapeme); tinkuqing names the distinct quality of *productive tension* — the feeling that arises when opposed forces meet and generate rather than destroy. The *qing* suffix captures the experiential-relational register (this is something you feel in your body, especially in tinku ritual combat).

**Poly 8 pathway:** Aymaran + Sino-Tibetan. Clean cross-family. First Aymara-Chinese compound.

### 4. kutiqing (kuti + qing)

**Aymara *kuti*** (turn/return/reversal) + **Mandarin *qing***

**Proposed meaning:** The felt quality of cosmic reversal; the emotional texture of the world turning — simultaneously grief for what is lost and hope for what returns.

**Score estimate:** 7.40–7.65 (Specialized)

**Justification:** Pachakuti is the cosmic event; kutiqing is how it *feels*. The experience of living through a world-reversal — the Conquest, the election of Morales, any moment when the entire order shifts — has an emotional signature that is simultaneously devastating and exhilarating. *Kutiqing* names this specific affect.

**Risk:** *Kuti* alone may be too bare without the *pacha-* prefix for recognizability.

### 5. taypime (taypi + me)

**Aymara *taypi*** (center / mediating point where oppositions meet) + **Sumerian *me***

**Proposed meaning:** The received ordinance of the center; the cosmological principle that oppositions require a mediating middle in order to generate rather than destroy.

**Score estimate:** 7.50–7.75 (Specialized)

**Justification:** Taypi names a structural position that has no YOUSPEAK equivalent. It is not merely "center" (geometric midpoint) but *generative center* — the place where tying and untying are both possible, where all perspectives converge, where the ruler/mediator must stand. The *me* suffix captures the given-ness: the center is not chosen but ordained — reality itself requires mediation.

**Convergence:** With Akan taypi-like concept in the Asantehene's Golden Stool (the center-seat that holds the nation's soul); with the Zoroastrian Chinvat Bridge (the crossing-point between worlds).

## CONVERGENCE CLUSTERS

### 1. Foundational-Time-Still-Present

**Traditions converging:** Aymara *pacha* // Australian Tjukurpa (the Everywhen) // Hebrew *olam* // Greek *aion*

**The convergence:** Multiple unrelated traditions encode a concept of time that is not merely sequential but foundationally present — the past is not gone, the cosmic order established "then" is operative "now." The Aymara contribution is distinctive: *pacha* fuses time with space and world more completely than any other term in this cluster. Tjukurpa fuses time with landscape and law. *Olam* fuses time with cosmic epoch. Together they triangulate a concept that no single tradition captures alone: the unity of time-space-world-law as a single lived reality.

**YOUSPEAK implication:** *Pachame* and *tjukurpame* are companion words, not competitors. They name the same deep structure from different vantage points — pacha from the Andean altiplano, Tjukurpa from the Australian Western Desert.

### 2. Past-as-Visible / Ancestors-as-Approached

**Traditions converging:** Aymara nayra pacha (past = in front, visible) // Akkadian kispum (ancestor-feeding to sustain the dead) // Akan kradin (soul-name system where name sustains person across dissolution) // Australian songlines (walking ancestral paths)

**The convergence:** In all four traditions, the dead are not "left behind" but actively maintained, approached, and sustained by the living. The Aymara contribution is the most radical spatialization: the ancestors are literally in front of you. You walk toward them. The Akkadian contribution is the most explicitly reciprocal: the dead *need* feeding. The Akan contribution is the most linguistically precise: the name IS the person. The Australian contribution is the most geographically extensive: the ancestors ARE the landscape you walk through.

**YOUSPEAK implication:** *Nayrame* would enter a cluster with *kispume* (if forged) — both naming the condition of the dead as actively present and approached.

### 3. Complementary Opposition (Not Dualism)

**Traditions converging:** Aymara tinku/yanantin // Chinese yin-yang // Zoroastrian spenta/angra (the original "two spirits" — but note: Zoroastrian trajectory trends toward ethical dualism, unlike the Andean) // Akan Nyame/Asase Yaa (sky/earth complementarity)

**The convergence:** Multiple traditions encode the principle that reality requires the balanced tension of opposed forces, and that eliminating one pole would destroy the whole. The Aymara contribution is the most structurally precise, with the yanantin/masintin distinction (mirror-opposition vs. parallel-similarity) adding a layer of analysis absent from other traditions. Chinese yin-yang comes closest but lacks the explicit distinction between two modes of pairing.

**YOUSPEAK implication:** *Tinkuqing* fills a gap — the felt quality of necessary opposition. No existing compound in the cathedral names this specific emotional-cosmological register.

### 4. Cosmic Reversal / World-Turning

**Traditions converging:** Aymara pachakuti // Hebrew *tikkun olam* (repair of the world) // Australian Dreaming (the foundational events that shaped the world could, in some narratives, be re-enacted to renew it) // Zoroastrian frashegird (universal restoration)

**The convergence:** All four traditions contain a concept of cosmic renewal or reversal — a moment when the damaged/inverted order is set right. The Aymara contribution is distinctive in two ways: (1) pachakuti is *cyclical*, not eschatological — it has happened before and will happen again, whereas tikkun olam and frashegird are oriented toward a final resolution; (2) pachakuti is *politically active* — it is invoked by living movements to describe and enact present transformation, not merely awaited.

**YOUSPEAK implication:** *Kutiqing* (if forged) would complement *frashaqing* (Avestan, if forged) — both naming the experience of cosmic restoration but with different temporal orientations: kutiqing cyclical, frashaqing eschatological.

### 5. Libation-as-Navigation-as-Memory

**Traditions converging:** Aymara ch'alla / t'aki (libation pathways as cosmological navigation) // Australian songlines (song as navigation and law) // Akkadian kispum (ancestor-feeding as community maintenance) // Akan nsaguo (libation as cosmological communication)

**The convergence:** In all four traditions, a ritual act of offering (liquid, song, food) simultaneously traces a path through sacred geography, maintains the cosmos, and sustains the relationship between living and dead. The Aymara t'aki is the most explicitly navigational: the libation *follows a route*. The Australian songline is the most spatially extensive. The Akkadian kispum is the most socially structured (integrated into the royal cult). The Akan nsaguo is the most verbally elaborate (the libation prayer is a theological performance).

**YOUSPEAK implication:** This cluster suggests a potential grammar-level observation: sacred offering-as-path-tracing may be a near-universal structure. Worth a grammars/ essay.

### 6. Evidentiality as Epistemic-Theological Grammar

**Traditions converging:** Aymara evidentiality (=wa, -tay, siwa — obligatory marking of knowledge-source) // Ojibwe grammatical animacy (obligatory marking of personhood-status) // Akan grammatical monotheism (no admissible plural of Onyame)

**The convergence:** In all three traditions, the deepest theological commitments are encoded not in vocabulary but in *grammar* — in obligatory morphological structures that force every speaker to take a position on cosmological questions (What have I witnessed? Is this entity a person? Can God be plural?). Aymara evidentiality is the most epistemologically precise: every sentence is an epistemic commitment. Ojibwe animacy is the most ontologically radical: every noun declares a personhood judgment. Akan monotheism is the most theologically direct.

**YOUSPEAK implication:** This cluster belongs in a grammars/ essay on "theology-in-grammar" — how languages encode cosmological commitments below the level of conscious articulation. Aymara evidentiality adds the epistemic dimension to the ontological (Ojibwe) and theological (Akan) dimensions already documented.

## OPEN QUESTIONS AND UNCERTAINTIES

1. **The three-pacha colonial critique** remains unresolved. The compound terms may be colonial coinages describing pre-colonial concepts — or the concepts themselves may have been more fluid and less systematized before missionary intervention. More primary-source work in Bertonio and in the Huarochirí manuscript is needed.

2. **Puquina etymology** of *pacha* is probable but not certain. Puquina documentation is extremely limited (the language is extinct), and reconstructions rely on Kallawaya survivals and comparative inference.

3. **The relationship between Aymara and Quechua pacha-concepts** needs careful untangling. The two languages share the term and much of the conceptual structure, but the Aymara temporal reversal (nayra/qhipa) is not fully paralleled in Quechua, suggesting the languages may have developed different philosophical implications from the shared root.

4. **Sallqa/uywa as Aymara vs. Quechua terminology.** The bulk of the ethnographic evidence on sallqa/uywa comes from Quechua-speaking communities (Flores Ochoa, Allen). The degree to which this specific binary maps onto Aymara cosmology needs verification from Aymara-specific ethnographies.

5. **Living-tradition sensitivity.** Aymara cosmological concepts are not academic abstractions — they are active frameworks for political mobilization, agricultural practice, and spiritual life. Any YOUSPEAK use must respect this vitality and avoid reducing these concepts to "raw material" for compound-forging.

6. **Generational erosion of nayra/qhipa.** Núñez and Sweetser's 2006 data showed younger speakers losing the backward-future pattern. Twenty years later, the erosion may have progressed further. Updated gestural studies would be valuable.
