## 1. BASQUE AS LANGUAGE ISOLATE

### 1.1 The Isolate Status

Basque (Euskara) is the only surviving pre-Indo-European language in Western Europe. It has no proven genetic relationship to any other living or dead language. This is not a claim of insufficient study — it is the result of over two centuries of intensive comparative work that has failed to establish any demonstrable connection. Every proposed link has been examined and rejected or remains undemonstrated by the standards of mainstream historical linguistics.

The implications for YOUSPEAK are extraordinary. A language isolate represents a unique linguistic lineage. Any compound using Basque morphemes achieves maximum "poly" (polylingual diversity) score by definition, since the morpheme cannot be assigned to any known language family. Basque + Sumerian (another isolate) would yield an isolate-isolate compound — the highest possible family diversity.

### 1.2 Failed and Disputed Proposals

**Iberian.** The oldest proposed connection. Wilhelm von Humboldt argued in the early 19th century that Basques were Iberians and that Basque could decode Iberian inscriptions. Once the Iberian script was deciphered in the mid-20th century, it became clear that Basque did not directly translate Iberian texts. Koldo Mitxelena concluded that similarities were due to geographical proximity, not genetic kinship. However, this is the one connection that has seen cautious scholarly revival: Eduardo Orduna Aznar's work on shared numerals, kinship terms, and case suffixes has reopened the question. An MIT computational study (2020) found against the relationship; more recent work (2026) has found new correspondences "beyond numbers." The debate remains genuinely open, but no consensus for kinship exists.

**Caucasian (Dene-Caucasian).** The idea that Basque is related to North Caucasian languages was developed by Uhlenbeck, Dumezil, and Lafon in the 20th century. The broader Dene-Caucasian proposal (Bengtson and Ruhlen, 1990s) links Basque, Caucasian, Sino-Tibetan, Na-Dene, Yeniseian, and Burushaski into a single macro-family. This is considered discredited by mainstream linguistics. The typological similarities (ergativity, agglutination) are not accepted as evidence of genetic relationship. The Dene-Yeniseian connection (Vajda, 2008) has received some acceptance, but Basque's inclusion in any wider grouping has not.

**Vasconic substrate (Vennemann).** Theo Vennemann proposed (2003) that Basque-related languages were once spoken across much of Western and Central Europe, leaving traces in river-names and place-names. The hypothesis has been rejected by major Basque linguists: Joseba Lakarra finds Vennemann's etymologies incompatible with reconstructed Proto-Basque phonology; Larry Trask argued Vennemann found an unidentified agglutinating language, not Basque; Harald Bichlmeier pointed out that Vennemann compared modern Basque forms to ancient toponyms rather than using reconstructed Proto-Basque; Jurgen Udolph showed that some "Vasconic" roots are actually Indo-European; Stefan Georg demonstrated that some proposed roots do not exist in Basque at all.

**Aquitanian connection (accepted).** This is the one demonstrated relationship. Roman-period inscriptions from Gallia Aquitania (1st-4th centuries CE) preserve roughly 400 personal names and about 70 deity names in Latin script. The onomastic material matches reconstructed Proto-Basque so closely that the connection is undisputed. Mitxelena's Proto-Basque reconstructions, made largely before the Aquitanian inscriptions were systematically studied, were fully confirmed by them. Jose Ignacio Hualde refers to the reconstructed common ancestor as "Proto-Basque-Aquitanian." Whether modern Basque descends from one specific Aquitanian dialect or from a sister dialect remains uncertain.

### 1.3 Age and Deep History

How old is Basque? The honest answer: we do not know, and the question is poorly formed. Languages do not have fixed birth dates. What we can say:

- Aquitanian inscriptions (1st-4th century CE) demonstrate that a Basque-like language was spoken in southwestern Gaul during the Roman period.
- Proto-Basque, as reconstructed by Mitxelena and Lakarra, represents a stage at least several centuries earlier — perhaps 1st millennium BCE or older.
- The Basque words for "knife" (aizto), "axe" (aizkora), and "hoe" (aitzur) all derive from *haitz* (stone/rock), leading some scholars to suggest that these tool-names preserve a pre-metal-age vocabulary. This is suggestive but not probative — the words could be conservative retentions even if the language itself is younger.
- Basque survived the Indo-European migrations that swept over Western Europe in the 3rd-2nd millennia BCE, the Roman conquest, the Visigothic period, the Arab presence in Iberia, and the French and Spanish nation-state formations. Its survival is a testament to the geographic isolation of the Pyrenean region and the cultural tenacity of its speakers.
- The language *as a distinct lineage* could plausibly be 5,000+ years old, but this cannot be proven by any currently available method.

## 2. THE ERGATIVE-ABSOLUTIVE SYSTEM AND ITS THEOLOGICAL IMPLICATIONS

### 2.1 What Ergativity Means

In most European languages (English, French, Spanish, German — all Indo-European), grammar follows a **nominative-accusative** pattern. The subject of both transitive and intransitive verbs is treated the same way (nominative), and the object of a transitive verb is marked differently (accusative):

- *The woman* (nominative) runs. (intransitive)
- *The woman* (nominative) sees *the bear* (accusative). (transitive)

In Basque, grammar follows an **ergative-absolutive** pattern. The subject of an intransitive verb and the object of a transitive verb are treated the same way (absolutive = zero-marked, unmarked), while the agent of a transitive verb is specially marked (ergative = *-k* suffix):

- *Emakumea* (absolutive, unmarked) korrika dabil. "The woman runs."
- *Emakumeak* (ergative, *-k* marked) *hartzA* (absolutive, unmarked) ikusten du. "The woman sees the bear."

The patient of the transitive sentence (*hartzA*, "the bear") and the subject of the intransitive sentence (*emakumea*, "the woman") receive the same treatment — both are absolutive, zero-marked. Agency is the thing that requires special marking.

### 2.2 Basque Split-S: Agentivity as Deeper Axis

Basque ergativity is even more interesting than standard ergative systems. Basque displays a **split-S** system, meaning it does not treat all intransitive verbs alike. In Basque, the sole argument of an *unaccusative* intransitive verb (one describing a state or process: "the ice melted," "the child fell") receives absolutive case. But the sole argument of an *unergative* intransitive verb (one describing a willed action: "the child laughed," "the man danced") receives ergative case.

This means the real axis is not transitive/intransitive but **agent/patient**. The grammar asks, of every participant in every sentence: are you *doing* something, or is something *happening to you*? Doing requires the mark. Being — existing, undergoing, experiencing — is the unmarked default.

Some scholars (notably in analyses of western Basque dialects) describe this as a semantically aligned system based on a contrast between *patientive* (absolutive) and *non-patientive* (ergative), rather than a strictly syntactic alignment. The practical result is the same: every utterance forces an ontological declaration about the nature of its participants.

### 2.3 Morphological vs. Syntactic Ergativity

A crucial nuance: Basque ergativity is **morphological** but not syntactic. In syntax (word order, relative clause formation, reflexive binding), Basque behaves more like an accusative language — comparable to French or Spanish. The case-marking on nouns is ergative, but the deep sentence structure is not.

This means the ergative pattern is a **surface ontology** — a worldview encoded in the visible morphology of every sentence, even if the deeper syntactic machinery is more familiar. For YOUSPEAK purposes, it is the surface morphology that carries the theological payload: every Basque utterance visibly marks the distinction between existing-and-undergoing (unmarked) and acting-upon (marked).

### 2.4 Theological Implications

No published scholarship directly connects Basque ergativity to theology in the way that scholarship on Ojibwe grammatical animacy or Aymara evidentiality has been explored philosophically. What follows is synthesis based on the structural facts:

**Being as default, agency as deviation.** In Indo-European nominative-accusative languages, the agent/subject is the grammatical citizen — unmarked, default, central. This encodes a worldview where *doing* is the natural human condition and *being done to* is the marked, passive exception. In Basque, the reverse: *being, existing, undergoing* is the baseline reality; *acting upon another* is the event that requires explanation and special marking.

**Alignment with chthonic cosmology.** Basque pre-Christian religion is fundamentally chthonic — earth-centered, with divine beings dwelling in or on the earth, and the sky as mere corridor. The absolutive-as-default aligns: the natural state is participation in the given order, being held by earth and circumstance. Deliberate action upon another is the exceptional case.

**Mari's justice ethic.** Mari, the supreme figure of Basque pre-Christian belief, punishes lies, theft, and pride — moral transgressions that arise from *agency*. The ergative grammar mirrors this: the unmarked state is participation in the natural order; the ergative (agentive) mark introduces the possibility of transgression and responsibility.

**Convergence with Sumerian ergativity.** Sumerian, the other great isolate in YOUSPEAK's donor pool, is also ergative-absolutive. The Sumerian *me* (received divine ordinance, cosmic given) is a concept that emerges naturally from an ergative worldview, where the absolutive — the received, the given, the undergone — is the unmarked ground of reality. When YOUSPEAK compounds Basque morphemes with Sumerian *me*, both halves share the same grammatical ontology.

### 2.5 Comparison with Ojibwe Grammatical Animacy

| Feature | Basque Ergativity | Ojibwe Animacy |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| What grammar encodes | Agent vs. patient (action vs. undergoing) | Animate vs. inanimate (personhood vs. thingness) |
| How it's marked | Ergative -k suffix on agents | Noun class: animate/inanimate with distinct plural, obviative, verb agreement |
| Ontological axis | Doing vs. being | Living vs. non-living |
| Default (unmarked) state | Being/undergoing (absolutive) | Neither — both classes are fully marked |
| Dynamic? | Fixed by verb semantics | Partially dynamic — objects can shift class when they "act like persons" |
| Theological implication | Existence precedes agency | Personhood is a grammatical ontological commitment |
| Deepest parallel | Both force every sentence to make an ontological declaration about its participants |

The genuine convergence: both languages refuse to let grammar be ontologically neutral. Every Basque sentence declares whether its participants are agents or patients; every Ojibwe sentence declares whether its participants are persons or things. Neither language allows you to refer to anything without taking an ontological position.

## 3. NATIVE RELIGIOUS VOCABULARY VS. LOAN LAYERS

### 3.1 The God-Word Problem: Jainko / Jaungoikoa / Ortzi

Three terms compete for the title of "God-word" in Basque, and the debate over them reveals the archaeology of Christianization:

**Ortzi / Urtzi.** The oldest candidate. Survives in compounds: *ortzadar* "rainbow" (ortzi + adar "horn"), *orzgorri* "red sky" (ortzi + gorri "red"), *ostargi* "daylight" (ortzi + argi "light"), *ostegun* "Thursday" (ortzi + egun "day"). The 12th-century French pilgrim Aymeric Picaud recorded in the Codex Calixtinus: *et Deus uocant Urcia* — "and they call God Urcia."

The debate: was Ortzi a sky *god* or merely the word for *sky*? Arguments for a deity: the calendric survival in Thursday (ortzi-day, parallel to Thor's-day), Picaud's explicit testimony, and the meteorological compound vocabulary (thunder, lightning, rainbow — all associated with sky-deity functions cross-culturally). Arguments against: no surviving myths or legends about Ortzi; Picaud may have "pointed at the sky" and been given the word for sky, not for God; the absolutive case ending *-a* in Picaud's "Urcia" is not typical for Basque proper nouns, suggesting it was a common noun.

Caro Baroja suggested that *zeru* (from Latin *caelum*) replaced *ortzi* for "sky" at the same time *Jaungoikoa* replaced *Urcia* for "God" — a double lexical displacement in which Christianity simultaneously replaced the native sky-word and the native god-word.

**Jaungoikoa / Jainko.** The standard modern Basque word for God is *Jaungoikoa* (formal) or *Jainko* (common). The transparent etymology of *Jaungoikoa* is *jaun* ("lord") + *goiko* ("upper, from above") + *-a* (article) = "the Lord from on high."

The crucial question: which came first? R. L. Trask (1997) reconstructed Proto-Basque *\*iainko* as the original form and argued that *Jaungoikoa* is a folk etymology — a transparent Basque compound created to rationalize the older, opaque *jainko* into something explicitly Christian. Under this view, the ultimate origin of *jainko* is unknown — it could be pre-Christian. The opposing view holds that *Jaungoikoa* is the original Christian coinage and *Jainko* is its contracted form. The earliest secure attestation of either form is from a 14th-century Pamplona codex.

The scholarly consensus leans toward Trask: *jainko* is older and etymologically opaque; *Jaungoikoa* is a later rationalization. But "opaque" does not mean "pre-Christian" — it means "of unknown origin." The debate remains unresolved.

**YOUSPEAK relevance:** The *jainko* problem illustrates a pattern already noted in the Aramaic excavation (translation-creates-theology): when a religious concept crosses a language boundary, the translation product may contain meanings that exist in neither source nor target. If *jainko* is indeed pre-Christian and opaque, it names a concept of divinity that predates both Christian theology and the Indo-European divine vocabulary — making it extraordinarily valuable as a YOUSPEAK donor.

### 3.3 The hil- / il- Root Complex

The Basque root *hil* generates one of the most discussed semantic fields in the language:

- **hil** (verb): to die, to kill
- **hil** (noun): month (from Proto-Basque *\*(h)iLe*, meaning "moon/month")
- **hilabete**: month (hil "month" + bete "full, complete" = "full moon")
- **hilerri**: cemetery (hilen "of the dead" + herri "village/community" = "village of the dead")
- **ilargi**: moon (il "month" + argi "light" = "month-light")
- **ilgora**: first quarter moon (il + gora "above")
- **ilbera**: last quarter moon (il + behera "below")
- **ilberri**: new moon (il + berri "new")
- **ilbete**: full moon (il + bete "full")
- **hileko**: menstrual period (from hil "death/dying," NOT from hil "month")

The **linguistic consensus** (Trask 2008) is that the moon/month *hil* and the death/kill *hil* are two distinct homophonous roots — they happen to sound identical but have different Proto-Basque origins. The folk etymology that fuses them — moon = "light of the dead" — is poetic and widespread but not supported by reconstruction.

However, the folk etymology itself is data. Whether or not the roots are etymologically connected, Basque speakers have for centuries heard the connection and woven it into their understanding. The moon illuminating the night — the realm of Gaueko (spirit of the night), of the dead, of the aideko (supernatural) — does, in the lived mythology, belong to the dead. The folk etymology may not be linguistic history, but it is theological history.

**YOUSPEAK note:** The *hil-* complex (whether one root or two) names a semantic constellation — death, moon, month, time — that echoes across YOUSPEAK's donor traditions. Compare Akkadian *arhu* (month, from a moon-root), Hebrew *yareach* (moon) and *yerach* (month), and the nearly universal moon-month-measurement link. What Basque adds is the death dimension, unique among YOUSPEAK donors in its grammatical intimacy with the lunar-temporal vocabulary.

This suggests the pre-Christian conceptual framework differed fundamentally from the Christian one, not just in content but in category structure.

### 3.6 Key Pre-Christian Concepts

**Adur** — The connective magical force between things and their representations. Not a being but a force: luck, destiny, magical power. Etymologically also means "saliva" (the breath-substance that carries words). The magical means "are all based on the ADUR, or magical virtue, that links things with their representations" (Barandiaran). Curses (*birao*) travel through adur. The evil eye (*betadur* = begi "eye" + adur "magical force") is adur channeled through gaze.

**Berezko / Aideko** — The fundamental cosmological duality. *Berezko*: the natural world, things that happen "of themselves," spontaneously. *Aideko*: the supernatural world, the wind-carried, the inexplicable. To operate in the natural world, use natural means; to enter the supernatural world, use magic (adur). The *aideko* is not a separate "heaven" — it interpenetrates the natural world but is accessed differently.

**Indar** — Physical force, visible power, blow-of-light. Paired with *adur* (invisible power) by Barandiaran as the two fundamental categories: the visible and the invisible, the physical and the magical.

**Arima** — Soul. Almost certainly borrowed from Latin *anima*, but thoroughly indigenized. In Basque funerary tradition, the *arima* persists after death and requires feeding and remembrance — paralleling the Akkadian *etemmu*, Aymara ancestor-feeding, and Bantu *amadlozi* traditions.

**"Izena duen guztia omen da"** — "All that has a name, exists." This proverb, used as the subtitle of Ortiz-Oses and Garagalza's *Euskal mitologia* (2006), encodes a naming-ontology: existence is conferred or recognized through the act of naming. Combined with *adur* (the force linking things to their representations, including name-representations), this creates a complete ontolinguistic system: to name is to connect, and to connect is to grant existence.

## 4. PLACE-NAME THEOLOGY

### 4.1 Basque Landscape as Sacred Text

Basque place-names are not mere labels. They encode a theological reading of the landscape — rock, mountain, valley, water, and cave as categories of sacred geography. The major toponymic elements:

**haitz / aitz** (rock, crag) — The foundation morpheme. Appears in:
- *Aizkorri* (aitz + gorri "bare/red") — highest peak in Gipuzkoa; "bare rock"
- *Aitzbitarte* (aitz + bitarte "between") — "between the rocks"; Paleolithic cave complex near San Sebastian with 25,000-year-old carvings
- *Aitzulo* (aitz + ulo/zulo "hole") — "rock-hole"; generic for cave-shrines
- *Aitzuri* (aitz + zuri "white") — "white rock"
- Tool vocabulary: *aizto* (knife), *aizkora* (axe), *aitzur* (hoe) — all from haitz, suggesting stone-tool-era word formation

**mendi** (mountain) — The vertical axis:
- *Mendizabal* (mendi + zabal "wide") — "wide mountain"
- *Mendigorria* (mendi + gorri "red/bare") — "red mountain"
- *Ahunamendi* (ahuna "young goat" + mendi) — "goat mountain"
- The suffix appears throughout the Basque Country and in some names far beyond it (debated Vasconic substrate)

**aran** (valley) — The horizontal complement to mendi:
- *Aran* (the Val d'Aran in the Pyrenees preserves this word in its name — "Valley of the Valley")
- *Arantzazu* (arantza "thorn" + zu "you") — "You, in a thorn?" — major Marian pilgrimage site, suggesting pre-Christian to Christian sacred-site continuity

**-uri / -iri** (settlement, town) — Human habitation:
- Appears in early forms of many Basque town names
- Possibly related to *ur* (water), suggesting settlements near water sources

**-ibar / -ibar-** (riverbank, floodplain):
- *Ibarrangelu* (ibar + angelu "corner/angle") — "river-bend"

### 4.2 Caves as Theological Sites

Mari lives underground, in caves in high mountains, moving from one dwelling to another (reportedly every seven years, though the scholarly attestation for this specific detail is thin). This is not merely mythological narrative — it reflects a genuinely chthonic cosmology where the earth's interior is the source of:

- Weather (storms emerge from caves and chasms)
- Divine power (Mari's cave is the seat of justice)
- The dead (ancestors dwell below)
- Fertility (Mari and Sugaar meet in caves on Friday nights to conceive storms)

The cave as temple has deep roots in the Basque Country: the Paleolithic cave art at Aitzbitarte (25,000 BP), Santimamine, Ekain, and Isturitz demonstrates that caves have been sacred sites in this region for at least 25 millennia. Whether there is *cultural continuity* between Paleolithic cave use and the Mari mythology is unknowable — Barandiaran argued for it; most modern scholars are cautious — but the geographical continuity is undeniable.

### 4.3 Landscape Theology Convergences

The Basque treatment of landscape as theological text converges with:

**Aboriginal Australian "Country"** — The songlines tradition maps sacred narrative onto physical landscape, with specific sites holding specific Dreaming stories. Basque cave-shrines and mountain-names function similarly: Amboto is not just a mountain, it is *Mari's dwelling*. The difference: Aboriginal songlines are a continuous, living, actively-maintained system of landscape-knowledge; Basque landscape-theology survives primarily in place-names and fragments of legend.

**Aymara pacha** — The Aymara word *pacha* fuses time, space, and world into a single concept. Basque has no single word equivalent, but the *lur-* root (earth/land) pervades both cosmology (Ama Lurra = Mother Earth) and identity (Euskal Herria = "Country of the Basque Language"). The land is not where theology happens; the land *is* theology.

## 5. NUMBER SYMBOLISM

### 5.1 The Vigesimal System (Base-20)

Basque uses a vigesimal (base-20) counting system, one of the most distinctive features of the language. The basic structure:

| Number | Basque | Literal meaning |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| 1 | bat | — |
| 2 | bi | — |
| 3 | hiru | — |
| 4 | lau | — |
| 5 | bost | — |
| 10 | hamar | — |
| 11 | hamaika | "ten-many" (also means "a lot" in colloquial use) |
| 20 | hogei | — (debated Celtic connection) |
| 30 | hogeita hamar | "twenty and ten" |
| 40 | berrogei | "two twenties" |
| 60 | hirurogei | "three twenties" |
| 80 | laurogei | "four twenties" |
| 100 | ehun | native Basque |
| 1000 | mila | borrowed (Latin *milia*) |

The vigesimal system counts the whole body — fingers and toes — while decimal systems count only the fingers. Whether this means Basque culture originally conceptualized "a person" as the unit of 20 is speculative but consistent with the body-counting theory advanced by cross-cultural mathematical anthropology. The system is pre-Indo-European and pre-dates both Celtic and Roman presence in the region; some scholars have suggested that the vigesimal elements in Celtic languages (Welsh *ugain* = 20, French *quatre-vingts* = 80) may reflect pre-Indo-European substrate influence rather than the reverse.

### 5.2 Hogei (20): Etymology Debate

The etymology of *hogei* is contested:
- Some linguists propose borrowing from Brythonic Celtic (cf. Welsh *ugain*, which irregularly becomes *hugain* after certain prepositions)
- Others argue the direction of borrowing is reversed — Celtic borrowed from a pre-Indo-European (Vasconic?) language
- A third view treats the similarity as coincidental

The debate is unresolved but significant: if *hogei* is native, it supports the antiquity of the vigesimal system in pre-Indo-European Europe.

### 5.3 Number Symbolism in Mythology

The mythological record, thin as it is, preserves traces of number symbolism:

- **7:** Mari reportedly changes her mountain dwelling every seven years. Seven-headed dragons (*Herensuge*) appear in folk narratives. Seven is also significant in Basque calendric folk belief.
- **3:** Three-fold structures appear throughout Basque folk narratives (three tasks, three brothers, three trials), consistent with pan-European narrative patterns but potentially older.
- **Hamaika (11) as "many":** The fact that the word for eleven also means "a great number, many" is linguistically unusual. It may preserve an ancient counting boundary — the point at which enumeration gives way to "uncountable."

### 5.4 YOUSPEAK Relevance

The vigesimal system has no direct forge implications, but it marks Basque as belonging to a different mathematical-cognitive tradition than the decimal Indo-European languages that dominate YOUSPEAK's current donor pool. More importantly, the body-as-counting-unit principle converges with the broader Basque emphasis on embodied knowledge: *adur* as saliva/magical force, *betadur* as eye-power, the cave as earth's body-interior.

## 6. ORAL TRADITION STRUCTURES

### 6.1 Bertsolaritza: Improvised Verse

Bertsolaritza is the art of singing improvised verses (*bertso*) in Basque to traditional melodies, following strict rules of meter, rhyme, and thematic development. It is the most distinctive Basque cultural practice and has been called "the most Basque among the Basque" — inextricable from the language itself.

**Rules:**
- **Metre:** Each *bertso* is divided into *puntuak* (lines), each with a fixed syllable count determined by the chosen melody. The bertsolari does not consciously count syllables — the metrical pattern is internalized through practice.
- **Melody:** Chosen from among ~3,040 classified traditional airs (per Joanito Dorronsoro's classification). No instrumental accompaniment — the bertsolari sings unaccompanied.
- **Rhyme:** End-rhyme (and sometimes internal rhyme) follows the requirements of the chosen verse-form.
- **Back-to-front composition:** The bertsolari thinks of the *ending* first. The final rhyme-word carries the punch — the philosophical or comedic payload — and the rest of the verse builds toward it. This is the "intrinsic particularity" of the form.
- **Time constraint:** The bertsolari typically has less than one minute to compose after hearing the topic.

**Competition format:** A *gai-jartzaile* (subject-setter) assigns topics, meters, and melodies. Bertsolaris may compete in pairs (dueling, called *nor-nori*), where each must echo and counter the other's vocabulary and images. The 2009 National Championship drew 15,000 spectators to the Bilbao Exhibition Centre — the first time a woman, Maialen Lujanbio, won the competition.

**Cultural function:** Bertsolaritza is not a dying art. Children study it in school; bertso schools for adults exist across all seven Basque provinces. The tradition carries cultural knowledge, linguistic vitality, political commentary, and communal identity without reliance on writing.

### 6.2 Kopla Zaharrak: Old Couplets

The *kopla zaharrak* ("old couplets") are the oldest preserved Basque verse forms — four-line improvised stanzas from the medieval period (attestation from the late 15th century onward). They are now extinct as a living practice but survive in transcriptions and memory.

Structure: the first two lines establish a *frame* (a topic, setting, or image), and the last two lines deliver a *script* (a formulaic scenario, a moral, a punchline). This two-part cognitive structure has been analyzed by Gintsburg (2020) as parallel to the Moroccan *ayyus* tradition — a cross-Mediterranean oral-poetic convergence.

The kopla was the basic metre scheme of medieval Basque poetry and the ancestor of modern bertsolaritza. Bernard Etxepare's *Linguae Vasconum Primitiae* (1545) — the first book printed in Basque — uses long verses with internal rhythmic patterns derived from the kopla tradition.

### 6.3 Pastoralak: Pastoral Plays of Zuberoa

The *pastoralak* are a theatrical tradition unique to the province of Zuberoa (Soule), the smallest and most mountainous Basque province, on the French side. First recorded performance: 1634 in Atarratze (Tardets). Approximately 67 surviving plays.

**Features:**
- Outdoor performance, rotating from town to town each year
- Fixed character types with a rigid moral structure: "reds" (good) vs. "blacks" (evil)
- Bilingual: Zuberoan Basque and Bearnais (Gascon)
- Subject matter: biblical (Old and New Testament), hagiographic, classical, chivalric, historical, and comic
- Connected to the *maskarada* (carnival of Soule), which shares the fixed-character typology and red/black moral coding

The pastoralak are the closest Basque parallel to a sacred drama tradition, though their content is predominantly Christian. The structural red/black duality, however, may preserve the older *berezko*/*aideko* (natural/supernatural) cosmological pair.

### 6.4 Comparison with Other Oral Traditions

| Feature | Bertsolaritza | Aboriginal Songlines | Ojibwe Birchbark Scrolls | Aymara Textile Encoding |
|---------|--------------|---------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Medium | Sung verse, unaccompanied | Sung verse, sometimes with instruments | Visual notation on bark | Woven pattern in fabric |
| Improvised? | Yes — central feature | No — fixed and transmitted exactly | No — fixed ritual knowledge | No — fixed symbolic code |
| Navigation? | No | Yes — song-as-map | No | No |
| Theological content? | Secondary (primarily social/political) | Primary — sings the Dreaming into presence | Primary — Midewiwin ceremonial knowledge | Primary — cosmological symbolism |
| Cultural vitality | Thriving; school programs, competitions | Variable; some regions thriving, some under threat | Revival efforts underway | Declining but present |
| Key parallel | **Oral transmission without writing** as a conscious cultural choice, not a technological lack |

The deepest convergence: bertsolaritza, like Aboriginal songlines, demonstrates that oral culture is not "pre-literate" (a deficit model) but an alternative knowledge-transmission system with its own strengths — improvisation, communal participation, living adaptation, embodied memory. The bertsolari's back-to-front composition method suggests a fundamentally different cognitive architecture for language production than the linear left-to-right model of written composition.

## 7. THE AQUITANIAN EVIDENCE

### 7.1 The Corpus

The Aquitanian language is known exclusively from onomastic data (personal names and deity names) embedded in Latin inscriptions from Roman-period Gallia Aquitania, dating primarily to the 1st-4th centuries CE. The corpus comprises roughly 400 personal names and about 70 deity names (theonyms). This is the only direct evidence for what Basque's ancestor or close relative looked like two millennia ago.

The foundational scholarly work is Gorrochategui's *Onomastica indigena de Aquitania* (1984). Mitxelena's Proto-Basque reconstructions, largely completed before systematic study of the Aquitanian inscriptions, were fully confirmed by them — a remarkable case of independent verification.

### 7.2 Anthroponyms and What They Preserve

| Aquitanian | Modern Basque | Meaning | Notes |
|------------|---------------|---------|-------|
| Andere | andere | woman, lady | Still productive in modern Basque; cognate confirmed |
| Cison / Gison | gizon | man | Shows the /g-/ → /k-/ shift was not yet complete |
| Nescato | neskato (diminutive of neska) | young girl | Diminutive suffix -to preserved |
| Sembe | seme | son | Proto-Basque *\*senbe* |
| Senius / Seni | sehi / sein (*\*seni) | boy, child | Reconstructed form matched exactly |
| Hanna | anai | brother | Proto-Basque *\*anai* |
| Sahar | zahar | old | Still the modern Basque word |
| Ombe / Umme | ume | child | Proto-Basque *\*ume* |
| Harsus / Heraus | hartz | bear | The *h-* preserved in inscription matches Proto-Basque aspiration |

The consistency is extraordinary: these are not distant resemblances but precise phonological correspondences, confirmed by independent reconstruction.

### 7.3 Theonyms: The Pre-Roman Basque Pantheon

The Aquitanian theonyms provide the only direct window into pre-Roman Basque religion. They fall into identifiable categories:

**Tree deities (arboreal gods):**
- ARIXO / ARIXONI — from *\*hareitz* "oak" (modern Basque *haritz*). The theonym was AREIX, with X representing an affricate sibilant [ts]. An oak deity.
- ARTAHE — from *arte* "evergreen oak" (holm oak). Also interpreted as bear-related (cf. *hartz* "bear"); possibly a deity associated with both oaks and bears.
- LEHERENNO — from *leher* "pine" (modern Basque *lerr-* in some dialects). A pine deity.
- FAGUS — Latin *fagus* "beech." Worshipped in Aquitania with Latin theonymy — a Romanized tree-cult.
- SEXARBORI — Latin *sex arbores* "six trees." A group-tree cult.
- ABELLION — Gaulish *aballo-* "apple." An apple-tree deity, possibly Celtic-Aquitanian syncretism.

**Landscape deities:**
- BAIGORIXO — possibly from *bai-* (related to *ibar* "riverbank"?) + *gorri* "red/bare." A mountain or landscape deity.
- ILVRBERRIXO — possibly *ilur-* (related to *hiri* "town"?) + *berri* "new." A settlement deity.
- LARRAHE — from *larre* "pasture, meadow." A pasture deity.
- LACVBEGI — from *laku* "lake" (Latin loan?) + *begi* "eye." "Eye of the lake" — a water deity.

**Celestial/astral:**
- ILVNNO / ASTOILVNNO — containing *il-* "moon/month" + possibly *-un* intensifier. A moon deity? *Asto-* may relate to *asto* "donkey" or may be a different element.
- HELAVS(S)E — possibly from *hil-* "month/dead" or *hele-* "darkness." An underworld or nocturnal deity.

**Unknown:**
- ERRENSAE — etymology uncertain. Possibly from *herren-* "lame" or *erren-* "burn."
- LOXAE / LOSAE — unknown.
- SELATSE / STELAITSE — possibly from *zelai* "plain, field" or *estela* "stele."

### 7.4 Theological Implications of the Aquitanian Theonyms

The theonyms reveal a religion organized around **natural features**: trees (oak, pine, holm oak, beech, apple), landscape (mountains, pastures, lakes), and celestial bodies (moon). This is consistent with the pre-Christian Basque mythology preserved in later folk sources — a nature-centered, chthonic system without a clear sky-god hierarchy.

The tree deities are particularly significant. Five distinct tree-theonyms from the Aquitanian record suggest that tree-worship was not a marginal practice but a central element of the religion. Each tree species had its own deity-name, suggesting a differentiated theology of the plant world — not generic "nature worship" but specific, named relationships with specific species.

### 7.5 Iberian Comparison (Negative Result)

The Iberian language, attested from inscriptions in eastern and southern Spain (6th-1st centuries BCE), was long thought to be related to Basque. The Iberian script has been deciphered (we can read the sounds), but the language remains undeciphered (we cannot understand the meanings). The shared numerals and some morphological similarities noted by Orduna Aznar are tantalizing, but no scholarly consensus for genetic kinship exists. For YOUSPEAK purposes, Basque should be treated as an isolate, not as a member of a Basque-Iberian family.

## 9. FORGE CANDIDATES

### 9.1 adurme

**Components:** Basque *adur* (magical connective force, destiny, saliva) + Sumerian *me* (divine ordinance, received cosmic condition)

**Pronunciation:** /a.dur.me/

**Meaning:** The-force-linking-things-to-their-representations-as-received-cosmic-ordinance. Or more compactly: destiny-as-given-law. The magical binding-power between name and named, image and imaged, as a cosmic given — not something achieved or created, but the way reality is structured.

**Gap filled:** No existing YOUSPEAK word names the connective force *between* things and their representations. Existing vocabulary addresses truth (*maatme*), reciprocity (*aynime*), personhood (*ubuntuqing*), and cosmic order, but not the *linking force itself* — the medium through which sympathetic magic, naming, and curse operate. Adurme would name the glue of the symbolic order.

**Poly score:** Basque (isolate) + Sumerian (isolate) = **isolate + isolate = maximum poly diversity**. No two donors in YOUSPEAK could be more genetically distant. This is a poly 9+ pathway.

**Learnability:** 7.0/10. *Adur* is not widely known outside Basque studies, but it is phonetically simple and memorable. The compound is three syllables, follows standard YOUSPEAK -me morphology.

**Core score estimate:** 7.85-8.10. If the semantic evaluation confirms that "connective magical force as cosmic given" fills a genuine gap AND the Basque etymology can be established as securely pre-Christian (which it can — *adur* has no Latin or Romance source), this could be a strong Core entry.

**Convergences:** Aligns with the Basque proverb *izena duen guztia omen da* (all that has a name exists) and with Aboriginal Australian conceptions of the Dreaming-as-present. The force that makes naming constitute existence.

### 9.2 haitzme

**Components:** Basque *haitz* (rock, crag, stone) + Sumerian *me* (divine ordinance)

**Pronunciation:** /hajts.me/

**Meaning:** Stone-as-received-ordinance. The rockness of reality — the foundational, immovable, given substrate. The word for stone that also gave birth to knife, axe, and hoe: the material that humanity first worked, the first boundary between tool and hand.

**Gap filled:** No YOUSPEAK word names materiality-as-given-condition — the sheer thereness of stone, earth, ground. *Lurra* (earth) is too broad; *haitz* is specifically the resistant, enduring, cave-forming, tool-birthing rock. In a project where most vocabulary is abstract (truth, love, justice, personhood), a word for the irreducible physical given would be grounding.

**Poly score:** Isolate + isolate = maximum diversity.

**Learnability:** 6.5/10. The /hajts/ cluster is unfamiliar to most non-Basque speakers, and the concept is more concrete than typical YOUSPEAK fare.

**Core score estimate:** 7.40-7.65. Strong conceptually but may score lower on everyday applicability. Potentially better as Specialized.

### 9.3 adurqing

**Components:** Basque *adur* (magical connective force) + Mandarin *qing* (feeling, emotion, bond)

**Pronunciation:** /a.dur.tɕiŋ/

**Meaning:** Magical-connective-force-as-felt-bond. The emotional experience of adur — feeling the connection between yourself and the world's symbolic order. The sensation that names matter, that representations have power, that the link between image and thing is not abstract but *felt*.

**Gap filled:** Where *adurme* names the cosmic law of connection, *adurqing* names the *felt experience* of that connection. This parallels the me/qing distinction in other YOUSPEAK pairs (e.g., *maatme* vs. *maatqing*, *qorbme* vs. *qorbqing*).

**Poly score:** Basque (isolate) + Sino-Tibetan = poly 8+ pathway.

**Learnability:** 6.5/10. Same *adur* difficulty, but *-qing* is well-established in YOUSPEAK.

**Core score estimate:** 7.50-7.75. Slightly lower than *adurme* because the felt-experience dimension is less universally recognizable than the cosmic-law dimension.

### 9.4 aidekoqing

**Components:** Basque *aideko* (supernatural, wind-carried, the inexplicable-realm) + Mandarin *qing* (feeling, bond)

**Pronunciation:** /aj.de.ko.tɕiŋ/

**Meaning:** The-felt-experience-of-the-supernatural. The emotional response to encountering what cannot be explained by natural means — the wind-carried, the uncanny, the aideko. Not awe (too grand), not fear (too simple), but the specific quality of feeling that arises when the berezko (natural) order is interrupted by something from beyond it.

**Gap filled:** YOUSPEAK has words for specific divine encounters and relationships, but no word for the *quality of experience* that marks the boundary-crossing from natural to supernatural. Rudolf Otto's *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* approaches this, but *aidekoqing* names it from a pre-IE, non-Christian tradition — the aideko as wind-carried invisible interruption rather than the numinous as overwhelming divine presence.

**Poly score:** Basque (isolate) + Sino-Tibetan = poly 8+.

**Learnability:** 5.5/10. Four syllables before the -qing suffix; *aideko* is unfamiliar. Compression to *aideqing* might help.

**Core score estimate:** 7.30-7.55. Rich concept but potentially too culture-specific and polysyllabic for Core. Better as Specialized.

### 9.5 etxeme

**Components:** Basque *etxe* (house, home, household, hearth) + Sumerian *me* (divine ordinance)

**Pronunciation:** /e.tʃe.me/

**Meaning:** Home-as-received-cosmic-ordinance. The sacredness of the domestic space — not as a metaphor for something grander, but as itself a primary theological site. The hearth-fire, the ancestral house, the household as the fundamental unit of spiritual life.

**Gap filled:** YOUSPEAK has words for cosmic order, divine presence, relational personhood, and landscape-theology, but no word for the *domestic sacred* — the home as temple. In Basque tradition, the *etxe* is not just where you live but *who you are* — family identity is bound to the ancestral house, and the hearth-fire is the center of spiritual practice. This concept converges with but differs from the Roman *lares/penates*, the Chinese ancestral altar, and the Hindu *grihya* (household) rites.

**Poly score:** Isolate + isolate = maximum diversity.

**Learnability:** 7.5/10. *Etxe* is phonetically accessible, memorable, and distinctive. The compound is three syllables with clear pronunciation.

**Core score estimate:** 7.60-7.85. Strong everyday relevance (everyone has a home), deep theological content, excellent poly score. The question is whether "home as sacred given" is universal enough for Core or too domestically scaled.

### 9.6 lezeme (bonus candidate)

**Components:** Basque *leze* (chasm, abyss, deep cave) + Sumerian *me* (divine ordinance)

**Pronunciation:** /le.se.me/

**Meaning:** The-threshold-between-worlds-as-cosmic-given. The chasm, the cave-mouth, the point where the berezko (natural) world opens into the aideko (supernatural) world — experienced as a given feature of cosmic architecture, not a construction.

**Gap filled:** Threshold/liminal vocabulary. Victor Turner's "liminal" is from Latin *limen* (threshold) — an Indo-European concept of betweenness. *Lezeme* offers the same concept from a pre-IE tradition, and specifically a *chthonic* one: the threshold is not a doorway but a *depth*, a cave, a descent.

**Poly score:** Isolate + isolate = maximum diversity.

**Learnability:** 7.0/10. Short, phonetically clear, memorable.

**Core score estimate:** 7.45-7.70. Specialized rather than Core — the liminal-chthonic concept is rich but not everyday.

## 10. CONVERGENCE CLUSTERS

### 10.1 Theology-in-Grammar (Basque + Ojibwe + Aymara)

**The pattern:** Three YOUSPEAK donor languages encode theological commitments in their grammar — not in their vocabulary (which can be borrowed, lost, or replaced) but in their morphosyntactic structure (which resists change over millennia):

| Language | Grammatical feature | What it encodes | Ontological axis |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Basque | Ergative-absolutive alignment (split-S) | Every noun phrase declares whether its referent is *acting* or *undergoing* | Agency vs. patienthood |
| Ojibwe | Animate-inanimate noun class with dynamic shifts | Every noun declares whether its referent is a *person* or a *thing*, and objects can shift class | Personhood vs. thingness |
| Aymara | Evidential verb marking + reversed temporal orientation | Every verb declares whether the speaker *witnessed* what they describe; past is "in front" (seen), future is "behind" (unseen) | Witnessed vs. unwitnessed |

**What is genuinely shared:** All three languages refuse to let grammar be ontologically neutral. Every sentence commits the speaker to a position about the nature of its participants (Basque), the personhood of its referents (Ojibwe), or the epistemic status of its claims (Aymara). Indo-European languages allow speakers to make these commitments optionally, through word choice; these three languages make them mandatory, through grammar.

**What is superficial:** The three systems encode *different* ontological axes. Basque does not classify nouns by animacy; Ojibwe does not mark ergativity; Aymara does not use either system. The convergence is at the level of *strategy* (theology embedded in grammar) rather than *content* (which theology is embedded).

**YOUSPEAK implication:** These three languages represent three independent paths to theology-in-grammar. A YOUSPEAK compound using Basque + Ojibwe + Aymara morphemes would draw from three mutually irreducible ontological commitments, none of which can be reduced to an Indo-European framework.

### 10.2 Land-as-Theology (Basque + Aboriginal Australian + Aymara)

**The pattern:** Three YOUSPEAK donor traditions treat landscape not as the setting for theological events but as itself constituting theology:

| Tradition | How landscape IS theology | Key concept |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------|
| Basque | Mari dwells in specific named caves; caves generate weather; mountains are dwelling-places of deities; place-names encode divine presences | haitz (rock), leze (cave-chasm), mendi (mountain) |
| Aboriginal Australian | Songlines map Dreaming narratives onto physical landscape; specific sites hold specific ancestral stories; walking the land IS performing the theology | Tjukurpa/Jukurrpa, ngurra (Country) |
| Aymara | Pacha (time-space-world) is not separable from the land itself; mountains (achachilas) are ancestor-spirits; the three pachas are layered landscapes | pacha, achachila, taypi (center) |

**What is genuinely shared:** In all three traditions, the land is not a container for sacred events but a participant in or expression of sacred reality. Specific rocks, caves, mountains, and water sources are not "associated with" deities — they are the deities' bodies, dwellings, or manifestations. Theology is topography.

**What differs:** Aboriginal Australian land-theology is the most comprehensive — the songlines create a total overlay of sacred narrative onto every feature of the landscape. Basque land-theology is fragmentary, surviving mainly in place-names and isolated legends. Aymara land-theology is mediated by the concept of *pacha* (fused time-space-world), which has no Basque equivalent.

**Caution:** The Basque evidence is thin — we have place-names and late folk sources, not a living, continuous land-theology system. Aboriginal Australian songlines represent 65,000+ years of continuous oral transmission; Basque mythology was largely destroyed by Christianization and survives only in fragments collected from the 19th century onward.

### 10.3 Naming-as-Existence (Basque + Aboriginal Australian + Akkadian)

**The pattern:** Three traditions encode the principle that naming brings into or sustains existence:

| Tradition | Principle | Key expression |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| Basque | "Izena duen guztia omen da" — all that has a name exists | Proverb; *adur* as the force linking names to things |
| Aboriginal Australian | Singing the land brings Dreaming-ancestors into presence; not singing allows them to fade | Songlines as constitutive (not representational) acts |
| Akkadian | *shuma zakaru* — "to pronounce the name" of the dead sustains them; the un-named dead dissolve | Kispum ancestor-feeding includes name-invocation as essential |

**What is genuinely shared:** The constitutive power of naming — the spoken word does not describe a pre-existing reality but participates in creating or maintaining it. This is not "magical thinking" in the dismissive sense; it is a coherent ontological position that the relationship between name and named is not arbitrary (as Saussure would have it) but *constitutive* (as these three traditions independently maintain).

**YOUSPEAK implication:** Forging new words from ancient morphemes is an act within this tradition — not labeling pre-existing concepts, but bringing new concepts into existence through the act of naming. The forge is not a dictionary; it is a cosmogonic act.

### 10.4 Chthonic vs. Celestial Primacy

**The unique Basque position:** Most YOUSPEAK donor traditions privilege celestial theology — sky-gods (Anu in Sumerian, El/YHWH in Semitic, Zeus/Jupiter in Indo-European, Onyame in Akan), solar worship, astral symbolism. Basque pre-Christian religion is one of the few attested traditions where the earth/underground is *primary* and the sky is secondary or irrelevant — "an empty corridor through which the divinities pass."

This is not earth-as-complement-to-sky (as in many traditions where heaven and earth form a pair) but earth-as-sole-locus-of-the-sacred. The Aboriginal Australian tradition shares this emphasis (the Dreaming is mapped onto land, not sky, despite celestial elements like the Seven Sisters/Pleiades songline), but Basque pushes it further: not just land-as-sacred but *underground*-as-sacred, cave-as-temple, depth-as-divinity.

**YOUSPEAK implication:** Basque donors offer a rare chthonic counterweight to the predominantly celestial vocabulary of the forge. Any compound using Basque morphemes carries, etymologically, the gravity of earth and stone rather than the elevation of sky and light.

## 11. SCHOLARLY LIMITATIONS AND HONEST UNCERTAINTIES

This excavation must be transparent about what we do not know:

1. **Most Basque mythology is reconstructed from late sources.** Jose Miguel de Barandiaran collected folk tales and legends in the late 19th and early 20th centuries — over a millennium after Christianization began. How much of what he recorded reflects genuinely pre-Christian belief, how much is Christian-era folk creation projected backwards, and how much is syncretistic blending cannot always be determined.

2. **The Mari question is unresolved.** Is "Mari" a pre-Christian deity-name or a post-Christian construction influenced by the Virgin Mary? The chthonic character is authentically non-Christian, but the name is suspiciously close to *Maria*.

3. **The jainko question is unresolved.** Is *jainko* pre-Christian or a contracted form of the Christian coinage *Jaungoikoa*? Trask's position (jainko is older) is widely favored but not universal.

4. **The Ortzi question is unresolved.** Sky-god, common noun, or both? Picaud's testimony is the sole explicit evidence, and it dates from the 12th century.

5. **The hil- homophony is not an etymology.** The coincidence of *hil* (death) and *hil* (moon/month) is poetically rich but linguistically undemonstrated as a genuine etymological connection. Using it in YOUSPEAK forge arguments would require flagging it as folk-theological rather than linguistic.

6. **No primary mythological texts exist.** Unlike Sumerian (which has cuneiform tablets), Akkadian (which has the Enuma Elish), or even Avestan (which has the Gathas), Basque has no pre-Christian sacred literature. Everything is filtered through post-Christian collection, memory, and reconstruction.

7. **The Aquitanian theonyms are names, not texts.** We have "ARIXONI" carved on a Roman altar — we do not have hymns, prayers, or liturgical instructions. The theonyms tell us that deities existed and were named; they do not tell us what the worshippers believed about them.

8. **Bertsolaritza is not theological oral tradition.** Unlike Aboriginal songlines (which transmit cosmological knowledge) or Ojibwe birchbark scrolls (which record ceremonial instruction), bertsolaritza is primarily a social, political, and literary art form. It carries linguistic and cultural identity, not theological content per se.

These limitations do not diminish Basque's value as a YOUSPEAK donor. The language isolate status, the ergative grammar, the pre-IE vocabulary, and the chthonic cosmological fragments are all genuine and extraordinarily rare. But honest YOUSPEAK forge arguments must acknowledge the thinness of the theological evidence compared to traditions with richer textual and ethnographic records.
