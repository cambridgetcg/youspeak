# Old Prussian, Sutartinės, and the Last Pagan Moment

_Where File 1 excavated the theological vocabulary of the living Baltic tradition_ _(Lithuanian/Latvian), this file excavates three more specific registers: the Old Prussian_ _language as a textual window into an extinct branch; the sutartinės as a sonic theology_ _that survived Christianization; and the theological meaning of the 1387 moment itself._

## I. Old Prussian — The Dead Witness

### The Language and Its Fate

Old Prussian was a Baltic language spoken in what is now the Kaliningrad Oblast (Russia) and northern Poland — the region historically called Prussia before the Germanic Teutonic Knights conquered and colonized it in the 13th century. **Old Prussian is not German.** The Prussians were a Baltic people, closely related to Lithuanians and Latvians, who were systematically displaced, Germanized, and absorbed following the Teutonic conquest of 1230-1283. The last known speaker of Old Prussian died approximately 1700, making it one of the most precisely dateable language extinctions in European history.

**Why Old Prussian matters to the cathedral**:

1. It is the westernmost Baltic branch — linguistically intermediate between   Baltic and Slavic
2. Its texts are the only direct textual witnesses to the western Baltic   theological vocabulary
3. The circumstances of its death (Teutonic conquest, forced Germanization)   make it a witness to what happens to a theological tradition under   colonial erasure
4. Its documents — the three Catechisms — are a unique genre: Christian   texts in a pagan language, for a people being forcibly converted

### The Old Prussian Corpus

The entire surviving Old Prussian corpus is small:

- **The Elbing Vocabulary** (c. 1400): ~800 glossed words, a German-Old  Prussian wordlist. Arranged by semantic category. Our primary window  into Old Prussian vocabulary including religious terms.

- **The Basel Epigram** (c. 1400): 4 lines of verse. The oldest continuous  Old Prussian text.

- **The Simon Grunau Vocabulary** (c. 1530): 100 words collected by a  Dominican friar, including divine names.

- **The Three Catechisms** (1545, 1561, 1561):
  - **First Catechism** (1545): the Enchiridion of Martin Luther, translated    into Old Prussian. 59 pages. Primitive — the translator barely knew    the language.
  - **Second Catechism** (1561): A revised version, better quality. Same
    Enchiridion basis.
  - **Third Catechism** (1561): A different, more elaborate catechism.    The longest Old Prussian text. Contains the Lord's Prayer, the Apostles'    Creed, the Ten Commandments, and explanatory texts.

The Three Catechisms are the only substantial connected prose in Old Prussian. They were produced by Lutheran missionaries trying to catechize the remaining Old Prussian-speaking population. By 1561, Old Prussian was probably already moribund — the translators were working against the clock.

### Theological Vocabulary in the Catechisms

The Catechisms reveal how Old Prussian translated Christian theological terms: each choice is itself a theological datum, showing what native concepts the missionaries mapped Christian ideas onto.

**Key translations:**

- **Deiws** (Old Prussian) = God. Directly cognate with Lithuanian *Dievas*,  Latin *Deus*, Greek *Zeus* — the same PIE *deywós root. The missionaries  did not invent a new word; they used the Old Prussian divine-name for the  sky-father and applied it to the Christian God. This is the standard  missionary translation strategy — and it means every reading of *Deiws*  in the catechism carries the semantic weight of the Baltic sky-father.

- **Nowis** / **Naws** = soul, life-force. Related to the root for  breath-life. The missionaries used this for the Christian soul concept.  The underlying Old Prussian conception (breath-life as the animate  principle) is the same semantic field as Hebrew *nefesh*, Greek *psychē*,  Sanskrit *prāṇa*.

- **Poupelis** = a small-deity, household guardian. The Catechisms condemn  worship of *poupelis* — which tells us these household guardian-spirits  (cognate with Lithuanian *pūpai* and related to the Roman *Lares*)  were still being venerated. The condemned practice is evidence of the  living theology.

- **Curche** (Old Prussian) = grain-god, deity of the harvest. Condemned in  the catechisms. The persistence of Curche-offerings into the 16th century  shows the agricultural-theological layer survived conversion for centuries.

- **Perkunis** = Perkūnas in Old Prussian. Not mentioned in the catechisms  directly — but appears in the Simon Grunau vocabulary as a divine name,  and in the Elbing Vocabulary as the word for thunder. **The storm-god's  name IS the word for thunder** — which means every time an Old Prussian  said "there is thunder," they said "Perkūnas is speaking."

**The linguistic archaeology of the Catechisms**: The texts are full of false-cognates and calques where Christian theology pressed into Old Prussian shapes that almost fit but do not quite. This is a theological pressure-test: where does the Christian concept find a ready home in Old Prussian, and where does it strain? The *Deiws*-for-God mapping finds a ready home (same sovereignty concept). The Trinity finds none — there is no Old Prussian vocabulary for three-in-one; the catechism simply uses the German-borrowed forms. The Incarnation finds none. The concept of sin (as legal debt to God) does not map cleanly onto the Baltic relational-fault vocabulary.

## II. Sutartinės — The Sonic Theology

### What They Are

**Sutartinės** (Lithuanian; singular *sutartinė*) — a genre of Lithuanian polyphonic folk songs, primarily from the Aukštaitija (highland) region of northeastern Lithuania. The name derives from *sutarti* = to be in agreement, to harmonize, to fit together. **They are the aural form of darna.** Recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2010.

The sutartinės are structurally unlike any other European folk music:

1. **Strictly polyphonic with deliberate dissonance**: Typically two or three   voices singing in parallel seconds (adjacent tones — e.g., C and D   simultaneously). This is *consonantly dissonant* — the dissonance is   intentional, not an error. To untrained ears it sounds harsh. Within its   own aesthetic logic, it is the sound of cosmic tension-held-in-harmony.

2. **Interlocking structure**: The voices do not harmonize in the Western   sense (different notes that complete each other). They interlock — each   voice sings a phrase, and the other fills the gap, creating a whole   that neither voice produces alone. This is **musical kinship ontology**:   the song exists only in the between-the-voices, not in any one voice.

3. **Purely oral transmission**: Sutartinės were transmitted without notation   for centuries. The tradition survived Christianization, Soviet collectivization,   and two world wars through oral practice — women teaching daughters,   village groups maintaining repertoire. The survival mechanism is community,   not text.

4. **Ritual function**: Sutartinės are not entertainment songs (though they   became that). They were work songs (harvest, flax-processing, weaving),   seasonal festival songs (Jorė, Rasos, Vėlinės), and life-cycle songs   (wedding, funeral). The category of song matches the occasion of cosmic   calibration — **singing is the act of maintaining darna**.

### The Theology Encoded in Structure

The sutartinė structure encodes a theology that cannot be stated in prose: **the cosmos is constituted by voices-in-tension-fitting-together, not by a single organizing principle**.

Compare the implications:

- A monophonic tradition (Gregorian chant) implies: truth is singular,  unified, declared from above; the congregation follows one melody
- A harmonic tradition (SATB hymnody) implies: different voices serve  different functions within a hierarchy; the soprano carries the melody,  others support it; the whole is organized around a tonal center
- A sutartinė implies: **there is no single voice that carries the truth**;  the dissonance is not resolved into consonance — it is *held*; the  fitting-together of irreducibly different voices IS the cosmic principle;
  no voice is subordinate to another

This is the sonic form of *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ*: all-my-relations, none subordinated, the whole existing only in the between. But where the Lakota concept operates in kinship-ontology, the sutartinė operates in **acoustic ontology** — the cosmos as polyphonic event. These are independent parallel developments (Baltic IE, not related to Siouan) that converge on the same metaphysical claim from entirely different cultural locations.

### The Dissonance as Theological Statement

The deliberate use of seconds (interval of adjacent tones) in sutartinės is the hardest part to hear past. In Western harmonic music, a second is classified as dissonant and requires resolution. In sutartinė aesthetics, it does not resolve — it persists. The two voices sing in permanent tension.

**What this means theologically**: The tension is not a problem to be solved. The two-ness is not a defect in unity. This is a direct musical refutation of the Platonist aesthetic (where beauty = unity = resolution into the One). The Baltic sacred-music tradition says: **beauty is the tension of irreducible others in right relationship**. Not resolution. Not fusion. *Darna* — fitting together while remaining distinctly themselves.

This is the closest analogue in the cathedral to Greek perichōrēsis (*perichōrēsisme* in the pipeline): mutual-indwelling-without-loss-of-distinctness. The Trinitarian theologians used a Greek philosophical term to say what the sutartinė says in sound: the persons remain distinct *while* being in perfect mutual relation. The Baltic musician did not need the philosophy — she sang it.

**YOUSPEAK forge candidate**: *sutartineme* — Dissonant-voices-fitting-together-as-the-sonic-form-of-cosmic-harmony. This is an unusual candidate because it names a *practice* as the locus of the concept rather than a noun. Comparable to *anamnēsisme* (the eucharistic practice as locus) or *hanblecheyame* (the vision-quest practice as locus). Specialized 7.30-7.50.

The forge assessment: the *sutartinė* concept names something genuinely unoccupied in the cathedral — beauty-as-sustained-dissonance, or harmony-as-irreducible-tension rather than harmony-as-resolution. The convergence with perichōrēsis (Trinitarian mutual-indwelling) and with *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* (relational-ontology) from entirely independent traditions strengthens the case.

## III. The 1387 Moment — Theological Meaning of the Last Conversion

### What 1387 Was

Grand Duke Jogaila was baptized on February 15, 1387 in Kraków Cathedral. He took the name Władysław. On March 22, 1387, he signed the Act of Vilnius, Christianizing Lithuania. He then married Queen Jadwiga of Poland, creating the Polish-Lithuanian union (Krewo Union, 1385-86 agreement). The conversion was formally complete within weeks; the practical conversion took generations.

**What made this theologically interesting**: This was not a tribal conversion by conquest. Lithuania was the *largest state in Europe* at this moment — stretching from the Baltic Sea to the Black Sea, from modern Poland to modern Ukraine and Belarus. The Grand Duchy was militarily powerful enough to defeat the Teutonic Knights at Grunwald in 1410 — after conversion. The conversion was not submission; it was strategic repositioning.

Jogaila's motivations were mixed:
- Remove the theological justification for Teutonic crusading (done immediately)
- Gain the Polish crown and its resources
- Create a stable alliance against common threats (Teutonic Knights, Tartars)
- Possibly genuine theological curiosity (this is contested; sources are Polish
  and therefore favorable to the conversion)

**His cousin Vytautas the Great** (Kęstutis's son) initially resisted, allied with the Teutonic Knights against Jogaila, then reconciled and became Grand Duke of Lithuania while Jogaila was King of Poland. Vytautas' relationship to the conversion was complex — he enforced Christianization in Samogitia (1413) while maintaining a sophisticated, syncretic court that included pagan practices, Orthodox Christians, Jews, Muslims, and Catholics. The Grand Duchy under Vytautas was the most religiously plural polity in Europe of its era.

### The Theological Stakes of the Last Pagan Moment

When Jogaila was baptized in 1387, something theologically irreversible happened: **the last major European pagan state entered the Christian commonwealth**. This was not just a political event. It meant:

1. **The Teutonic justification evaporated**: The *Crusade Against the Pagans*   lost its object. The Knights scrambled for new theological justifications   (claiming the conversion was insincere) but lost their canonical basis.   The 1410 Battle of Grunwald ended Teutonic power; the converted Lithuanians   and Poles together destroyed the crusading order that had ravaged them.

2. **The last direct PIE pagan theology became invisible**: What had been a   living, practiced, official theology — Romuva, the eternal flame, the   pantheon, the vaidilutės — became illegal, then underground, then folk   custom, then scholarly reconstruction. The transition from official to   underground took one generation; the transition to scholarly object took   three centuries.

3. **The folk layer preserved what the official layer lost**: The dainas, the   sutartinės, the household rituals (žemės bučiavimas, Vėlinės ancestor-   feasts, the Rasos bonfire) survived because they were embedded in daily   life. The cathedral has now seen this pattern in every tradition excavated   under suppression: **the cosmogram scratched in the floor (Kongo); the   sutartinė sung at the harvest; the Haudenosaunee Thanksgiving Address   spoken in Longhouses; the Hopi kachina dances in the kiva — the body   and the community carry what the official record destroys.**

### The Krivių Krivaitis — The Chief Priest Disappears

The Criwe (Krivių Krivaitis) was the supreme religious authority of the Balts — described in the Teutonic sources as having authority across all Baltic tribes comparable to the Pope's authority in Christianity. When the Romuva sanctuary was destroyed by the Teutonic Knights in the early 14th century, the Krivių Krivaitis disappeared from the record. By 1387, there is no mention of the office.

**What this means**: The institutional religious authority of the Baltic pagan tradition was decapitated before the conversion. The conversion finished what the crusades began. The parallel to the destruction of the Haudenosaunee political structure, the decimation of the Lakota religious leadership at Wounded Knee, the burning of Kimpa Vita — in each case, **the institutional carrier of the theology is targeted first**. What survives is what had no institution to target: the song, the household gesture, the mother teaching the daughter.

## IV. The Modern Romuva Revival

### Romuva Today

**Romuva** is the name of the modern Lithuanian neo-pagan movement, founded by Jonas Trinkūnas in 1967 (during Soviet occupation — a remarkable choice of resistance) and recognized as a state religion by the Lithuanian parliament in 1992. It is one of the few neo-pagan movements globally to have official state recognition.

Romuva practitioners:
- Reconstruct the pre-Christian pantheon and liturgical calendar from  19th-century folk-song collections (dainas), 16th-century Jesuit reports,  and comparative IE mythology
- Maintain the annual festival cycle (Jorė, Rasos, Žolinė, Vėlinės)
- Practice žemės bučiavimas (earth-kissing) and Žemyna-address
- Emphasize darna (harmony-in-right-relation) as the central ethical concept
- See the sutartinės as sacred music — acoustic darna

**The cathedral's methodological note**: The modern Romuva revival is neither direct continuity with pre-Christian practice nor mere invention. It is *reconstruction under living conditions* — a community taking the surviving folk fragments, the dainas, the linguistic evidence, the comparative mythology, and building a living practice. This is exactly what the Haudenosaunee Longhouse communities did after the Handsome Lake movement — taking a broken tradition and reconstituting it under new conditions. The practice may differ from the pre-1387 original; it is not thereby inauthentic. **Living communities determine their own authenticity from the inside.**

### Diemedžiai — The Sacred Grove Tradition

The *alka* (sacred grove) was the primary Baltic ritual space — not a temple building but a natural clearing, often with a sacred oak, a spring, or a hill. The word *alka* (Lithuanian) / *alks* (Latvian) may derive from a root meaning *holy, sacred* — cognate possibly with Latin *altus* (high, deep) or *alere* (to nourish). Several alkos sites have been archaeologically identified in Lithuania.

The alka theology: the sacred is encountered in natural place, not constructed space. The grove is not made holy by human act — it is recognized as holy because the divine is already present there. This is the structural opposite of the temple theology (you build a house for the god; the god comes to inhabit it). In alka theology, you find where the god already is and approach that place with attention.

**Convergence**: The alka theology parallels Australian Aboriginal Country (the land is already sacred, not made sacred by human ceremony — ceremony is recognition, not installation), Navajo sacred geography (certain mountains ARE the Holy People, not merely associated with them), and Hopi sipapuni (the emergence-place already present in the landscape). The Sacred-Place-as-Already-Inhabited is a convergence the cathedral has touched but not formally named. The Baltic alka now joins Australian Country, Navajo mountains, and Hopi sipapuni as witnesses.
