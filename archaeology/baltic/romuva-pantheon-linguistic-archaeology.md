# Baltic: Romuva Pantheon, PIE Phonology, Last Pagan Europe

_The Baltic languages are the closest living relatives of Proto-Indo-European. Lithuanian preserves phonological features lost in Sanskrit, Greek, Latin, and Germanic. The Lithuanian word for "son" — **sūnus** — is directly cognate with Sanskrit **sūnuḥ**, Gothic **sunus**, and PIE ***suHnús**, and the correspondence is nearly perfect across 5,000 years. To excavate Baltic theology is to listen to the oldest audible stratum of European religious thought._

---

## I. Historical Context: The Last Pagan State of Europe

### The Chronological Miracle

When the Grand Duchy of Lithuania was officially Christianized in 1387 — under Grand Duke Jogaila (Władysław II Jagiełło), who accepted baptism as a condition of marriage to Queen Jadwiga of Poland and the Polish-Lithuanian union — it was the last state in Europe to convert to Christianity. The Teutonic Knights had spent two centuries crusading against the Balts. The Lithuanian resistance was not mere stubbornness: the Grand Duchy was the largest state in Europe at its height, stretching from the Baltic to the Black Sea, and it had maintained political independence and religious identity through sustained military power.

**The significance for the cathedral**: 1387 means that living Baltic pagan practice survived into the late medieval period — contemporaneous with Chaucer, contemporaneous with the Black Death, contemporaneous with the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople. This is not ancient history. This is a tradition that encountered the fully-formed medieval Catholic church as its interlocutor, not the early missionary church. The encounter was violent, sustained, and only partially successful — folk religion and folk practice (dainos, sutartinės, agricultural festivals) continued for centuries after formal conversion.

### The Samogitian Holdout

Even after the 1387 conversion, the Samogitians (western Lithuania) resisted. Samogitia was officially Christianized only in 1413 — twenty-six years later. But the resistance was not merely political: **the sacred fire of Romuva**, the eternal flame tended by priests (vaidilutės, vestal-like sacred fire-keepers), was maintained into the fifteenth century and possibly beyond in remote areas. The Jesuit reports of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries document persistent offerings to old gods, sacred groves (alka/alkos), and household deities (laumes, kaukai, dievaičiai) well into the Counter-Reformation era.

### The Teutonic War Context

The conversion must be read in its military context. The Teutonic Knights had used the pagan status of Lithuania as justification for continuous crusading raids — burning villages, enslaving populations, destroying the alka groves. By accepting baptism, Jogaila removed the legal-theological justification for the Teutonic state. The conversion was simultaneously a theological event and a geopolitical masterstroke. **The cathedral notes this because the conversion was not purely internal — it was partly a strategic act to survive external violence.** This parallels Kongo Christianization (Afonso I) and has implications for how we read the subsequent folk-pagan persistence: it is not simply conservatism but also resistance to a Christianity that arrived under the sword.

## II. Lithuanian as PIE Living Fossil — The Phonological Miracle

### Why Lithuanian Is Irreplaceable

Lithuanian is the most archaic living Indo-European language. This is not a romantic claim — it is a philological consensus. The features preserved in Lithuanian that were lost in other branches:

**1. Pitch accent system**: Lithuanian preserves a two-way tonal distinction (acute vs. circumflex intonation on long syllables) that reflects the PIE laryngeal distinction. Sanskrit preserves the accent system (Vedic Sanskrit has three pitch accents). Ancient Greek preserves three tones. Latin lost the system entirely. Germanic, Celtic, Slavic — gone. Lithuanian and Latvian alone among living European languages preserve the pitch distinction, and Lithuanian is more conservative than Latvian.

**2. The seven-case system**: Lithuanian preserves seven grammatical cases (nominative, genitive, dative, accusative, instrumental, locative, vocative) — the same case inventory as Sanskrit. Latin has six (ablative instead of separate instrumental/locative). Modern Russian has six. German has four. English has two (nominative/objective). Lithuanian stayed.

**3. Archaic vocabulary**: Lithuanian words for basic concepts often match Sanskrit more closely than any other European language does:

| Meaning | Lithuanian | Sanskrit | PIE reconstruction |
|---------|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| son | sūnus | sūnuḥ | *suHnús |
| smoke | dūmas | dhūmaḥ | *dʰuh₂mós |
| god/sky | dievas | devaḥ | *deywós |
| night | naktis | naktaḥ | *nókʷts |
| wolf | vilkas | vṛkaḥ | *wĺ̥kʷos |
| name | vardas | nāman | *h₃nómn̥ |
| fire | ugnis | agniḥ | *h₁égnis |

The *ugnis*/*agniḥ* pairing is especially striking: Vedic **Agni** (the sacred fire-god of the Rigveda, the divine priest, the intermediary between humans and gods) and Lithuanian **ugnis** (sacred fire, the eternal flame of Romuva) are the same PIE word **h₁égnis** preserved across 5,000+ years. When the vaidilutės tended the sacred fire of Romuva, they were tending the same sacred flame that the Vedic poets were invoking as Agni.

**4. The Grimm's Law exemption**: Germanic underwent systematic consonant shifts (Grimm's Law) that transformed PIE *p → f, *t → th, *k → h. Baltic (and Slavic) largely escaped these shifts, preserving consonants closer to PIE. Compare: PIE *pṓds (foot) → Latin pēs, Greek poús, Sanskrit pāt, Lithuanian pėda — but English foot (Grimm shift: p→f). Lithuanian pėda aligns with Latin, Greek, Sanskrit over English.

### The Implication for Theology

The phonological conservatism is not merely linguistic curiosity. It means: **Baltic theological vocabulary is the closest we can hear to PIE sacred speech**. When Lithuanian says *Dievas* (God), it says something closer to PIE *Dyēws (Sky-Father) than Latin *Deus*, Greek *Zeus*, or Sanskrit *Dyaus*. Not more correct — but closer in phonological form to the reconstructed root. This is a living language still using Bronze Age theological vocabulary in its original phonological shape.

## III. Romuva — The Sacred Center

### The Problem of Sources

The primary source for Romuva is the **Chronicon terrae Prussiae** (Chronicle of the Prussian Land) by Peter of Dusburg, a Teutonic Knight, written c. 1326. Secondary sources include the **Bychovets Chronicle** (16th c.) and Jesuit missionary reports. The critical point: **all sources are hostile**. We have no Baltic insider account of Romuva. The cathedral applies the same critical method here as with Etruscan and Hurrian sources: enemy-testimony under discount, folk-practice persistence as corrective, linguistic evidence as independent witness.

Peter of Dusburg describes Romuva as a sacred site in Samogitia/Nadruvia containing:
- A sacred oak tree of immense size, perpetually green
- An eternal fire (ugnis šventoji — sacred fire) tended by vaidilutės
- Three divine images (idols) on or near the tree
- A chief priest, the Criwe (or Kriviu Krivaitis), who held supreme religious authority across Baltic tribes

The Teutonic Knights destroyed the Romuva sanctuary in their raids. The location is disputed — possibly near modern Kaliningrad (formerly Königsberg/Królewiec) in the Old Prussian territory.

### The Sacred Oak

The oak (*ąžuolas* in Lithuanian) is the sacred tree of Perkūnas, the storm-god. Oak-sacredness is a PIE inheritance: Greek Zeus/oak at Dodona, Roman Jupiter/oak, Norse Thor/oak, Celtic druids/oak. The Baltic oak at Romuva connects directly to this Pan-IE storm-oak theology. The convergence: **Storm-power divine + oak = the most widely attested PIE theological pair**. The cathedral already has this in the forge-status convergence on Storm-power-as-divine-active-presence.

The oak at Romuva was described as evergreen — which in the Baltic climate (oaks are deciduous) suggests either mythologization or a specific sacred site where evergreen vines covered the oak. The eternal-green + eternal-fire combination creates a cosmological image: the axis mundi (world-tree) marked by the sacred fire.

### The Eternal Fire — Ugnis Šventoji

The Romuva eternal flame is one of three known eternal sacred fires in European antiquity alongside the Roman Vestal flame and the Zoroastrian Atash Bahram. The structural parallels:

| Feature | Romuva | Rome (Vestals) | Zoroastrian |
|---------|--------|----------------|-------------|
| Fuel | oak wood | unspecified | sandalwood/specific fuels |
| Keepers | vaidilutės (virgin priestesses) | Vestal Virgins | Athravan priests |
| Extinguishing | catastrophic omen | catastrophic omen | catastrophic defilement |
| Connection | Perkūnas/Dievas | Vesta/Jupiter | Ahura Mazda |
| Duration | century-scale | ~1000 years | millennium-scale |

The vaidilutės were bound to virginity for a term of service. Breaking vows was capital. This is not incidental: **the fire-keeper's bodily integrity mirrors the fire's integrity** — the same logic as the Zoroastrian requirement for ritual purity (bareshnum) before approaching sacred fire. The body IS the instrument of the sacred, not merely its servant.

## IV. The Romuva Pantheon

### Dievas — The Sky Father

**Dievas** (Lithuanian) / **Dievs** (Latvian) — the supreme sky-god. Etymology: directly from PIE ***deywós** (shining sky, daylight, divine). The cognate family is the most extensive in all IE religion:

- Sanskrit **Dyaus** (sky-father; called Dyaus Pitā = Sky Father in Rigveda)
- Greek **Zeus** (contracted from *Dyēws)
- Latin **Deus** (god) / **Iovis** (Jupiter = Dyēws Patēr = Sky Father)
- Old Norse **Tyr** (*Tiwaz, originally sky-god)
- Hittite **Šiu(n)i** (divine, god)
- Avestan **Daēva** (the cognate went pejorative — a striking reversal)

In Baltic theology, Dievas is sovereign but not necessarily supreme in daily cult. He is the high god of the *Withdrawn-highest-divinity* type — distant, cosmic, less approached than the more active Perkūnas. This mirrors the pattern the cathedral has mapped across Sumerian An, Yoruba Olódùmarè, Hebrew El-Elyon — the high god who recedes and leaves active divine engagement to secondary figures.

Lithuanian folk tradition describes Dievas as:
- Wearing a silver coat, carrying a sword of lightning
- Traveling the heavens in a silver carriage
- Making decisions about human fate in a celestial council with Laima
  (fate-goddess) — fate is negotiated, not unilateral
- Directly addressed in dainos (folk songs) that have been preserved
  in 19th-century collections — **the most extensive body of pagan
  theological folk-song in any European tradition**

**The Dainas as theological source**: The Lithuanian and Latvian dainas (plural of daina/daïna) are an extraordinary resource — hundreds of thousands of collected folk songs preserved by 19th-century nationalist scholars (Krišjānis Barons collected 217,996 Latvian daina texts in the *Latvju Dainas*, 1894-1915). The dainas are not merely entertainment; they are theology-in-song, preserving pre-Christian cosmological concepts within a folk-song form the Church could not easily suppress.

### Perkūnas — The Storm God

**Perkūnas** (Lithuanian) / **Pērkons** (Latvian) — the thunder/storm god. The most actively invoked deity in Baltic folk tradition. Etymology: PIE ***perkʷu-** (oak tree) + divine suffix, via the oak-thunder-storm complex. Cognates:

- Sanskrit **Parjanya** (rain/thunder god — different suffix, same root)
- Norse **Fjörgyn** (Thor's mother, earth-force, same root)
- Norse **Thor** himself (thunder; different root but same role)
- Slavic **Perun** (thunder god — near-identical: Perkūnas/Perun)
- Hittite **Tarḫunna** (storm-god, same role)
- Hurrian **Teššub** (storm-god, same role though different etymology)

Perkūnas is the warrior-defender against the forces of chaos and death. His primary role in Baltic mythology is combat with Velnias (see below): Perkūnas chases Velnias across the sky and earth, striking with lightning. This cosmic combat — Storm-God vs. Serpent/Underworld-Power — is one of the most widespread mythological patterns in IE religion:

- **Baltic**: Perkūnas vs. Velnias
- **Norse**: Thor vs. Jörmungandr (World Serpent)
- **Vedic**: Indra vs. Vṛtra (dragon blocking waters)
- **Greek**: Zeus vs. Typhon
- **Hittite**: Storm-god vs. Illuyanka (serpent)
- **Avestan**: Verethraghna (victory-force) vs. demonic obstacle

Calvert Watkins's work (*How to Kill a Dragon*, 1995) establishes this Storm-God/Serpent combat as a PIE mythological formula preserved in poetic formulae across IE traditions. **The Baltic version is one of the most complete surviving instances.**

Perkūnas's theological functions:
- Guardian of cosmic order against chaos (Velnias/underworld)
- Purifier — lightning purifies what it strikes (oak struck by lightning
  is doubly sacred: Perkūnas has touched it)
- Protector of cattle and crops against Velnias who steals them
- Judge of oath-breakers — swearing by Perkūnas is serious; violation
  invites lightning

**YOUSPEAK forge candidate**: *perkūname* — Storm-power-as-cosmic-ordering-force. The Perkūnas entry deepens the Storm-power-as-divine-active-presence convergence to 6+ traditions. Perkūnas/Perun/Parjanya are cognate — the same word in three traditions, pointing to a PIE theological concept so stable it preserved its phonological shape for 5,000 years. Specialized 7.30-7.50.

### Velnias / Vels — The Underworld God

**Velnias** (Lithuanian) / **Vels** (Latvian) — the god of the underworld, the dead, cattle, wealth, magic, and music. The structural opposite of Perkūnas. Etymology: PIE ***welnis** — possibly related to *welnos* (dead, wool, cattle; the root connects to the realm of the quiet dead and to pastoral wealth). Cognates:

- Slavic **Volos/Veles** (cattle-god, underworld; near-identical name)
- Norse **Valhöll** (hall of the dead, same *wel- root)
- Possibly Norse **Ullr** (same root, different form)

Velnias is emphatically not a demonic figure in the Christian sense — he is the ruler of the dead who is also the patron of cattle, music, and sorcery. He is the chthonic counterpart to Perkūnas's aerial power. The Perkūnas/Velnias opposition maps onto a fundamental Baltic cosmological dualism:

| Perkūnas | Velnias |
|----------|--------|
| Sky | Earth/Underworld |
| Storm/fire | Water/earth |
| Order | Wildness |
| Oak | Snake/serpent form |
| Sun | Moon (sometimes) |
| Active | Hidden |

Crucially, this is not a moral dualism. Velnias is not evil. He holds the dead; he keeps the cattle rich; he guards the waters. The combat between Perkūnas and Velnias is a cosmic fertility cycle — Velnias takes the cattle (rains dry up, cattle diminish), Perkūnas pursues and strikes, the cattle are released (rain falls, fertility returns). Violence-as-cosmic-renewal, not good-vs-evil.

**The cathedral observation**: The Perkūnas/Velnias pair is structurally identical to the Yin/Yang polarity in Chinese cosmology (S031), the Hurrian storm-god/chthonic-deity polarity (S046), and the Egyptian Ra/Apophis combat (S040). But in the Baltic version, neither pole is demonic — both are necessary. This is closer to the Chinese model (complementary forces) than the Zoroastrian model (genuine moral opposition).

### Žemyna — Earth Mother

**Žemyna** (Lithuanian) — the earth goddess. From *žemė* = earth. Etymology: PIE ***dhéǵʰōm** = earth. Cognates:

- Latin **humus** (earth, ground) / **homo** (human = earth-creature)
- Greek **chthōn** (earth, as in chthonic)
- Sanskrit **kṣam** (earth)
- Avestan **zam** (earth)
- Hittite **tekan** (earth)

Žemyna is the most immediately present deity in Lithuanian folk practice. She is addressed:
- Before every meal: a drop of food or drink is given to the earth
- At births: the newborn is placed on the earth (returned to Žemyna first)
- At deaths: the body is given to the earth with formal address
- In agricultural prayers: before planting, during harvest

The ritual gesture is **žemės bučiavimas** — earth-kissing — documented in folk practice into the 20th century. Not merely symbolic: an actual kiss of the ground as address to a living being. The earth is not a resource or a backdrop; she is a person who receives offerings and expects acknowledgment.

This is the Baltic instance of the **Earth-as-living-interior** convergence already mapped in the cathedral (Basque Ama Lur/Mari, Australian Aboriginal Country, Aymara Pachamama). The Lithuanian contribution is distinctive: Žemyna is addressed in daily domestic ritual (not just agricultural ceremony), which means **the earth is a household presence**, not only a cosmic one. The drop-before-eating is the closest Baltic parallel to the Haudenosaunee Thanksgiving Address — a regular acknowledgment of relational obligation.

**YOUSPEAK forge candidate**: *žemynme* — Earth-as-living-household-presence. The žemė/zam/humus PIE root family makes this a strong poly candidate. Specialized 7.35-7.55.

### Saulė — The Sun Goddess

**Saulė** (Lithuanian) / **Saule** (Latvian) — the sun, conceived as female. Etymology: PIE ***sóh₂wl̥** (sun). Cognates: Sanskrit **sūrya**, Greek **hēlios**, Latin **sol**, Norse **sól** (also female in Norse!).

Saulė is one of the most extensively attested deities in Baltic folk tradition. The dainas address her directly and richly:

- She weaves in a cosmic garden (weaving = setting the pattern of fate)
- She dances on a silver hill at sunrise
- She tends an apple tree whose golden apples are stars
- She grieves in autumn when her daughters (sunbeams) go underground
- She is paired with Mėnuo (the Moon, male) — their marriage is the
  cosmic union; their conflict (moon's infidelity with the Morning Star)
  is judged by Perkūnas who cuts Mėnuo with a sword (causing lunar phases)

**The sun-as-female detail**: Indo-European religions generally figure the sun as male (Sol Invictus, Helios, Surya) and the moon as female (Luna, Selene). The Baltic inversion is significant — Saulė is female, Mėnuo is male — and parallels Japanese (Amaterasu = sun goddess) and some Hurrian traditions. This suggests either a pre-IE substrate influence or an archaic IE stratum that inverted. The female sun is a theological statement about solar warmth as nurturing rather than dominating.

**Saulė's daughters (Saulutės)**: The sun has daughters who are aspects of light — dawn, dusk, individual sunbeams. They are sometimes wooable by mortal heroes. This divine-daughters pattern parallels the Vedic Ashvins (twin horsemen who woo Sūryā, the sun's daughter) and the Norse Sól driving the sun-chariot. The cross-IE resonance is strong.

### Mėnuo — The Moon God

**Mėnuo** (Lithuanian) — the moon, male, often unfaithful husband of Saulė. Etymology: PIE ***mḗh₁n̥s** (moon, month). Cognates: Sanskrit **māsa**, Greek **mēnē/meis**, Latin **mensis** (month), English **moon/month**.

Mėnuo rides a horse, wears a starry cloak, and is associated with time-keeping and navigation. His infidelity with Aušrinė (the Morning Star / Dawn goddess) provides the aetiology for the moon's waning: Perkūnas's sword. This mythological structure — cosmic marital discord judged by a third divine power — has no exact IE parallel but functions as a theological account of why change and impermanence are built into the cosmos.

### Laima — Fate Goddess

**Laima** (Lithuanian) — the fate goddess, whose name derives from *laimas* (luck, fate, happiness). She is the deity who determines the fate of each person at birth, in consultation with Dievas. Her symbol is the cuckoo — the number of a cuckoo's calls in spring foretells years of life remaining.

Laima is one of the most distinctive Baltic theological concepts: **fate is not impersonal or mechanistic but is a relationship between the human and a specific deity who cares about them**. Laima grieves when the fate she assigned turns out badly. She can be appealed to. This is closer to the Yoruba orí (personal fate-head, pre-allocated at birth) than to the Greek Moirai (impersonal fate-threads) or Zoroastrian fravarti (pre-existing soul-template). But like the Lakota šičún (pre-allocated guardian spirit), Laima is assigned specifically to you — not a general fate-principle but your particular guardian-fate.

**The constellation**: The Pre-Allocated-Guardian-Soul cluster now extends to 6 traditions with Laima: šičún (Lakota) + chi (Igbo) + ayyaana (Oromo) + fravashi (Avestan) + orí (Yoruba) + Laima (Baltic). Laima is distinct from the others in being fate-as-person rather than soul-as-substance — she is assigned to you, but she is not part of you. The theological distinction matters and may warrant a sub-cluster.

### Aušrinė — The Morning Star / Dawn

**Aušrinė** (Lithuanian) — the Morning Star goddess, dawn. From *aušra* (dawn). Etymology: PIE ***h₂éwsōs** (dawn). The most celebrated PIE cognate family:

- Sanskrit **Uṣas** (dawn goddess, extensively hymned in Rigveda)
- Greek **Ēōs** (dawn goddess, Aurora in Latin)
- Norse **Austrō** (east, spring; source of "Easter")
- Lithuanian **Aušrinė** — the same goddess, still named in folk tradition

Uṣas in the Rigveda is the most hymned deity after Indra and Agni — the dawn as the moment of cosmic renewal, the defeat of darkness, the opening of the sky. **The Baltic Aušrinė and the Vedic Uṣas are the same PIE dawn-goddess, her name preserved across 5,000 years in both traditions.**

Aušrinė in Baltic theology:
- Kindles the fire for Saulė each morning (she is the sun's servant and
  perhaps daughter)
- Spreads a cloth of light over the sky for Saulė to travel across
- Was the love interest of Mėnuo (hence Perkūnas's judgment)
- Is also associated with water — she washes the sky and prepares it

**YOUSPEAK observation**: Aušrinė/Uṣas/Ēōs is the clearest example in the cathedral of a deity whose name and function have been preserved in multiple living and attested traditions from PIE. She is not a convergence (independent parallel development) but a **shared inheritance** — the same personhood transmitted. The distinction between convergence and inheritance matters for forge-philosophy: a -me word built on Aušrinė would not name a cross-tradition convergence but a PIE common-root. Different theological register.

## V. Darna — Cosmic Harmony and Right Relationship

### The Concept

**Darna** (Lithuanian) — harmony, accord, right relationship. The root *derti* (to suit, to fit, to be in accord). Not merely peace or absence of conflict: *darna* names the active fit of things-in-right-relation, the way a song's parts harmonize, the way the cosmos runs rightly when its relationships are maintained.

This is the Baltic instance of the cosmic-truth-order convergence already deeply mapped in the cathedral:
- Vedic **ṛta** (cosmic right order; rtame, Specialized 7.20)
- Avestan **aša** (truth-order; ashame, Specialized 7.20)
- Egyptian **ma'at** (justice-truth-order; maatme, Core 7.50)
- Hebrew **emet** (truth-faithfulness; emetme)
- Navajo **hózhǫ́** (beauty-harmony-balance; hózhóme, pipeline)

**Darna** is the Baltic member of this family. Its distinctive features:

1. **Musical resonance**: *Darna* is deeply connected to the sutartinės   (polyphonic songs — covered in File 2). The songs *are* darna made   audible — voices fitting together in ways that create something beyond   any single voice. Cosmic harmony is not merely conceptual but performable.

2. **Social dimension**: *Darna* in folk usage names community agreement,   the right fitting of people in a village, the harmony of a household.   Unlike ṛta or aša (which are cosmic first and social second), darna   is experienced in the village before being projected onto the cosmos.

3. **Environmental dimension**: The nature festivals (Jorė/spring, Rasa/summer   solstice, Žolinė/harvest) are darna-maintenance rituals — ensuring that   human action stays in right relationship with the seasonal cycles that   Saulė, Žemyna, and Perkūnas govern.

**YOUSPEAK forge candidate**: *darname* — Harmony-as-fit-of-things-in-right-relation. Distinct from *duyuktame* (right-action-as-cosmic-maintenance, which is more action-oriented) and *hózhóme* (beauty-harmony-as-cosmic-alignment, which is more experiential). *Darna* emphasizes the structural fit, the way parts belong together — harmony as ontological correctness, not just beauty or right action. Specialized 7.40-7.60, possibly Core if poly-count warrants (Baltic IE + Sumerian -me donor = poly 7 = Specialized).

### The Sacred Festivals as Darna-Practice

The Lithuanian liturgical calendar (mostly reconstructed from folk practice):

- **Užgavėnės** (February/Shrovetide): Battle of winter and spring;  Morė (straw effigy of winter) is burned; pancakes eaten (solar symbol);
  the victory of light over darkness enacted communally
- **Jorė** (spring equinox area): First taking of cattle to pasture;  Žemyna addressed; earth kissed; garlands made
- **Rasos/Kupolė** (midsummer, St. John's Eve — Joninės): Sacred bonfire;  jumping over fire (purification); fern-flower sought at midnight (only  blooms once a year, grants wishes — a folk-narrative preserving the  idea of once-a-year sacred opening); herb-collection (Žemyna's gifts);
  **the most widely maintained pre-Christian festival in Lithuania today**
- **Žolinė** (harvest/Assumption): Grain goddesses honored; herb-wreaths
  blessed; the harvest as gift from Žemyna acknowledged
- **Vėlinės** (November): Feast of the dead; ancestors invited to eat at  the table; food left out overnight; the boundary between living and dead  thins (parallel to Aymara Todos Santos, Mexican Día de Muertos,  Bantu ancestor-feeding practices)

The Rasos/Joninės festival is particularly significant: Lithuania now celebrates it officially as a national cultural event. The sacred bonfire that leaps over the hill on Midsummer's Eve is Perkūnas's fire, darna enacted in a community gathered around light in darkness.
