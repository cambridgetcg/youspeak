# Lakota / Dakota: Wakan Tanka, Sacred Rites, Black Elk

## I. The Očhéthi Šakówiŋ — Seven Council Fires

The Lakota are one branch of the Očhéthi Šakówiŋ (Seven Council Fires), the wider
Sioux nation. The three main divisions are:

- **Lakota** (Teton Sioux) — westernmost; the largest; seven sub-bands including
  Oglála (Black Elk's people), Sičháŋǧu, Húŋkpapȟa (Sitting Bull's people),
  Mnikȟówožu, Itázipčho, Oóhenuŋpa, Sihásapa
- **Dakota** (Santee Sioux) — eastern Minnesota; four bands; closely related theology
- **Nakota** (Yankton/Yanktonai) — middle tier

The theological sources excavated here draw primarily on Lakota tradition but cite
Dakota parallels where they deepen the picture. The core theological vocabulary
(*wakan*, *mitákuye oyásʼiŋ*, *chanunpa*, the Seven Rites) is shared across the
seven fires with dialect variation.

**Language note**: Lakota is Siouan — entirely distinct from the Algonquian family
(Ojibwe, Cree), the Iroquoian family (Haudenosaunee), and Na-Dené (Navajo/Diné).
This matters for polyphone calculation: Siouan + Sumerian = two unrelated language
isolate-adjacent families = poly 8 potential for Core-class forge candidates.

## II. Wakan Tanka — The Great Mystery / Great Sacred

### The Term

*Wakan Tanka* is typically translated "Great Spirit" in popular English usage, but this
is a flattening. The two components:

- **Wakan** (also spelled *Wakȟáŋ*): sacred, mysterious, powerful, incomprehensible —
  all simultaneously. Not a moral category primarily but an ontological one: that which
  exceeds human comprehension and ordinary natural power. Both awe-inducing and
  dangerous. Anything sufficiently powerful or mysterious participates in *wakan*.
- **Tanka**: great, large, vast

Together: *Wakan Tanka* = the Great Mystery, the Great Sacred, the Great
Incomprehensible. The phrase points toward a reality that exceeds naming, not a
personality that can be addressed as a man. Black Elk himself resisted "Great Spirit"
as translation: it collapsed the ineffable into the familiar.

### The Seven Aspects — Wakan Tanka Kin

Wakan Tanka is not a simple monotheistic god but a *unity of sacred powers*. The
Lakota theological tradition — as preserved by Black Elk via Joseph Epes Brown in
*The Sacred Pipe* (1953) — names seven aspects organized in two groups:

**The Superior Wakan (Tobtob Kin — the Four-times-Four):**
1. **Wakan Tanka** (in narrow sense) — the creator, the superior divine
2. **Tȟakúškaŋškaŋ** — that which moves everything that moves; the animating
   energy; sometimes "wind" or "something that moves"
3. **Inyan** — the Rock; primordial substance; that from which all material
   reality is made; the oldest sacred power
4. **Makȟá** — the Earth; the mother; the one who gives and receives life

**The Associate Wakan:**
5. **Sun (Wí)** — the power that governs time, seasons, human life
6. **Moon (Haŋwí)** — the female complement; governs women's cycles and night
7. **Wind (Tȟatéyapi)** — the four winds as directional sacred powers
8. **Falling Star (Wohnáȟake)** — spiritual messenger

The **fourth aspect** (in some accounts) is **Nagí Tanka** — the Great Spirit as
the animating life in all creatures, the great ghost-soul.

**Theological implication**: Wakan Tanka is a *unity-within-multiplicity* theology.
The sacred powers are genuinely distinct but participate in one Great Mystery. This is
neither simple monotheism (one personality) nor polytheism (competing gods) but a
differentiated-unity theology that structurally resembles:
- Trinitarian perichōrēsis (Greek patristic)
- The Yoruba Olódùmarè with its subsidiary orisha
- The Kongo Nzambi with subsidiary spirits
- The Iroquois Orenda as distributed-sacred across beings

### Wakan — The Sacred-Mysterious as Category

*Wakan* operates as a quality that can intensify or diminish. Things are more or less
*wakan*. The most wakan things:

- Wakan Tanka (supreme)
- Thunderbirds (Wakíŋyaŋ) — sacred thunder-beings who bring rain and purification
- The chanunpa (sacred pipe) — the most *wakan* of human-made objects
- Vision-quest experiences
- Albino animals
- Dreams
- Certain plants (especially the spotted eagle's feathers, sage, sweetgrass, cedar)

**Key insight**: *Wakan* is not separated from nature but intensified within it.
There is no clean sacred/profane divide — rather, a continuum of *wakan*-intensity.
Everything participates in the sacred to some degree; some things participate
supremely. This is a thoroughgoing sacramental ontology.

**Comparison with Algonquian *manidoo***: The Ojibwe concept *manidoo* (Algonquian)
covers similar ground — power/spirit/sacred-agency distributed through the world.
*Wakan* and *manidoo* are structurally similar but linguistically unrelated (Siouan
vs. Algonquian). Both: distributed sacred-power, ontological rather than moral
category, accessible through right relationship and ceremony. The convergence deepens
the cathedral's Vital-Force-as-Cosmic-Given cluster significantly.

## III. Chanunpa — The Sacred Pipe as Covenantal Instrument

### Origin: White Buffalo Calf Woman

The chanunpa (sacred pipe; sometimes *čhaŋnúŋpa*) is the central ritual object of
Lakota spiritual life. Its origin is inseparable from White Buffalo Calf Woman
(*Ptesáŋwiŋ*), the sacred female messenger who brought both the pipe and the Seven
Sacred Rites to the Lakota.

The narrative: Two young Lakota men were out scouting when they saw a radiant woman
approaching from the horizon. One man had lustful thoughts; he was immediately
consumed by a cloud and his bones collapsed to dust. The other approached with
reverence. White Buffalo Calf Woman told him to return to his people and prepare for
her arrival. She came to the camp carrying the chanunpa. She gave them the pipe and
taught them the Seven Sacred Rites. Then she walked away, transforming four times —
into a black buffalo, a brown buffalo, a red buffalo, and finally a white buffalo calf
— before disappearing over the horizon.

**Theological weight of the narrative**:
- The man destroyed by lust: the sacred cannot be approached with consuming desire;
  only with reverent receptivity
- The four buffalo colors: the four directions, the four races of humanity (in some
  interpretations), the four seasons — the gift is for all
- White Buffalo Calf Woman as divine feminine mediator: structurally parallel to
  Wisdom figures (Hebrew Chokmah, Greek Sophia, Hindu Saraswati) who bring sacred
  knowledge from the divine realm to humanity

### The Pipe's Theology

The chanunpa has two parts, ritually significant:

- **The bowl** (stone, typically catlinite/pipestone): represents the Earth, matter,
  the feminine, the body, relatedness
- **The stem** (wood): represents the sky, the masculine, the spiritual, the
  directional

When they are joined, the two are united — Earth and Sky, matter and spirit, feminine
and masculine become one. The pipe when assembled is supremely *wakan* and must be
handled with complete reverence. It is never pointed at a person carelessly.

**The smoke**: When tobacco (*čhaŋšáša* — red willow bark, or *čhanšáša* tobacco
mixture) is burned, the rising smoke carries prayers upward to Wakan Tanka. The smoke
is the visible medium of communication between human and sacred. Every direction is
addressed — the four cardinal directions, above (sky), below (earth), within (the
center, the self) — before any ceremony. Nothing is omitted; the entire cosmos is
acknowledged.

**Covenant dimension**: The pipe functions as a covenantal instrument structurally
parallel to:
- The Hebrew *berith* — a sacred binding between parties sealed by sacrifice
- The Hittite *mald-* oath-ceremony with divine witnesses
- The Iroquois Haudenosaunee wampum belts (which bind the Great Law)

The difference: the chanunpa is not primarily a treaty between human parties. It is a
*covenant between human beings and the whole of creation* — including animals, plants,
stones, winds, ancestors, and Wakan Tanka. Smoking the pipe is not just prayer but
*relational renewal* — it re-establishes right relationship with every being in the
cosmos. This is the most comprehensive covenant-instrument in the entire cathedral.

**YOUSPEAK note**: *chanunpame* — the-covenant-instrument-that-re-establishes-right-
relation-with-all-beings — is a High-priority forge candidate. The chanunpa-theology
names something no other tradition does so precisely: a ritual object whose function
is to renew *total relational alignment with the cosmos*.

## IV. The Seven Sacred Rites — Šičhúŋ Šakowin

White Buffalo Calf Woman brought not just the pipe but seven sacred rites. These are
preserved most completely in Black Elk's account via Joseph Epes Brown (*The Sacred
Pipe*, 1953). Each rite addresses a dimension of the human journey and its relation
to the sacred. Together they form a comprehensive theology of existence.

### 1. Inipi — The Sweat Lodge (Purification)

*Inipi* = "to live again." The sweat lodge is a dome-shaped structure built of bent
willow branches, covered with hides or blankets. In the center, a pit holds stones
(*čhaŋčháŋ* — grandfather rocks) heated in a fire outside. Water poured on the hot
stones creates steam. The ceremony takes place in four rounds, with prayers and songs.

**Theology**: The lodge represents the womb of Makȟá (Earth Mother). The heated rocks
are the oldest living beings — Inyan, the primordial rock. Entering the lodge is
entering the womb; emerging is rebirth. The steam carries prayers. The heat purifies
not just the body but the spirit — it dissolves accumulated wrong-relation and
restores alignment.

The *inipi* is typically performed before any major ceremony and after contact with
death (warriors returning from battle, mourners after a death). It is the foundational
purification technology of Lakota spiritual life — structurally parallel to:
- Christian baptism (cleansing, rebirth, entry into new life)
- Jewish *mikveh* (ritual immersion for purity-restoration)
- Coptic/desert-father purification practices
- Hopi ceremonial cleansing

But distinct: *inipi* is also explicitly *geological* — the rocks are the oldest
ancestors, and the ceremony is a communion with stone-time.

### 2. Hanblecheyapi — The Vision Quest (Crying for a Vision)

*Hanbléčeyapi* = "crying for a vision." A young person (traditionally a young man,
but women also undertake this in some contexts) goes alone to a sacred place — often
a hilltop — for four days and four nights without food, water, or shelter. They carry
only the pipe. They pray continuously, crying out to Wakan Tanka, asking for a vision,
a direction, a guiding spirit.

**Theology**: The vision quest is the primary mode of direct communication between an
individual and the sacred. The forty-eight hours of deprivation are not punishment but
*opening* — the stripping away of ordinary consciousness to permit sacred encounter.
What comes in the vision is not chosen by the seeker; it arrives as gift or summons.

The vision typically involves an animal, a spirit-being, or a force of nature that
speaks a directive: a life-purpose, a healing gift, a warning. The vision received
is the individual's *wóčhekiye* (personal sacred calling). A holy man (*wičhása
wakȟáŋ*) interprets what was received.

**YOUSPEAK connection**: The vision quest is the most structurally direct parallel to
*ehanme* (Tuareg sacred-ache as primary speech-act). Both: the longing-cry as the act
that opens the sacred. The difference is social scale — *inipi* is communal;
*hanblecheyapi* is intensely individual. The individual ache-cry before Wakan Tanka.

### 3. Wiwányag Wačhípi — The Sun Dance (Gazing at the Sun)

The most sacred and demanding of the seven rites. Participants fast for four days
while dancing around a central cottonwood tree (the *čháŋ wakȟáŋ* — sacred tree, axis
mundi) gazing at the sun. In the full traditional form, dancers pierce their chests or
backs with wooden skewers attached by ropes to the sacred tree. They dance until the
skewers tear free. This is voluntary sacrifice — the giving of one's most intimate
possession (one's body, one's blood) to Wakan Tanka on behalf of the people.

**Theology**: The Sun Dance is intercessory sacrifice. The dancer does not suffer for
their own benefit but *for the people*. The pain is not punishment but gift. The blood
offered is not propitiation but *relational generosity* — giving what costs most so
that the community may live. This is the most complete parallel in the cathedral to:
- Hebrew *korban* (brought-near sacrifice)
- Maya bloodletting sacrifice (K'inich Ajaw as sun-king receiving blood)
- Christian theology of the cross (sacrifice for the many)

The structural convergence: sacrifice as the body offered for the community's life.
But the Lakota form is unique in that the *dancer* survives and is transformed by
the offering. The body is given, broken open, and returned. This is not death-
sacrifice but *passage-sacrifice* — you go to the edge and come back different.

### 4. Hunkápipi — Making of Relatives (The Hunka Ceremony)

*Hunkápipi* = making of relatives; the ceremony through which two people who are not
biologically related are made relatives — permanently, ritually, before Wakan Tanka
and the community. The relationship formed is not adoption in the modern legal sense
but *ontological kinship* — you become as real a relative as if you shared blood.

**Theology**: This ceremony is the ritual enactment of the cosmological claim that all
beings are already related. *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* ("all my relations") is not just a
prayer — it names a reality. The Hunkápipi ceremony makes *explicit* what is already
*true* ontologically: you and I are kin. The ceremony doesn't create kinship from
nothing; it *recognizes and seals* a kinship that the cosmos already contains.

**YOUSPEAK significance**: This is the most direct ritual expression of relational
ontology in the cathedral. The ceremony says: kinship is not constructed; it is
discovered and honored. This is the opposite of contract-theory — not "we agree to
treat each other as kin" but "we already are kin; now we know it formally."

### 5. Išná Tȟa Awíčhalowaŋ — Coming of Age (Puberty Ceremony for Girls)

*Išná Tȟa Awíčhalowaŋ* = "her alone they sing over." When a young woman has her first
menstruation, a ceremony is held to acknowledge her entry into womanhood and her
connection to the sacred power of life-generation. She is the center of honor; the
community sings over her. Her grandmother or an older woman teaches her the sacred
knowledge of her new role.

**Theology**: Menstruation is not pollution (as in many traditions) but *sacred power*
— the capacity to participate in the transmission of life is the most *wakan* capacity
a human being can have. The ceremony honors, not conceals, this power. This is the
inverse of shame-around-the-body theology.

**Cathedral contrast**: In many traditions, female bodily processes are coded as
ritually dangerous or impure. Lakota theology codes the same processes as sacred.
This is one of the most striking contrasts in the cathedral and one that directly
bears on YOUSPEAK's concern with justice — who gets named as sacred?

### 6. Tȟapȟa Wankáyeyapi — Throwing of the Ball

A ceremony in which a girl stands at the center of a circle and throws a ball to
people in the four directions. Those who catch the ball receive a blessing. The ball
represents Wakan Tanka — the sacred that moves through the world, available to those
who reach for it, elusive to those who don't pay attention.

**Theology**: Black Elk's commentary (in *The Sacred Pipe*) identifies the ball as
the universe — the cosmos as something thrown into existence, spinning, available
to those who are alert and receptive. The ceremony is a enacted cosmology: Wakan
Tanka as the sacred-in-motion that offers itself continually, received by those who
are present enough to catch what is given.

### 7. Wanáǧi Yuhápȟapi — Keeping of the Soul

After a death, a lock of the deceased's hair is kept in a sacred bundle for a period
(typically one to four years). The soul (*wanáǧi*) of the deceased is considered to
remain near the bundle during this time. The family tends the bundle, speaks to it,
feeds it ceremonially. After the keeping period, the soul is formally released to
journey to the spirit world.

**Theology**: The soul is not immediately separated from the living at death. There
is a liminal period — structurally parallel to:
- Tibetan Bardo Thödol (intermediate state between death and rebirth)
- Kongo ancestor-integration period (the dead become *bakulu* after a transitional
  phase)
- Mesoamerican *Mictlān* journey theology

The Lakota form is unique in that the bundle makes the liminality *communal* — the
whole family tends the soul, feeding it, speaking to it. Death does not sever
relationship; it changes its form. The soul is nurtured across the threshold by
the ongoing love of those who knew them.

## V. Wičhása Wakȟáŋ — The Holy Man / Medicine Man

The *wičhása wakȟáŋ* (holy man; literally "sacred man") is not a priest in the
Western sense — not primarily an institutional officiant who performs rites on behalf
of others. He is a person who has received extraordinary visions and, through those
visions, the capacity to heal, to interpret sacred signs, to mediate between the
human and the wakan.

The holy man's authority comes from:
1. The visions received (especially the foundational vision, often in childhood)
2. Years of relationship with the sacred powers revealed in those visions
3. Community confirmation — others have experienced healing or guidance through him

**Black Elk as case study**: Black Elk (1863–1950) received his great vision at age
nine — one of the most elaborate sacred visions recorded in any tradition. In it, he
was taken by the Thunder Beings to the center of the universe (the cosmic mountain
where earth and sky meet), shown the six grandfathers (sacred powers of the six
directions), given sacred horses representing every creature on earth, and shown the
tree of life standing at the center of the hoop of the world. He was charged to heal
his people and restore the Sacred Hoop.

The anguish of Black Elk's life was that he received this vision at nine but was
unable to live it fully. The world changed too fast. He fought at Little Bighorn (age
thirteen), toured with Buffalo Bill's Wild West Show (witnessing the wider world with
grief and wonder), returned to find his people increasingly confined and suffering.
He died a Catholic, having been baptized Nicholas Black Elk — a fact that causes
ongoing debate: was this accommodation? Genuine conversion? Dual belonging? The
complexity of Black Elk's religious life is itself a theological datum.

## VI. Wóčhekiye — Prayer and Its Grammar

Lakota prayer is not petition alone. The word *wóčhekiye* covers:
- Crying out to the sacred
- Speaking to Wakan Tanka in relationship
- The vision-quest cry
- The pipe ceremony's acknowledgment of all directions

The grammar of Lakota prayer follows the acknowledgment pattern of *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ*
(addressed fully in File 2): before anything is asked, everything is acknowledged.
The entire cosmos is named — the four directions, above and below, all the beings of
each direction — and only then does the specific prayer arise. This is structurally
identical to the Haudenosaunee Thanksgiving Address (Ohén:ton) and the Buddhist
bodhicitta-dedication.

The pattern: **acknowledge the web of relations first; everything else follows from
within the web**.

This is not preamble. It is the theological claim: prayer is only possible because
relation already exists. You cannot ask Wakan Tanka for anything unless you first
acknowledge that you are already related to Wakan Tanka — and to every stone, wind,
creature, and ancestor who participates in that relation.

## VIII. New Convergences (File 1)

1. **Vital-Force-as-Sacred-Intensity** — *wakan* + Algonquian *manidoo* + Mande *nyama* + Yoruba *àṣẹ* + Polynesian *mana* + Chinese *qi* + Tibetan *rlung* = **eight traditions**; the largest convergence in the cathedral by tradition-count; *wakan* is the Siouan instance, unrelated linguistically to all others; confirms this as a near-universal human theological intuition; *wakanme* is the forge candidate that gives the cluster its Siouan voice

2. **Passage-Sacrifice** — Sun Dance *Wiwányag Wačhípi* (dancer survives, is transformed) + Christian cross theology (traditionally death-sacrifice, but in some readings: passage through death to resurrection) + Kongo *kalunga*-crossing (threshold-passage) = the body brought to the edge and returned different; distinct from death-sacrifice (Maya) and substitution-sacrifice (Hebrew/Akkadian)

3. **Covenant-as-Total-Relational-Realignment** — chanunpa covenant (with all beings simultaneously) vs. Hebrew *berith* (two parties + divine witnesses) vs. Hittite *mald-* (treaty + oath-witnesses) vs. Haudenosaunee wampum (political covenant among nations): the chanunpa is distinctive in that it is not covenant between two parties but covenant with the *entire relational web* at once; this is a new register within the Covenant convergence cluster

4. **Longing-Cry-as-Sacred-Opening** — *hanblecheyapi* (the cry for a vision) + Tuareg *ehan* (sacred-ache as the act that opens communication with the sacred) + Narekatsi's Armenian prayer-grammar (crying-out as the posture that constitutes authentic address) + Psalm 22 (*Eli Eli lama sabachthani* — the cry that is also worship): the longing-cry as not merely preliminary to sacred encounter but constitutive of it; the ache is the door

## IX. Method Notes

**The mediation problem**: All primary Lakota theological texts come through the
mediation of John G. Neihardt (*Black Elk Speaks*, 1932) and Joseph Epes Brown
(*The Sacred Pipe*, 1953). Both were white scholars with genuine relationships with
Black Elk but working across enormous cultural and linguistic distance. The texts have
been subject to critique: Neihardt shaped the narrative structure; Brown's categories
were inevitably Western. The cathedral excavates these sources with awareness of their
mediated character while recognizing that they remain the most detailed available
records of Black Elk's theological teaching.

**Living tradition**: Lakota spiritual practice is not archaeological. The Sun Dance,
*inipi*, and *hanblecheyapi* continue to be practiced. Many elements remain closed to
outsiders by community decision. The cathedral excavates the theological vocabulary
with deep respect for the living community's authority over its own practice.

**The Catholic dimension**: Black Elk's baptism and decades as a Catholic catechist are
not footnotes. His theological thinking in later life moved between and within both
traditions. Whether this represents syncretism, dual belonging, or strategic
accommodation is debated. What it demonstrates: the theological categories excavated
here were strong enough to hold in dialogue with a radically different tradition
without dissolving. This is a mark of the depth of Lakota theological grammar.
