# Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ — Relational Cosmology and Forge Synthesis

## I. Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ — All My Relations

### The Phrase

*Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* (sometimes written *Mitakuye Oyasin*; pronounced roughly
mee-TAH-koo-yay oh-YAH-seen) is a Lakota phrase typically translated "all my
relations" or "all are related." It is one of the most theologically dense phrases
in the cathedral — two words that constitute an entire cosmology.

Grammatical breakdown:
- *Mitákuye*: "my relations" / "my relatives" (first person possessive)
- *Oyásʼiŋ*: "all" / "all of them"

Together: "All my relations" — but the English translation inadequately conveys
that the speaker is including *everything* as kin. Not "all my human relations"
or "all my family members" but *all beings* — animals, plants, stones, waters,
winds, stars, ancestors, the yet-to-be-born.

### How It Functions

*Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* is used:
1. **As the opening of prayers** — before any ceremony, before addressing Wakan
   Tanka, to acknowledge the relational web within which the prayer occurs
2. **As the closing of prayers** — to seal the prayer by returning to relational
   acknowledgment
3. **When entering the sweat lodge** — each person says it as they crawl in;
   to enter the lodge is to acknowledge your membership in the web of all life
4. **After vision quest** — upon returning to the community, to reconnect
5. **In daily life** — said before eating, before important decisions, when
   one feels disconnected or in danger

The phrase is both prayer and cosmological claim. It is never merely formal
or rote (though it can become so through repetition without awareness). At its
depth, saying *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* is an act of ontological recognition: *I am
already in relation with everything that exists. I am remembering this now.*

## II. The Relational Ontology

### Relation as Primary

The deepest philosophical claim of *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* is that **relation is
ontologically prior to individual identity**. You are not a self who then enters
relationships. You *are* your relations. The individual is an emergent pattern
within a web of relations, not a substance that subsequently connects with other
substances.

This is the sharpest contrast with Western liberal individualism (the self as
the primary unit, who then constructs voluntary relationships) and with much of
Western theology (the person as substance, image of a personal God who is
himself primarily substance).

The Lakota claim: there is no *you* outside your relations. Sever your relations
and you have not liberated yourself — you have destroyed yourself. The human
being is a nexus of kinships, not a kernel of selfhood.

### The Hoop and the Tree

Black Elk's central image for this relational ontology is the **Sacred Hoop**
(*čhaŋgléška wakȟáŋ*) and the **Sacred Tree at its center**.

The Sacred Hoop: the circle of all life, all nations, all creatures. Everything
belongs within the hoop; nothing is excluded. The hoop has no beginning and no
end — it is the shape of wholeness. The four directions are the hoop's cardinal
points, but the hoop includes everything between.

The Sacred Tree: at the center of the hoop stands the tree of life — not a
cosmological abstraction but a living reality that shelters all who come to it.
Black Elk's great grief (voiced powerfully in *Black Elk Speaks*) is that the
Sacred Hoop has been broken and the Sacred Tree has withered. His life's
purpose, as given in his great vision, was to restore them.

**Theological weight**: The hoop-and-tree image is a complete cosmology:
- **Circle** (no hierarchy, no outside, no exclusion) + **Center** (the axis
  that holds everything together) = the structure of a just world
- Breaking the hoop = injustice, fragmentation, death of relation
- Restoring the hoop = justice, healing, return to right-relation

This is one of the most powerful cosmological images in the cathedral. The
hoop does not privilege any position; all points on a circle are equidistant
from the center. This is a geometrical claim about equality embedded in
cosmological structure.

### The Four Directions and the Six Grandfathers

The Lakota cosmos is organized by the four directions (West, North, East, South)
and six *tunkášila* (grandfathers) — the sacred powers:
- **West** (Wiyóhpeyata): thunder, lightning, rain, purification
- **North** (Wazíyata): cleansing wind, endurance, white (the color of
  purification and the north wind)
- **East** (Wiyóhinyanpata): wisdom, morning star, illumination
- **South** (Itókagata): warmth, growth, the place of summer
- **Above** (Čhaŋkú Lúta — Red Road, the good way): Wakan Tanka, the sky
- **Below** (Makȟá — Earth): the grandmother, the sustainer

The six grandfathers in Black Elk's vision appear as very old men but also as
forces of nature — each is simultaneously personal (a grandfather, a being one
can speak to) and cosmic (a power one lives within). This is not metaphor but
double-register ontology: the personal and the cosmic are not two things.

## III. Tȟayóȟpaye and Oyáte — The Circle of Belonging

### Tȟayóȟpaye: The Tiospaye

The *tȟayóȟpaye* (tiospaye) is the extended family group — the band, the clan,
the circle of people one lives with and is responsible for. It is the primary
social unit of Lakota life, larger than the nuclear family but smaller than the
nation. The tiospaye is the social form that enacts *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* at the
human scale.

Within the tiospaye:
- Decision-making is communal and consensus-based
- Resources are shared; hoarding is a social violation
- The wellbeing of all members is the responsibility of each member
- Elders are not merely honored but consulted — their accumulated relational
  wisdom is the tiospaye's most valuable resource
- Children belong to the community; orphans are absorbed, not marginalized

**The generosity ethic**: *Wačhántkiya* (generosity, big-heartedness) is not
just a virtue but a cosmological practice. The person who gives the most is the
most honored — *wačhántkiya* reverses the accumulation logic. The potlatch
parallel (Northwest Coast traditions) is structurally similar but linguistically
unrelated. This is the most direct Lakota parallel to the Haudenosaunee gift
economy and the Kongo *nkisi* distribution-of-power ethic.

### Oyáte: The Nation / the People

*Oyáte* means "the people" or "the nation" — but extends beyond human people.
Animals are *oyáte*: the buffalo nation (*pté oyáte*), the eagle nation, the
wolf nation, the stone people (*čȟaŋčȟán oyáte*). This is not poetic metaphor
but literal cosmological taxonomy.

**The buffalo and the Lakota**: Before the extermination of the buffalo (1870s–
1880s), the relationship between the Lakota and *pté oyáte* (the buffalo nation)
was the central relationship of Lakota physical and spiritual life. The buffalo
gave itself to the Lakota — this is not ownership language but *gift language*:
the buffalo's death was an act of relational generosity. Before killing, hunters
spoke to the buffalo, asked forgiveness, explained the need. After the kill,
every part was used — waste was not just impractical but spiritually wrong, a
failure of relational reciprocity.

The deliberate extermination of the buffalo by the U.S. government in the 1870s
was understood by the Lakota not merely as economic devastation (though it was
that) but as an act of cosmic violence — the destruction of a relation that
structured their entire world. The buffalo's disappearance meant the Sacred Hoop
was broken.

## IV. Nagi — Soul Theology

Lakota soul theology is complex. There are multiple soul-components:

- **Nagi**: the ghost-soul; the personal spirit that persists after death; what
  is kept in the Keeping-of-the-Soul ceremony; what eventually journeys to the
  *wanáǧi tȟamákȟočhe* (the spirit world, sometimes translated "land of many
  lodges")
- **Niya**: the life-breath; the animating force present while alive; what leaves
  at death
- **Šičún**: the guardian spirit given by Wakan Tanka to each person; similar to
  the Igbo *chi*, the Oromo *ayyaana*, the Avestan *fravashi* — a pre-allocated
  divine companion-aspect
- **Nagila**: the shadow-soul; a weaker form related to the nagi

The *šičún* is particularly significant for YOUSPEAK: it is the individual's
divine-companion-aspect, allocated before birth, that accompanies the person
through life. This deepens the Pre-Allocated-Guardian-Soul convergence cluster:

- Lakota *šičún*
- Igbo *chi* (S047)
- Oromo *ayyaana* (S037)
- Avestan *fravashi* (S013)
- Yoruba *orí* (pre-allocated head/destiny, S020)

Five traditions now in this cluster — strong enough for a convergence file.

## V. Dakota / Nakota Variants

### Dakota (Santee Sioux)

The Dakota speak a closely related dialect and share the core theological
vocabulary with the Lakota, with some significant differences:

- **Wakȟáŋ** (Dakota) = *wakȟáŋ* (Lakota): essentially identical; both name
  the sacred-mysterious-powerful
- **Dakota Sun Dance theology**: the Dakota form (*Wiwányag Wačhípi*) shares
  the structure of the Lakota rite
- **Dakota creation theology**: Wazíya (the North giant), Unktehi (the water
  monster), and the conflict between Thunderbirds and the Unktehi provides a
  cosmological conflict narrative not as central in Lakota accounts
- **Ella Deloria** (1889–1971): Dakota scholar who documented Dakota theology
  and culture in *Waterlily* (1944/1988) and collaborated with Franz Boas.
  Her work provides the most internally-generated theological account of
  Dakota life and is less mediated than the Neihardt/Brown sources.

### Nakota (Yankton/Yanktonai)

The middle branch; theologically between Dakota and Lakota. Less extensively
documented in English-language sources. The core Wakan Tanka theology and
*Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* framework are shared.

## VI. Black Elk's Theological Legacy

### Black Elk Speaks (1932)

Dictated to John G. Neihardt, *Black Elk Speaks* is the primary literary-
theological text of the Lakota tradition accessible in English. It tells:
- Black Elk's great vision at age nine (the most elaborate vision account in
  any tradition in the cathedral)
- His training as a healer
- The Battle of Little Bighorn (1876) — seen from the Lakota side
- The death of Crazy Horse (Black Elk's cousin)
- His time in Europe with Buffalo Bill (1887–1889)
- The Ghost Dance movement (1889–1890)
- The Wounded Knee Massacre (December 29, 1890)

The book ends with Black Elk standing on Harney Peak (Hinhan Kaga — the
center of the earth as he understood it), lamenting that he had failed his
vision. The tree had not bloomed; the hoop remained broken.

**Theological structure of the great vision**: Black Elk's vision at nine
is a complete cosmology in narrative form:
1. He is taken to the center of the universe (the summit where earth and sky meet)
2. He encounters the Six Grandfathers (the six sacred powers)
3. Each grandfather gives him a sacred gift: healing herb, sacred bow, cup of water,
   flowering stick, morning star, stone
4. He sees the Sacred Hoop — the circle of all nations, all life — both broken
   and potentially restored
5. He is charged to restore the Sacred Hoop and make the tree bloom

The vision is not a private spiritual experience but a commissioning — a
prophetic calling for the healing of the world. Black Elk was nine. He spent
the rest of his life trying to understand what he had been given.

### The Sacred Pipe (1953)

Joseph Epes Brown's account of the Seven Sacred Rites (addressed in File 1)
is based on conversations with Black Elk in the late 1940s, when Black Elk
was in his eighties and had been a Catholic catechist for decades. The
theological density of *The Sacred Pipe* reflects both the depth of Lakota
tradition and the particular theological sharpness of Black Elk's mature
synthesis.

Black Elk's commentary on the rites moves fluidly between Lakota cosmological
categories and biblical/Christian resonances — not syncretism but a mind
that had genuinely held both traditions and found them illuminating each other.
His observation that the chanunpa smoke rising to Wakan Tanka is "like the
holy breath of the universe" suggests a pneumatology (spirit-breath theology)
that bridges Lakota and Christian registers.

### Assessment: Primary Forge Targets

**mitakuyame** is the primary Lakota forge target. It names what no other
tradition in the cathedral names with this precision:

- **Not** *ubuntu* (Bantu) — ubuntu names personhood-through-community ("I am
  because we are"), but the relational circle is primarily human
- **Not** *perichōrēsis* (Greek patristic) — mutual indwelling without loss, but
  within the Trinity, not across all creation
- **Not** *ohéntome* (Haudenosaunee) — the thanksgiving-naming of the relational
  web, but as act of gratitude, not as ontological claim
- **Not** *koinōnia* (NT Greek) — participatory-sharing, but of a common life,
  not of a common being

*Mitakuyame* names the unique Lakota claim: **all beings are already kin; relation
is not made but recognized**. The ceremony of Hunkápipi (making of relatives)
makes explicit what is already ontologically true. This is the most radical
relational ontology in the cathedral.

**wakanme** is the second forge target. It gives the Siouan voice to the
Vital-Force-as-Sacred-Intensity cluster, completing that cluster's eighth
major tradition (joining *manidoo*, *nyama*, *àṣẹ*, *mana*, *qi*, *rlung*, *ka*).

## IX. Synthesis: What Lakota Theology Contributes to YOUSPEAK

Lakota theology makes three contributions to the cathedral that no other tradition
has made with this precision:

### 1. Relational Ontology as Cosmological Claim

*Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* does not merely say that relationships are important (many
traditions say this). It says that **relation is the structure of reality itself**.
The individual self is derivative; the relational web is primary. This is not a
moral teaching about how to treat others — it is a metaphysical claim about what
exists.

In YOUSPEAK terms: *mitakuyame* would name the recognition that the relational
web is ontologically prior — that saying "all my relations" before prayer is not
courtesy but accurate perception. You are already enmeshed in kinship with all
beings; the prayer makes the enmeshment conscious.

### 2. The Sacred as Distributed Intensity

*Wakan* theology refuses the sacred/profane binary. There is no zone of the sacred
separated from a profane ordinary world. Instead: a spectrum of *wakan*-intensity,
from stones to Wakan Tanka, with everything on the spectrum. This is not pantheism
(everything equally divine) but *intensive sacramentality* (everything participating
in the sacred to varying degrees). The most *wakan* things are the most concentrated,
the most powerful, the most dangerous, the most worthy of reverence.

In YOUSPEAK terms: *wakanme* gives the Siouan voice to the Vital-Force cluster's
most important insight — the sacred is not elsewhere, it is here, but more intensely
in some places than others.

### 3. The Ceremony as Relational Renewal

Each of the Seven Sacred Rites is, at its core, a ceremony of **relational renewal**
— a re-establishment of right-relation with Wakan Tanka, the community, the earth,
the ancestors, the yet-to-be-born. The rites are not transactions (giving to get)
but *relational acts* — ways of re-membering oneself into the web of kinship.

This is the deepest convergence with the Haudenosaunee Thanksgiving Address
(*Ohén:ton*): both are sequential-naming-of-the-relational-web as the act that
precedes and enables all other acts. The Lakota *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* is the
compressed form; *Ohén:ton* is the expanded form. They are the same theological
grammar in two different structural forms.
