# Old Persian — Behistun and the Achaemenid Royal Theology

_Old Persian is not a priestly language. Unlike Avestan — which was the language of the Zoroastrian priestly tradition, preserved in liturgical memory and used in ritual by the maga/magi — Old Persian was the language of royal proclamation: carved into cliff faces, inscribed on palace foundations, stamped on administrative tablets. Where Avestan theology is ecstatic, prophetic, cosmic (the Gathas are Zarathustra's direct addresses to Ahura Mazda), Old Persian theology is imperial, legitimating, judicial. The same divine name — Ahuramazda — appears in both registers, but the register-shift is total._

_The relationship between the two is the central question of Old Persian theology: is the Achaemenid royal theology a form of Zoroastrianism? An adaptation of it? A parallel development from shared Proto-Iranian roots? The question has not been fully resolved. What is clear: the kings used Ahuramazda's name to legitimate their power, the magi functioned as religious specialists across the empire, and the Achaemenid period was the moment when Iranian religion became a world-historical force — shaping Second Temple Judaism, interacting with Babylonian theology, and presenting Greek observers with a form of monotheism they found simultaneously strange and compelling._

## I. The Behistun Inscription — Theology as Imperial Autobiography

### The Physical Context

The Behistun Inscription (Old Persian: *Bagastāna* — "place of the gods/divine-powers") is carved into a cliff face 100 meters above a royal road in western Iran, commissioned by Darius I around 520 BCE. It is written simultaneously in three languages: Old Persian (cuneiform), Elamite (cuneiform), and Babylonian Akkadian (cuneiform). The trilingual format is not redundancy — it is an imperial statement: this message reaches every part of the empire.

The inscription narrates Darius's seizure of power after the crisis following Cambyses' death (522 BCE): the impostor Gaumāta (who claimed to be Smerdis/Bardiya, Cambyses' brother), Darius's killing of Gaumāta, and his suppression of nineteen rebellions in one year. It is simultaneously an autobiography, a political manifesto, and a theological document.

### The Ahuramazda-Grants-Kingship Formula

The opening of Behistun establishes the theological framework that governs every Old Persian royal inscription:

> *Adam Dārayavauš xšāyaθiya vazraka, xšāyaθiya xšāyaθiyanām... vašnā Auramazdāha adam xšāyaθiya amiy.*

"I am Darius the Great King, King of Kings... by the will/favor of Ahuramazda I am king."

The formula *vašnā Auramazdāha* — "by the will/favor of Ahuramazda" — appears in virtually every significant Old Persian inscription. It is the theological load-bearing phrase of Achaemenid royal ideology.

**What the formula does**:
1. **Establishes divine authorization**: The king does not rule by hereditary right alone, nor by military conquest alone, but by Ahuramazda's explicit grant.
2. **Creates a accountability structure**: If the king rules by Ahuramazda's *vašnā* (will, favor — from PIE root related to *wish*, *want*), then the king is obligated to Ahuramazda. The grant is conditional.
3. **Explains resistance as theological crime**: Those who rebel against the king rebel against Ahuramazda's arrangement. This is draugha (Lie/Cosmic-Disorder) expressed in political form.

**Comparison with other divine-kingship theologies**:
- **Egyptian**: Pharaoh IS the son of Rē, IS Horus; the identification is ontological, not merely functional.
- **Mesopotamian/Akkadian**: The king is *šar kiššati* (king of the universe), appointed by Marduk; the appointment is through the temple system and the *en* (high priest).
- **Achaemenid**: The king is *not* divine in himself — he is a human king who has received Ahuramazda's favor. The king serves Ahuramazda; he is not identified with him. **This is the most significant theological difference.**

**Forge candidate: *vašnāme*** — the divine-favor-that-constitutes-authority; the grant of kingship/power by the supreme divine will; legitimate authority as received-not-claimed. Old Persian *vašnā* ("by the will/favor of") is structurally parallel to: Hebrew *berak* (blessing as divine empowerment), Mesopotamian *šimtu* (divinely-assigned fate/portion), Chinese *tianming* (天命, Mandate of Heaven). NEW convergence: **Divine-Mandate-as-Condition-of-Legitimate-Authority** — Old Persian + Chinese *tianming* + Hebrew *mashiaḥ* (anointed = divinely-appointed) + Mesopotamian divine-appointment. 4+ traditions. **Forge candidate: Core 7.45–7.65.** This names something architecturally fundamental: authority is not self-generated but received, and the reception creates both the power and the accountability.

### The Draugha-Rebellion Equation

Throughout Behistun, Darius consistently frames the rebels as followers of *drauga* (Old Persian form of Avestan *druj*) — the Lie, the Cosmic Disorder. The rebellion against his kingship is not merely political treachery; it is participation in the cosmic force opposed to Ahuramazda's order (*arta*/*aša*).

> *Pasāva mām Auramazdā upastām abara... hamaranam akunavam. Vašnā Auramazdāha adam hauv martiya agarbāyam... gauθā drauga āha.*

"Then Ahuramazda brought me aid... I fought the battle. By Ahuramazda's favor I seized that man... he was full of draugha, a deceiver."

The political vocabulary: *drauga* (liar, deceiver, rebel), *draujana* (those who follow the Lie), *haumavargiš* (the rebel's followers). These are theological categories applied to political actors.

**Theological significance**: The Achaemenid inscriptions perform the most explicit fusion of **cosmic** and **political** categories in the ancient world. The Lie (*draugha*) is simultaneously: the cosmic force opposed to Ahuramazda, the quality of false speech (perjury, deception), and the quality of political rebellion. To lie, to rebel, to participate in cosmic disorder — these are the same act at different levels of reality.

This is not metaphor. Darius appears to genuinely understand his role as the cosmic-political representative of *arta*: by suppressing rebellion, he is doing Ahuramazda's work of maintaining cosmic order against the encroachment of *draugha*.

**YOUSPEAK resonance**: This deepens the *asha/druj* polarity from the Avestan excavation. In Avestan, the polarity is cosmic-ethical (right-order vs. lie). In Old Persian, it becomes explicitly political: the king's governing function is the expression of *arta* in the political realm. This is the **theopolitical application of the Zoroastrian cosmic polarity** — and it created the framework through which the Persian Empire understood its own legitimacy for 200 years.

## II. The King of Kings — Theology of Imperial Hierarchy

### *Xšāyaθiya Xšāyaθiyanām*

The Achaemenid royal title *xšāyaθiya xšāyaθiyanām* — "King of Kings" — is the theological center of the imperial self-understanding. Not merely "greatest king" (which would be comparative) but "king whose kingship encompasses all other kingships" — the king at the apex of a hierarchy of legitimate authorities.

**Etymology**: *xšāyaθiya* from PIE *\*ksayati* (he rules, he has power) — related to Sanskrit *kṣāti* (rules) and Greek *ktaomai* (possess). The "king of kings" formulation appears independently in:
- Babylonian tradition: *šar šarrāni* (king of kings — Assyrian title)
- Revelation 17:14 and 19:16: Christ as "King of kings and Lord of lords" (using the Achaemenid title for the eschatological sovereign)
- The Ethiopian imperial title *Negusa Nagast* (King of Kings — still used by Haile Selassie)

**The theological structure**: The "king of kings" title is not mere boasting. It encodes a specific political theology: legitimate authority exists at multiple levels (local kings, regional governors, tributary rulers), but all legitimate authority derives ultimately from the supreme king, who in turn derives his authority from Ahuramazda. The hierarchy is: Ahuramazda → King of Kings → vassal kings → governors → subjects. It is a **descending hierarchy of divinely-authorized power**.

This maps onto the Avestan theology in a specific way: Ahuramazda delegated the maintenance of *arta* (cosmic order) to Zarathustra's line (the priestly tradition). The Achaemenid kings claim to have received a parallel delegation for the political realm. Whether this is consistent with Zaroastrian theology or a royal adaptation of it is precisely the question the magoi navigated.

**New convergence: Descending-Hierarchy-of-Delegated-Authority** — Achaemenid king-of-kings + Byzantine *basileus* (emperor as God's *vice-gerent*) + Chinese *tianming* hierarchical structure + medieval European *rex dei gratia* (king by grace of God) + Indian *cakravartin* (wheel-turning universal monarch). Each tradition develops a specific theological account of how divine authority flows downward through legitimate hierarchies. 5+ traditions. This convergence touches the core political-theological question of every major civilization.

## III. The Naqsh-e Rustam Inscriptions — Death and Kingship

### The Context

The Naqsh-e Rustam inscriptions are carved near the cliff tombs of the Achaemenid kings at Persepolis. Darius's tomb inscription (DNb) is among the most personal and theologically interesting Old Persian texts — the king speaks directly about what kind of person he is, what Ahuramazda has given him, and what he owes.

### The Virtue Catalog

DNb contains an extraordinary list of virtues that Darius claims as his own, placed there by Ahuramazda's gift:

> *Adam xšnūtamahy... ciçam adam draujana naiy aham, naiy zūrakara aham...*
> "I am pleased... I was not a liar, I was not violent..."

The virtues: *naiy draujana* (not a liar), *naiy zūrakara* (not violent/wrongdoer), justice to the weak and the strong equally, the disciplined use of anger (not acting in anger but acting with *xšnūta* — satisfaction/calm judgment). The king's legitimacy is moral, not merely ritual: he must be genuinely virtuous, not just properly anointed.

**Theological structure**: The Naqsh-e Rustam inscriptions reveal that the Achaemenid theology of kingship is **virtue-constituted**. The king is king because Ahuramazda wills it AND because the king embodies the virtues Ahuramazda values. The two are not separable: a king who lies, who is violent, who lacks *xšnūta* (disciplined calm) has, in some sense, forfeited the divine mandate.

This connects to the Confucian *tianming* more tightly than the Mesopotamian divine-kingship traditions: in Mesopotamia, the king's legitimacy is primarily ritual (properly conducting the New Year festival, rebuilding the temple, maintaining the cult). In the Achaemenid and Confucian models, it is primarily **moral** — the king's virtue is the evidence of the mandate, and the loss of virtue is the forfeiture of it.

**New convergence: Virtue-as-Evidence-of-Divine-Mandate** DEEPENED — Achaemenid DNb + Chinese *tianming* (lost when the ruler loses virtue) + Hebrew *davidic covenant* (conditional on keeping Torah) + Islamic *khalīfah* (caliphate as stewardship requiring justice). 4+ traditions.

## IV. Ahuramazda and Arta — The Cosmic Order in Imperial Form

### The Old Persian Arta

Old Persian *arta* (Avestan *aša*) is the cosmic truth-order — the same concept from the Avestan theology, now applied in the political-imperial register.

In Avestan, *aša* is the cosmic-truth-order maintained by Ahura Mazda and sought by the righteous (*ashavan*). It is simultaneously: the order of the cosmos, the truth of speech, the right conduct of ritual, the ethical quality of genuine action.

In Old Persian, *arta* appears in:
- **Personal names**: *Artaxerxes* (Artaxšaçā: "whose kingdom is through *arta*"), *Artaphernes* (*Artafarna*: "glory/splendor of *arta*"), *Darius* (Dārayavauš: "holding firm the good"). The names of the royal family encode the theology.
- **Political theology**: the king's rule IS the expression of *arta* in the political realm; rebels are *draugha*-followers.
- **Cosmological frame**: Ahuramazda is the creator of *arta*, and the king is *arta*'s human representative.

**Theological transformation from Avestan to Old Persian**: In the Gathas, *asha* is primarily an ethical-cosmic category — it names the right-ordering of reality that the prophet seeks and the divine maintains. In the Old Persian inscriptions, *arta* becomes additionally a **political category** — the right-ordering of the empire is *arta* expressed in political form. The king who maintains order is maintaining *arta*; the rebel who disrupts it is following *draugha*.

This is not distortion of the Avestan theology — it is its political application. But the application has significant consequences: it means **every political disruption is a theological crime**, and every act of imperial violence against rebels is a cosmic service to Ahuramazda. The theopolitical fusion cuts both ways.

## V. The Relationship Between Old Persian State Cult and Avestan Priesthood

### The Magoi and the Achaemenids

The *magoi* (singular *magus*; Greek rendering of Old Persian *magu*, Avestan *maga*) were the Median priestly clan who served as ritual specialists across the Achaemenid empire. Their relationship to the Achaemenid kings is complex:

**What we know**:
- The Achaemenid kings mention fire-altars and sacrifices but do not explicitly name Zoroastrian ritual structures in their inscriptions.
- Herodotus describes the *magoi* as performing sacrifices, maintaining sacred fire, and interpreting dreams for the Persian kings.
- The Avestan texts describe a tradition maintained by professional priests who were literate in Avestan and trained in the Yasna ritual.
- Darius's Behistun inscription does not mention Zarathustra by name, nor does any other Old Persian inscription.

**The interpretive question**: Were the Achaemenid kings Zoroastrians? Three positions:
1. **Full Zoroastrian**: The Achaemenid theology is orthodox Zoroastrianism applied to imperial governance.
2. **Proto-Zoroastrian parallel**: The Achaemenids shared the same Old Iranian religious substrate as Zarathustra but may not have known the Gathas specifically.
3. **Pragmatic adapter**: The Achaemenid kings used Ahuramazda as their divine patron while maintaining polytheistic flexibility (they sponsored Babylonian, Egyptian, and Greek cults as well).

The evidence supports position 3 with strong elements of position 2. The Cyrus Cylinder (next file) shows Cyrus invoking Marduk rather than Ahuramazda — a deliberate religious-political decision, not an oversight. The Achaemenid kings were sophisticated religious politicians who understood that **religious legitimacy is local** and adapted accordingly.

**Theological significance for YOUSPEAK**: The Achaemenid magoi represent one of the earliest documented examples of **professional religious specialists as imperial infrastructure**. The magoi are not just temple priests (like Mesopotamian *ēn* and *kārum*); they function as:
- Dream interpreters for the king
- Ritual specialists for state sacrifices
- Mediators between the divine and political realms
- Gatekeepers of the sacred knowledge required for proper cosmic maintenance

This is the priestly-specialist role at imperial scale. **New convergence: Religious-Specialist-as-Imperial-Infrastructure** — Achaemenid magoi + Egyptian *ḥm-nṯr* (prophet-priests of Pharaoh) + Mesopotamian *āšipu* (exorcist-priest serving the king) + Chinese *shī* (ritual specialists of the Zhou court) + Roman *pontifices* (bridge-builders between divine and political). 5+ traditions, NEW.
