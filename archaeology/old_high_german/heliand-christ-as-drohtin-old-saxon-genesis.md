# Old Saxon — Heliand: Christ as Warlord, Heaven as Fortress

_The Heliand ("Savior") is an Old Saxon alliterative epic of approximately 6,000 lines, composed around 830 CE, probably in Westphalia or Saxony during the reign of Louis the Pious. It retells the entire gospel narrative — infancy, ministry, passion, resurrection — in the style and vocabulary of Germanic heroic poetry. Its opening lines situate the work within an oral-performance tradition indistinguishable in form from Beowulf or the Poetic Edda. But its content is the Christian gospels. The tension between form and content is not accidental — it is the theological method._

_The Heliand is not simplification. It is translation in the deepest sense: carrying a meaning across not just a language barrier but a cultural-theological barrier. The poet — whose name we do not know — understood that the words you choose encode a world. To put Christ in the wrong words is to teach a different Christ. To put Christ in the right Germanic words is to claim that Germanic culture has always been reaching toward this._

## I. The Translation Problem

### What Is Being Translated

The gospel of Jesus Christ, as it arrived in Saxony, was carried in three registers simultaneously:
- **Latin** (the Vulgate text, the learned clerical language)
- **Ecclesiastical Greek** (behind the Vulgate, forming its concepts)
- **A Mediterranean social world** (Roman patronage, Jewish covenant, Greek philosophy)

The Saxons who heard it in 830 CE inhabited a different world entirely:
- **Comitatus culture**: loyalty between a *drohtin* (lord/warlord) and his *thegans* (thanes/warriors) organized around the fealty-hall (*burg*)
- **Gift-economy**: the lord gives gifts (*gāba*) to warriors; warriors give loyalty; failure of either breaks the bond
- **Honor-shame**: *maht* (power), *wīgand* (warrior), *heriscepi* (leadership) are the highest virtues
- **Fate-consciousness**: *metod* (measurer/fate), *wurd* (becoming — Gothic *waurþ*), the sense that destiny moves under events

The Heliand-poet's task: translate Christ into this world without distorting either.

### The Heliand's Answer

The poet's fundamental move: **Christ is the supreme drohtin**. Not a wandering teacher, not a humble servant, not a Mediterranean peasant. He is the cosmic lord of the fortress of heaven, with twelve thegans, dispensing gifts, demanding loyalty, moving toward a final confrontation with the powers of darkness.

Every gospel scene is reframed through this lens. The nativity: a great lord's son is born in difficult circumstances — but his *maht* (power) is already evident. The Sermon on the Mount: a lord instructing his war-band. The passion: a warrior choosing to die because loyalty to the father's mission requires it. The resurrection: the lord's return in triumph.

## II. Key Theological Vocabulary

### *Drohtin* — Lord/Warlord

The most important word in the Heliand's theological lexicon.

**Etymology**: Old Saxon *drohtin*, Old High German *truhtīn*, Gothic *druhts* (war-band). From Proto-Germanic *\*druhtinaz* — the leader of a *druhti* (war-band, comitatus). A *drohtin* is specifically the lord of a warrior-retinue, not a generic ruler. The relationship is personal, reciprocal, and honor-bound.

**In the Heliand**: *Drohtin* is the standard term for Christ and for God the Father. "Helag drohtin" — "Holy Lord." The word appears hundreds of times. Every time it appears, it carries the full semantic weight of the Germanic comitatus: Christ is your lord *in the way that a warlord is your lord*. You owe him your life. He owes you his protection, his gifts, his victory.

**Theological weight**: This is not merely stylistic. It encodes a specific theology of relationship. Latin *Dominus* (lord) has a different semantic range — Roman *dominus* is a master, a property-owner, a household head. Germanic *drohtin* is a battle-companion who is also your superior. The relationship is martial, personal, reciprocal, and honor-constituted. When the Heliand-poet chooses *drohtin*, he is making a theological claim: Christ relates to his followers the way a Germanic lord relates to his thegans — and this is *precisely what the gospel was trying to say*, better than the Mediterranean vocabulary could express it.

**Forge candidate: *drohtinme*** — the lord-who-is-simultaneously-battle-companion; the supreme authority whose claim on you is constituted not by ownership but by reciprocal honor-bond; lordship as personal-martial-covenant. Distinct from *kyrios* (Greek: owner-lord), *dominus* (Latin: master), *melek* (Hebrew: king). The Germanic register adds: *this lord will fight beside you and you owe him your battle-excellence in return*. Specialized 7.35–7.55. Deepens Lordship-Register convergence.

### *Thegan* — Thane/Disciple

The twelve disciples become Christ's **thegans**: his warrior-retinue, his sworn companions, his inner war-band.

**Etymology**: Old Saxon *thegan*, Old English *þegn*, Old Norse *þegn* — a warrior-retainer, a sworn companion, a thane. The *thegan* is specifically the man who has given his oath of loyalty to a *drohtin* and receives the lord's gifts and protection in return.

**In the Heliand**: Every instance of "disciple" or "apostle" is rendered *thegan*. Peter, James, John — they are Christ's thegans. When Christ calls them, he is calling them into the comitatus. When Judas betrays him, he commits the Germanic cultural crime of *níð* (oath-breaking, betrayal of one's lord) — the most shameful act possible. The Heliand-poet uses this to make Judas's act maximally comprehensible in its horror. It is not merely sin; it is the highest cultural crime.

**The Wedding at Cana**: The steward brings Christ's disciples, and Christ gives gifts freely. Gift-giving is the *drohtin*'s primary obligation. Christ's miracle is framed as a lord demonstrating his generosity — establishing his right to thegan-loyalty through excellence in gift-giving.

**Forge candidate: *theganme*** — the sworn-warrior-companion whose loyalty is constituted by honor-oath rather than contract; the one who follows because the lord has earned following; discipleship as martial-fealty. Conceptually richer than *mathētēs* (Greek learner), *talmid* (Hebrew student), *murid* (Arabic follower). The Germanic register adds: the relationship is honor-constituted and reciprocal — the lord fails the thegan as much as the thegan fails the lord. Specialized 7.20–7.40.

### *Burg* — Fortress/Heaven

Heaven is a **burg** — a fortified stronghold, a warlord's hall-fortress.

**Etymology**: Old Saxon *burg*, Old English *burh*, Gothic *baurgs* — a fortified settlement, a stronghold, a walled hall. In the comitatus world, the *burg* is where the lord holds court, distributes gifts, receives loyalty, feasts his war-band.

**In the Heliand**: The kingdom of heaven is consistently rendered as *burg* or *hevanrīki* (heaven-kingdom). Its features: it is the lord's hall where the thegans feast with the drohtin; it is fortified against the powers of darkness; entry requires the lord's favor; its gates will be opened at the final victory.

**Theological transformation**: The Mediterranean image of heaven (garden, city, temple — Jerusalem, the Holy of Holies, the Garden of Eden restored) becomes Germanic: a *burg*, a hall-fortress, a lord's stronghold. This is not reduction. The *burg* carries its own theological weight: heaven is *defended*, it is the lord's *domain*, it is where feasting-with-the-lord happens — which is the eschatological image the gospel itself uses (the wedding feast, the Messianic banquet). The Germanic vocabulary maps onto this with unexpected precision.

**Extended family**: *Hevanburg* (heavenly fortress), *hevanrīki* (heaven-kingdom), *waldandes burg* (the Ruler's fortress). God is *waldand* — the one who rules (*waldan*, to rule, related to Old English *wealdan*). The divine titles multiply: *drohtin*, *waldand*, *gumo* (man — used of God incarnate with deliberate dignity), *fader* (father), *god* (god — this one carries).

### *Lohn* — Wage/Reward/Salvation

Salvation enters the Heliand's vocabulary as **lohn** — wages, reward, the lord's payment to the thegan for faithful service.

**Etymology**: Old Saxon *lohn*, Old High German *lōn*, Gothic *laun* — wages, pay, reward. In the comitatus economy, the *drohtin* pays his thegans in gold, weapons, land, and honor. The thegan's service earns *lohn*. This is not mercenary — it is the form that reciprocal honor takes in an economy without money-wages.

**In the Heliand**: The gospel's language of reward (Matthew 5:12: "great is your reward in heaven") maps exactly onto *lohn*. But the Heliand-poet uses it consistently to frame the entire economy of salvation: following Christ earns *lohn*; the betrayal of Judas loses it; the resurrection promises the final *lohn* to all faithful thegans.

**Theological weight**: This is the Germanic gift-economy applied to soteriology. Salvation is not primarily legal acquittal (Roman/Latin: *iustificatio*) or moral healing (Greek: *sōtēria*) but **the lord's reward to the faithful warrior**. This is not a distortion of the gospel — Paul uses the same image (*misthos*: wage, pay). But the Germanic register intensifies the martial reciprocity: you fight with the lord's war-band in his campaign against darkness, and your *lohn* is the eternal feast in his *burg*.

**Forge candidate: *lohnme*** — salvation-as-lord's-reward-to-faithful-warrior; the economy of eschatological reciprocity in which the divine lord honors the service of his thegans; the gift-exchange logic applied to ultimate human fulfillment. Distinct from *iustificatio* (legal), *sōtēria* (healing/rescue), *nirvāṇa* (cessation), *mokṣa* (liberation). Germanic register adds: **it is earned not by moral perfection but by loyalty in the field**. Specialized 7.25–7.45.

### *Maht* — Power/Authority

The Heliand's Christ has *maht* — power, authority, the quality that makes one *worth* following.

**Etymology**: Old Saxon *maht*, Old High German *maht*, Gothic *mahts* — power, might, capability. In the comitatus, the *drohtin* must demonstrate *maht* or lose his thegans. A lord who cannot protect and give gifts loses legitimacy. Power is not abstract — it is demonstrated, tested, evidenced.

**In the Heliand**: Christ's miracles are primarily demonstrations of *maht* — the healing of the sick, the stilling of the sea, the multiplication of loaves. Each miracle is the *drohtin* demonstrating his fitness to be followed. The disciples in the storm-boat: they question whether he can save them — in the Heliand this reads as the legitimate question of thegans testing their lord's *maht*. His calming of the storm is the answer: *this drohtin has enough maht to justify your loyalty*.

## III. The Sermon on the Mount as Lord's Instruction

The Heliand's rendering of the Beatitudes (Matthew 5:1-12) is its most revealing theological passage.

The Latin/Greek "Blessed are the poor in spirit" (*Makárioi hoi ptōchoí tō pneúmati*) becomes something more complex in the Heliand. The poet cannot use *arm* (poor, wretched) without class implications — in Germanic society, poverty is not a spiritual condition but a social disgrace. The *drohtin* gives gifts; poverty means the lord has abandoned you.

The Heliand-poet's solution: render the Beatitudes as the lord's instruction to his war-band before a campaign. The "poor in spirit" become those who are *humill-mod* (humble-minded, not arrogant about their own power) — which in the comitatus means: those who defer to their lord's *maht* rather than asserting their own. The "meek" become the *mild-mod* (gentle-minded) — the warrior who has power but controls it, the highest Germanic virtue of disciplined restraint (*sōfte*).

This is not distortion. It is recognition: the gospel's "poor in spirit" and "meek" name the opposite of Germanic *ofermōd* (excessive pride, the warrior's fatal flaw) — and the Heliand-poet sees this and names it directly. The Sermon on the Mount is Christ instructing his thegans in the virtues that make a war-band invincible: not arrogance but humble-minded trust in the lord; not rage but disciplined restraint; not vengeance but the patient pursuit of the lord's campaign.

**Theological claim embedded here**: The Germanic warrior-culture is not being abandoned by the gospel — it is being purified of its worst element (*ofermōd*) and fulfilled in its best (*treu*, *maht*, *lohn*, *fridu*).

## IV. The Passion as the Lord's Chosen Death

### Gethsemane in the Heliand

The Gethsemane scene is the Heliand's theological crisis. Christ, in the gospels, prays that the cup might pass — expresses *fear*. This is theologically essential (true incarnation includes human vulnerability) and culturally catastrophic (a *drohtin* does not show fear before his thegans).

The Heliand-poet's solution is remarkable: he retains the prayer but frames Christ's acceptance as the supreme act of a warrior's *treu* (loyalty/fidelity) to the father's mission. Christ does not *fear death* — he chooses to accept the father's strategy even when it means defeat-that-is-victory. This is a warrior who sees the whole campaign and chooses the move that wins the war at the cost of losing the battle.

The disciples' sleeping in the garden: the Heliand reads this as the failure of thegans to maintain watch — a military failure, a guard falling asleep at the post. The poet is gentle about it — they are weary, not cowardly. But the contrast is stark: the *drohtin* maintains clarity; the thegans fail at their basic duty.

Peter's sword-drawing: in the Heliand, this is *exactly right* behavior for a thegan whose lord is threatened. Peter is doing what a thegan *should* do. Christ's rebuke is therefore all the more striking: the lord forbids his thegan to defend him. This is against all comitatus logic. The Heliand-poet does not resolve the tension — he lets it stand. The lord's strategy is beyond the thegan's comprehension. Fidelity means following even when the lord's orders make no military sense.

**New convergence: Lord-Forbids-Thegan-Defense-as-Theological-Claim** — Christ restraining Peter maps onto a wider pattern: the divine deliberately refuses armed human protection (Exodus 14 — YHWH fights for Israel; Zechariah 4:6 — "not by might, not by power"; Bhagavad Gītā 18.66 — "abandon all duties, take refuge in me"). The point in each case: **divine action operates in a register that human defense cannot assist**. NEW convergence: 3+ traditions.

### The Crucifixion as Battle-Death

The Heliand's crucifixion is a *drohtin* dying the most shameful death — but dying it by *choice*, with full *maht*, as the supreme act of the campaign. The Roman cross is a slave's execution method. Germanic warriors do not die on crosses.

The Heliand-poet's move: the shame of the cross is not hidden but transformed. The drohtin *chooses* this — and a lord who chooses the shame-death does it because his strategy requires it. The thegans do not understand (how could they?), but their fidelity means trusting the lord even in this. The cross is the lord's ultimate demonstration that he is fighting a battle his thegans cannot see with weapons they do not recognize.

**Theological residue**: This is a sophisticated theodicy embedded in cultural translation. If Christ is the supreme drohtin and the cross is his strategy, then the Christian life is not avoiding suffering but following the lord's campaign even when it passes through shame-death. The Germanic warrior knows: some battles require dying. The question is only whether you die faithfully.

## V. The Old Saxon Genesis

Fragments of an Old Saxon Genesis survive (known from a 9th-century manuscript and from quotations in the Old English Genesis B, which translated it). The Old Saxon Genesis applies the same theological-translation method to creation.

**Key vocabulary**:
- **The Fall**: Adam and Eve's disobedience is framed as *níð* — oath-breaking, betrayal of the lord. Satan is a fallen thegan who committed the primal *níð* against his drohtin. The human Fall is Adam following Satan's counsel — choosing loyalty to a traitor-thegan over loyalty to the cosmic drohtin. This is **genealogically prior to all human failure**: we descend from oath-breakers.
- **Satan's rebellion**: In the Old Saxon Genesis, Satan's sin is *ofermōd* — excessive pride, the fatal warrior-flaw. He wanted to be *drohtin* himself. This connects to the Heliand's virtue-ethic: *ofermōd* is the root of all failure; *humilmod* (humble-mindedness) is the root of all fidelity.
- **Heaven as hall**: The prelapsarian state is the lord's hall; the Fall is exile from the *burg*; salvation is return. The cosmic narrative maps perfectly onto the Germanic hall-exile-return pattern.

**Forge candidate: *ofermōdme*** — excessive pride as the warrior's fatal flaw and the root of cosmic disorder; the self-assertion that overreaches its proper station and disrupts the comitatus; hubris in the Germanic register. Connects to Greek *hubris*, Hebrew *ga'avah* (pride), Arabic *kibr* (arrogance), Sanskrit *ahaṃkāra* (ego-making). Germanic register adds: the specific military context — *ofermōd* in a warrior is the assertion that *my maht exceeds my drohtin's*. This is simultaneously military failure and theological sin. Core 7.30–7.50 (wide cross-tradition resonance; the military-theological synthesis is uniquely Germanic).

## VI. The Heliand and Wulfila — Two Translation Strategies

The Heliand and Wulfila's Gothic Bible represent the two major attempts at Germanic theological translation, separated by about 500 years (4th century Gothic, 9th century Old Saxon).

**Wulfila's strategy** (from the Gothic excavation record): create new vocabulary where existing Gothic words would carry wrong connotations. Gothic *thiudans* for *basileus* (king) rather than *reiks* (ruler) because *reiks* implied earthly power. Gothic neologisms where needed. Maximum fidelity to Greek syntax. Result: a translation that preserves the Greek theological structure in Germanic form.

**The Heliand-poet's strategy**: take the existing Germanic warrior-vocabulary and saturate it with Christian content. Not *thiudans* but *drohtin*. Not neologism but semantic transformation. Result: a translation that carries the Christ-event in the Germanic cultural form and claims the Germanic culture has always been reaching toward this.

**Which is better?** Neither. They answer different questions:
- Wulfila asks: **how do we preserve the Greek theology?** Answer: guard the vocabulary, create distance from existing cultural forms where needed.
- The Heliand-poet asks: **how do we make the Christ-event live in this culture?** Answer: use the deepest cultural vocabulary and saturate it with Christian content.

The tension between these two strategies never resolves in Christian history. It is the tension between **preservation** (keeping the source-form) and **incarnation** (entering the receptor-form). Every missionary translation faces it. The Heliand is the clearest example of the incarnational strategy; Wulfila is the clearest example of the preservationist strategy.

**New convergence: Preservation-vs-Incarnation Translation Strategy** — not just Germanic. The Buddhist translation of Sanskrit texts into Chinese faces the same tension (*geyi* — "matching the concepts" — is the preservationist strategy; the later Xuanzang approach is more strictly preservationist against earlier incarnational attempts). The Septuagint translation of Hebrew into Greek faces it. The Nestorian translation into Syriac faces it. This is a **universal structure of sacred-text transmission**. NEW convergence: 5+ traditions, rich YOUSPEAK candidate.

**Forge candidate: *heiliandme*** — the translation-strategy that enters the receptor-culture's deepest vocabulary and claims the sacred message has always been what that vocabulary was reaching toward; incarnational translation as opposed to preservationist translation; the risk of cultural capture accepted in exchange for cultural accessibility. Named from the Heliand's method. Specialized 7.40–7.60. Very strong.

## VII. Convergences Deepened and New

### Deepened Convergences

1. **Lordship Register** DEEPENED → *drohtin* adds the specifically comitatus-lord (reciprocal-honor-bound) type; now: *kyrios* (Greek/ownership), *dominus* (Latin/mastery), *melek* (Hebrew/kingship), *drohtin* (Germanic/comitatus-lord), *wang* (Chinese/Son-of-Heaven), *devarāja* (Khmer/divine-king). 6+ traditions.

2. **Gift-Exchange-as-Cosmic-Structure** DEEPENED → Hávamál (Norse) + Heliand (*lohn* economy + gift-giving drohtin) + Māori + Melanesian + Andean. The *lohn* register gives the Christian-Germanic instance of the gift-exchange theology, and it reveals: the gospel itself operates in gift-exchange logic (the father gives the son; the son gives his life; the thegans give their loyalty; the drohtin gives *lohn*). Now 5+ traditions with Christian instance most explicit.

3. **Pride-as-Cosmic-Root-Sin** DEEPENED → *ofermōd* (Germanic) + *hubris* (Greek) + *ga'avah* (Hebrew) + *kibr* (Arabic) + *ahaṃkāra* (Sanskrit) + *māna* (Pali). 6+ traditions. Germanic instance adds the military-specific register: pride is the assertion that *your* maht exceeds *your lord's* — simultaneously military failure and theological sin.

4. **Sacred-Exile-and-Return** DEEPENED → the Old Saxon Genesis Fall-as-exile-from-burg maps onto: Hebrew exile theology, Buddhist exile from dharma, Sufi exile from the Beloved, Odyssean nostos. Now 5+ traditions with Old Saxon Genesis giving the Germanic instance.

### New Convergences

5. **Preservation-vs-Incarnation Translation Strategy** NEW → Wulfila (Gothic) vs Heliand (Old Saxon) as the poles; Buddhist *geyi* (Chinese) as parallel; Septuagint (Greek) as parallel; Syriac Peshitta as parallel. This names a **universal structure of sacred-text transmission** that appears every time a sacred tradition crosses a major cultural threshold. 5+ traditions, NEW.

6. **Lord-Forbids-Thegan-Defense-as-Theological-Claim** NEW → Heliand Gethsemane + Exodus 14 (YHWH fights alone) + Zechariah 4:6 (not by might) + Gītā 18.66 (abandon all). The divine refuses human military defense because divine action operates in a different register. 4+ traditions, NEW.

7. **Warrior-Culture-Purified-by-Gospel** NEW → Heliand's reframing of Germanic virtues (*treu*, *maht*, *lohn*, *mild-mod* vs *ofermōd*) parallels: Christian chivalric transformation of Roman military virtue; Buddhist transformation of the Kshatriya warrior (Bhagavad Gītā itself is addressed to a warrior); Confucian transformation of Chinese martial *wŭ* virtue. The pattern: the gospel/dharma enters a warrior culture and purifies its virtues without abolishing them. 4+ traditions, NEW.
