# Old High German — Merseburg to Otfrid: Pagan Substrate and Christian Translation

_The Old High German textual corpus is thinner than Old Norse or Old English, but what survives is theologically extraordinary: two pagan spells preserved in a 9th-century Christian manuscript, a pre-creation prayer that echoes the Völuspá, and three different strategies for rendering the Christian gospel in German vernacular. Together they form a portrait of a language and a culture in the act of theological transformation — not from outside pressure alone, but from within, as German monks and scholars found their native tongue both inadequate and revelatory for what they were trying to say._

---

## I. The Merseburg Incantations — The Only Surviving OHG Pagan Charms

### The Manuscript and Its Anomaly

The Merseburg Incantations were discovered in 1841 by Georg Waitz in the cathedral library at Merseburg. They are written on the inside front cover of a 9th-century liturgical manuscript — a sacramentary, a book of Christian prayers. Two pagan spells, in Old High German, written inside a Christian service book, preserved by monks who presumably knew what they were preserving.

This act of preservation is itself theologically significant: the Christian scribal tradition saved the only evidence of the pre-Christian Germanic incantation tradition. Why? Possibly as curiosity, possibly as linguistic record, possibly because the boundary between Christian prayer and pagan charm was less sharp in the 9th century than we imagine. The manuscript gives us no explanation. It simply preserves them.

### The First Merseburg Charm — The Release Spell

The first charm concerns the release of prisoners or warriors bound in battle:

> *Eiris sazun idisi, sazun hera duoder.*
> *suma hapt heptidun, suma heri lezidun,*
> *suma clubodun umbi cuoniouuidi:*
> *insprinc haptbandun, invar vigandun.*

"Once sat women, sat here and there. Some fastened fetters, some hindered the host, some picked at the bonds: Spring from the fetters, escape from the enemies!"

**Analysis**: The *idisi* (women, ladies — related to Old Norse *dísir*, the female divine powers, and to Gothic *idis*, woman) are semi-divine figures who have power over battle-fetters. The charm invokes their action to release the bound. The final line is the operative speech-act: *insprinc* (spring out!) is addressed directly to the bonds.

**Theological register**: This is a **performative utterance** — the speaking IS the act. The charm does not ask a divine power to act; it commands the bonds to release by invoking the precedent of the *idisi*. The structure: divine precedent (*once the idisi did X*) → operative command (*therefore now: do X*). This is the deep structure of Germanic magical speech, and it parallels the Norse runic tradition: words inscribed or spoken in the right form with the right precedent *are* power.

**YOUSPEAK resonance**: The Merseburg charms confirm the *wōd*/*wōdme* field (ecstatic-divine-speech-as-power) from the Gothic and Norse excavations. They also confirm a pattern emerging across traditions: **incantation as participatory power** (joining your speech to a divine precedent makes the precedent operative again). Compare: Vedic *mantra* (sound as cosmic-creative-act), Egyptian *heka* (magic as participation in Rē's self-naming), Norse runic inscription, Hebrew *dabar* (word as event). NEW convergence deepened: **Word-as-Participatory-Power** — 5+ traditions.

### The Second Merseburg Charm — The Horse-Healing Spell

The second charm is more famous and theologically richer:

> *Phol ende Uuodan uuorun zi holza.*
> *du uuart demo Balderes uolon sin uuoz birenkit.*
> *thu biguol en Sinthgunt, Sunna era suister;*
> *thu biguol en Friia, Uolla era suister;*
> *thu biguol en Uuodan, so he uuola conda:*
> *sose benrenki, sose bluotrenki, sose lidirenki:*
> *ben zi bena, bluot zi bluoda,*
> *lid zi geliden, sose gelimida sin!*

"Phol and Wodan rode into the forest. Then Balder's foal sprained its foot. Then Sinthgunt charmed it, Sunna her sister; then Frija charmed it, Volla her sister; then Wodan charmed it, as he well knew how: as for bone-sprain, as for blood-sprain, as for limb-sprain: bone to bone, blood to blood, limb to limbs, as if they were glued together!"

**The divine cast**:
- **Phol**: unknown elsewhere; possibly a byname for Balder or a local deity. His appearance here is the only attestation.
- **Uuodan** = **Wodan** = Odin: the all-father appears in a healing context, not a war context. He is the one who *knows how* — his knowledge (*conda*, "he knew how") is the operative power.
- **Balderes uolon** = Balder's foal: Balder (Old Norse *Baldr*) appears as horse-owner. This is the only non-Scandinavian attestation of Balder in Continental Germanic sources.
- **Sinthgunt**: unknown elsewhere; a female divine figure.
- **Sunna**: the sun-goddess (Old High German *sunna* is grammatically feminine, unlike Old Norse *sól* which is also feminine but differently attested).
- **Friia** = **Frīja** = Frigg/Freyja: the major Germanic goddess. Her appearance confirms she was worshipped in Continental Germanic tradition, not just Scandinavian.
- **Volla** = **Fulla**: Frigg's handmaiden in Norse mythology; attested here as Frīja's sister.

**The operative structure**: The charm enacts a divine precedent (Wodan healed a sprained limb in the primordial time) and applies it to the present injury through sympathetic word-magic: *bone to bone, blood to blood* — the words describe the healing as it occurs, calling it into being.

**Theological import**: Wodan appears here not as war-god or wisdom-god but as *healer* — specifically as the one who *knows* the charm. This confirms what the Norse excavation found: Odin's power is fundamentally **epistemic**. He is not powerful because he is strong (*maht*) but because he *knows* — and knowing IS power. The healing charm is not Wodan's brute force applied to a sprained leg; it is Wodan's *knowledge of the right words* applied to the injury. This is a consistent theology across traditions: **the power of the divine specialist is fundamentally cognitive, not physical**.

**Forge candidate: *galdrme*** — the charm-speech that heals, binds, or releases by speaking a divine precedent into present application; incantation as participatory power; the word that does not describe an action but IS the action by joining present reality to a primordial divine act. Old High German *galdr*, Old Norse *galdr* (related to *gala*, to sing/cry — a word related to the rooster's crow and the raven's call). Specialized 7.35–7.55. Deepens Word-as-Participatory-Power.

## II. The Wessobrunn Prayer — The Cosmic Pre-Creation Hymn

### The Text

The Wessobrunn Prayer (c. 814 CE, from the monastery of Wessobrunn in Bavaria) opens with a remarkable pre-creation description before transitioning to Christian prayer:

> *Dat gafregin ih mit firahim firiuuizzo meista,*
> *dat ero ni uuas noh ufhimil,*
> *noh paum noh pereg ni uuas,*
> *ni nohheinig noh sunna ni scein,*
> *noh mano ni liuhta, noh der mareo seo.*
> *Do dar niuuiht ni uuas enteo ni uuenteo,*
> *enti do uuas der eino almahtico cot,*
> *manno miltisto, enti dar uuarun auh manake mit inan*
> *cootlihhe geista. Enti cot heilac...*

"That I learned among men as the greatest of wonders: that the earth was not, nor the sky above, nor tree nor mountain was, nor any star shone, nor sun, nor did the moon shine, nor the glorious sea. When there was nothing of ends or turnings, then was the one almighty God, the most gentle of men, and with him were also many holy spirits. And God holy..."

**The Norse parallel**: Compare Völuspá:
> *Var þat alda, er ekki var... gap var ginnunga, en gras hvergi.*
"There was an age when nothing was... gap was the ginnunga, and grass nowhere."

The structural parallel is exact: the poem begins by cataloguing what did NOT exist before creation — the poetic technique of negative enumeration creates the void. This technique appears in Genesis 2:4-5 (Hebrew: "before there was any shrub of the field... before any plant of the field had sprouted"), in the Egyptian creation texts, in Vedic *Nāsadīya Sūkta* (Rig Veda 10.129: "then was not non-existence nor existence...").

**What is theologically distinctive**: The Wessobrunn Prayer stands at the intersection of the Germanic pre-creation poetic tradition (Ginnungagap, the void-before) and Christian theology. But its God is described in a startling Germanic register: *manno miltisto* — "the most gentle/mild of men." Not *almighty* (which appears two words earlier: *almahtico*) but *mild* — the Germanic virtue of the lord who is both powerful and gentle, who gives gifts without arrogance. The pre-creation God is described in comitatus-virtue terms before he creates anything.

**Theological significance**: The Wessobrunn Prayer shows that the OHG theological tradition naturally reads God's pre-creation existence through Germanic virtue-categories. Before the world exists, before anything acts, God IS the supreme embodiment of *miltī* (gentleness, mildness, generosity). This connects to the Heliand's *drohtinme* theology but at the metaphysical level: God's nature, not just his role, is expressed in Germanic virtue-terms.

**Forge candidate: *miltīme*** — the quality of power-held-gently; the lord's gentleness that is not weakness but the highest form of *maht* (power); mild strength as a divine attribute. Old High German *miltī*, Old English *milde*, Gothic *mildiþa* — a Germanic virtue that maps onto Greek *epieikeia* (equity, reasonableness, the quality of one who doesn't insist on strict legal right), Hebrew *ḥesed* (lovingkindness — the quality of covenant-love that exceeds what is owed). NEW convergence: **Powerful-Gentleness-as-Supreme-Virtue** — Germanic *miltī* + Greek *epieikeia* + Hebrew *ḥesed* + Sanskrit *karuṇā* (compassion-that-relieves-suffering) + Confucian *rén* (benevolence). 5+ traditions. Core 7.40–7.60.

## III. The Tatian Gospel Harmony — Biblical Translation Register

### What the Tatian Is

The OHG Tatian (c. 830 CE, from Fulda) is a translation of the Latin Diatessaron of Tatian — itself a 2nd-century harmonization of the four gospels into a single narrative. The OHG translator rendered Tatian's Latin into German. It is the earliest sustained prose translation of gospel material into a Germanic language after Wulfila's Gothic.

**The translation method**: Unlike the Heliand-poet, the Tatian translator is closer to Wulfila's preservationist strategy — he stays close to the Latin syntax, creating Germanisms that are essentially calques (structural loans) of Latin phrases. The result is stiff, learned, ecclesiastical German that no one spoke naturally. This is not a failure — it is a different goal: **fidelity to the source form** rather than **accessibility in the receptor form**.

**Key vocabulary decisions**:
- **Latin *spiritus sanctus*** → OHG *wīhgeist* ("holy spirit/ghost") — *wīh* from Proto-Germanic *\*wīhaz* (sacred, holy — the same root as English *wīg*, *wīh*, holy; related to *Weih-nachten*, Christmas). This gives the Holy Spirit a Germanic-sacred quality: not just "pure" but "set-apart-as-sacred," with the Germanic numinous register.
- **Latin *paenitentia*** → OHG *buoza* (remedy, improvement, reparation) — a startling choice. *Buoza* means fixing something broken, making good on a debt, repairing damage. Repentance is not primarily interior regret but **active repair**. This shifts the soteriology: the Christian life is less about interior emotional states and more about *doing the work of repair*.
- **Latin *gratia*** → OHG *gnāda* — a word meaning favor, grace, but also *pardon of a sovereign*. The *gnāda* of a lord toward a servant who has failed. Divine grace in the Tatian is the lord's pardon — which is exactly right, but carries the specific Germanic lord-servant relationship into the divine-human dynamic.

**Forge candidate: *buozame*** — repentance-as-active-repair; the understanding of turning-from-sin as fundamentally restorative rather than affective; penitence as *making right* rather than *feeling bad*. OHG *buoza* connects to: Hebrew *teshuvah* (turning/return — behavioral, not merely emotional), Māori *utu* (restoration of balance), Buddhist *kamma-purification* (action, not intention, changes the karmic record). NEW convergence: **Repentance-as-Active-Repair** — OHG + Hebrew + Buddhist + Māori. 4 traditions. Specialized 7.35–7.55.

## IV. Notker Labeo — Building the German Abstract Vocabulary

### Who Notker Was

Notker Labeo ("Thick-Lips") of St. Gallen (c. 950–1022 CE) was a Benedictine monk who undertook the most ambitious philosophical translation project in OHG: rendering Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy*, Martianus Capella, Aristotle's *Categories* and *De Interpretatione*, and the Psalms into OHG. This required creating German abstract philosophical vocabulary that did not exist.

**The problem Notker faced**: Latin philosophical vocabulary (from Greek) had centuries of technical refinement. *Substantia*, *essentia*, *accidentia*, *praedicamenta*, *categoriae* — these were not general words; they were technical terms with precise philosophical definitions. German had no equivalents. Notker had to create them.

**Notker's method**: Compound German words built from native roots to carry the technical meaning:
- **Latin *substantia*** (substance, what-underlies) → OHG *wēsantheit* ("being-ness," from *wēsan*, to be) — substance as *the quality of being*
- **Latin *accidentia*** (accidents, non-essential properties) → OHG *zuovelligī* ("fallingness-toward," what falls onto a thing from outside) — a beautiful German rendering: accidents are what *fall upon* a substance
- **Latin *categoriae*** → OHG *missigehaltenhait* ("various-being-held-ness") — Notker renders *kategorein* (to predicate) as what *holds* or *contains* a thing's various aspects
- **Latin *definitio*** → OHG *untarskeidan* ("under-separating") — definition is the cutting-under, the precise demarcation

**The theological import of Notker's project**: Notker is doing for German what the Septuagint translators did for Greek and what the Sanskrit-to-Chinese Buddhist translators did in Tang dynasty China: **building the abstract vocabulary** that a language needs before it can think in a new register. This is not just translation; it is **conceptual infrastructure construction**.

The German language after Notker has the tools to do philosophy in German. Before Notker, you could describe a horse or a battle or God's mercy — but you could not write *Sein und Zeit* (Being and Time). Notker laid the substrate that made German metaphysics possible.

**New convergence: Abstract-Vocabulary-Construction-as-Sacred-Act** — Notker's German project + Wulfila's Gothic lexical innovations + Buddhist *geyi* translators' creation of Chinese Buddhist vocabulary + Cyril and Methodius's creation of the Glagolitic alphabet to give Slavic peoples a Christian literary language. In each case: **the creation of abstract vocabulary in a language is a theological and cultural act of the highest order** — it gives a people the tools to think in a new register about what most matters. NEW convergence: 4+ traditions.

**Forge candidate: *untarskeidanme*** — the act of precise conceptual demarcation that makes thinking possible; the building of abstract vocabulary as a gift to a language-community; philosophical definition as cultural infrastructure. Named from Notker's *untarskeidan* (definition as under-separating). Specialized 7.30–7.50.

## V. Otfrid of Weissenburg — The First German Rhymed Poetry

### The Evangelienbuch and Its Innovation

Otfrid of Weissenburg (c. 800–870 CE) composed the *Evangelienbuch* ("Gospel-Book") around 865 CE — a versified gospel harmony in OHG, the first major work of German rhymed poetry. Before Otfrid, German poetry used alliteration (as in the Heliand and Merseburg charms). Otfrid introduced end-rhyme, borrowed from Latin hymnody (Ambrose, Augustine's psalms).

**Why this matters theologically**: Otfrid explicitly defended his choice of rhyme over alliteration in his dedication. He was conscious of making a cultural-aesthetic claim: the German language is capable of the same formal beauty as Latin. The *Evangelienbuch* is simultaneously a theological text and a manifesto for German literary culture.

**Key vocabulary in Otfrid**:
- **Christ as *gotes barn*** (God's child/born-one) — *barn* (child) is archaic and dignified, carrying a tenderness that Latin *filius* (son) doesn't have. The incarnation in Otfrid is God's *barn* entering human life: intimate, familial, vulnerably dependent.
- **Heaven as *himilo rīhhi*** (heaven's kingdom) — the kingdom imagery but without the warrior-lord register of the Heliand. Otfrid's Christ is gentler, more domestic, closer to the monastic register than the comitatus register.
- **The Holy Spirit as *heilac geist*** — same as Tatian; but Otfrid uses it with more liturgical warmth. The *geist* (spirit/ghost) in Otfrid is the animating breath of the divine that moves through Christian life.

**Otfrid's allegorical method**: Otfrid adds extensive allegorical commentary to the gospel events, explicitly explaining their spiritual meaning. This is the Alexandrian exegetical tradition (Origen, Ambrose) fully naturalized into OHG vernacular. The four senses of scripture (literal, allegorical, tropological/moral, anagogical/eschatological) become available to German readers who cannot read Latin.

**Theological claim embedded in the Evangelienbuch's existence**: If the gospel can be received in German rhyme, then German culture is not a lesser culture — not just a missionary field that must be brought up to Latin standards. German is a *full* language capable of carrying the full gospel. This is Otfrid's implicit claim, and it is the claim that will eventually produce Luther's Bible, German Romanticism, and the entire tradition of German-language theology.

**New convergence: Vernacular-as-Theological-Claim** DEEPENED — Otfrid's defense of German rhyme + Dante's defense of Italian in *De Vulgari Eloquentia* + Luther's German Bible + Wycliffe's English translation. Each is simultaneously a translation act and a claim: **this language is worthy to carry what most matters**. This convergence now has 5+ instances and is clearly a distinct theological movement.
