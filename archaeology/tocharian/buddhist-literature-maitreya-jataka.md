# Tocharian — Buddhist Literature, Maitreya Cult, Jātaka/Avadāna

*YOUSPEAK Archaeological Excavation — Session 068*

---

## Overview

Tocharian is linguistically remarkable — the westernmost *centum* IE branch mysteriously isolated in the far east. But what makes it theologically extraordinary is simpler: **virtually everything we have is Buddhist.** Unlike Sanskrit, which carries Vedic, epic, philosophical, legal, and Buddhist material; unlike Pali, which carries a deliberately restricted Buddhist canon; Tocharian A and B manuscripts are almost entirely Buddhist in content. The language's literate life, as it survives to us, *is* Buddhist.

This is not coincidence but consequence. The Tocharian-speaking people of the Tarim Basin oasis towns — Kucha, Karasahr (Agni), Turfan — were deeply integrated into the Buddhist monastic economy. Monasteries were major landowners, employers, and cultural centers. Literacy was primarily monastic. When European expeditions excavated the Turfan Depression in 1902–1914 (German Turfan Expeditions I–IV, led by Albert Grünwedel and Albert von Le Coq), what they found were manuscripts in monastery ruins. The archive is a monastic archive.

**The scale of the corpus**: approximately 3,000 Tocharian B fragments survive (Berlin, Paris, St. Petersburg collections); fewer but important Tocharian A texts. Much remains unpublished. The critical edition project (CEToM — Comprehensive Edition of Tocharian Manuscripts) continues today.

## 1. Tocharian A vs. Tocharian B: Two Languages, Not Two Dialects

This point is crucial and underappreciated. Tocharian A (also called "East Tocharian" or "Agnean," after Agni = Karasahr) and Tocharian B (also called "West Tocharian" or "Kuchean") are **not dialects of one language**. They are distinct languages, as different from each other as French from Spanish. They share common ancestry (Proto-Tocharian) but diverged completely.

**Tocharian A** features:
- Associated with Karasahr and eastern Turfan
- More archaic-liturgical feel in surviving texts
- Primarily Buddhist translation texts — Udānavarga, Āgamas, abhidharma
- Less extensive survival than B

**Tocharian B** features:
- Associated with Kucha (Kizil caves, Kucha monastic complex) and western Turfan
- More extensive literary output — narrative jātakas, Maitreya texts, medical texts, letters, commercial documents
- The language of the Kuchean cultural florescence (see kuchean-music file)
- Preserves more secular/administrative vocabulary alongside Buddhist

**Theological significance of the two-language situation**: Both communities were Buddhist. But they maintained distinct languages — they didn't merge. This means the IE substrate was strong enough to resist fusion even under shared religious culture. The Buddhism unified them theologically; the languages separated them culturally. They were *religiously convergent, linguistically divergent* — a fascinating structural case.

### Key manuscript collections

- **Berlin Turfan Collections**: The largest. German expeditions brought back thousands of fragments, many still in German state museums (now jointly administered post-reunification).
- **Pelliot Koutchéen (Paris, BnF)**: Paul Pelliot's Kuchean materials, including major Tocharian B literary texts.
- **Hoernle Collection (London, BL)**: Earlier finds, pre-systematic excavation.
- **St. Petersburg collection**: Russian expedition materials.

## 2. Buddhist Genres in Tocharian: What Survived

### 2a. Sūtra Translation

Both Tocharian A and B preserve translations of canonical Buddhist texts:

**Udānavarga** (collection of inspired utterances, Buddhist equivalent of a wisdom anthology): The most complete Tocharian translation surviving. The Tocharian A *Udānastotra* and Tocharian B versions allow scholars to study translation choices systematically. When the Sanskrit term *dharma* became Tocharian B *pelaikne* ("law/ordinance"), we can see the translator choosing the closest available native concept and pressing it into new service.

**Āgama / Nikāya material**: Portions of canonical sūtras in both languages. These are translation texts — the translator had a Sanskrit or Prakrit source and rendered it. The surviving choices illuminate what concepts had ready native-language equivalents vs. what required calque or direct borrowing.

**Abhidharma texts**: The scholastic-analytical tradition. Tocharian B preserves what appears to be abhidharma material in fragmentary form. These texts are technically dense — working through them required specialized vocabulary.

### 2b. Vinaya (Monastic Discipline)

Monastic rule-texts survive in both languages. These are practically critical — they govern daily life in the monasteries. The Vinaya vocabulary is interesting because it includes terms for both spiritual practice *and* institutional life: how to handle a dispute between monks, what constitutes a transgression, the procedures for communal decision-making. This means the Tocharian monasteries were not just translating elevated philosophy; they were managing real communities in the language.

**Secular intrusions**: The Vinaya texts and related monastic documents contain some of the most interesting vocabulary because they're practical: weights and measures, agricultural terms, labor disputes, property. This is where Tocharian's non-Buddhist substrate vocabulary surfaces most naturally.

### 2c. Narrative: Jātaka and Avadāna

The *jātaka* genre (stories of the Buddha's previous lives, each illustrating a virtue or karmic principle) and *avadāna* genre (stories of meritorious acts and their results, not restricted to the Buddha's own lives) are heavily represented in Tocharian B especially.

**Why jātakas thrived**: The jātaka story is narrative, entertaining, ethically instructive, and culturally flexible. You don't need to understand abhidharma to engage with a story where the Bodhisattva-in-a-previous-life acts with generosity and is rewarded. These texts were probably used in lay instruction and public teaching — they're the Buddhist literature most accessible to non-specialists.

**Tocharian B jātaka translations** include versions of well-known Sanskrit jātaka texts. What makes them linguistically interesting:
- Where Sanskrit has *bodhi* ("awakening/enlightenment"), Tocharian B must either borrow directly or find a native equivalent
- Where Sanskrit has *karuṇā* ("compassion"), Tocharian B uses terms in the *kretaunt-* family (related to love/mercy)
- The kinship terms used for the Bodhisattva's relationships across lives reveal native IE kinship vocabulary pressed into Buddhist narrative service

**The Maitreyasamiti-Nāṭaka**: The most spectacular Tocharian literary text is not a translation but a *composition* — a dramatic text in Tocharian B (and a related A version) about Maitreya, the future Buddha. This is treated in detail in section 3.

### 2d. Paracanonical and Commentary Literature

Hymns (*stotra*), devotional verses, and commentarial fragments survive. These are often the most creative linguistically — the composer had more freedom than a translator, and the hymns show native IE poetic patterns interacting with Buddhist devotional forms.

## 3. The Maitreya Cult: Why Tocharian Loved the Future Buddha

Of all the Buddhist figures and traditions, **Maitreya** — the future Buddha, currently a bodhisattva dwelling in Tuṣita heaven awaiting his time to descend and bring a new age of the Dharma — is disproportionately prominent in Tocharian materials. This is not random.

### Who Maitreya is

In Buddhist cosmology, the historical Śākyamuni Buddha appeared in a specific world-age and taught the Dharma. The Dharma he taught will, in time, decline and be forgotten. At the end of a vast cosmic period, another Buddha will arise: Maitreya, whose very name comes from *maitrī* (loving-kindness, Tocharian B: *maitri* — a direct borrowing). He will rediscover the Dharma and teach it anew, inaugurating a new golden age.

**Maitreya is fundamentally a figure of eschatological hope** — the one who will come when things have gotten bad enough, the future-redeemer figure of Buddhism. His cult is most intense in communities that feel the present is degraded, the Dharma declining, and the future world-age desperately needed.

### Why Tocharian people were particularly drawn to Maitreya

Several converging factors:

**1. Silk Road vulnerability**: The Tocharian oasis towns were permanently exposed to migration, conquest, and cultural disruption. The Xiongnu, Yuezhi, Kushana, Huna, and eventually Turks and Tang Chinese all passed through or controlled the Tarim Basin at various points. A community that has repeatedly watched political structures collapse has reason to place hope in a *cosmic* future rather than a political one. Maitreya's promised age is beyond any historical dynasty.

**2. The Kizil cave complex**: The most extensive visual Buddhist art from the Tocharian world is the Kizil Caves (Ming-Öy, "Thousand Houses") near Kucha — over 236 caves with murals, many dating from 400–700 CE. Maitreya appears prominently. The famous "musician cave" and other Kizil murals show a Kuchean visual-theological tradition deeply shaped by Maitreya devotion. The caves are a pilgrimage site, meditation retreat, and monastic library simultaneously.

**3. The bodhisattva ideal**: In Mahāyāna Buddhism (which reached Tocharia through its Central Asian corridor), the bodhisattva — one who delays full liberation to help all beings — is the ideal. Maitreya as the supreme bodhisattva currently dwelling in Tuṣita heaven models this exactly: he has the capacity for liberation but waits. This resonates deeply with a community whose own cultural role is *waiting at the crossing* — transmitting, holding, passing through. They didn't originate Buddhism; they carried it. They didn't receive it as final destination; they transmitted it eastward. The Maitreya-who-waits mirrors the Tocharian-who-transmit.

**4. The Maitreyasamiti-Nāṭaka** (Maitreya's Meeting Festival Play)

The *Maitreyasamiti-Nāṭaka* is one of the most remarkable texts to survive from the ancient world: a dramatic performance — a play, in the full theatrical sense — about the veneration of Maitreya, written in both Tocharian A (the "Śardulakarṇāvadāna" adjacent text) and Tocharian B. The extant Tocharian B version runs to thousands of lines.

**Structure**: The drama depicts a council or assembly (*samiti*) in which figures venerate Maitreya, tell stories of merit and past lives, and anticipate Maitreya's future Buddhahood. It includes direct speech, stage directions (in Tocharian), and what appear to be hymns interspersed with narrative.

**Uniqueness**: This is not a translation — it is a *composition* in Tocharian B. The text was created for performance in the Kuchean cultural world. It uses native IE narrative conventions (including features that recall IE oral-epic patterns) to carry Buddhist devotional content. This is the highest literary achievement of the Tocharian tradition: genuinely native Buddhist art.

**Theological payload**: The Maitreyasamiti is not just entertainment. It is a ritualized *enactment of expectation* — the community gathers, performs the drama, and in doing so rehearses their orientation toward the future age. The performance itself is an act of Maitreya-veneration. The drama makes present what it depicts as future.

### Maitreya vocabulary in Tocharian B

| Tocharian B | Sanskrit source | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **maitri** | *maitrī* | loving-kindness; Maitreya's defining virtue and name-source |
| **metrākk** / **metreyye** | *Maitreya* | the name itself, adapted to Tocharian phonology |
| **tuṣit** | *Tuṣita* (heaven) | direct borrowing; Maitreya's current dwelling |
| **bodhisātv** | *bodhisattva* | direct borrowing; enlightenment-being |
| **kaunen** | *kalyāṇa* (good, beautiful) | used of Maitreya's qualities |
| **ypeśne / yparwe** | presence, coming | used of Maitreya's anticipated descent |

## 4. Jātaka and Avadāna Translation Vocabulary: The Forge Territory

The translation vocabulary — the specific choices made when Buddhist story-genres were rendered into Tocharian — is the most linguistically productive territory for YOUSPEAK, because it shows **concepts being formed at the moment of translation**. The translator is forging vocabulary under pressure.

### Core translation-vocabulary patterns

**Pattern 1: Native calque** — taking an IE root and pressing it to carry the Buddhist concept:
- *Pelaikne* (Toch B, "ordinance/law") → *dharma* carrier
- *Klyomo* (Toch B, "noble/elevated") → *ārya* carrier
- *Lakle* (Toch B, "suffering/pain") → *duḥkha* carrier

These words now carry double meaning: their native sense AND their Buddhist loading. The semantic shimmer between the two is the gap YOUSPEAK was built to name (see crossing.md).

**Pattern 2: Direct Sanskrit borrowing** — some concepts had no native analog:
- *Kṣānti* (patience/forbearance) — borrowed whole
- *Bodhi* → *poti* (adapted) — awakening/enlightenment
- *Karma* → *karmmā* (adapted) — action/consequence
- *Nirvāṇa* → *nirvān* (adapted) — extinguishing

**Pattern 3: Compound formation** — creating new words by compounding native and borrowed elements:
- *Pūdñäkte* / *pañäkte* (Toch B) = *pū-* (honorific/reverential prefix, possibly IE root) + *-dñäkte* (related to *ñakte*, "god") — "the god-revered-one" for Buddha

**The avadāna register** (merit-narrative): In avadāna texts, the structure is formulaic — someone performs a meritorious act, the narrative describes the deed, then traces the karmic consequence across many lives. The Tocharian translation vocabulary for this genre includes:

| Tocharian B term | Approximate meaning | Buddhist context |
|---|---|---|
| **aiśamñe** | gift, donation | merit-generating giving (*dāna*) |
| **wīna** | deed, act | karmic act |
| **postäñcai** | future (lives) | karmic future-orientation |
| **waike** | falsehood | the act to avoid; merit from truth-telling |
| **snai lakle** | without suffering | soteriological goal in narrative phrasing |
| **yatsi** | to go, travel | movement through lives, narrative motion |

**The jātaka's specific vocabulary challenge**: Each jātaka story positions the Bodhisattva (future Buddha) in a specific past-life role — as an animal, a king, a poor man, a deity. The narrative must identify "this is who the Bodhisattva was in that life" (often at the story's end: "At that time the noble being [Bodhisattva] was this figure"). This creates pressure on kinship/identity vocabulary:

- *Keś* / *kuse* (Toch B) — relative pronoun used to identify "who this was"
- *Ekä* / *ekañi* — at that time, in that period
- *Ñäś / ñäkcye* — "I/the Buddha (speaking from the present, identifying past self)"

## 5. Convergences — Maitreya and Cross-Traditional Parallels

The Maitreya figure opens striking convergences with non-Buddhist traditions:

### Convergence 1: The Awaited Future Redeemer
**Tocharian Maitreya** + **Jewish/Christian Messianism** + **Zoroastrian Saoshyant** + **Shia Mahdī**: Multiple traditions develop a figure who will appear at the end of a deteriorated age to restore or complete the cosmic order. Maitreya's distinctiveness: the redeemer is *not* a warrior-king or divine-avenger but a *teacher* — the future Buddha comes to teach, not to punish. The eschatological act is a pedagogical act.

### Convergence 2: Loving-Kindness as Redeemer's Name
**Maitreya** (from *maitrī*, loving-kindness) + **Christ** (Greek *Christos*, "anointed," but named *Emmanuel* = "God with us") + **Avalokiteśvara's** core virtue (*karuṇā*, compassion) + the **Paraclete** (Greek for "one called alongside," with connotation of consolation): The awaited one is named for a relational quality — the name IS the virtue. Maitreya's name means "he whose nature is loving-kindness." This is not incidental — the future savior is defined by relationship-toward-others.

### Convergence 3: The Drama-as-Participatory-Eschatology
**Maitreyasamiti-Nāṭaka** (dramatic performance of Maitreya's anticipated coming) + **Christian Passion plays** (enacting the redemptive event as present-participation) + **Jewish Passover Seder** (*as if you yourself came out of Egypt* — the ritual makes the participant a contemporary of the redemptive event) + **Zen Noh theater** (performing the reality while pointing beyond it): ritual drama is not *about* the sacred event but *enacts* it; the performance bridges present and the sacred time it depicts.

### Convergence 4: Translation-as-Sacred-Act
**Tocharian Buddhist translation** + **Wycliffe's "divine necessity" for vernacular scripture** + **Jerome's Vulgate as spiritual labor** + **Origen's Hexapla as comparative-sacred-labor**: the act of translating sacred texts into new languages is not merely linguistic work but spiritual service. The translator preserves the Dharma/Gospel/Torah by giving it a new body. Tocharian is the earliest IE case of full-corpus Buddhist translation — they were doing what Jerome did for Latin and Wycliffe for English, but 900 years before Jerome and 1600 years before Wycliffe.

### Convergence 5: The Language-Community-as-Transmission-Vessel
**Tocharian** (carried Buddhism; then absorbed into Uyghur) + **Etruscan** (carried Sibylline oracles and haruspicy; absorbed into Latin) + **Sogdian** (carried Buddhism, Zoroastrianism, Manichaeism across the Silk Road; absorbed into Persian and Turkic) + **Alexandrian Greek** (carried Jewish scripture as LXX; gradually displaced by Arabic in Egypt): some language communities are not endpoints but *transmission vessels* — they exist, culturally and historically, to carry a tradition from one civilization to another. Their "death" is not extinction but successful delivery. This is a theology of language-community-as-sacrifice: they give themselves to carry what must be transmitted.

## Summary: What Tocharian Buddhist Literature Reveals

1. **Buddhism was not overlay but infrastructure**: In Tocharian communities, the monastery was the economic, educational, and cultural center. Buddhism did not happen inside Tocharian life; Tocharian life happened inside Buddhist structure.

2. **Maitreya's prominence is not random**: A community permanently at a crossing point — vulnerable, transmitting, never fully settled — oriented toward a future redeemer rather than a present guardian. The future-teacher figure is exactly what a transmission-corridor community needs.

3. **Translation vocabulary is philosophical work**: The Tocharian translators were not just converting words. They were making decisions about which IE concepts could carry Buddhist meaning and which required direct borrowing. These decisions are preserved as fossils in the vocabulary — every *pelaikne* for *dharma* records a moment of cross-cultural philosophy.

4. **The Maitreyasamiti is the crown**: A dramatic composition — not a translation but an original — in Tocharian B, performing eschatological hope. This is what a language can do when it has fully assimilated a tradition: not just translate but create. Tocharian Buddhism produced art.
