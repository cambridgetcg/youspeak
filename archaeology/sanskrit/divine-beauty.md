# Sanskrit — Divine Beauty

_The tongue in which beauty is most systematically attached to taste (rasa), auspiciousness (śrī), and attractive-magnetism (lāvaṇya). Sanskrit's beauty-theology is least ontological and most phenomenological: beauty is what is tasted, what radiates, what attracts — named by what it does to the beholder._

### Rasa (taste-aesthetics)

| Form | Transliteration | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| रस | *rasa* | taste, aesthetic-essence, juice, savor |
| भक्ति-रस | *bhakti-rasa* | the taste-of-devotion |
| श्रृङ्गार-रस | *śṛṅgāra-rasa* | the taste-of-love-beauty (one of the nine primary rasas) |
| शान्त-रस | *śānta-rasa* | the taste-of-peace |
| अद्भुत-रस | *adbhuta-rasa* | the taste-of-wonder |

The rasa tradition (Bharata's *Nāṭyaśāstra*, Abhinavagupta's elaboration) treats beauty as the tasted-essence of an aesthetic experience. Nine standard rasas; in Vaiṣṇava bhakti, variations (mādhurya-rasa = sweetness).

### Divine-beauty specific in Vaiṣṇava theology

| Form | Gloss |
|---|---|
| *mādhurya* | the sweetness-beauty of Kṛṣṇa; specifically intimate divine beauty |
| *aiśvarya* | the majestic divine beauty; lordship-beauty |
| *līlā* | divine play; the beauty-of-the-sport-of-GoD |

*mādhurya* and *aiśvarya* function analogously to Sufi *jamāl*/*jalāl* — the intimate-beauty versus the majestic-beauty of divine self-presentation. Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavism specifically privileges *mādhurya* as the highest beauty-of-Kṛṣṇa.

## Semantic territories

### Territory A — śrī as auspicious-radiance

*Śrī* is complex: it names prosperity, auspiciousness, radiance, and the Goddess Lakshmi simultaneously. As a divine attribute, *śrī* is the prosperity-beauty of the divine. Its carrying:

- Q1 in devotional usage (śrī- as honorific prefix on divine names: *Śrī Viṣṇu*, *Śrī Kṛṣṇa*)
- Q3 strongly — śrī draws and prospers
- Q2 partial — śrī is a quality but bundled with prosperity
- Q4 absent — auspicious but not specifically ordered-to-the-bottom

*Śrī* is foundational for devotional beauty-theology but too thick with prosperity-associations for the doxakallos target.

### Territory B — mādhurya as divine-sweetness-beauty

*Mādhurya* specifically names the sweet-beauty of the personal GoD (Kṛṣṇa in Gauḍīya; analogously in other traditions). It is:

- Q1 YES (divine-specific)
- Q3 YES (attracts, through sweetness)
- Q2 YES (ontological-attribute of Kṛṣṇa's form)
- Q4 partial — sweetness is not structural-ordering as such
- Q5 not paired

Strong candidate-donor for a *sweetness-specific* divine-beauty forge. Less load-bearing for the ordered-to-the-bottom aspect of doxakallos.

### Territory C — rasa and the tasted-beauty

Rasa-aesthetics treats divine beauty as *tasted*. The beholder's beholding is gustatory. Abhinavagupta develops this as *camatkāra* — the sudden-taste, the aesthetic-shock of recognition. 

**This is structurally close to doxalgia.** The rasa-tradition names the aesthetic-shock that doxalgia names as structural-ache. The traditions converge on the *event* of beholding-divine-beauty, though they name its quality differently (camatkāra is shock-taste; doxalgia is structural-ache).

Rasa does not give a single-word for the beauty-in-itself; it names beauty-as-tasted, which is phenomenological-relational rather than ontological.

### Territory D — lāvaṇya as attractive-magnetism

*Lāvaṇya* comes from *lavaṇa* (salt). Its metaphor: the quality that makes a dish flavourful — not what the dish is, but the invisible quality that makes it irresistible. Transferred to beauty: the magnetic-attractive-quality that makes a form compelling.

Q-fit:
- Q3 very strong — lāvaṇya is pure call-forth
- Q1 absent — used of humans, arts, forms generally
- Q2 partial — names a quality, but the quality is defined by its effect on the taster
- Q4 absent

Donor potential: lāvaṇya could seed a candidate for the *magnetism* aspect of beauty, but not for the unitary ontological quality.

## Negative space

Sanskrit's beauty-theology is **phenomenological-first, ontological-second**. Beauty is named most richly by what it *does* (tastes, attracts, prospers, draws) rather than by what it *is* in the beholder-independent sense. The uncreated-beauty-in-itself target sits to the side of Sanskrit's main concerns.

What Sanskrit contributes that Greek/Hebrew/Arabic do not:

1. **The gustatory register.** Beauty-as-tasted. Relevant for future sweetness-forges; not load-bearing for doxakallos.

2. **The magnetism register.** Lāvaṇya as the quality-that-draws. Parallel to doxa's calling-forth sense but with aesthetic rather than weight-metaphor.

3. **The play register.** *Līlā* — divine beauty as the sport-of-GoD. Process-beauty, not static-beauty. Relevant for future forges on the event-side.

For doxakallos specifically, Sanskrit contributes methodological validation: multiple traditions converge on "divine beauty needs a compound or dyad or triad to be named fully." *Mādhurya*/*aiśvarya* is parallel to *jamāl*/*jalāl*; both are dyads; neither is a single-word unitary quality. This pattern makes the forge-necessity clearer: a single-word unitary beauty-in-GoD is a genuine gap across traditions.
