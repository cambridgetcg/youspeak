# Catuṣkoṭi — the four-cornered logic

*This is the third Madhyamaka excavation. The first (prapañca) opened the disease — the mind's compulsive proliferation of conceptual constructions onto reality. The second was the broader doctrinal context. Catuṣkoṭi is the therapy — the precise logical instrument Nāgārjuna wields to suspend every position the proliferating mind can take. It is not a negation in the ordinary sense (which would establish "is not"); it is the simultaneous suspension of all four positions, which opens the space in which emptiness (śūnyatā) can be seen. This is the structural-heart of Madhyamaka's method.*

---

## 1. The concept in compact form

**Catuṣkoṭi** (Sanskrit चतुष्कोटि) is a compound of **catuḥ** (four) and **koṭi** (corner, vertex, extremity, limit — from the root kuṭi, to curve/bend, giving "corner" as the point where two lines meet). The term names **the four-cornered logical structure** — the four possible positions one can take on any thesis: (1) it **is** (asti / affirmation), (2) it **is not** (nāsti / negation), (3) it **both is and is not** (asti ca nāsti ca / both), (4) it **neither is nor is not** (naivāsti na nāsti / neither).

The doctrine: in ordinary (two-valued) logic, a thesis is either true or false. Buddhist logic, inheriting the earlier Pali Canon's "undeclared questions" (the Buddha's refusal to answer certain metaphysical questions with any of the four positions), recognizes four positions. Nāgārjuna's Madhyamaka innovation is to **suspend all four simultaneously** — not as a fifth position (a "neither-neither" that becomes its own thesis) but as the systematic demonstration that each of the four fails when examined. The result is not nihilism (position 2) or paradox (position 3) or mystical ineffability (position 4); it is the **opening of the space in which the emptiness of all positions can be apprehended directly**.

The structural marks the forge must preserve:

1. **It is quadri-valent, not bivalent.** The structure recognizes four positions, not two. This is not a failure to reach binary clarity — it is a structural claim that reality (or at least the reality of conceptual constructs) exceeds the binary. The four corners are the complete set of positions available to the conceptual mind; there is no fifth.

2. **It suspends, not negates.** The catuṣkoṭi does not say "position 2 is correct" (is not). It says all four positions fail. The failure is not refutation-of-one-position but the demonstration that the framework within which positions are taken is itself empty. This is what distinguishes catuṣkoṭi from Western reductio ad absurdum (which negates one position to establish another) and from Hegelian dialectic (which synthesizes two into a third).

3. **It is methodological, not ontological.** Catuṣkoṭi is not a claim about what reality IS (that would be position 1) but an instrument for dismantling claims about what reality is. The MMK opens by examining "conditions" (pratyaya) — not to establish a theory of conditions but to show that every theory of conditions falls into the tetralemma and fails. The therapy works on the *claiming*, not on the *claimed*.

4. **It opens onto emptiness, not into it.** The suspension of all four positions is the *doorway* to śūnyatā, not śūnyatā itself. Śūnyatā is not position 4 ("neither is nor is not") — that would reify emptiness into a thesis. The catuṣkoṭi opens the space; what is seen in that space is emptiness. This is why Nāgārjuna says "emptiness itself is empty" (śūnyatāṃ śūnyatām) — even the openness is not a position.

**Sibling-distinctions:** catuṣkoṭi is not prapañca (conceptual-proliferation — the disease that the catuṣkoṭi treats; prapañca is the mind's compulsive generation of the four positions, catuṣkoṭi is the logical instrument that dismantles them); it is not śūnyatā (emptiness — śūnyatā is what catuṣkoṭi opens onto, not what it IS); it is not vyavahāra (conventional truth — the domain in which the four positions are taken and where they function adequately for ordinary purposes; catuṣkoṭi operates at the level of paramārtha-satya, ultimate truth, not vyavahāra-satya); it is not prasaṅga (reductio — the argumentative *method* Nāgārjuna uses, of which catuṣkoṭi is the *structure*). Catuṣkoṭi names the specific four-cornered logical shape; prapañca is what it dissolves; śūnyatā is what it opens onto; prasaṅga is how it argues.

## 3. Primary attestations and scholarly references

**The locus classicus** is Nāgārjuna's *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* (MMK), the opening of Chapter 1 on conditions (pratyaya), which sets the tetralemma as the governing structure of the entire work:

> *na svato nāpi parato na dvābhyāṃ nāpy ahetutaḥ / utpannā jāyate bhāvāḥ saṃvṛdhiḥ svata eva hi //*
>
> Wait — the precise tetralemma formulation appears in MMK v. 1.14 and in the dedicatory verse. The dedicatory verse (maṅgala-śloka) states:

> *ye dharmā hetuprabhavā hetuṃ teṣāṃ tathāgato hy avadat / teṣāṃ ca yo nirodha evaṃvādī mahāśramaṇaḥ //*

But the clearest catuṣkoṭi statement is in the Buddha's earlier refusal to answer the "undeclared questions" (avyākṛta-vastūni) in the Pali Canon (e.g., Majjhima Nikāya 72, the *Aggivacchagotta Sutta*), where the Buddha refuses all four positions on whether the world is eternal, whether the Tathāgata exists after death, etc. Nāgārjuna systematizes this refusal into a logical structure.

The MMK applies the tetralemma systematically to every category: conditions (ch. 1), motion (ch. 2), the senses (ch. 3), the aggregates (ch. 5), desire and the desirer (ch. 6), birth and death (ch. 7), the Tathāgata (ch. 22), and emptiness itself (ch. 24 — "emptiness is empty"). In each case, the argument shows that the entity in question cannot be said to exist (position 1), not exist (position 2), both (position 3), or neither (position 4) — and that the failure of all four opens the space for the conventional functioning of the entity *as empty*.

**Āryadeva's *Catuḥśataka*** (Four Hundred Verses) extends the tetralemma into practical philosophy, showing how attachment to any of the four positions sustains suffering.

**Candrakīrti's *Prasannapadā*** (Clear Words, 7th c. CE) is the authoritative commentary, defending the Prāsaṅgika reading: the catuṣkoṭi is not a thesis about reality but a method of prasaṅga (consequence) — showing that the opponent's own premises lead to all four positions being untenable.

**Scholarly afterlife:** The catuṣkoṭi has been the subject of extensive cross-tradition philosophical engagement. Jñānasrīmitra and Ratnakīrti (Buddhist logicians, 10th–11th c.) attempted to assimilate it to bivalent logic. J. F. Staal (1962) and K. N. Jayatilleke (1963) opened the modern comparative study. Graham Priest (in *Beyond the Limits of Thought*, 2002) and Jay L. Garfield (*Empty Words*, 2002) have argued that the catuṣkoṭi is not merely mystical refusal but a genuine logical structure — one that challenges the universal validity of bivalence without collapsing into contradiction. The cathedral receives the structural-form: a four-valued logic whose therapeutic function is to suspend all four values simultaneously.

## 4. Etymology and family

- **catuḥ**: from Proto-Indo-European *kʷetwor- (four) — cognate with Latin quattuor, English four, Greek τέτταρα. The numeral is across-Indo-European stable; the "four" in catuṣkoṭi is not arbitrary but structural: the four positions exhaust the logical space available to the conceptual mind.
- **koṭi**: from the root kuṭi (to curve, bend) — "corner" as the point where two boundaries meet, the extremity, the vertex. Also: a number (10 million, the "limit" of counting in classical Indian numerology) — the sense of "limit" or "extremity" is primary for the logical sense. The four koṭi-s are the four extremes, the four corners of the logical space.
- **Morphology**: compound noun, feminine. The compound is a *karmadhāraya* (descriptive) — catuḥ modifies koṭi: "four-cornered," "having four corners."
- **Doctrinal-family**: the term sits in the cluster {prapañca (the proliferation catuṣkoṭi dissolves), śūnyatā (the emptiness catuṣkoṭi opens onto), dvaya-satya (the two truths — conventional and ultimate — within which catuṣkoṭi operates at the ultimate level), prasaṅga (the argumentative method), madhyamā-pratipad (the middle way — between the four extremes), hetu (the logical reason catuṣkoṭi examines)}. It presupposes the Pali undeclared-questions tradition and opens into śūnyatā.

## 5. The unnamed region — the gap-shadows

English speakers feel the phenomenon but cannot name it: the logical structure that **suspends all available positions simultaneously** — not as indecision, not as paradox-mongering, but as a precise therapeutic act that opens a space no position can occupy. One encounters this in: the moment when every available framing of a situation has been tried and each fails, not because the situation is unsolvable but because the framing-framework itself is the problem; the therapeutic technique of holding all interpretations in suspension rather than choosing one; the contemplative practice of letting go of every answer to a question rather than accepting any; the mathematical experience of a proof by exhaustion where every case is eliminated and what remains is not a case but the recognition that the case-framework was wrong.

Near-misses, each failing:

| Candidate | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| paradox | names the *appearance* of contradiction; catuṣkoṭi is not contradictory but systematically suspends positions — the four corners are not in tension with each other but are each independently shown to fail |
| apophatic theology | names the *tradition* of negation; catuṣkoṭi is a *logical structure* — it is not theology-specific and applies to any thesis, theological or not |
| dialectic | Hegel's dialectic synthesizes two positions into a third; catuṣkoṭi suspends four positions and does not synthesize — there is no fifth position, only the opening |
| koan | a Zen *practice* that may *employ* catuṣkoṭi-like structure (the mu-koan suspends "is" and "is not"); catuṣkoṭi is the formal *structure*, koan is one *technology* that deploys it |

## 7. Connectable canon-words

- **asraya-paravrttime** (Core, canonized 2026-07-08) — the turning of the basis; structurally distinct (ontological transformation vs. logical instrument) but methodologically complementary: catuṣkoṭi is the instrument that *prepares* the space in which asraya-paravrttime occurs.
- **alayame** (Core, canonized 2026-05-13) — the storehouse-consciousness; the basis whose empty nature catuṣkoṭi demonstrates. Catuṣkoṭi does not say "the basis does not exist" (position 2) but shows that all four positions on the basis fail, revealing its emptiness.
- **rigpame** (Core, canonized) — primordial-awareness; the seeing that occurs in the space catuṣkoṭi opens. Catuṣkoṭi is the clearing; rigpame is what sees in the clearing.
- **debweme** (Core, canonized 2026-07-06) — experiential-truth; the epistemological boundary-truth that catuṣkoṭi's suspension is not merely logical but experiential — the four positions are suspended in lived contemplative practice, not merely in argument.
- **shunyatame** (if forged) — emptiness; the space catuṣkoṭi opens onto. Catuṣkoṭi is the door; śūnyatā is the room.
