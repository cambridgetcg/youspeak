## 1. Overview — Why Georgian Matters for YOUSPEAK

Georgian (ქართული, *kartuli*) is one of the most theologically significant yet underrepresented languages in comparative religious studies. Its importance for YOUSPEAK is structural:

**Kartvelian is a language isolate family.** Georgian, Mingrelian, Laz, and Svan form the Kartvelian (South Caucasian) family, related to no other known language family. Not Indo-European, not Semitic, not Turkic, not Uralic. This makes it one of the few surviving primary linguistic lineages — meaning its theological vocabulary cannot be explained by borrowing from better-known systems. When Georgian develops a word for "grace" or "covenant" or "soul," it is building from Kartvelian roots that predate all contact with these concepts.

**Georgian has the oldest continuously used Christian alphabet tradition.** The Georgian scripts were created (according to tradition) in the 5th century CE for Christian scripture and have been in unbroken liturgical use since. Three distinct scripts exist, each with its own theological register — the only language in the world where three scripts from the same family are all still in active use simultaneously.

**Georgian Christianity is Chalcedonian**, distinguishing it crucially from its neighbor Armenia (Miaphysite). The Georgian Orthodox Church accepted the Council of Chalcedon (451 CE), which defined Christ as having two natures (divine and human) united in one person without confusion, change, division, or separation. This Christological commitment shapes every major theological term.

**The pre-Christian substrate is fascinatingly layered**: Kartvelian indigenous religion + Hellenistic influence from the kingdom of Iberia's contact with the Greek world + Zoroastrian Persian influence + local mountain-spirits that have never been fully Christianized.

**Primary scholars**:

## 2. The Three Scripts — A Theological Archaeology

Unlike any other writing tradition in the world, Georgian has three distinct scripts in use today, all descended from or related to each other, each carrying different registers:

### Mrgvlovani (მრგვლოვანი) — "Round Script" / Asomtavruli (ასომთავრული)

*Asomtavruli* means "majuscule letters" — large, capital letters. This is the **oldest Georgian script**, attested from the 5th century CE. The earliest surviving inscription in Asomtavruli is the **Bir el Qutt inscription** from Palestine (430 CE), carved by Georgian pilgrims in a monastery near Bethlehem — which means the script's first surviving appearance is not in Georgia but in the Holy Land, inscribed by Georgians who had traveled there. The ache of pilgrimage is encoded in the script's first appearance.

**Physical character**: The letters are rounded, monumental, designed for inscription in stone. The roundness (*mrgvlovani* = "rounded") reflects their origin as a script for hard surfaces where rounded strokes cut more cleanly than angular ones. The letters stand alone, not joined — **scriptio continua** (no word spacing) is standard in early Asomtavruli texts.

**Theological register**: Asomtavruli is the script of the **oldest liturgical and biblical texts**. It is used today primarily for:
- Chapter headings in liturgical books
- The first letter of a biblical passage (drop-cap function)
- Inscriptions on church architecture and sacred objects
- Opening lines of the most ancient prayers

It functions as SACRED INSCRIPTION — when you see Asomtavruli in a Georgian church today, you know you are reading something that participates in the oldest Christian Georgian identity. The script itself is a theological claim about antiquity and legitimacy.

**Script creation tradition**: Georgian tradition attributes the creation of the alphabet to **King Parnavaz I** (3rd-2nd century BCE) or to **St. Mesrop Mashtots** (who also created Armenian and, by some accounts, Caucasian Albanian scripts) in the early 5th century CE. The Mashtots attribution is contested by modern scholars but was the dominant medieval account — and it served the theological purpose of linking Georgian script-creation to the Armenian saint whose own alphabet-invention was itself understood as divine revelation. If Mashtots received the Armenian alphabet from God and also gave Georgia its script, then the Georgian alphabet participates in the same divine disclosure.

**The deeper scholarly consensus**: Most paleographers now believe the Georgian script was created by Georgians independently in the late 4th or early 5th century CE, using the conceptual framework of Greek alphabet-design (letters in a fixed order, vowels given dedicated letters) but designing entirely new letterforms. The Georgian letters look like nothing else — not Greek, not Armenian, not Syriac. They are genuinely original forms.

---

### Nuskhuri (ნუსხური) — "Minuscule Script" / Ecclesiastical Script

Nuskhuri is the **cursive ecclesiastical script** that developed from Asomtavruli, emerging fully by the 9th-10th century CE. The name *nuskhuri* likely derives from *nuskha* (copy, manuscript) — it is the script of manuscripts, of the copying monk, of the scripture-copyist's hand moving rapidly across parchment.

**Physical character**: Where Asomtavruli is monumental and stone-suited, Nuskhuri is flowing and pen-suited. The letters are smaller, more connected, designed for rapid writing with a reed pen or quill. It is the Georgian equivalent of uncial or minuscule script in the Latin manuscript tradition.

**Theological register**: Nuskhuri is still the primary script of **Georgian Orthodox Church liturgical books today**. When a Georgian deacon sings from the liturgical book (the *Jvaredi* or the *Kurthxevanis Wignaki*), the text he reads is printed in Nuskhuri. It is the script of active liturgy — not inscription but living worship.

**The copyist's theology**: The Georgian monastic tradition of manuscript copying was one of the great intellectual enterprises of medieval Christianity. Georgian monasteries in Palestine (the Monastery of the Cross, Jerusalem), Mount Sinai (St. Catherine's), and Athos (Iviron Monastery — literally "the Iberian monastery") produced enormous manuscript collections. The Iviron Monastery on Mount Athos, founded c. 980 CE, was a Georgian monastery and remains active today — its collection includes thousands of medieval Georgian manuscripts. Georgian monks were systematically translating Greek patristic literature (Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, John Chrysostom, Pseudo-Dionysius) into Georgian through the 9th-11th centuries — the great "Golden Age" of Georgian monasticism. Nuskhuri is the script in which that theological encounter happened.

---

### Mkhedruli (მხედრული) — "Warrior Script" / Modern Georgian

*Mkhedruli* means "of the cavalry" or "of the warrior" — a secular, civil script that developed in parallel with Nuskhuri from the 11th century CE onward. Where Nuskhuri was the church's script, Mkhedruli was the court's, the marketplace's, the secular literature's.

**Physical character**: Mkhedruli has descenders and ascenders — letters that extend above and below the midline — giving it a flowing, dynamic appearance unlike the more contained Asomtavruli and Nuskhuri. It is the most calligraphically flexible of the three scripts.

**Theological irony**: Today Mkhedruli is the *only* script taught in Georgian schools and used in secular life. It is the script of modernity, of newspapers, of road signs, of text messages. But in the church, Nuskhuri and Asomtavruli remain the sacred scripts. This creates a remarkable bi-script literacy in Georgian Orthodox Christians: they read secular life in Mkhedruli and encounter the sacred in Nuskhuri/Asomtavruli. The transition between scripts is itself a crossing of thresholds — moving from the everyday to the sacred is visible in the letterforms.

**The Rustaveli connection**: The greatest work of Georgian literature, Shota Rustaveli's *Vepkhistqaosani* (The Knight in the Panther's Skin, c. 1200 CE), was written to be read in both registers. Early manuscripts use Nuskhuri or Asomtavruli; modern editions use Mkhedruli. The poem exists across all three scripts — its theological depth is accessible from any of them.

## 3. Khanmeti and Haemeti Texts — Earliest Biblical Georgian

### The Khanmeti Palimpsests

The **Khanmeti texts** are the oldest surviving Old Georgian biblical manuscripts, dating to the 5th-7th centuries CE. They are named for their most distinctive grammatical feature: a verbal prefix **khan-** (or **h-** in later forms) that appears in certain verb paradigms. This prefix is archaic — it appears in the oldest Georgian texts but has entirely disappeared from later Georgian. Its presence marks a text as OLD.

**What Khanmeti texts are**: They are largely **palimpsests** — manuscripts where an older text was scraped off the parchment and a newer text written over it. Modern scholars using ultraviolet photography and multispectral imaging have recovered the underlying Khanmeti texts from beneath the later writings. The act of recovery mirrors the content: ancient words hidden beneath later layers, made visible by new tools.

**Key Khanmeti manuscripts**:
- **Sinai Khanmeti fragments**: Discovered at St. Catherine's Monastery, Sinai; include Old Georgian Gospel texts
- **Codex Petropolitanus (Georgian)**: Contains early Khanmeti Psalter fragments
- **Tbilisi National Centre of Manuscripts** (K collection): Houses the largest collection of early Georgian manuscripts

**Theological significance**: The Khanmeti Gospel texts are a critical witness to early Gospel translation — they predate many standard recensions and preserve readings that parallel the Old Syriac Gospels (*Vetus Syra*). This is historically important: early Georgian Christianity received the Gospel from Syriac-speaking missionaries (tradition names Nino the Illuminatrix, a Cappadocian woman, as the converter of Iberia in 327 CE, but the textual tradition shows Syriac influence alongside Greek). The Gospel text itself carries traces of multiple missionary pathways.

### Haemeti Texts

A closely related group called **Haemeti** (with the verbal prefix **hae-**) represents a slightly later archaic layer. Together, Khanmeti and Haemeti texts form the earliest stratum of Georgian biblical literature.

**YOUSPEAK significance**: The existence of these archaic verbal forms — grammar that carries antiquity in its morphology, that says "this is old, this is foundational" — parallels YOUSPEAK's own interest in roots and origins. The grammar of a language carries theology: old forms signal sacred weight. When Asomtavruli script meets Khanmeti verbal morphology, you have double-layered antiquity — letterform AND grammar both saying "this was first."

## 4. Pre-Christian Georgian Religion — The Substrate

The pre-Christian Georgian pantheon is recoverable mainly through three sources: (1) the Chronicle of Kartli (*Kartlis Tskhovreba*, "Life of Kartli"), a medieval compilation of earlier materials; (2) Greek and Latin geographers describing the kingdom of Iberia/Kolkhis; and (3) folk-religious survivals in Svan and mountain communities that never fully Christianized.

### Armazi (არმაზი) — The Supreme God

**Armazi** was the supreme deity of the kingdom of Iberia (eastern Georgia). His cult center was at **Armaztsikhe** (fortress of Armazi) near the capital Mtskheta, at the confluence of the Mtkvari (Kura) and Aragvi rivers.

**Nature of Armazi**: Greek sources from the 1st century BCE-CE describe a colossal golden statue at Armaztsikhe. The statue was armored, held a sword, wore a golden helmet inlaid with precious stones, and had eyes of beryl and ruby. This is a war-god, a protector of the kingdom, a deity whose presence was embodied in a physical idol at a specific place.

**Etymology**: The name Armazi is almost certainly connected to **Ahura Mazda** — the supreme deity of Zoroastrianism — through an Armenian intermediate form (*Aramazd*). This means the pre-Christian supreme deity of Georgia was already a Zoroastrian-influenced figure, a localization of the Iranian divine principle rather than an indigenous Kartvelian creation. Just as the Armenian Aramazd was a localization of Ahura Mazda, so Georgian Armazi represents the same Iranianizing layer.

**Christian displacement**: The Chronicle of Kartli records that when King Mirian III converted to Christianity (327 CE, following the evangelization by St. Nino), he ordered the destruction of the Armazi idol. A subsequent storm miraculously destroyed it. This narrative is structurally identical to the conversion-of-pagan-idol stories across every Christianized tradition — the idol's destruction is the theological climax of conversion. But unlike some traditions where the pagan deity was simply renamed (Armazi → God), the Georgian Christian tradition is clear: Armazi was a demon, not a form of the true God. The displacement was total in the official theological register.

**Folk survival**: Despite official displacement, the name Armazi survived in place-names, in folk oaths ("by Armazi!"), and in some folk prayers recorded in the 19th-20th centuries. The location of his cult — the fortress hilltop at the confluence of two rivers — became a Christian site (the Svetitskhoveli Cathedral at Mtskheta is downriver). Sacred geography persisted even when sacred personnel changed.

---

### Gatsi and Gaim — The Secondary Deities

The Chronicle of Kartli names two other major deities alongside Armazi at the Armaztsikhe complex: **Gatsi** and **Gaim**.

**Gatsi** (from Zoroastrian *Gad/Gad*, a spirit of fortune and luck, related to the Hebrew *gad*, "good fortune"): A deity of fortune and prosperity. The statue at Armaztsikhe was described as golden with a cloak of gold.

**Gaim** (possibly related to the Iranian concept of the life-principle): A deity of life and vitality. The statue was reportedly silver.

**The triad structure**: Armazi (supreme, war, protection) + Gatsi (fortune) + Gaim (life) = a divine triad governing the three main concerns of a pre-Christian kingdom: military success, material abundance, and vital force. This triad structure is not accidental — it mirrors Zoroastrian divine ordering (Ahura Mazda + the Amesha Spenta) and will map onto the Christian Trinity with different personnel.

**The idol-colors theology**: Gold (Armazi) → Silver (Gaim) → Bronze/Gold (Gatsi) is a descending hierarchy of substance-value. This is consistent with Near Eastern and Iranian divine hierarchies where material value of the cult object signals divine rank. Compare the gold/silver/bronze/iron progression in Daniel's statue (Dan. 2) or the gold → silver → bronze → iron ages of Greek mythology (Hesiod).

### The Indigenous Layer — Kartvelian Cosmology

Beneath the Zoroastrian-influenced official state pantheon, Kartvelian folk religion preserved older structures visible in Svan (the most archaic Kartvelian language) and in mountain-community practice:

**Morige** (მორიგე): A concept of a presiding, watching spirit — neither Zeus nor Ahura Mazda but a being who oversees and orders. *Mori-* is a Kartvelian root meaning "to look after, to guard" (compare Georgian *morigi*, "one on duty, on watch"). The sense is guardian-as-ontological-role rather than creator-as-theological-category. This is a profoundly this-worldly divine function: the divine as what watches over what is happening.

**Adgilis deda** (ადგილის დედა): "Mother of the place" — the spirit that inhabits and guards a specific location. Every spring, crossroads, mountain pass, and valley might have its own *adgilis deda*. This is not animism in the reductive sense but a theology of **place-specific divine presence**: the sacred is not uniformly distributed but concentrated in particular nodes of the landscape. When Christianity came, many *adgilis deda* were renamed — the spring's mother became the Virgin Mary, the crossroads guardian became St. George. But the theological structure (place-specific feminine divine presence) persisted.

**Dali** (დალი): The most fascinating pre-Christian Georgian divine figure — the goddess of the hunt, mountain, and wild spaces. Dali is a golden woman who appears to hunters in the high mountains. She is the protector of wild game and the lover of exceptional hunters — but if her hunter-lover is unfaithful or boastful, she destroys him. The Svan epic cycle about Dali (the *Amirani* cycle) is one of the oldest surviving Georgian literary traditions. Dali is:
- Radiantly beautiful and terrifying
- Associated with gold, high places, and wildness
- A being whose love is both gift and demand — she requires total devotion and punishes divided loyalty
- Connected to the mountain-spirits (*lasharis jvari*, *kviria*) that dot the Caucasus peaks

**YOUSPEAK note**: Dali represents a Kartvelian concept of **sacred encounter with the wild** that has no direct analogue in the standard theological vocabulary. The hunter who encounters Dali crosses a threshold — he enters a relationship with the sacred that imposes conditions. This is not merely the numinous (*fascinans et tremendum*); it is the sacred as *relational claimant* — the divine who comes to you demands something back. There may be a YOUSPEAK forge candidate here: the experience of encountering the sacred in wildness and being claimed by it.

**Amirani** (ამირანი): The Georgian Prometheus — a hero who steals fire from the gods and is punished by being chained to a mountain (in some versions, Mount Kazbegi). The parallel to Prometheus is so precise that scholars debate whether this is an independent Caucasian myth or a very old borrowing. What is distinctive in the Georgian version: Amirani is not simply a benefactor of humanity but a *cosmic rebel* whose punishment sustains the world (the chains must not break or the world will end). His dog licks the chains to thin them; a blacksmith hammers them back every year at the new year. This is **cosmic maintenance through sacred punishment** — a theology of how the world is held together through ongoing suffering and counter-action.

## 5. The Script as YOUSPEAK Lesson

The Georgian three-script system teaches a specific YOUSPEAK lesson about register and sacred access:

**Script IS register.** In Georgian, you don't merely switch vocabulary to move from secular to sacred — you switch the letterforms themselves. The same word written in Mkhedruli (secular) and Nuskhuri (ecclesiastical) is the same word differently held. The material form of the writing is not neutral packaging but participates in what is being said.

**YOUSPEAK implication**: The -me suffix system does something analogous. A word with -me is not merely any word with a suffix — the suffix signals a register, a mode of holding the word, a claim about what this word is doing in the sentence. Just as Asomtavruli signals "this is the inscribed foundational text" and Nuskhuri signals "this is the living liturgy," -me signals "this word is carrying meaning that ordinary language has left unnamed."

**Antiquity as theological claim**: The Khanmeti verbal prefix (khan-) signals "this is old — this goes back to the source." When Georgians write Khanmeti texts, the grammar itself is a form of reverence — they are writing in a way that says "we are as close as possible to the original reception of these words." YOUSPEAK's interest in root-forms and etymology is a cognate instinct: finding the oldest layer of a word's meaning is a way of being close to the first moment it was needed.
