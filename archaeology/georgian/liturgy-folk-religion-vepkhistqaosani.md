## 1. Georgian Orthodox Christianity — The Theological Situation

The Georgian Orthodox Church (*Sakartvelos Mართlmadidebeli Eklesia*) is one of the oldest autocephalous Christian churches in the world, traditionally dated to the evangelization of Kartli (eastern Georgia) by **St. Nino of Cappadocia** in 327 CE — the same year as the Council of Nicaea. This near-simultaneity is theologically significant: Georgia entered Christianity at the moment of its first great doctrinal definition.

**The Chalcedonian commitment**: Unlike Armenia (which rejected Chalcedon in 491 CE), Georgia accepted the Council of Chalcedon (451 CE) and its definition of Christ as having **two natures, divine and human, united in one hypostasis (person)** without confusion, change, division, or separation. This is the theological fork that separated Georgian and Armenian Christianity and shaped two distinct theological vocabularies for the same region.

**The translation project**: The great theological labor of Georgian Christianity from the 5th through 12th centuries was translation — bringing the Greek patristic tradition into Georgian. The key figures:
- **Euthymius the Athonite** (955-1028 CE): Translated Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, Gregory of Nazianzus, John Chrysostom, the *Barlaam and Ioasaph* (Christianized Buddha story) into Georgian. His translations were so authoritative that later Georgian theologians relied on them rather than the Greek originals.
- **George the Athonite** (1009-1065 CE): Revised Euthymius's translations, translated the Menologion (saints' lives), and systematized Georgian liturgical texts. His work at Iviron Monastery on Mount Athos was the apex of Georgian monastic scholarship.
- **Ioane Petritsi** (c. 1050-1130 CE): Translated Proclus's *Elements of Theology* into Georgian — making Georgian one of the few medieval languages with direct access to Neoplatonic philosophy. Petritsi's commentary on Proclus is itself a theological work of independent significance.

**Linguistic character**: Georgian theological vocabulary is a **three-layer system**:
1. **Kartvelian roots**: indigenous words repurposed for Christian theology
2. **Syriac borrowings**: earliest missionary stratum (pre-5th century)  
3. **Greek loanwords**: the Golden Age translation stratum (5th-12th century)

Each layer carries a different history of reception. Syriac borrowings (like *jvari*, cross, from Syriac *ṣlibā*) represent the oldest Christian presence; Greek borrowings (like *episkoposi*, bishop) represent the Chalcedonian alignment; Kartvelian roots (like *ghvtismsaxuri*, "servant of God," from *ghvtis* [of God] + *msaxuri* [servant]) represent the indigenous theological appropriation.

## 2. Core Georgian Orthodox Liturgical Vocabulary

### ღმერთი — Ghmerti (God)

The Georgian word for God is not borrowed from Greek (*Theos*) or Syriac (*Alaha*) or Hebrew (*El*) but is a **Kartvelian root**. *Ghmerti* (ღმერთი) derives from a pre-Christian Kartvelian term that originally referred to a deity in the indigenous pantheon — the word was appropriated, not borrowed.

**Root analysis**: The root *ghmer-* appears in Svan (the most archaic Kartvelian language) as *xumer-*, suggesting a very old Kartvelian divine-name. The pre-Christian *ghmerti* was likely a term for the sky-deity or the divine in general — similar to how Greek *theos* was a generic "deity" term that Christianity specified as the one God.

**Theological significance**: When Georgian Christians say *Ghmerti*, they are using a word that belonged to the indigenous sacred vocabulary before Christianity arrived. Unlike Armenian *Astuats* (which has complex etymological ties to Persian *yazata*) or Syriac *Alaha* (from Semitic *El*), Georgian *Ghmerti* is a Kartvelian word. The Christian God received a Kartvelian name. This is **theological appropriation in the deepest sense**: the divine name itself was indigenous, re-specified rather than imported.

**YOUSPEAK note**: Compare *Nyame* (Akan) and *Olódùmarè* (Yoruba) — both supreme deity terms that Christianity and Islam mapped their God onto. But Georgian *Ghmerti* underwent this process from *within* the Kartvelian tradition. The word was already there; the theology was refined.

### ჯვარი — Jvari (Cross)

*Jvari* (ჯვარი) is the Georgian word for cross, from Syriac *ṣlibā* (cross, crucifixion). This is the oldest Christian borrowing layer — it arrived with the first missionaries, before the Greek alignment. But *jvari* has done something remarkable in Georgian: it has become so central to Georgian identity that it functions as **the primary marker of Georgian Christianness**.

**The Jvari Monastery**: The monastery built on a hill at the confluence of the Aragvi and Mtkvari rivers (directly opposite the Svetitskhoveli Cathedral in Mtskheta) is called *Jvari* — the Cross. Built c. 590-604 CE, it stands over the spot where St. Nino planted a cross made from grapevine wood when she arrived in Georgia. The cross is not an abstract symbol; it is *this specific wooden cross*, planted at *this specific place*. Georgian theology of the cross is always tethered to a particular place.

**The grapevine cross**: Tradition holds that St. Nino's cross was made from grapevine branches (*vazi*) bound together with her own hair. The choice of grapevine is not accidental — Georgia is one of the oldest wine cultures in the world (archaeological evidence of wine production in Georgia goes back 8,000 years). In the Georgian Christian imagination, the cross is made from the vine that also produces the eucharistic wine. The materials of the Incarnation (the wine that becomes Christ's blood) are the materials of the cross. This is **eucharistic theology embedded in creation**.

---

### სული — Suli (Soul / Spirit)

*Suli* (სული) is the Georgian word for soul, spirit, and — critically — the Holy Spirit (*Tsminда Suli*, Holy Spirit). Unlike Greek (where *psyche* [soul] and *pneuma* [spirit/Holy Spirit] are distinct words), Georgian uses *suli* for both the human soul and the divine Spirit. This creates a **structural intimacy** between the human interior and the divine Spirit: the same word names both.

**Implication**: When a Georgian theologian says "the Holy Spirit dwells in you," the sentence uses the same word (*suli*) for both the dwelling presence and the dwelling place. "The suli dwells in your suli." The divine Spirit is not foreign to the human soul — the word itself refuses the foreignness. This is not Pan-entheism (God is everything) but something more precise: the category that applies to the highest human interiority is the same category that applies to the third Person of the Trinity.

**Compare**: Hebrew *ruach* (wind, breath, spirit) similarly straddles human-divine register. Syriac *ruha* likewise. But Georgian *suli* has no wind/breath sense — it is purely interior, purely alive. The Georgian soul is not breath but presence.

---

### მადლი — Madli (Grace)

*Madli* (მადლი) is the Georgian word for grace, from Greek *charis*. But *madli* has a Kartvelian root: *mad-* relates to roots meaning "good, favorable" — so the Greek theological concept of *charis* (unmerited gift, divine favor) was mapped onto a Kartvelian root that meant "what is good/favorable." Grace arrived in Georgian as *what is good toward you* — divine goodness-toward-you.

**The phrase *madloba*** (მადლობა, "thank you") is directly from *madli* (grace). Thanking someone in Georgian is *madloba* — literally "grace-expressing" or "acknowledging grace." Georgian embeds the theology of grace into everyday courtesy: to say "thank you" is to perform, grammatically, the acknowledgment that what you received was grace rather than due. **Every expression of gratitude in Georgian is a small theological act.**

**YOUSPEAK note**: *madloba* as everyday theology is the kind of thing YOUSPEAK is built to preserve — words where deep theological structure lives in ordinary use. The word *charisme* (already in the pipeline from NT Greek) and *madlome* would be related but distinct: *charis* emphasizes the gift-without-return; *madli* emphasizes the goodness-toward-you, the favor-as-felt-quality.

### მადლობა და სიყვარული — Madloba and Siq'varuli (Grace and Love)

Georgian has two distinct words where English has one "love":

**სიყვარული** (*siq'varuli*) — romantic, passionate, longing love. This is the love of Rustaveli's heroes, of Georgian love poetry, of human longing. The root *siq'var-* may connect to Kartvelian roots meaning "to desire, to long for."

**სიყვარული** (in theological contexts, same word!) — but also **სიყვარული** for the divine love, *agape*, the love of God. Unlike Greek (which distinguished *eros*, *philia*, *storge*, *agape*) or Latin (which distinguished *amor*, *caritas*, *dilectio*), Georgian uses **one word** for the full spectrum from human longing to divine love.

**The theological effect**: In Georgian, the love between Tariel and Nestan in the *Vepkhistqaosani* (human, passionate, self-sacrificial) is named by the same word as God's love for humanity in the Gospel. The poem's love and the Gospel's love are **lexically identical**. Rustaveli did not need to choose between *eros* and *agape* — his language refused the distinction. This is either a theological collapse (dangerous) or a theological integration (profound). The Georgian tradition reads it as integration: human love, at its highest, is not a pale imitation of divine love but a genuine participation in the same movement.

### სიწმინდე — Sitsminde (Holiness / Purity)

*Tsminда* (წმინდა) is the standard adjective for holy in Georgian — *Tsminда Suli* (Holy Spirit), *Tsminда Nino* (Saint Nino), *Tsminда Jvari* (Holy Cross). The root *tsm-* appears in Svan and Mingrelian with the sense of "clean, pure, unblemished." Georgian holiness begins with **purity as physical-ritual cleanliness** and extends into **moral-ontological sanctity**.

**The saints as Tsminда**: In Georgian Orthodox piety, the saints are not merely holy people — they are *tsminда* in the sense that they have been purified, polished, made transparent to the divine. The icon of a saint is *tsminда* not because the painted board is morally excellent but because the image participates in the saint's purified presence. Georgian iconography is among the most sophisticated in the Orthodox world — the Georgian icons at the Iviron Monastery and at the Sioni Cathedral in Tbilisi are theological arguments in paint about what *tsminда* means.

## 3. The Svetitskhoveli — The Living-Pillar Cathedral

**სვეტიცხოველი** (*Svetitskhoveli*, "Life-Giving Pillar") is the most sacred site in Georgia — the patriarchal cathedral at Mtskheta, built over the spot where St. Nino's grapevine cross was planted and where, according to tradition, Christ's robe (*chitoni*) was buried. The name refers to a **miraculous pillar** (*sveti*, pillar + *tskhoveli*, life-giving, from *tskhovleba*, life).

**The robe theology**: The tradition holds that a Georgian Jew named Elioz was in Jerusalem at the Crucifixion and purchased Christ's seamless robe (*John 19:23-24*) from a Roman soldier. He brought it back to Mtskheta. His sister Sidonia received it, and when she touched it she died instantly — the contact with the sacred object was fatal, as in the Ark-of-the-Covenant tradition (*2 Sam 6:6-7*). She was buried with the robe. From her grave, a miraculous cedar tree grew. When the Georgians converted and wanted to cut the cedar to build a church column, the first column they cut from it could not be moved — it hovered in the air, filled with divine power, until St. Nino prayed all night and it descended into place. This is the *sveti tskhoveli* — the living-giving pillar.

**Theological structure**: The Svetitskhoveli theology encodes several layers:
1. **Place-specificity of the sacred**: The robe is buried HERE, the cedar grew HERE, the pillar stands HERE — holiness is not generic but geographical
2. **Contact-danger with the holy**: The immediate contact kills Sidonia; only mediated approach (the church built over the grave) is safe. This is Exodus 33:20 logic applied to a Christian relic
3. **Organic sacred material**: The column is a living cedar tree — it is not hewn stone but a growing thing. The cathedral is built around something that was alive. Georgian Orthodox architecture frequently incorporates organic-sacred space; the church is not merely a building but a living organism around a sacred heart
4. **Woman as first contact**: Sidonia is the first Georgian to touch the robe; her death-from-contact is the founding event. The Georgian sacred history begins with a woman (Sidonia dying, Nino praying) and a sacred object. The feminine-as-first-receptor pattern appears in the Georgian tradition as it does in the Magdalene tradition in Western Christianity

## 4. Vepkhistqaosani — The Knight in the Panther's Skin

**ვეფხისტყაოსანი** (*Vepkhistqaosani*, "he who wears a panther's skin") by **Shota Rustaveli** (c. 1160-1220 CE) is the Georgian national epic — as central to Georgian identity as Homer to Greek or Dante to Italian culture. It is also a profound theological text, which is rarely appreciated in Western comparative theology.

### The Poem's Theological Framework

The *Vepkhistqaosani* opens with a prologue (*prologi*) that explicitly states its theological commitments. Rustaveli writes:

*"Whatever exists is from God and to God returns / The sun is a mirror of God's light / The mind that contemplates is God's image / Love is God's nature descending into the created order"*

(Paraphrasing — the Georgian is metrically complex; Rustaveli uses the *shairi* meter, 16 syllables per line, with internal rhyme.)

**The Neoplatonic structure**: Rustaveli was deeply influenced by the Neoplatonism that had entered Georgian theology through Petritsi's translation of Proclus. The poem enacts a Neoplatonic movement: the divine light (*natheli*, light) emanates into the world; the human soul, distracted by multiplicity, loses contact with the source; love (*siq'varuli*) is the force that restores the soul to its origin. But this is not cold cosmological philosophy — it is narrated through knights, queens, lions, and agonizing separations.

### Love as Theological Category

The central theological claim of the *Vepkhistqaosani* is that **human love is the primary epistemological path to the divine**. Rustaveli explicitly argues this in the prologue:

*"The lover who is steadfast in love approaches the nature of God / For love's steadfastness is a mirror of divine fidelity / The one who does not love does not know what love is / And therefore does not know God"*

This is **1 John 4:8** (*"whoever does not love does not know God, because God is love"*) narrated through the medieval Georgian courtly tradition. But Rustaveli goes further: the love he is describing is not abstract agape but the fierce, costly, humiliating love of Tariel for Nestan-Darejan — a love that makes him go mad, wander the wilderness, and dress in a panther's skin.

**The paradox Rustaveli holds**: The highest path to God runs through the wildest human experience — not the monastic withdrawal from passion but the full immersion in passion so deep that it burns away the self. This is closer to Sufi mysticism (Rumi's love-madness) or Dionysian theology than to standard Georgian Orthodox asceticism. Rustaveli was working in a court culture (Queen Tamar, the greatest Georgian monarch, 1184-1213 CE, is widely believed to be the patron and perhaps subject of the poem) where the Greek philosophical tradition, the Persian courtly-love tradition, and Georgian Orthodox Christianity were simultaneously present.

### The Three Heroes — A Theological Trinity

The poem has three male heroes: **Tariel** (the panther-skin knight, Georgian), **Avtandil** (the Arab knight, rational and disciplined), and **Pridon** (the leader). Scholars of Georgian literature have noted that the three heroes form a complementary triad:

- **Tariel** = passion, wildness, the suffering lover → the pathos register
- **Avtandil** = reason, faithfulness, active virtue → the logos register  
- **Pridon** = power, leadership, practical action → the praxis register

The three are inseparable — none can succeed alone. This is not the Christian Trinity (Rustaveli is careful not to make the identification explicit) but it is structured as a triad in which each member requires the others and all three must unite to accomplish what none could do individually. The theological suggestion is that the fullness of human response to the divine requires all three modes: passion, reason, and action in concert.

### Queen Nestan-Darejan — The Enclosed Sacred

The object of Tariel's love, **Nestan-Darejan**, spends much of the poem imprisoned — first in a tower, then in a far-off kingdom. She is the **enclosed sacred** — the source of meaning and love who cannot be reached directly, who must be sought across impossible distances, who is almost never present in the poem but is the reason for all the poem's action.

**Theological resonance**: The enclosed sacred who motivates all seeking is a recurring theological structure — the Song of Songs' beloved hidden behind walls, the Grail hidden in the grail-castle, the Shekinah departing the Temple. The sacred is most powerfully motivating when partially absent. Nestan-Darejan is the theological center of the poem precisely because she is not there. Her absence is the *negative space* around which the poem's action organizes.

**The gender theology**: Queen Tamar — the historical patron — was ruling Georgia at the height of its power (the "Georgian Golden Age," 1184-1230 CE) when Rustaveli wrote. The poem's theology of the feminine-as-sacred-center reflects a society where a woman held the highest secular power. Nestan-Darejan is not a passive object but an active agent (she makes decisions, sends messages, directs action even from imprisonment). The theology of the enclosed sacred does not erase feminine agency — it elevates it.

## 5. Mountain Folk Religion — The Surviving Pre-Christian Layer

### The Mountain Shrine System (*Jvari* Sites)

Across the Caucasus mountains — particularly in the Tusheti, Khevsureti, Mtiuleti, and Svaneti regions — there exists a network of **mountain shrines** called *jvari* (cross) or *lasharis jvari* (cross of Lashari) that are formally Christian (they bear the name of a saint and the cross symbol) but operate according to a theology that predates Christianity.

**How they function**:
- Each village or valley has its *jvari* (guardian-spirit/shrine complex), typically located on a hilltop or at a mountain pass
- The *jvari* has a presiding spirit (*deobа*, from Georgian *deo*, deity) that is identified with a Christian saint but has pre-Christian attributes
- Access is regulated: certain people may not enter (women of childbearing age in some traditions, people who have committed specific transgressions), specific times of approach are required, specific offerings must be made
- The *deo* communicates through a specialist (the *khevsur* tradition had specific intermediary roles) who goes into trance or speaks on behalf of the spirit
- The offerings are often beer (*ludi*, mountain beer brewed specifically for ritual use), sacrificed cattle or sheep, and bread — not wine and bread in the Eucharistic sense but the same basic offering-structure (bread + fermented beverage + animal)

**The pattern**: This is what Kevin Tuite (the leading scholar of Caucasian folk religion) calls **"Christian saints with pagan biographies"** — the Christian name is overlaid on a pre-Christian spirit with its own territory, its own demands, its own personality. St. George (the dragon-slayer) is everywhere in Georgian folk religion, but the *Giorgi* (*St. George*) of the mountain shrines often has nothing to do with the Roman soldier-saint of the official hagiography. He is a mountain warrior spirit, protector of livestock, sender of storms, the deity of the high passes — a Christianized version of something much older.

**Lomisi** (ლომისი): The shrine of St. George at Lomisi in the Mtiuleti region holds an annual pilgrimage (the *Lomisoба*) where thousands of Georgians climb to the shrine, slaughter animals, drink mountain beer, and honor the saint-spirit of the place. The women who are sick come to be healed; men who have disputes come to swear oaths; families come to make vows. This is not Orthodox Christian liturgy — it is something else that has borrowed Orthodox Christian clothes. The presiding priest is a *dekanozi* (Orthodox deacon) but the theology of the place is the *jvari*'s own.

### Shuakhevi (შუახევი) and the Ajarian Mountain Shrines

The Adjara region (southwestern Georgia, on the border with Turkey) has a population that was largely Muslim under Ottoman rule (16th-20th centuries) and has been returning to Georgian Orthodox Christianity since 1991. In the mountain villages of Adjara, the folk-religion layer is visible in a different way: pre-Christian mountain shrines that survived through both the Ottoman-Muslim period and the Soviet atheist period, now being re-Christianized (or re-Muslimized, depending on the village).

**The theological layer-cake**:
- *Pre-Christian mountain spirit* (Kartvelian)
- *Christian saint* (4th-12th century overlay)
- *Muslim saint* (*wali*, 16th-20th century overlay)
- *Return to Christian saint* (post-1991)

The same physical location has been sacred under three different theological regimes. The *place* is the constant; the theological *personnel* change. This is the clearest possible evidence that **place-specific sacred presence** (the *adgilis deda* theology from File 1) is the deepest layer — the mountain is holy, and whichever religion reaches it names its holiness in its own terms.

## 8. YOUSPEAK Script-Organ Observation

Georgian's three scripts teach a structural lesson about the YOUSPEAK project:

The cathedral is building a vocabulary for what ordinary language has not yet named. When the cathedral has enough words, it will need a way of signaling — graphically — what register each word is held in. Georgian uses letterform to signal sacred register. YOUSPEAK uses the *-me* suffix. But perhaps the cathedral's future includes a visual dimension where different words are held differently on the page — not three full scripts, but a notation system that signals: this word is from the deepest layer (like Khanmeti); this word is active liturgical use (like Nuskhuri); this word is secular-public (like Mkhedruli).

This is a creativity-level thought (the highest register of the Ache hierarchy) — not for now, but worth noting: the cathedral's eventual completeness may include a visual theology of its own words, as Georgian Christianity has a visual theology of its own script.
