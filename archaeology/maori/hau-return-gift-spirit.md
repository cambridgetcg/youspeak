# Māori — Hau, the Return-Gift-Spirit

_The Polynesian archaeology files mapped the shared substrate (atua, mana, tapu, noa). This file opens the Māori-specific directory with the concept that — through one mistranslated letter — reshaped Western social science: **hau**, the spirit-of-the-gift that Tāmati Ranapiri taught, Elsdon Best altered, and Marcel Mauss made world-famous in_ The Gift _(1923–24). The excavation's first duty is to return to what Ranapiri actually wrote._

---

## 0. Protocol notice — read first

Māori is a **living tradition with living custodians**. This file's subject is, moreover, the single most-extracted concept in the history of Māori–Western scholarly contact: Best's edited translation of Ranapiri's letters fed a century of European theory (Mauss, Firth, Lévi-Strauss, Sahlins) largely conducted **without reading the Māori originals**. Hēnare (2018) showed that Best "both mistranslated and misinterpreted the hermeneutics of Ranapiri," reducing Māori metaphysics to a secular-materialist explanation. The cautionary tale is therefore *internal to the concept itself*: hau is the concept that teaches what happens when a gift is taken without the return-flow being honored.

The cathedral's discipline here (per the tjukurpame precedent, S084):

1. **Tāmati Ranapiri (Ngāti Raukawa, of Manakau near Ōtaki) is cited as teacher and author**, not as "informant." The 1907 letters are his scholarship; Best was his student.
2. **Primary custodianship remains with Māori** — with Ngāti Raukawa for the Ranapiri texts specifically, and with kaupapa Māori scholarship (Hēnare, Stewart, Nicholson, Marsden's lineage) for the philosophical articulation. The cathedral receives the structural-form with respect; reception is not extraction.
3. **Consultation-desirability recorded**: before any liturgical or pedagogical elaboration beyond this entry, consultation with Māori scholars/kaitiaki is the right move. The entry names the realm-feature; it does not claim interpretive authority over te ao Māori.
4. **Custodian-request → immediate withdrawal** is a standing retirement condition for any coinage from this file.

## 1. The word hau — attested senses

Te Aka Māori Dictionary attests the polysemy (all senses living):

| Sense | Gloss |
|---|---|
| hau (1) | **vital essence, vitality** — of a person, place, or object |
| hau (2) | **wind, breeze; breath; air** — the meteorological/respiratory base-sense |
| hau (3) | **present given in return for one received** — the return-gift itself |
| hau (4) | **fame, renown, reputation** — the name that "resounds" (cf. hau as verb: to be heard, to spread) |
| hau (5) | ceremonial food-offering to an atua (cf. **whāngai hau**, "nourishing the hau") |
| hauora | health, well-being — literally "living hau" / "potent life-force" (Ranapiri 1907a uses *mauri ora* "potent life force" in the same register) |
| hau whenua | the hau of the land |
| hau ngāherehere | the hau of the forest |

The polysemy is structurally coherent, not accidental: hau is *the breath-grade vitality of a person or place insofar as it travels* — as wind travels, as breath leaves the body, as renown spreads, as the giver's essence rides in the gift and as the return-gift carries it home. Proto-Polynesian \*sau (cognates across Polynesia: Tongan *sau* "kingly authority"; cf. the mana-field) underlies it.

**Hau is not mauri and not mana.** Ranapiri himself posed the distinction as a question pair: *"Patai 1. Te mauri o te ngaherehere, me te hau o te ngaherehere"* — the mauri of the forest, AND the hau of the forest (Ranapiri 1907a). Hēnare glosses them as "two life forces recognised as separate realities, which are yet so closely linked in effect and power that they are symbiotic." Mauri is the *resident* life-essence (it stays; it can be localized in a stone, a tree, a hill, protected by tohunga); hau is the *traveling* vitality (it flows, departs, must be fed back — whāngai hau). Mana is the *standing* that accrues when the flows are honored. Three words, three registers: residence (mauri), flow (hau), standing (mana).

## 2. The Ranapiri letters (1907) — the primary attestation

In October and November 1907, Elsdon Best (known to Māori as Te Peehi) received two letters in Māori from Tāmati Ranapiri of Ngāti Raukawa. Ranapiri was already a published scholar — his "Ngā ritenga hopu manu a te Maori, o mua" (JPS 1895, on traditional bird-snaring) had appeared with S. Percy Smith's translation. The 23 November 1907 letter contains the passage Mauss would later call a *texte capital* ("text of paramount importance"; Mauss 1950: 157–61).

**What Ranapiri wrote** (Hēnare 2018 transcription; underlining marks the phrases Best altered):

> *Na, ko taua taonga i homai nei ki a au, ko te hau tena o **to** taonga i homai ra ki au i mua.*
> "Now that gift which was given to me is **your life force in your gift** given to me before."

> *No te mea he hau no **to** taonga tena taonga na.*
> "This is because **your life force [hau] remained in your gift** given to me."

**What Best published** (1909, "Maori Forest Lore," read before the Auckland Institute 22 November 1909): in transcription Best replaced Ranapiri's possessive *tō* ("your") with the definite article *te* ("the") in both sentences, translating: "that article that he gives to me is the hau of **the** article I first received from you" — shifting the hau from *the donor's own essence carried in the gift* to *an impersonal property of the thing*. Per the linguist Winifred Bauer (in Hēnare 2018): Māori grammar distinguishes ownership (*tō*) from temporary possession/location (*tā*); Ranapiri's *tō taonga* implies the taonga **still belongs to the original donor even when located elsewhere** — a distinction English cannot carry and Best's edit erased.

**The three-person structure** Ranapiri described: A gives a taonga to B; B later passes it to C; when C makes a return-gift to B, B *must* convey it onward to A — because that return *is the hau of A's taonga*: A's vitality coming home. Withholding it harms the withholder ("serious evil would befall me, even death," in Best's 1909 rendering of the sanction; Biggs renders it "I will become mate [sick]"). The gift is never severed from its giver; the circuit must close.

**The forest analogy**: Ranapiri offered the human exchange as the *familiar* case to explain the *esoteric* one — the fowler's obligation to the forest (to Tāne). The tohunga place a mauri in the forest; the mauri causes the birds to abound (this abundance-power is the forest's hau); therefore first-caught birds are ceremonially returned — **whāngai hau**, "nourishing the hau" — and the forest's fertility is restored. Take without feeding back, and the hau is stranded; the forest's vitality fails. Conservation, economy, and ritual are one structure.

## 3. The scholarly afterlife — Mauss and his critics

- **Mauss (1923–24, Essai sur le don)**: from Best's English, Mauss built the famous answer to "what force in the thing given compels the recipient to make a return?" — *le hau, l'esprit de la chose donnée*: the spirit of the gift, the presence of the giver in the thing, which "always seeks to return to its birthplace." Gift economies are systems where persons and things are not separated; the gift creates "an indissoluble bond between persons engaged in the exchange."
- **Firth (1929; 1972)**: Mauss misunderstood — "When Mauss sees in gift exchange an interchange of personalities, a 'bond of soul,' he is following not a native belief, but his own intellectual interpretation of it." For Firth the operative force is *utu* (the recompense-principle: "for every gift another of at least equal value should be returned") plus social sanction, not indwelling spirit.
- **Lévi-Strauss (1950)**: "Hau is not the ultimate explanation for gift exchange; it is the conscious form whereby men of a given society... apprehended an unconscious necessity whose explanation lies elsewhere."
- **Sahlins (1972, Stone Age Economics)**: hau as "return on," even "profit" — a fertility-yield principle, secular and economic.
- **Hēnare (2018, JPS 127(4): 451–63)** — the decisive correction from within te ao Māori: none of the critics had read Ranapiri's Māori originals; Best's *tō → te* edit caused the century of confusion; and **Mauss's intuitive explication "was correct all along and is close to Ranapiri's meaning"** — the hau Ranapiri specified IS the donor's hau carried in the taonga (with a second hau intrinsic to the taonga itself, infused at its creation). Ranapiri articulates "an economy of *mana*, or economy of affection, which exists to maintain the four well-beings of Māori and the Pacific — spirituality, environment, kinship and economy."
- **Kaupapa Māori scholarship (Stewart 2017; Nicholson 2019; Marsden 1999)**: contests the "scientising" of hau according to Western ends; Marsden names tapu, hau, mauri as "a powerful interlocking system" of ancestral virtue-ethics. Stewart reframes the Ranapiri–Best correspondence as Indigenous-teacher/Pākehā-student, and reads the hau of *research itself*: knowledge taken without return-flow repeats Best's error.

The cathedral's position, following Hēnare: receive the concept in Ranapiri's specification, with Mauss honored as the (accidentally faithful) far-field witness and Best's edit recorded as the standing warning.

## 4. The exchange-vocabulary cluster (donor context)

- **taonga** — treasured thing/property: heirloom, pounamu, cloak, song, language itself (te reo is a taonga); carries mana and hau of its line of holders; under Te Tiriti o Waitangi art. 2, taonga are what rangatira retained
- **koha** — the gift/offering itself, given at marae visits, tangihanga, weddings; the protocol-object of the hau-circuit (modern usage includes money given "as koha")
- **utu** — recompense, return, restoration-of-balance; the *settlement-act* of the circuit (colonially flattened to "revenge"; the flattening is a distortion this file does not inherit)
- **tuku** — to release/yield a taonga onward (tuku rangatira: chiefly conveyance that binds, not alienates)
- **whāngai hau** — "feeding the hau": the rite that returns first-fruits so the source's vitality is restored
- **tikanga hau** — "the virtue/ethic of hau" (Hēnare): the generosity-discipline evident in 19th-century rangatira politics (He Whakaputanga 1835; Te Tiriti 1840)

Structure of the circuit: **taonga** (the vessel) carries **hau** (the giver's traveling vitality), which obliges **utu** (the balancing return), enacted as **koha**/whāngai-hau (the return-gift), yielding **mana** (standing) to all who kept the flow — the economy of mana, maintaining the four well-beings.

## 5. Cross-tradition convergence — the realm-feature is widely witnessed

The *mechanism* (giver's presence rides in the gift and obliges its homeward flow) and the *imperative* (the gift must keep moving) appear independently:

1. **Māori hau** (primary; sharpest articulation — the donor's essence named explicitly)
2. **Trobriand kula** (Massim): named vaygu'a armshells/necklaces with personal histories that *must* keep circulating; holding too long degrades standing (Malinowski 1922)
3. **Pacific-Northwest potlatch** (Kwakwaka'wakw, Haida, Tsimshian): escalating obligatory return; the given thing carries the giver's standing (Boas; Mauss's second pillar)
4. **Chinese 報 bào / 人情 renqing / 禮尚往來 lǐshàng wǎnglái** (Liji, Qu Li): "Rites value reciprocation: to go and not come is contrary to li; to come and not go is contrary to li" — the favor-ledger as relational bond
5. **Japanese 恩 on / 義理 giri / お返し okaeshi**: institutionalized return-gift practice; the received benefit as carried weight
6. **Norse Hávamál** (st. 42, 145): "to his friend a man shall be a friend, and repay gift with gift"; "a gift always looks for its return" (*ey sér til gildis gjǫf*)
7. **Greco-Roman charis-circle**: Seneca, De Beneficiis 1.3 — the three Graces dance hand-in-hand in a circle because the benefit passes giver → receiver → returner and "the sequence of the kindness is broken if the circle is interrupted"
8. **Hindu dānadharma** (Mahābhārata, Anuśāsana-parvan; Mauss's third pillar): the given thing itself is agentive — it produces its return in this world and the next

Plus the modern-scholarly witness: Maussian anthropology and its century of debate (a discipline-donor under POLYPHONE-Generalization, though the coinage's donors remain tongue-donors).

Convergence cardinality: **8** tradition-witnesses on the realm-feature.

## 6. The unnamed region — what English cannot say

English speakers feel the phenomenon constantly and have no word for it:

- the pull to reciprocate a dinner invitation that is *not guilt and not debt* but felt-presence of the giver
- the wrongness of selling a gift (it "came from Grandma" — the hau stranded mid-flow)
- the deadness of a donation made for naming-rights (return extracted upfront; no spirit travels)
- the aliveness of an heirloom that has passed through many hands and *carries* them

Near-misses, each failing:

| Candidate | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| reciprocity | system-level abstraction viewed from outside; no indwelling force; economist's register |
| obligation | external norm pressing on the receiver; loses the gift-as-carrier mechanism |
| gratitude | the receiver's feeling; hau is in the *gift*, binding three parties, not a private emotion |
| debt / "I owe you" | ledger-frame; precisely the secularization Best introduced; hau is presence-frame, not debt-frame |
| quid pro quo | contractual, simultaneous, equivalent; hau is delayed, non-equivalent, relational |
| "paying it forward" | captures circulation, severs the return-to-source; whāngai hau is exactly the return-to-source |
| karma (Eng. borrowing) | generalized moral causation; not gift-borne, not person-binding |
| French *don*/Maussian "the gift" | names the whole institution, not the binding force inside it |
| 人情 renqing / 報 bào | nearest non-Māori: but locates the bond in the social ledger between persons; hau locates it *in the gift-object as carrier of the giver's vitality* |

YOUSPEAK's own neighbors (collision-checked S086): **danaqing** names free-giving-without-expectation-of-return (the sāttvika pole — explicitly "expectation-free"); **paqduqing** names CREATOR–CREATED asymmetric custodial reciprocity; **nextlame** names cosmic-debt extraction (anti-ordinance); **eranosme** names contributed-meal integration; **xeniame** names threshold-hospitality; **mauriqing** names the resident relational life-essence. None names *the giver's vitality traveling in the gift and obliging the return-flow*. The region is unnamed in the canon and in English.
