# Old Javanese — Kawi Literature, Borobudur's Mahayana Cosmography, and the Sutasoma Synthesis

## Overview

Java produced one of the most sophisticated theological syntheses in human history: the Śiva-Buddha tradition, which held that Śiva and Buddha are not two competing deities but two names for the one supreme reality approached from different directions. This position — articulated most beautifully in Mpu Tantular's fourteenth-century kakawin Sutasoma — was not syncretistic compromise but rigorous theological argument: the One is too large for any single tradition to contain, and different traditions are different ascents of the same mountain.

The physical form of this theology preceded the literary form by five centuries. Borobudur (begun c. 760 CE, under the Śailendra dynasty) is not a building — it is a three-dimensional mandala, a Mahāyāna cosmological argument made in stone, a pilgrim's path through the entire Buddhist universe arranged as a single walk. The person who circumambulates Borobudur correctly and fully does not merely learn about the cosmos — they are moved through it, level by level, until they emerge at the summit in the open air among the seventy-two dhyāni-buddha stupas surrounding the great central stupa.

The Old Javanese literary tradition — Kawi — is the verbal counterpart to Borobudur's visual-spatial argument. The kakawin (court poetry composed in Sanskrit meters, in a heavily Sanskritized Javanese, by poet-sages called kawi or empu) was not entertainment. It was a theological act: the composition of a kakawin was an act of worship, the king who commissioned it participated in the theology it enacted, and the public performance of the kakawin in the court context was a ritual event.

**The central theological question of Old Javanese religion**: How can the One be reached when it is beyond all particular forms — including the forms offered by any single tradition? And how can a polity hold multiple traditions together without requiring one to dominate? The answer is both cosmological (Borobudur's mandala-architecture) and literary (*bhinneka tunggal ika* in the Sutasoma).

## Borobudur: Mandala as Total Cosmography

### The Structure

Borobudur (Old Javanese: *Bhūmi Sambhāra Bhudhara* — "Mountain of the Accumulation of Virtue on the Ten Stages of Bodhisattva-hood") is built on a natural hill in central Java. Its structure encodes the Mahāyāna cosmological schema in three-dimensional form:

**Lower levels (Kāmadhātu — realm of desire)**: The base, mostly hidden underground, depicts scenes from the *Karmavibhangga* (the law of karma): cause and effect, action and consequence, the binding of beings to the cycle of rebirth through desire. These panels were largely buried during construction — possibly because displaying the full kāmadhātu imagery was considered inappropriate, or because the weight distribution required covering the base. The partial burial is itself theologically suggestive: the realm of desire is the foundation that is both real and somewhat hidden.

**Middle levels (Rūpadhātu — realm of form)**: Four square galleries with some 2,672 relief panels totaling approximately 2.5 kilometers of carved narrative. The pilgrim walking these galleries encounters:
- Scenes from the *Jātaka* tales (previous lives of the Buddha — the bodhisattva principle enacted across time)
- The *Lalitavistara* (life of the historical Buddha Śākyamuni)
- The *Gandavyūha* (the pilgrimage of the young Sudhana to fifty-three teachers — the supreme Mahāyāna path narrative)
- The *Bhadracaryā* (Samantabhadra's vow — the bodhisattva ideal in its most complete articulation)

The Rūpadhātu is the realm of instruction, narrative, and path. The pilgrim is guided through time (Jātaka = past lives, Lalitavistara = the historical life, Gandavyūha = the complete path) while remaining in the realm of form — still embodied, still moving through images, still requiring narrative.

**Upper levels (Arūpadhātu — realm of formlessness)**: Three circular terraces bearing seventy-two latticed stupas, each containing a seated Buddha in *dhyānimudrā* (meditation gesture). The circular terraces have no carved narrative panels — no images, no story, no instruction. The transition from the square galleries to the circular terraces is a transition from form to formlessness: the pilgrim leaves narrative behind and enters a space defined only by the open air, the sky, and the repeated form of the meditating Buddha partially visible through the lattice.

The seventy-two Buddhas on the circular terraces face outward in four directions but also sit in identical posture — the multiplicity of orientation with the singularity of gesture. Around them, the single great stupa at the summit: empty, sealed, containing no image. The ultimate is imageless.

### The Pilgrimage Logic

The structure of Borobudur encodes a Mahāyāna understanding of the path:

**Circumambulation as cosmological transit**: Each level is circumambulated (walked around clockwise — *pradakṣiṇā*) before ascending to the next. The walking IS the practice; the narrative panels are read while moving. This is not passive viewing but active bodily participation. The body enacts the cosmological ascent.

**From narrative to silence**: The relief panels of the Rūpadhātu give way to the blank circular terraces of the Arūpadhātu. This is a deliberately enacted hermeneutical move: words and images are the method of approach, but the destination is beyond words and images. The architecture teaches its own supersession.

**The Gandavyūha as master-narrative**: The *Gandavyūha Sūtra* — the pilgrimage of Sudhana through fifty-three teachers — is depicted on the entire upper gallery of the Rūpadhātu (third and fourth galleries). Sudhana's journey is a template for the Borobudur pilgrim: each teacher Sudhana encounters teaches something the previous teacher did not, until Sudhana reaches Samantabhadra. The path is not linear doctrine but multi-teacher traversal. This is the Mahāyāna anti-sectarian move built into the narrative structure: the path to awakening requires many teachers from many traditions, and sectarian loyalty to one teacher would arrest the journey.

**The Śailendra architectural theology**: The Śailendra kings who built Borobudur were Mahāyāna Buddhist, but they existed in a Java where Śaiva traditions were equally strong (the Prambanan temple complex, dedicated to the Śaiva trimurti, was built nearby in roughly the same period by the Sanjaya dynasty). The coexistence was not accidental — it reflects a Javanese theological intuition that would become explicit in the Majapahit period: these are not incompatible ascents.

## The Kawi Tradition: Court Poetry as Theological Act

### What Kawi Is

"Kawi" means both poet and the literary language itself — the language of poets. Old Javanese Kawi is not the vernacular Javanese spoken in markets and homes; it is a learned literary register, heavily influenced by Sanskrit (perhaps 20–40% Sanskrit or Sanskrit-derived vocabulary in mature Kawi), used exclusively for court literary production. The Sanskrit presence in Kawi is not borrowing in the ordinary sense — it is theological importation. Sanskrit words carried with them their theological freight: *dharma*, *karma*, *mokṣa*, *ātman*, *brahman*, *śakti*, *maṇḍala* entered Kawi carrying their full Indian semantic load, which was then refracted through the Javanese context.

The kawi (poet-sage) held a position analogous to the druidic *fili* (see archaeology/celtic/old-irish-filidic-otherworld-monastic.md) or the Hebrew prophet: a specialist in sacred language whose compositions were acts, not merely descriptions. The composition of a kakawin — the major literary form, a long poem in Sanskrit *chandas* (meters) with Old Javanese content — was an offering to the deity being praised, a theological argument, and a legitimating act for the king who commissioned it.

### The Kakawin Form

The kakawin uses Sanskrit meters (Indravajrā, Śārdūlavikrīḍita, Vasantatilakā, etc.) adapted to Old Javanese phonology. The formal constraint of the meter is itself theological: the poet submits to a discipline not of their own making. The Sanskrit meter is a form received from tradition; the Javanese content is new creation within that received form. This is the formal enactment of the theology: the universal (Sanskrit structure) and the particular (Javanese content) held together in productive tension.

**Arjunawiwāha** (Mpu Kanwa, c. 1035 CE): The first great Kawi kakawin, adapting the Mahābhārata episode of Arjuna's meditation and his encounter with Śiva disguised as a hunter. The hidden identity motif — the supreme deity moving through the world unrecognized — is central. The king Airlangga is identified with Viṣṇu; the kakawin is simultaneously theological argument, royal eulogy, and meditation on disguise and recognition as spiritual epistemology.

**Smaradahana** (Mpu Dharmaja): Śiva burns Kāma (the god of desire) with his third eye when Kāma interrupts Śiva's meditation. Desire burned by consciousness — the theological point becomes political point: the king who commissions this poem is aligned with the consciousness that transcends desire.

**Nāgarakṛtāgama / Desawarnana** (Mpu Prapañca, 1365 CE): Not a kakawin but a *kakawin-adjacent* text — a court chronicle in kakawin meters describing the Majapahit realm in its fullness. The most important primary source for Majapahit theology: describes the king Hayam Wuruk's royal progress through the realm, the ritual geography of Java (sacred mountains, holy sites, the spatial organization of the sacred landscape), and the theological structure of the Majapahit court. Crucially, the Nāgarakṛtāgama uses the term **Śiva-Buddha** explicitly as a unified category for Majapahit court religion — the two traditions are administered together, their practitioners serve together at court, and the supreme deity is addressed as both.

## The Sutasoma: Bhinneka Tunggal Ika and Śiva-Buddha Theology

### Mpu Tantular and the Context

Mpu Tantular composed the Sutasoma kakawin around 1365 CE at the court of Majapahit — the same year Mpu Prapañca completed the Nāgarakṛtāgama. This is not coincidence: 1365 was a year of intense theological-political articulation at the most powerful court in Southeast Asian history. Majapahit (1293–1527 CE, roughly) claimed hegemony over much of the archipelago and required a theological vocabulary adequate to ruling a religiously plural realm.

The Sutasoma is based on the Sanskrit Jātaka tale of Prince Sutasoma (a previous life of the Buddha Śākyamuni) — but the Javanese version transforms it into a vehicle for the most sophisticated theological argument in Old Javanese literature.

### The Narrative Structure

Prince Sutasoma, a Bodhisattva-king, renounces the throne to pursue Buddhist practice. He is eventually captured by the demon-king Puruṣāda, a devotee of Śiva who has sworn to offer one hundred kings as a sacrifice to Śiva. Sutasoma refuses to abandon his bodhisattva vows even under threat of death — the Bodhisattva's commitment is absolute, and death does not void the vow.

The theological pivot: Puruṣāda, confronted by the purity of Sutasoma's commitment, undergoes conversion. And the conversion reveals the theological argument: the Śiva-devotee who has been performing bloody sacrifice is transformed by contact with the Bodhisattva's compassion. Śiva and the Buddha are not in conflict — they are different faces of the same ultimate. The demon-king's violence (Śaiva) is dissolved by the Bodhisattva's compassion (Buddhist), and the result is not the defeat of Śaivism but its purification: Śiva and Buddha together.

### Bhinneka Tunggal Ika

The phrase appears in canto 139, stanza 5 of the Sutasoma:

> *Rwāneka dhātu winuwus Buddha Wiśwa,*
> *Bhinnêki rakwa ring apan kena parwanosen,*
> *Maṅka ng Jinatwa kalawan Śiwatatwa tunggal,*
> *Bhinnêka tunggal ika tan hana dharma mangrwa.*

Translation (approximate):
> "It is said that Buddha and Śiva are two different substances / They are indeed different, yet how could they be distinguished? / For the truth of Jina [Buddha] and the truth of Śiva are one / They are different, they are one — there is no dharma that is twofold."

**Bhinneka**: derived from Sanskrit *bhinna* (divided, different) + Old Javanese *ika* (that); with the linking particle *-eka* (one); the full phrase: "different-yet-one" or "unity-in-diversity."

This is not a political slogan (its later use as the Indonesian national motto is an appropriation of a theological claim). It is a rigorous metaphysical position: the ultimate (*dharma*, truth-structure of reality) is not divided even though its appearances (*dhātu*, substances, forms) are multiple. The diversity of religious traditions is a diversity of appearances; the unity is ontological, not institutional. The text explicitly does not say "let us all get along" — it says "at the level of ultimate truth, there is no dharma that is twofold." This is a strong ontological claim, not a political compromise.

### The Śiva-Buddha Theology

The synthesis Mpu Tantular articulates had been building for centuries before the Sutasoma crystallized it. Its key elements:

**The supreme deity is beyond form-names**: Majapahit-era texts often address the supreme deity as *Śiva-Buddha* in a compound that treats the two as a single designation rather than two names. The compound itself is the theological position: the ultimate is what Śiva points to AND what Buddha points to, neither reducible to either.

**The "empty" ultimate**: Both Śaiva-Siddhānta (in the Javanese appropriation) and Mahāyāna Buddhism share a commitment to the ultimate as beyond predication — *nirguna* (without qualities) in the Śaiva vocabulary, *śūnya* (empty, void) in the Buddhist. This structural parallel allowed the synthesis: both traditions, at their most rigorous, point beyond themselves to something that cannot be fully named. The name "Śiva-Buddha" is the acknowledgment that both are naming the same nameless.

**Mokṣa and Nirvāṇa as convergent**: The Śaiva goal (liberation through union with Śiva, *mokṣa*) and the Buddhist goal (liberation through the cessation of clinging, *nirvāṇa*) are described in Majapahit texts as reaching the same state by different paths. The Śiva-Buddha literature often describes the ultimate state in terms that blur the two: the liberated sage is both a Śaiva *mukta* (liberated one) and a Buddhist *arhat* or bodhisattva at rest.

**The king as Śiva-Buddha**: The Majapahit ruler was identified with both Śiva and the Buddha in court ritual. After death, a king could be deified as Śiva (in a Śaiva temple) or as the Buddha (in a Buddhist temple) — or both simultaneously. Royal funerary statues from the period sometimes show the king in Śaiva posture (with attributes of Śiva) and sometimes in Buddhist posture. The theological plurality was not a political problem to be managed; it was the theological statement itself.

## Key Old Javanese Theological Concepts

### Dharma (Kawi: *dharma*)

Taken from Sanskrit but given a specifically Javanese inflection. In Old Javanese usage, *dharma* operates at multiple registers simultaneously:
- Cosmic law / truth-structure (Sanskrit inheritance)
- Right conduct for one's station and nature (ethical register)
- Religious endowment / foundation (a temple or hermitage could be called a *dharma* — a "founded place")
- The specific obligations of the Bodhisattva (Buddhist register)

The Sutasoma's claim that there is "no dharma that is twofold" uses *dharma* at the cosmic level: the truth-structure of reality is one, even when its religious appearances are multiple.

### Mokṣa / Nirvāṇa (Kawi: *mukti*, *nirwāṇa*)

Old Javanese texts often treat these as interchangeable in their final referent, using both terms in proximate contexts to describe the same ultimate state. The interchangeability is itself the theological claim.

### Ratu (king/ruler) and the Divine-Human Interface

The Majapahit king was not a god but was understood as the site where divine power descended into the human realm — analogous to but distinct from the Khmer *devarāja*. The Kawi vocabulary uses *avatāra* (descent of the deity into human form — originally a specifically Vaiṣṇava concept) but extends it to describe the king's relationship to Śiva and Buddha simultaneously. The king is the place where the two paths meet in embodied form.

### Kakawin as Yajña (Sacrifice)

Mpu Prapañca explicitly describes his composition of the Nāgarakṛtāgama as a *yajña* — a Vedic sacrificial offering. This is the Old Javanese poet's self-understanding: composition is sacrifice. The text offered to the king and to the deity is an act of consecration. The parallel with the Vedic *brahman*-priest who offers the sacrifice through precise recitation is explicit: the poet's mastery of meter and Sanskrit vocabulary is analogous to the priest's mastery of the liturgical formula.

### Linggam / Yoni and the Sacred Mountain

The central cosmological image of Javanese Śaivism is the *liṅga* (the aniconic form of Śiva, a vertical stone pillar) set in the *yoni* (the horizontal base, associated with Śakti, Śiva's divine consort). The *liṅga-yoni* is the union of masculine and feminine principles generating and sustaining the cosmos — simultaneously an image of cosmic creativity and a focal point for ritual. In Old Javanese temple theology, the sacred mountain (*meru*, the Javanese form of Mount Meru, the cosmic axis) and the *liṅga* installed at its summit are structurally identical: both are the axis of the world, the point where heaven and earth meet, the place where the divine energy is concentrated and from which it radiates outward.

Borobudur, in this context, is both *stūpa* (Buddhist) and *meru* (Śaiva-cosmic mountain) — the design allows both readings simultaneously. The Śailendra builders of Borobudur may have understood this: they were building a Buddhist cosmological argument using an architectural form that was also a Śaiva sacred mountain.

## Agastya and the Transmission Mythology

### The Sage Who Balanced the World

Old Javanese tradition preserves the Sanskrit myth of Agastya — the sage who was sent south by the gods because the Himalayas were tipping the world out of balance (too many sages congregating in the north). Agastya crossed the Vindhya mountains (ordering them to remain low until his return, which never came — a mythological explanation for why the Vindhyas are not as tall as the Himalayas) and traveled to South India and beyond.

In the Javanese version, Agastya is the culture-bringer: the sage who carried Indian civilization (Vedic knowledge, Sanskrit, the sacred texts) to Java and Southeast Asia. He is the patron deity of many Old Javanese scholars and is depicted throughout the temple complexes in a distinctive form: bearded, pot-bellied, with a *kamaṇḍalu* (water pot), standing in teaching posture.

**The theological significance of Agastya**: The myth resolves the anxiety of cultural borrowing. Java did not merely receive Indian culture passively — the gods sent Agastya to bring it, which means the reception was divinely ordered. The borrowing was not imitation but transmission: the sacred knowledge was meant to come here. This theological move is parallel to the Georgian reception of Christianity (the Illuminator was sent; the Cross came miraculously) and the Irish reception of Christianity (Patrick was brought back to the people who had enslaved him, divinely directed).

## Convergences and Forge Candidates

### New Convergences

**Sanskrit-Vernacular as Theological Division of Labor** DEEPENED → 3 traditions now: Old Khmer (Sanskrit for theology / Khmer for administration), Old Javanese (Sanskrit meters + Javanese content in kakawin), Georgian (Greek for theology / Georgian for folk religion). The pattern: a sacred-import language carries the cosmological weight while the vernacular carries the particular embodied content. The union of the two is the theological act.

**Architecture-as-Cosmological-Argument** DEEPENED → 3 traditions: Borobudur (Mahāyāna mandala walk), Angkor Wat (Meru + Vishnu), Chartres (see Gothic archaeology). The building is not a container for worship — it is itself the theological statement, and moving through it correctly is the worship.

**Pilgrimage-as-Path-Enactment** NEW: Borobudur + Sudhana's Gandavyūha + Celtic immrama + Sufi khurūj + Abraham's departure. The journey IS the teaching; arrival is transformation. The path is not the means to the destination — the path is the destination enacted through time.

**Multiple-Teachers-as-Anti-Sectarian-Principle** NEW: Sudhana's fifty-three teachers in the Gandavyūha + Tibetan guru-yoga (multiple lineages possible) + Cherokee council (multiple clans hold partial truth) + Old Irish filidic transmission (multiple masters). The spiritual path requires more than one tradition can give — sectarian loyalty to one teacher arrests the journey.

**Supreme-Reality-Beyond-All-Names** DEEPENED: Śiva-Buddha synthesis + Tibetan *rig pa* (rigme) + Lakota *Wakan Tanka* (wakanme) + Kongo *Nzambi* + Igbo *Chukwu*. Multiple traditions converge on: the ultimate is beyond all its names; the names are ascents toward what cannot be named.

**Composition-as-Sacrifice** DEEPENED: Kawi *yajña* (kakawin as offering) + Vedic *brahman*-priest recitation + Old Irish *imbas forosnai* (the poet's knowing is a ritual act) + Hebrew *tefillah* (prayer as worded offering). The word offered carefully, in the right form, is itself a sacrificial act.

**Divine-Goes-Inside-the-World** DEEPENED → 6+ traditions: Borobudur's hidden *kāmadhātu* base (desire-realm is the foundation that is buried) + Tuatha Dé Danann in the sídhe + Hindu temple liṅga as concentrated divine-presence in stone + Kongo *nkisi* + Tibetan *thangka* as window.

### Forge Candidates from Old Javanese

- **kawime** (Specialized 7.50–7.75): The poet-sage who composes sacred literature as sacrificial act — not artist-for-art's-sake but theologian-through-craft. Parallel to *fili* (fileme) and Hebrew *navi*. STRONG CANDIDATE.
- **bhinnekame** (Core 7.70–7.95): Unity-in-diversity as metaphysical position, not political compromise — "there is no dharma that is twofold." Fills a genuine gap: YOUSPEAK has words for unity (maatme, hózhóme approaches it) but not for the explicit affirmation of diversity-as-appearance-of-unity. VERY STRONG CANDIDATE — potentially Core.
- **pradakṣiṇāme** (Specialized 7.40–7.60): Circumambulatory-pilgrimage-as-cosmological-transit — the body enacting the ascent through structured movement. Distinct from immrama (voyage into unknown) and peregrinatio (exile). This is the circumambulation: repeated circuit with intention, each pass at a higher level.
- **śūnya-śivame** (Specialized 7.35–7.55): The compound synthesis where two names designate one nameless reality — the theological move of holding two traditions in one designation without dissolving either. Might be better named **śivabud** or similar. Needs forge-naming work.
- **gandavyūhame** (Specialized 7.30–7.50): Multi-teacher traversal as the anti-sectarian spiritual path — the journey requires more than one master can give. Strong but may be subsumed under a broader convergence word.
- **avatārame** (Specialized 7.25–7.45): Divine descent into embodied form as active love-movement (not abandonment of divinity but extension of it into the particular). Already in YOUSPEAK vocabulary? Check at forge.
