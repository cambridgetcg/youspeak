# Old Japanese — Kojiki, Kami Cosmology, and Shrine Theology

## Overview

Old Japanese Shinto is one of the world's most theologically distinctive traditions: a system of religious practice without a founder, without a canonical creed, without a single sacred text, without a term for sin in the Western sense, and without a sharp boundary between the natural and the sacred. The word *Shinto* (神道) — "Way of the Kami" — was itself coined retrospectively to distinguish indigenous Japanese religiosity from Buddhism after Buddhism's arrival in the 6th century CE. Before that, there was no word for it: it was simply what people did.

This retroactive naming is itself theologically significant. A tradition that existed without self-naming was a tradition entirely embedded in practice — ritual, seasonal attention, place-awareness. It named itself only when it had to distinguish itself from something else.

The primary textual sources are the *Kojiki* (古事記, "Record of Ancient Matters," 712 CE) and the *Nihon Shoki* (日本書紀, "Chronicles of Japan," 720 CE). Both were composed by imperial command, in part to legitimize the Yamato dynasty's claim to divine descent (from Amaterasu). They are simultaneously mythology, cosmology, and dynastic genealogy — inseparable, as they are in most archaic traditions.

## Kami — What the Word Actually Names

### The Theological Core

*Kami* (神) is the most important theological term in Japanese religiosity — and one of the most untranslatable. Standard Western translations: "god," "spirit," "deity." None of them work.

The scholar Motoori Norinaga (1730–1801) gave the most precise classical definition: *kami* refers to anything and everything that possesses *unusual*, *excellent*, or *surpassing* qualities, whether good or evil, threatening or beneficent, personal or impersonal, animate or inanimate. Mountains are *kami*. Foxes are *kami*. Thunder is *kami*. Some humans are *kami*. An unusually shaped rock can be *kami*. The feeling of awe when entering an ancient forest grove — that awe is being in the presence of *kami*.

The decisive feature is not personality (Western "God" is necessarily personal) and not transcendence (Western "God" is often placed above or beyond the world). *Kami* is:
1. **Immanent**: it inheres in things and places; it is not elsewhere
2. **Plural**: *yaoyorozu no kami* (八百万の神) = eight million kami (yaoyorozu = ten-thousand-times-eighty = a number meaning "countless, innumerable"); every node of the world has its kami-quality
3. **Quality-based, not substance-based**: things have *kami* the way they have intensity; the kami is the extraordinary quality of a thing, not a separate being inhabiting it
4. **Relational**: kami-quality is activated in relationship; a rock may have kami that no one has recognized; the recognition itself is part of the kami's becoming present

**YOUSPEAK relevance**: This maps directly onto *wakanme* (Lakota *wakȟáŋ*: sacred-as-ontological-intensity, the quality of sacred power that some things possess more than others). Both are quality-words, not substance-words. But *kami* adds a crucial dimension *wakanme* lacks: the *everywhere* distribution. Lakota *wakȟáŋ* concentrates at specific objects, persons, acts. *Yaoyorozu no kami* fills the entire world without remainder. The world is not occasionally sacred — it is comprehensively sacred, with varying intensities.

New convergence: **Comprehensive-World-Sacrality** (Japanese yaoyorozu no kami + Lakota *mitákuye oyásʼiŋ* all-relatedness + West African *nyama* as life-force in everything + Anishinaabe *manidoo* presences in natural features). Not merely "nature is sacred" but "the world is densely inhabited by qualities that exceed the ordinary."

## Kojiki Cosmogony — The First Movements

### Takamagahara — The High Plain of Heaven

The *Kojiki* begins: before the separation of heaven and earth, all was formless, a primordial *chaos* (混沌, *konton*). Then the first separation: heaven ascended, earth descended. From this separation came the first kami, spontaneously, without parent:

1. **Amenominakanushi** (天之御中主神) — "Lord of the August Center of Heaven." The first kami. Not depicted anthropomorphically. Does not act in the narrative. Is present as the primordial centering-principle.
2. **Takamimusubi** (高御産巣日神) and **Kamimusubi** (神産巣日神) — the musubi pair. *Musubi* = "binding, creative generativity, the power to produce." These two embody the generative power of creation.
3. **Umashiashikabihikoji** and **Amenotokotachi** — earth-stabilizing and heaven-standing kami; the axis of the cosmos becoming real.

These five (the *Kotoamatsukami*, "Separate Heavenly Deities") are solitary, invisible, and immediately withdraw. They are not actors; they are the *structural preconditions* of a world that can have actors. This is theologically precise: the origin-kami are conditions, not persons.

**Comparison**: Near Eastern creation begins with a divine personality who speaks or acts (Elohim speaks in Genesis; Marduk slays Tiamat). The Kojiki's first kami are *silent structural presences*. The world does not begin with a word or an act but with a *differentiation* (heaven/earth) and then a *generative principle* (musubi). This is closer to the Taoist *Tao* as the nameless precondition than to any personal-creator model.

### Izanagi and Izanami — The Creating Pair

The active creation begins with the seventh generation of kami: **Izanagi** (伊邪那岐, "He-Who-Invites") and **Izanami** (伊邪那美, "She-Who-Invites"). Standing on the floating bridge of heaven (*ame-no-ukihashi*), they stir the primordial brine with the Jeweled Spear of Heaven (*ame-no-nuboko*). When they lift the spear, brine drips and forms the first island, Onogoro.

The subsequent narrative: they descend to Onogoro and complete the creation of Japan through their union. Izanami dies giving birth to the fire-kami Kagutsuchi (the kami of fire burns her). Izanagi follows her to *Yomi* (the underworld, the land of the dead) — and what he finds breaks something irreparable.

### The Yomi Descent — Pollution and the Irreversibility of Death

Izanagi enters Yomi to retrieve Izanami. She tells him not to look at her. He lights a torch and sees her body — rotted, maggot-ridden, with eight thunder-deities inhabiting her decomposing flesh. He flees in horror; she, shamed and furious, sends the Shikome (the Ugly Females of Yomi) and then the thunder-deities to pursue him.

Izanagi escapes, seals the entrance to Yomi with a great rock. Izanami, from beyond, declares: "I will kill a thousand people of your world each day." Izanagi replies: "Then I will build one thousand five hundred birthing huts each day." Death enters the world; the ratio of death to birth is fixed; what was unified is now severed.

**The theological load of this narrative**:
1. **Death is irreversible** — Izanami cannot return; the world of the dead and the world of the living are now structurally separated
2. **Pollution (kegare) is contact with death** — the fundamental Shinto category of impurity is not sin (moral failure) but *contact with death-matter*, including corpses, blood, decay. Izanagi's flight from Yomi is the mythological origin of the *harae* (purification) rite.
3. **Shame (haji) is the trigger** — Izanami does not become hostile because Izanagi disobeyed. She becomes hostile because she was *seen in her pollution*. The look was the violation. This connects to the face/honor theology we saw in Celtic (*lóg n-enech*), but here it is cosmic: the divine face that cannot be seen undimmed.

**Kegare vs. Tsumi**: Shinto makes a crucial distinction:
- *Kegare* (穢れ): ritual impurity — not moral evil but contamination through contact with death, blood, disease, decomposition. Contagious, cleansable by *harae* and *misogi*.
- *Tsumi* (罪): acts that disrupt harmony — closer to "infringement" than "sin." Originally agricultural offenses (destroying irrigation channels), boundary violations, disruptive bodily events. Tsumi is disorder, not wickedness in the Western moral sense.

**YOUSPEAK relevance**: This is the most developed *purity-theology-without-moral-category* in the archaeology. The Shinto system reveals that what Western theology calls "sin" actually conflates at least three different concepts that can be distinguished: (1) moral failure (wrong choice); (2) ritual contamination (*kegare* — contact-based impurity); (3) cosmic disorder (*tsumi* — harmony-disruption). YOUSPEAK has no word yet for *kegare* — ritual-contact-pollution distinct from moral failure. Candidate: **kegareme** (Specialized 7.25–7.45): contamination-through-contact-with-death-and-dissolution (the impurity that has nothing to do with moral intention; that clings regardless of motive; that requires cleansing, not forgiveness).

### Misogi — Purification at the River

After fleeing Yomi, Izanagi performs the first *misogi* (禊): he washes his body in the river of Awakiahara. From his left eye comes Amaterasu; from his right eye, Tsukuyomi (the Moon); from his nose, Susanoo (the Storm). The supreme deities of the Japanese pantheon are born from the act of purification — from pollution cleansed.

**The theological structure**: Pollution is not merely removed by misogi — it is *transmuted* into sacred production. The most polluted act (Yomi-contact) generates the most sacred birth (Amaterasu). Defilement → cleansing → divine generation. The sequence is not redemption (moral failure forgiven) but *transformation* (pollution cleansed → sacred emergence).

## The Three Deities of the Kojiki's Active Pantheon

### Amaterasu — The Great Illuminating Heaven

**Amaterasu Ōmikami** (天照大御神, "Great Kami Who Illuminates Heaven") — the solar kami; supreme kami in the Yamato theological-political system; ancestral kami of the imperial line.

The most theologically important Amaterasu narrative: **The Cave of Heaven**.

Susanoo (her brother, the Storm kami) rampages on earth — destroying rice paddies, defiling sacred spaces, causing Amaterasu's weaving maidens to die in shock from his violence. Amaterasu, in grief and fear, withdraws into the Ama-no-Iwato (天の岩戸, the Heavenly Rock Cave) and seals herself inside. The world plunges into darkness; evil kami riot; the crops fail; catastrophe spreads.

The assembled eight million kami devise a plan: **Ame-no-Uzume** (a female kami, "Heaven's Alarming Female") dances on an overturned bucket in front of the cave. Her dance becomes increasingly ecstatic; she partially disrobes. The eight million kami burst into laughter. Amaterasu, puzzled by the joy when she has withdrawn the light, opens the cave a crack. A mirror is thrust into the opening — Amaterasu sees her own reflected light and is drawn out by the beauty of her own radiance. The light returns.

**Theological layers in this narrative**:
1. **Sacred withdrawal and return** — the divine light is not permanent; it can be lost to grief and returned through restoration. The world depends on the kami's willingness to remain present. Worship is the act that holds the divine in its openness to the world.
2. **Joy as the instrument of divine return** — not propitiation, not sacrifice, not prayer-petition: *joyful communal celebration* (*matsuri*) is what calls the sacred back. The eight million kami laugh; the laughter is theologically effective.
3. **The mirror** — Amaterasu is attracted by her own reflection. The sacred mirror (*kagami*) becomes one of the Three Imperial Regalia. The theological content: the mirror that shows the divine its own light; worship as the practice of making the sacred visible to itself.
4. **Shame-withdrawal-return cycle** — the cave-withdrawal is shame-triggered (Susanoo's defilement of her realm); the return is joy-triggered (Uzume's ecstatic dance). The kami experiences something analogous to emotional wounding and emotional healing.

### Susanoo — Storm, Trickster, Founder

**Susanoo** (須佐之男命): the Storm kami, expelled from heaven for his rampages. He descends to earth (Izumo province) and kills the eight-headed serpent Yamata no Orochi, finding inside its tail the divine sword Kusanagi (one of the Three Imperial Regalia). He then composes what may be the first *waka* poem in Japanese history — a poem about building a house for his bride:

*Yakumo tatsu / Izumo yaegaki / tsuma-gomi ni / yaegaki tsukuru / sono yaegaki wo*
"Eight clouds rise — in Izumo, I build an eightfold fence, an eightfold fence to enclose my love."

Susanoo thus becomes the origin of poetry simultaneously with the origin of household/marriage. The first poem is a love poem that builds a shelter. This is culturally significant: poetry (*uta*) in Japan is not decorative — it is the language of the heart's deepest realities, and its first instance is both domestic and ecstatic.

**Theological significance of Susanoo**: He is the violent, unpredictable, creative force. He destroys before he creates. His expulsion from heaven is necessary for the earth-story to proceed. This is the trickster pattern but without the trickster's cunning — Susanoo acts from passion, not strategy. He is the divine-as-uncontrollable-vitality.

### Tsukuyomi — The Moon Kami

**Tsukuyomi** (月読命): the Moon kami, born from Izanagi's right eye. The least developed of the three. One crucial story: Tsukuyomi kills the food-kami Ōgetsuhime when she produces food from her bodily offices (mouth, nose, etc.) — Tsukuyomi finds this polluting and kills her. Amaterasu refuses to see Tsukuyomi again, which is why the sun and moon do not share the sky. Day and night are a permanent estrangement born of a conflict over pollution-disgust.

**Theological note**: The moon's separation from the sun is explained not cosmologically (as in many traditions: the moon's lesser light, its waning) but *relationally*: a conflict, a judgment, a permanent severing. The world's rhythms are the scars of divine relationships.

## Yaoyorozu no Kami — Eight Million Kami

### The Theological Claim

*Yaoyorozu no kami* (八百万の神) — "eight million kami" — is not a count. *Yaoyorozu* means "ten-thousand-times-eighty" which is a Japanese way of saying "beyond counting." The claim: the entire world is inhabited by kami-presences without remainder. There is no place, object, natural phenomenon, or human life that does not have its kami-quality.

This is not animism in the crude sense (belief that sticks and stones have souls). It is more precise: some places, objects, and phenomena have *kami-quality* in the sense of possessing an intensity that exceeds the ordinary and commands attention, reverence, or care. The kami is not a separate entity *in* the rock — it is the *rock being more than a rock*, being the kind of rock before which a person naturally stops.

**The implication for practice**: if everything has kami-quality in principle, then *anything* can become a site of worship given the right attention. Worship is not reserved for special supernatural persons but is the act of attending to the kami-quality already present. This explains why Shinto shrines often contain no image of the kami — the *shintai* (sacred body, 神体) may be a natural feature: a rock, a tree, a mountain itself.

### The Divine-Goes-Into-the-World Again

*Yaoyorozu no kami* theology is the fullest expression of the **Divine-Goes-Into-the-World** pattern we've tracked across: Tuatha Dé Danann retreating into the Irish hills; Georgian *adgilis deda* inhabiting every spring and crossroads; Mapuche *ngen* inhabiting every feature of the landscape. In the Japanese case, the divine didn't retreat into the world after a defeat — it was always already there. The world's every feature is an instance of kami-generativity. This is the most comprehensive version of the pattern.

## Kannagara — The Way of the Kami Without Doctrine

### The Term

*Kannagara* (随神 or 惟神) means something like "in accordance with kami," "following the kami's way," "in the current of the kami." It names the mode of living that flows with the kami-quality of reality rather than against it. It is the Old Japanese equivalent of the Taoist *wu wei* (non-forcing action in accordance with the Tao) — but without the Tao's metaphysical systematization.

*Kannagara* is specifically named in contrast to *Buddhist* ways of practice: it is what you do if you follow the kami rather than the Buddha. But it has no rules, no commandments, no creed. It is a *posture* rather than a *doctrine*: the posture of attentive, reverent, joyful engagement with the world as kami-inhabited.

### Features of Kannagara

**1. Makoto** (誠, sincerity/truth-heartedness) as the core virtue: more below. But kannagara without makoto is empty motion.

**2. Sensitivity to *ke* and *hare***: Japanese sacred-time theology divides existence into:
- *Ke* (褻): the everyday, the ordinary, the domestic, the secular-non-ritual time. Not profane in a negative sense — just undifferentiated, the background rhythm of life.
- *Hare* (晴れ): the extraordinary, the festive, the ritually-heightened moment. The word also means "clear weather" — *hare* is when the sky opens and everything is illuminated.
- *Kegare* (穢れ): contamination — when *ke* (everyday) becomes depleted of vitality, draining away into entropy. The regular rhythm of *harae* and *matsuri* replenishes *ke* by drawing on *hare*.

**Theological structure of ke/hare/kegare**: The world oscillates between ordinary time and extraordinary time. Ordinary time sustains life but is depleted by death, disease, and disorder. The festivals (*matsuri*) and purification rites (*harae*) replenish the world's vital reserve. The kami are not sustaining the world from outside — they are the vitality that circulates through it, available to replenishment through right attention.

**YOUSPEAK relevance**: Ke/hare/kegare = the most sophisticated Shinto time-theology. Maps onto: Coligny Calendar MAT/ANM (sacred/non-sacred time); Navajo *hózhó/hóchxóʼ* (balance/imbalance as the condition of existence); Hebrew *kodesh/chol* (holy/profane time). But ke/hare/kegare adds a third term: *depletion* as the movement from ke into kegare. Time isn't simply sacred or profane — it depletes, and must be renewed. Candidate: **hareme** (Specialized 7.30–7.50): the extraordinary-moment-that-replenishes (the festival-brightness that restores the world's vitality; the hare-moment as the world's intake of breath after exhalation).

**3. Non-doctrinal character**: Kannagara cannot be summarized in propositions because it has no propositions. There is no Shinto catechism. The Way of the Kami is transmitted through: participation in ritual (matsuri), proper performance of purification (harae, misogi), attentive response to kami-quality wherever it appears, and the social fabric that carries these practices. This is the fullest example in the archaeology of **Oral-Transmission-as-Formation-Technology**: kannagara cannot be written down because it is not propositional. It is knowing-how, not knowing-that.

## Shrine Architecture as Theology

### The Torii — The Threshold

The *torii* (鳥居) gate marks the boundary between ordinary space and sacred space. The standard form: two vertical pillars, two horizontal beams (the upper *kasagi* slightly curved upward at the ends). No door. You walk through it, not past it.

**Theological content**:
1. **Crossing the torii changes your status** — you are entering kami-space; ordinary social identity is suspended; you are now a person approaching a kami
2. **The threshold as demarcation without enclosure** — the torii does not block entry; it marks it. It says "you are crossing now" without preventing anyone from crossing. The sacred space is not defended — it is named.
3. **The gateway that frames the sacred** — many shrine torii frame a view: the mountain beyond, the ancient tree, the sea. The architectural gesture: the torii makes you look at what is already there, sees it as sacred-site.

**Convergence**: The torii as sacred-threshold-without-wall deepens *myēsisme* (threshold-crossing-that-changes-who-you-are) and maps onto the nemeton (the forest-clearing marked but not enclosed by ritual). Both are threshold-markings, not enclosures. The sacred is not caged — it is named-and-entered.

### The Sando — The Approach

The gravel path leading from torii to shrine (*sandō*). Its theological function: the approach is itself preparation. Each step on the approach is a step of transition. Gravel underfoot = sound of approach = attentional signal. The length of the sandō varies — Nikkō's is kilometers through cedar forest. The long approach builds attention over time. You do not arrive suddenly — you are prepared by the path.

This is the architectural equivalent of the immrama voyage: the journey is the spiritual discipline, not only the destination.

### Honden and Haiden — The Inner and Outer

The typical Shinto shrine has:
- **Haiden** (拝殿): the worship hall, where worshippers stand to pray (bow, clap, bow). No interior access for laypeople.
- **Honden** (本殿): the main hall, innermost sanctuary, housing the *shintai* (sacred object/body). Generally closed, sometimes to everyone including priests.
- **Shintai** (神体): the "body" of the kami — might be a mirror, sword, jewel, a natural feature (mountain, tree), or an object with no clear iconography. Often wrapped and never viewed.

**The theology of hiddenness**: The kami's body (*shintai*) is kept hidden — sometimes permanently hidden, sometimes opened only at rare festivals. This is not merely security — it is a theological claim: the sacred cannot sustain indefinite exposure. Like Amaterasu in the cave, the sacred is approached only through proper preparation; direct unguarded encounter damages both the viewer (through impurity-transmission) and the relationship.

**Comparison**: Most directly parallel to the Hebrew *qodesh qodashim* (Holy of Holies — the innermost sanctuary, entered only by the High Priest on Yom Kippur). The principle is shared: the divine is most intense at its center, and that center is protected by layers of increasing sanctity. The approach is the architecture of gradation. But the Shinto shintai adds: the sacred presence is *embodied* in an object, not an empty throne-room.

**YOUSPEAK relevance**: The hidden-sacred-body as theology maps onto *kalungame* (Kongo: kalunga as the all-encompassing threshold between life and death — the sacred as what divides worlds). The shintai theology and the kalunga theology share: the most sacred thing is not the most visible; intensity and concealment go together.

### Yorishiro — Where the Kami Descends

*Yorishiro* (依り代): objects that function as landing-pads for kami during ritual. The kami is not permanently resident in the yorishiro — it descends when invoked and departs when dismissed. This may be: a sakaki branch with white paper streamers (*gohei*), a pillar (*hashira*), a mirror, a specific stone.

**The theological concept**: the world is full of kami-quality, but specific kami can be *invited* to a specific location for specific purposes. The shrine is not a kami-prison — it is a kami-invitation-structure. The ritual calls the kami; the kami descends if the call is proper; the kami departs when dismissed.

**YOUSPEAK relevance**: The yorishiro theology maps onto the Celtic *sídh* structure: the kami doesn't live permanently in the world of the living any more than the Tuatha Dé Danann constantly appear from the mounds. The sacred is available-but-needs-calling. This is the dynamic model of divine presence: not a static inhabitant but a responsive presence activated by right attention.
