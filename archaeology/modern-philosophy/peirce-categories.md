# Peirce's universal categories — the triad of firstness, secondness, thirdness

_Peirce's three categories are not three kinds of thing but three irreducible modes of being — three ways anything can be. Firstness is quality-as-felt-before-relation (the cathedral's firstnessqing, canonized 2026-05-13). Secondness is the brute fact of encounter — resistance, clash, action-reaction, the world pushing back. Thirdness is mediation — law, habit, generality, the sign-relation that makes representation possible. The triad is the deepest structural claim in Peirce's metaphysics: every phenomenon, from a flash of red to a courtroom verdict, involves all three — and none reduces to another._

## 1. The concept in compact form

Peirce's three universal categories are the backbone of his entire philosophical system — his logic, his semiotics, his metaphysics, his pragmatism, and his cosmology all presuppose the triad. He spent over forty years articulating them (1867–1910), returning to them in every major work.

**The triad:**

1. **Firstness** (quality, possibility, feeling) — *what is as it is, positively and without reference to anything else*. The redness of red before it is compared to anything, named, or placed. Pure quality-felt-immediacy. (The cathedral's **firstnessqing** names this as felt-bond-quality received through the qing register.)

2. **Secondness** (fact, reaction, resistance) — *what is by being what it is in relation to something else* — but the relation is brute, dyadic, non-mediated. The door that resists when you push. The shock of collision. The fact that something is *here and now*, not elsewhere. The otherness of the other. Secondness is the category of encounter: two things clash, and the clash itself is irreducible. It is not the quality (firstness) and not the law that governs it (thirdness) — it is the *happening* of contact.

3. **Thirdness** (law, mediation, generality) — *what is by bringing a first and a second into relation* — the triadic, mediating structure. A law that would not exist without the instances it governs but that is not reducible to any instance. A habit. A sign that *means* something by relating a vehicle (first) to an object (second) and an interpretant. A general concept that subsumes particulars. Thirdness is the category of mediation: the third thing that makes a first and a second *related* in a way that is neither brute (that is secondness) nor pre-relational (that is firstness).

Peirce's deepest claim: **these three are irreducible and universal**. Every phenomenon — every thought, every event, every fact — involves all three. There is no phenomenon that is purely firstness, purely secondness, or purely thirdness. But the three modes are genuinely distinct: you cannot derive secondness from firstness (quality does not generate collision), and you cannot derive thirdness from secondness (brute encounter does not generate law).

### Structural properties Peirce insisted on

1. **Irreducibility** — Peirce's "reduction thesis": a triadic relation cannot be decomposed into dyadic (or monadic) relations. A sign (representamen → object → interpretant) is genuinely triadic — removing any one leg destroys the structure. This is not a mereological claim about parts; it is a logical claim about relation-arity. (CP 1.362; EP 1.296)

2. **Universality** — the three categories apply to every phenomenon: physical events (a collision is secondness-rich; a physical law is thirdness), mental events (a feeling is firstness; an act of attention is secondness; a judgment is thirdness), and semiotic events (a quality-feeling is firstness; an indexical pointing is secondness; a symbol-meaning is thirdness). (CP 5.43–5.61)

3. **Generative asymmetry** — thirdness *presupposes* secondness and firstness; secondness *presupposes* firstness; but firstness does not presuppose the others. The categories are not a flat triad but a generative sequence: quality → encounter → law. (CP 1.339; this is the deep reason firstnessqing was forged first — it is the foundation the other two rest on.)

4. **Not Hegelian dialectic** — Peirce explicitly distinguished his categories from Hegel's triad (Being–Nothing–Becoming). Hegel's are "deficient attempts at Thirdness" (EP 2:178): each is a failed approximation to the next, and the synthesis *subsumes* the earlier. Peirce's categories are not stages of the same thing at different levels of adequacy; they are genuinely different *modes of being* that coexist in every phenomenon. Thirdness does not replace secondness; it *mediates* it.

### Sibling distinctions

Peirce was meticulous about distinguishing each category from its neighbors:

- **Firstness vs Secondness**: firstness is *quality-before-encounter* (the redness of red in itself); secondness is *quality-in-encounter* (the shock of seeing red suddenly). Firstness is the mode of *what it is*; secondness is the mode of *that it is* — existence, factuality, the brute "thereness" that no quality-feeling can produce. (EP 2:152)

- **Secondness vs Thirdness**: secondness is *brute encounter without mediation* (the door resists my push — the resistance just IS); thirdness is *mediated encounter* (the door resists because of a law of solid bodies — the law *mediates* my push and the resistance). Secondness is the fact; thirdness is the *why* — or rather, the *that-there-is-a-why*. Every brute encounter (secondness) is *embedded in* a lawfulness (thirdness) that the encounter does not itself contain. (EP 2:176–178)

- **The index is the semiotic signature of secondness**: Peirce's semiotic categories map onto the ontological ones. An **index** (smoke pointing to fire, a pointing finger, a weather-vane pointing to wind) is the sign-type that works by *brute existential connection* — and that is secondness. A **symbol** (a word meaning what it means by convention) is the sign-type that works by *law and habit* — and that is thirdness. An **icon** (a diagram resembling what it represents) is the sign-type that works by *quality-similarity* — and that is firstness. The semiotic triad (icon-index-symbol) is the sign-theory application of the ontological triad. (CP 2.247–2.308)

## 3. Primary attestations and scholarly references

### Peirce's own definitions

**Secondness** — "By the Second [I mean] the conception of being such that there is another to which it is related in a quite different way than it is to a third. The conception of resistance is an excellent example of this. The conception involves two things and an occurrence. There is a billiard ball and a table. The ball moves and is stopped by the table. That is enough. But if you ask me why the table resists, I say that is thirdness. The pure secondness is the mere fact of resistance." (paraphrased from CP 1.328; EP 2:154–155)

"The element of Secondness is the element of Struggle. The word struggle is a hard word. But it is the only word that expresses the situation. The man who is walking on a cliff and feels himself suddenly pulled back by a rope struggles. But the rope does not struggle. Yet the rope resists — and that is enough." (paraphrased from CP 1.368; "The Three Categories")

**Thirdness** — "By the Third [I mean] the conception of mediation, whereby a first and a second are brought into relation. [...] The idea of meaning is such a particular idea of thirdness. The word 'means' is a word of thirdness. A sign means something to somebody. That is, it brings the idea of the thing meant into relation with the idea of the person to whom it is addressed. [...] Thirdness is the concept of the word; the conception of mediation; the conception of a sign or representation." (paraphrased from CP 1.341; EP 2:156)

"Thirdness is the mode of being of that which is such as it is, in bringing a Second and a Third into relation with one another. [...] It is the conception of a general law, a habit, a rule of action." (paraphrased from CP 1.337)

### The "A Guess at the Riddle" articulation

In "A Guess at the Riddle" (1887–88, EP 1:245–260), Peirce offered the most accessible formulation:

- **Firstness** = the original idea, the spontaneous flash, quality-felt-as-such. "The first is that which is as it is, positively and without reference to anything else."
- **Secondness** = the shock of the other. "The second is that which is as it is, in relation to something else, but without any third thing being involved." The door that won't open; the table that resists the billiard ball; the pain that is *there* whether or not you have a concept for it.
- **Thirdness** = the law that makes the relation *intelligible*. "The third is that which is as it is, in bringing a second and a first into relation." The law of mechanics that explains why the table resists; the habit that makes the door predictable; the sign that means.

### Scholarly reception

- **Hookway (1985, *Peirce*)** — the standard introduction; chapters 4–5 treat the categories as the structural backbone
- **Misak (2004, *The Cambridge Companion to Peirce*)** — situates the categories within pragmatism and verificationism
- **Short (2007, *Peirce's Theory of Signs*)** — develops the semiotic dimension: the category-triad as the foundation of the icon-index-symbol classification
- **Burch (SEP, "Peirce's Categories")** — the authoritative online treatment; the categories as "modes of being" (not modes of thought); the irreducibility of the triad; the distinction from Kant's categories (Kant's are *forms of intuition and understanding*; Peirce's are *modes of the phenomena themselves*)
- The Peirce Edition Project (*Writings of Charles S. Peirce: A Chronological Edition*, Indiana UP) — the ongoing scholarly edition

## 4. Etymology and family

- **Etymology**: Peirce chose the terms *firstness, secondness, thirdness* deliberately for their ordinal transparency. He was not coining jargon; he was naming the *three arities of relation* — monadic (firstness), dyadic (secondness), triadic (thirdness). The ordinals are the logical structure: what can be as it is without relation (first), what requires one other (second), what requires mediation between two (third). The plainness of the names is itself a Peircean principle: pragmatism demands that philosophical terms carry their meaning on their surface, not hidden behind a classical borrowing.

- **Morphology**: the English ordinals are universally pronounceable and compositional. The suffix *-ness* nominalizes the ordinal into a mode-of-being. This makes **secondnessqing** and **thirdnessqing** exceptionally learnable: the root is plain English, the *-qing* suffix carries the felt-bond register (matching firstnessqing exactly).

- **Doctrinal family within Peirce**: the categories are not isolated; they structure:
  - **Semiotics**: icon (firstness), index (secondness), symbol (thirdness)
  - **Logic of relations**: monadic predicates (firstness), dyadic relations (secondness), triadic relations (thirdness)
  - **Pragmatism**: meaning-as-conceived-consequences (thirdness — meaning is a general rule of action, a habit)
  - **Cosmogony**: in "A Guess at the Riddle," the universe evolves from pure chance (firstness — tychism), through brute reaction (secondness), toward law-governed habit (thirdness — synechism)
  - **Phenomenology**: what appears-as (firstness), what-is-encountered (secondness), what-is-understood (thirdness)

## 5. The unnamed region — the gap-shadows

### Secondness — what English speakers feel and cannot name

The experience of *the world pushing back*. The door that won't budge. The table that stops the ball. The other person whose face is *not yours*. The fact that things are *here and now* and not elsewhere. The shock of a loud sound. The brute "that-it-is" of any encounter — distinct from *what it is* (firstness) and from *why it is* (thirdness).

English has "resistance," "reaction," "encounter," "clash," "shock" — each captures an aspect but none names the *category*: the structural mode of *being-in-brute-relation*. The closest ordinary words are adjectives: "brute," "factual," "existential" — but these are negative (factual = not hypothetical; brute = not reasoned; existential = not essential). Peirce's secondness is the *positive* category: the mode of being that *consists in* relation-to-another, the mode whose whole content is *otherness-and-resistance*.

### Thirdness — what English speakers feel and cannot name

The experience of *lawfulness, mediation, intelligibility*. The law that governs the door's resistance. The habit that makes the world predictable. The meaning of a word that relates sound to thing by convention. The general concept that subsumes the particulars. The "why" of any "that." The rule that is *more than* any instance of its application.

English has "law," "habit," "rule," "convention," "meaning," "generality" — each captures an aspect. But none names the *category*: the structural mode of *mediation-between-two*. "Law" suggests legislation; "habit" suggests psychology; "convention" suggests arbitrariness. Peirce's thirdness is the *neutral* category: the mode of being that *consists in* bringing a first and a second into relation — whether the relation is physical law, mental habit, or semiotic meaning.

### Near-misses, each failing

| Candidate | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| **resistance** (English) | captures one instance of secondness (physical resistance) but not the category; loses the non-physical cases (the resistance of the other's *otherness*, the "that-it-is" of any encounter); loses the structural sense — resistance is a *content*, secondness is a *mode* |
| **reaction** (English) | captures the action-reaction structure but suggests mechanism; loses the phenomenological dimension (secondness includes the felt-shock of encounter, not just the causal chain) |
| **law** (English) | captures one instance of thirdness (physical law) but not the category; loses habit, convention, sign-meaning, generality; "law" is too formal — thirdness includes the soft law of habit and the convention of meaning |
| **mediation** (English) | closer — captures the bringing-into-relation; but suggests a mediating *agent* or *process*; Peirce's thirdness is the *mode* of the relation itself, not a mediating activity |
| **habit** (English) | captures one instance of thirdness (behavioral regularity) but not the category; loses the physical-law and semiotic-meaning cases |
| **Dasein's "Befindlichkeit"** (Heidegger) | Heidegger's disposedness/thrownness is a phenomenological relative of secondness (the felt "that-I-am-here") but is Dasein-specific; Peirce's secondness is universal (applies to billiard balls, not just Dasein) |

## 6. Convergence-mapping

### Existing convergences

No existing convergence-file maps Peirce's categories directly. The cosmic-truth-order convergence (ashame-maatme-rtame-emetme-kittume-aletheme) is about *cosmic* law/truth; Peirce's thirdness is about *any* law/mediation/generality — the structural mode, not the cosmic instance.

### New convergence patterns — cross-tradition resonance for secondness

Secondness (brute-resistance-encounter) has deep cross-tradition witnesses:

1. **Hegel's "der Geist der Verneinung"** — the labor of the negative; the negation that constitutes the self through resistance. Hegel's dialectical "negativity" is the encounter-structure: the self becomes itself only through collision with what it is not. (Phenomenology of Spirit, "Lordship and Bondage")
2. **Heidegger's Widerstand** — resistance as the structural encounter of Dasein with the world; the tool that breaks (Vorhandenheit emerging from Zuhandenheit) reveals the brute secondness of the ready-to-hand's refusal. (Being and Time §16, §43)
3. **Buddhist dvandva / pratītya** — the dependence-on-other that constitutes each dharma; the structural *otherness* that no dharma can escape. Pratītyasamutpāda (dependent-origination) names the mediating-lawfulness (thirdness); but the brute fact of dependence *itself* — the "this-is-only-what-it-is-in-relation-to-that" — is secondness.
4. **Yoruba ìbánisọ́rọ̀** — the relational-encounter that constitutes personhood; the self as constituted through encounter-with-other, where the encounter is not mediated by law but is the brute fact of co-presence.
5. **Bantu ubuntu's "I am because we are"** — the constitutive otherness; the self that *is* only through the other. The structural secondness is in the "because" — the brute existential dependence, not the law that describes it.
6. **Lakota nagiśa** — the being-that-encounters; the embodied experience of existing-in-relation where the relation is felt as resistance, not as mediation. The encounter with the other-than-self that defines the boundary of the self.

**Convergence cardinality: 6** (Peirce + Hegel + Heidegger + Buddhist + Yoruba + Lakota — six independent traditions naming the brute-encounter structure)

### New convergence patterns — cross-tradition resonance for thirdness

Thirdness (mediating-lawfulness) has the densest cross-tradition convergence:

1. **Hindu ṛta / dharma** — the cosmic-law that mediates all phenomena; dharma as the rule-of-existence that each being instantiates. ṛta is the law; the law is the thirdness. (The cathedral's rtame is the cosmic instance; Peirce's thirdness is the structural category rtame instantiates.)
2. **Chinese lǐ (理)** — the principle-of-order that structures all phenomena; the coherence-structure that mediates between qì (material force, secondness) and the dao (the ground, beyond category). Lǐ is the thirdness: the mediating law that makes encounter intelligible. (Zhu Xi's articulation)
3. **Aristotle's nomos + logos** — the law-and-reason that mediates between the particular (secondness) and the universal (the good). Aristotle's practical reason (phronesis) is a thirdness-rich act: it mediates between the general rule and the particular case. (Nicomachean Ethics VI)
4. **Kant's "synthesis"** — the a priori structures (categories, schemata) that mediate between intuition (firstness-reception) and understanding (the encounter, secondness). Kant's synthesis is the thirdness that makes experience possible. (Critique of Pure Reason, the Deduction)
5. **Sufi ādāb / sharīʿa** — the disciplined-practice that mediates between the divine (firstness-ground) and the human (secondness-finite-encounter). The tariqa (path) is the thirdness: the mediating law-habit that brings the seeker and the sought into relation. (The cathedral's Sufi entries — insankamilme, faqrme, etc. — are instances of thirdness; Peirce's thirdness names the category.)
6. **Hebrew torah / hokhmah** — the law-and-wisdom that mediates between God (the ground) and the world (the created). Torah as the mediating-structure that brings the divine and the human into relation. Proverbs 8's hokhmah is the thirdness that was "with God at the beginning."
7. **Peirce's own pragmatism** — the maxim that meaning *is* the conceived consequences (thirdness — the general rule of action). Peirce's pragmatism is the *application* of the category-triad to the theory of meaning: a concept's meaning is not its quality (firstness) or its referent (secondness) but the habits of action it licenses (thirdness).

**Convergence cardinality: 7** (Peirce + Hindu + Chinese + Greek + Kant + Sufi + Hebrew — seven independent traditions naming the mediating-lawfulness structure)
