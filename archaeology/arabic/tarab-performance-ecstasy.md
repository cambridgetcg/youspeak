# Arabic — Ṭarab (performance-ecstasy)

_The tongue in which a whole urban music-culture named itself after a felt-state. Where Sanskrit named the tasted content (rasa) and Greek named the mode's character (ethos), Arabic named the **circuit**: the heightened-feeling state that arises between a skilled performer and a knowing audience and is sustained by their mutual feeding. The word for "singer" in this culture is muṭrib — literally "the one who causes ṭarab." The profession is named by the state it induces._

---

## Native forms

### The root — ط ر ب (ṭ-r-b)

| Form | Transliteration | Gloss | Register |
|---|---|---|---|
| طَرِبَ | *ṭariba* | to be moved, stirred, agitated with emotion — **by joy OR grief** | classical verb; Lane's Lexicon: "emotion, or lively emotion... or agitation... by reason of joy or grief" |
| طَرَب | *ṭarab* | the stirred-state itself; in modern Eastern-Arab usage, ALSO the music that induces it and the milieu around it | the excavation-target |
| أَطْرَبَ | *aṭraba* | to cause ṭarab (Form IV causative) | the loop's performer-side verb |
| مُطْرِب / مُطْرِبَة | *muṭrib / muṭribah* | lead singer (m./f.) — literally "causer-of-ṭarab" | standard professional title to this day |
| طَرُوب / طَرَّاب | *ṭarūb / ṭarrāb* | one easily moved to ṭarab; emotionally responsive | the receptive disposition |

**Two structural facts the root carries.** First: classical *ṭarab* is emotional **commotion**, not pleasure — pre-Islamic and classical attestations include grief-stirring as well as joy-stirring. The modern musical sense keeps this width: a karuṇa-grade mawwāl induces ṭarab as surely as a bright dawr. Second: the causative chain is lexicalized — the culture built the word for *performer* out of the word for the *state*, which means the loop-structure is embedded in the morphology itself.

### The performance-culture lexicon (the loop's parts)

| Form | Transliteration | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| سَمِّيعَة | *sammīʿah* (sing. *sammīʿ*) | the initiated listeners — "those who listen"; aficionados with "the proper level of musical initiation and an innate disposition toward engaged and focused listening" (Racy) |
| سَلْطَنَة | *saltanah* | the performer's inspirational modal-absorption state (from *sulṭān*, "dominion") — arises when a tradition-grounded performer with conducive *rūḥ* (soul) performs among skilled fellow musicians AND present sammīʿah |
| قَفْلَة | *qaflah* (pl. *qaflāt*) | "closure" — the cadential pattern ending a melodic phrase; the listeners release their ecstatic exclamations in the brief pauses after the qaflāt |
| عُرَب | *ʿurab* | the smaller pitch-inflections whose masterful execution connoisseurs praise in the great ṭarab singers |
| خامة | *khāmah* | timbre, quality-of-sound — the voice as ṭarab medium par excellence |
| حَفْلَة / جَلْسَة | *ḥaflah / jalsah* | public musical event / intimate gathering of music lovers — the loop's two social containers |
| مَقَام / سَيْر | *maqām / sayr* | melodic mode / its characteristic course — saltanah is maqām-specific ("haunted by the musical feeling of Bayyātī") |
| آه! الله! يا سلام! | *āh! Allāh! yā salām!* | the attested exclamation-repertoire — the audience-side signal that closes the loop |

### The adjacent mystical register — deliberately NOT taken

| Form | Transliteration | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| سَمَاع | *samāʿ* | Sufi sacred listening — the disciplined audition-rite |
| وَجْد | *wajd* | finding-ecstasy — the mystical trance-state in samāʿ (root w-j-d, shared with *wujūd*, being; see archaeology/arabic/relationship-with-divine.md §wajd) |

Al-Ghazālī's *Iḥyāʾ ʿUlūm al-Dīn* contains the "Kitāb Ādāb al-Samāʿ wa-l-Wajd" ("Book on the Manners of Listening and Ecstasy") — the tradition itself distinguishes the **artistic** ṭarab register from the **mystical** samāʿ/wajd register, while knowing they are cousins. This excavation takes only the artistic side; wajd stays in its Sufi home (already woven into heurekin's convergence-set).

## Attestations

1. **Pre-Islamic and classical poetry-recitation** — ṭarab appears first for the stirred-state under chanted poetry, before instrumental music dominates the sense.
2. **Abbasid Baghdad** — court listeners, caliphs included, "moving their feet, dancing, sobbing, weeping, and tearing their garments" under sung poetry (Racy 2010, after George Sawa, *Music Performance Practice in the Early ʿAbbasid Era, 132–320 AH*); the *Kitāb al-Aghānī* (Book of Songs) of Abū l-Faraj al-Iṣfahānī (10th c.) is the great archive of this culture.
3. **al-Ghazālī (d. 1111)** — *Iḥyāʾ* vol. 2, the samāʿ/wajd chapter: the theological-juridical reception of listening-ecstasy.
4. **19th-century European observers** — Villoteau (*Description de l'Égypte*) and Edward Lane (*Manners and Customs of the Modern Egyptians*) both record "the local listeners' highly animated verbal responses during musical performances."
5. **The recorded golden age** — early 78 rpm discs (Sami al-Shawwa, al-Qasabji) punctuated by "animated ṭarab exclamations that praise the musicians and address them by name"; Umm Kulthum's 1940s–50s performances as the canonical loop-instances — connoisseurs "locate and obtain specific recordings of performances during which the artists have obviously been in a saltanah state."
6. **Modern scholarship** — A. J. Racy, *Making Music in the Arab World: The Culture and Artistry of Ṭarab* (Cambridge, 2003) and "Art and Ecstasy in Arab Music" (Georgetown CCAS Occasional Papers, 2010); Jonathan Shannon, *Among the Jasmine Trees* (Aleppo's sammīʿah); Kenneth Habib ("a common emotional experience to which both contribute"); New Grove: "a state of heightened emotion or ecstasy felt by musicians and audience that is central to Arab music performance."
7. **The living witness** — Sabah Fakhri to Racy: the connoisseurs' "sustained input... creates within him an inspirational state that enables him to excel musically." This sentence IS the loop, attested first-person.

## Semantic territories

### Territory A — ṭarab as listener-state (ecstasy)

The heightened-feeling state in the listener: "intense emotions, exaltation, a sense of yearning or absorption, feeling of timelessness" — listeners report "hours passed and we did not feel the time." Racy: "a multifaceted concept that has no exact equivalent in the English language," conventionally glossed "musical ecstasy" or "enchantment."

**This territory alone is NOT the gap** — English "musical ecstasy / rapture" approximates the individual-state reading, and the cathedral's **rasame** already names the tasted aesthetic-content. Donor-side: covered.

### Territory B — ṭarab as feedback-circuit (THE GAP)

The state is not individual-located; it is **loop-located**. The attested structure:

1. The performer, tradition-grounded, with conducive *rūḥ*, enters **saltanah** — but saltanah's *conditions of arising include the presence of sammīʿah*.
2. The saltanah-borne rendition (ʿurab held true, qaflah landed) induces ṭarab in the sammīʿah.
3. "The ecstatic responses of the audience members **further propel the performer** and cause him or her to develop and maintain a feeling of saltanah throughout the performance" (Racy 2010 — the loop in one sentence).
4. The circuit escalates; without it "the music becomes just ordinary."

Neither pole owns the state; "performers express themselves musically and audience members respond in affirming ways"; both contribute to "a common emotional experience." **This is the relational-quality structure — qing-family territory.** No English word names the circuit. This is what the tarabqing forge takes.

### Territory C — ṭarab as music-genre and milieu

"The word ṭarab refers to both the ecstatic state evoked by traditional Arab music and to the music itself" (Racy) — and by extension the social milieu of ṭarab makers and consumers in Cairo, Beirut, Aleppo, Damascus. **Closed for this forge** — the coinage takes the state-structure, sheds the genre/milieu senses (an honest semantic-coverage narrowing, recorded in assessment).

### Territory D — ṭarab vs wajd (artistic vs mystical ecstasy)

Arabic itself splits what English "ecstasy" bundles: *ṭarab* (artistic, secular-leaning, ḥaflah-located) vs *wajd* (mystical, samāʿ-located, finding-the-Beloved). The cathedral honors the split: ṭarab feeds this forge; wajd remains in the Sufi register. **Closed; protocol-respected.**

## Negative space — what the ṭarab lexicon reveals that English cannot say

1. **The loop has no English name.** "Musical ecstasy" (individual intensity), "flow" (individual absorption), "collective effervescence" (Durkheim — ritual-generic crowd arousal, no connoisseurship-pole, no performer↔audience asymmetric circuit), "communitas" (Turner — liminal equality-bond, not performance-feedback), "groove/swing" (musician-side rhythmic locking), "rapport" (generic attunement), "electric atmosphere" (atmosphere-located, structureless). Every near-miss locates the state in an individual, a crowd, or an atmosphere; none locates it in the **circuit**.
2. **The knowing-audience requirement.** Saltanah requires sammīʿah — the loop needs *initiated* listening, not mere attendance. English has "connoisseur" for taste but no term making connoisseurship a *causal condition of the performer's state*.
3. **The exclamation as load-bearing signal.** The āh/Allāh/yā-salām released after the qaflah is not interruption but the loop's return-path — disciplined receptive restraint (the sammīʿ listens through the phrase) followed by bold released speech (the exclamation in the pause). The ṭarab adab is the careful-and-open pattern at music-scale.
4. **Joy-and-grief width.** Classical ṭarab covers being-stirred by grief as well as joy — the circuit can run on karuṇa-grade content. English "ecstasy" tilts euphoric.

| Tradition | Term | Relation |
|---|---|---|
| Persian | *ḥāl* | "the spiritual mood of the audience and performer... [in which] the musician does things which are not necessarily planned, but just 'come out' because he is not himself" (Morteza Varzi) — closest cousin; still state-located rather than loop-named |
| Spanish flamenco | *duende* | Lorca: "a power and not a construct... a struggle and not a concept" — performer-side daimonic pole |

| Sufi | *samāʿ/wajd* | the mystical-register cousin (see Territory D) |

| Sociology (discipline-analog) | Durkheim's collective effervescence | structural analog at ritual-generic scale; lacks the connoisseurship-pole |

## Verdict on the excavation

Arabic contributes, in ṭarab + saltanah + sammīʿah, the most precisely **articulated** performer-audience ecstasy-circuit lexicon of any tradition surveyed: the loop's poles, its causal conditions, its signal-path (qaflah → exclamation → saltanah), and its morphological embedding (muṭrib = causer-of-the-state) are all native. Persian ḥāl knows the state; flamenco duende knows the performer's daemon; jazz knows the groove; only the ṭarab culture lexicalized the **circuit's full anatomy**. The gap is real for English; the donor is the strongest available; the relational structure assigns the coinage to the qing-family. Transliteration is not coinage — *tarabqing* carries the circuit into YOUSPEAK morphology with the felt-bond suffix making the loop-location visible.
