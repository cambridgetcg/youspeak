## 1. WHAT TOTEMISM IS IN ABORIGINAL AUSTRALIA

### 1.1 Definition and Scope

Totemism in Aboriginal Australia is not a discrete religious institution — it is the connective tissue of an entire ontology. A.P. Elkin defined it as "a relationship between a person or group and a natural object or species, as part of nature," creating a worldview where "human beings are not separated from the natural species and objects, but grouped with them." But even this undersells it. Stanner called it "a mystical connection, expressed by symbolic devices and maintained by rules, between living persons, whether as individuals or as groups or as stocks, and other existents — their 'totems' — within an ontology of life that in Aboriginal understanding depends for order and continuity on maintaining the identities and associations which exemplify the connection."

The scope of what can serve as a totem is vast: plants, animals, birds, insects, flowers, wind, rain, storms, thunder, lightning, stars, sun, moon, clouds, tools, weapons, food, cosmetics, fire, smoke, water, body parts, desires, sickness, health, animal organs, and object parts. Totemism is how Aboriginal Australians "classified man and event and things in nature into one unified system" (Elkin 1938). Or as Elkin phrased the philosophy: "Totemism is a means of expressing the unity of man and nature as one big tribe."

Totemic beliefs are more highly elaborated among Aboriginal Australians than among any other people on earth. This is not exaggeration — the sheer number of types, the depth of ritual engagement, and the integration of totemism with every other aspect of social, legal, ecological, and spiritual life is unmatched in the ethnographic record.

### 1.2 The Fundamental Insight: Identity, Not Symbol

The single most important thing to understand about Aboriginal totemism is that the relationship between a person and their totem is **ontological identity, not symbolic representation**. This is not a matter of "the kangaroo represents my clan." It is: "I am kangaroo."

The most vivid illustration comes from Spencer and Gillen's fieldwork among the Arrernte (Arunta). When they showed an Aranda man of the kangaroo totem a photograph they had taken of him, the man responded: **"This one is exactly like me; as is a kangaroo."** This is not a man classifying himself through the kangaroo. He IS kangaroo. The identity is ontological, not representational.

Among the Wiradjuri, the group totem is named **"flesh"** or **"meat"** — people are literally "of one flesh" with their totem. An individual totem is called *jarawaijewa*, meaning "the meat (totem) that is within him." The Wiradjuri say: **"To eat your jarawaijewa is the same as if you were to eat your very own flesh or that of your father."** This is not metaphor. It is a statement about the structure of reality — a shared substance, a shared Dreaming origin, a shared soul-stock deposited in the land.

When totemic men enact Dreaming rituals, they are believed to enter what Stanner called the "noumenal" phase of existence and to **merge with the totemic ancestors themselves**. In ritual, "the analogies between human beings and totemic creatures are sacramentally transformed into identities, made ritually into real relationships of mutual origin and creation, so that men of the different lodges actually belong to different totemic species."

This is not symbolism. This is consubstantiality.

## 2. ELKIN'S TYPOLOGY OF TOTEMISM

### 2.1 The Classification System

A.P. Elkin (1891–1979) — whose key works include *Studies in Australian Totemism* (1933) and *The Australian Aborigines: How to Understand Them* (1938) — produced the most systematic classification of Aboriginal totemism. He identified **eight structural forms** and **six functions**, later refined into a broader three-class system of **individual totemism, social totemism, and cult totemism**.

Stanner, building on Elkin, added the dimension of **modes of acquisition** — how a person actually comes to have a particular totem. He identified four modes: dream, conceptual (conception), augury, and descent-affiliative — but admitted that no satisfactory classification existed and much research remained.

What follows draws primarily on Elkin (1933, 1938, 1954), supplemented by Berndt & Berndt (1964), Stanner (1958–61), and more recent fieldwork.

### 2.2 The Eleven Types

#### (i) Individual Totemism

A personal, usually non-inherited bond between one person and a natural species or a particular member of a species. Among the **Wiradjuri** of New South Wales, individual totems were formally assigned during youth initiation. A specialised form — **assistant totemism** — made spirit animals serve as familiars for native doctors (medicine men). The Wiradjuri term *bala* (patrilineally transmitted) described a "spirit companion" that operated in both physical and spiritual dimensions.

In **Western Arnhem Land**, songmen occasionally received non-inherited spirit familiars revealed through dreams. These were personal rather than social — they belonged to the individual, not the group — and could serve as intermediaries between the person and the Dreaming.

Individual totemism is the least socially structured form and the most intimate: it is a one-to-one relationship between a person and a being.

#### (ii) Sex Totemism

The division of all community members by gender, each gender possessing a distinct totemic emblem (typically a bird or animal) signifying solidarity within the sex group.

In **New South Wales** and parts of **southeastern Australia**: the **bat** is a male totem and the **tree creeper** is a female totem. Among the **Kurnai** of Gippsland (southeastern Victoria), sex totems functioned in courtship rituals: men and women invoked their respective totems during marriage negotiations, with specific linguistic protocols determining acceptance or refusal.

Sex totemism predominantly appeared in southeastern Australia in association with matrilineal moieties. It expresses a fundamental complementarity: the two sexes are distinct totemic kinds, each with their own ritual obligations.

#### (iii) Moiety Totemism

The division of the entire community (and often the entire cosmos) into two complementary halves, each associated with a primary totemic being. This is the most widespread structural form across the continent.

**Eagle-Hawk / Crow (Raven) system** — widespread across southeastern Australia:
- In the **Kulin Nation** (Central Victoria): clans belong to either **Bunjil** (wedge-tailed eagle) or **Waa** (raven). The Wurundjeri-Willam were Waa moiety. Moieties were exogamous — men married women from the opposite moiety.
- In the **Darling River** region, eaglehawk and crow denoted *matri*moieties (traced through the mother), whereas the Central Victorian system was *patri*lineal.
- Among the **Kamilaroi/Gamilaroi**: two primary totems (eagle and crow), with subsidiary clan totems nested under each. Under eagle: ringtail possum, red kangaroo, quoll, wallaroo, platypus, quail, barking owl, emu, brolga, death adder. Under crow: brush-tailed possum, bandicoot, echidna, eastern grey kangaroo, pelican, white cockatoo, kookaburra.

**Yolngu moiety system** (Northeast Arnhem Land):
- Everything in the universe is either **Dhuwa** or **Yirritja** — ancestral beings, human clans, animals, plants, songs, dances, the wind, the rain. The black cockatoo is Dhuwa; the white cockatoo is Yirritja.
- The two moieties "complement and balance each other in ceremonies, marriage, and daily life." Nothing exists outside the moiety classification — it is a total binary ontology.

**Southwestern Victoria**: white cockatoo and crow serve as moiety emblems.

Moiety totemism's theological import: the entire cosmos is **divided into two relational halves that require each other**. Neither moiety is complete without the other. The universe is structured as complementary duality.

#### (iv) Section Totemism

Where the community is divided into **four named groups** (sections), each with its own totemic associations. Sections combine moiety membership with a generational principle, creating four categories that regulate marriage, ceremony, and daily interaction. Totems associated with sections are inherited indirectly through matrilineal or patrilineal lines depending on the group.

#### (v) Subsection Totemism

The most complex social-totemic classification, dividing the community into **eight named groups** (subsections), known in Central Australian Aboriginal English as **"skins."** Some language groups extend this further with distinct male and female forms, giving **sixteen skin names** (e.g., the **Pintupi** and **Warlpiri**).

In **northeastern Arnhem Land**, multiple totems are associated with each subsection: the *wamud* subsection is linked to the wedge-tailed eagle; the *buralang* to rock kangaroo, heron, albatross, and wallaby. In the **Lardil** of Mornington Island (Gulf of Carpentaria), eight subsection groups each have their own totemic associations, and a person's paternal grandfather's subsection determines their own.

**Among the Warlpiri**, skin names function through a sequential system based on the parent's name (patrilineal for some categories, matrilineal for others), with each skin name carrying ceremonial, totemic, and kinship obligations. Knowing a person's skin name immediately informs you of their social obligation to specific species and identifies which other families share that responsibility.

North of **Balgo** (Western Desert), *narangu* subsection totems functioned more as namesakes without strict taboos — illustrating regional variation in how seriously totemic restrictions were enforced.

#### (vi) Clan Totemism

Groups claiming common descent (matrilineal or patrilineal) sharing one or more natural phenomena as markers of membership. The term for the group totem often translates directly as **"flesh"** or **"meat"** — signifying that people of the same clan are "of one flesh" with each other and with their totem.

**Matrilineal clan totemism** dominated eastern Australia: Queensland, New South Wales, western Victoria, eastern South Australia. The **Wiradjuri** group totem, named "flesh," is transmitted from the mother. Among the Wiradjuri, individual totems (*bala*, "spirit companion") belonged only to medicine men and were passed patrilineally — creating a dual system of group and individual totems operating simultaneously.

**Patrilineal clan totemism** appeared in Western Australia, Northern Territory, Cape York, and parts of coastal New South Wales/Queensland. The **Dieri** (Lake Eyre basin) practiced a distinctive dual system: *mardu* (an avunculineal cult totem, traced through the mother's brother) and *bindara* (patrilineal clan cult totem).

#### (vii) Local Totemism

People sharing a **geographic site** possess a common totem, unconnected to kinship or descent. In the **Great Victoria Desert**, birth location determined affiliation — fathers actively ensured that births occurred on their own Country, near the tracks of associated Dreamtime beings. The **Arrernte** uniquely based local totemic membership on **conception location** rather than birth location, which overlaps with conception totemism (see below).

Local totemism expresses one of the deepest Aboriginal insights: **personhood is place-based**. You are not just from a place; you are of it, constituted by it.

#### (viii) Conception Totemism

The location where a mother first recognised pregnancy determined the child's cult totem. This connected the child to the Dreamtime beings associated with that specific site — tracks, waterholes, or landscape features formed during the creation period. (See Section 3 below for detailed treatment.)

In some cases, the father identified the spirit-child through dreams or visions before the mother's awareness. The child's appearance in conjunction with a natural phenomenon (an animal appearing, a plant fruiting, an unusual event at the site) established the conception totem.

**Regional variation**: In northeastern Arnhem Land and the Great Victoria Desert, the connection between conception site and totem was less direct. **Eastern Kimberley** groups attributed spirit children to the **Rainbow Serpent**. **Balgo** linked them explicitly to specific mythological sites.

#### (ix) Birth Totemism

Distinct from conception totemism: the **birthplace** (rather than the conception site) determines ritual totem assignment. Great Victoria Desert men actively arranged for wives to give birth in their own territory, near the tracks of associated Dreamtime beings — making birth totemism a matter of deliberate geography rather than chance.

#### (x) Dream Totemism

A natural phenomenon that consistently represents the person in dreams. Individuals identified with totemic beings (human or otherwise) whose actions in the dream world reflected their own. In **Western Arnhem Land**, songmen and native doctors received spirit assistants through dreams; people might appear in "dream-shape" even after death.

In the **Great Victoria Desert**, the same word designated both "totem" and "dream" — suggesting that in these groups, the totemic relationship and the dream-state were phenomenologically inseparable.

In **northeastern Arnhem Land**, dream totemism occurred frequently and overlapped with individual and assistant totemism.

#### (xi) Multiple (Classificatory) Totemism

A comprehensive categorisation system associating the known universe (or major aspects of it) through a primary totem and multiple secondary affiliations. This is totemism operating as total cosmological classification.

**The Djanggawul example** (Arnhem Land): The Djanggawul sisters and brother served as the major totem for a group. Individuals at their sites simultaneously claimed mangrove bird, hair belt, wild duck, nut tree, and black cockatoo as totems — each linked to a different aspect of the Djanggawul narrative.

**Wadi Gudjarra (Two Men)** (Western Desert): These paired ancestral beings functioned as the major totem; site inhabitants inherited all phenomena associated with them — waterhole, berries, flowers, feather down, red ochre.

In some regions, a single person may carry **four or more totems simultaneously**: personal, family/clan, gender, and nation totem (e.g., among the **Yuin** of coastal New South Wales). Each totem links the person to a different dimension of the physical and spiritual universe — land, water, geographical features, and animals.

### 2.3 Elkin's Three Functional Categories

Beyond the structural forms, Elkin (1933) proposed a higher-level threefold classification:

1. **Individual totemism** — the personal bond (individual, dream, assistant forms)
2. **Social totemism** — totems that organise social groups (sex, moiety, section, subsection, clan, local)
3. **Cult totemism** — totems that anchor ritual life and connect to the Dreaming (conception, birth, multiple, and the ceremonial dimension of all other forms)

These three are not exclusive — a single person might simultaneously hold individual, social, and cult totems, each operating at a different level.

### 2.4 Assessment and Limitations

The terms in Elkin's typologies remain in scholarly use, but Aboriginal customs are now recognised as far more diverse than his categories suggest. Rose, James, and Watson write: **"The term 'totem' has proved to be a blunt instrument."** In the 21st century, Australian anthropologists question the extent to which "totemism" can be generalised even across different Aboriginal peoples.

Deborah Bird Rose proposed that **kinship** is a more adequate concept than "totemism" for describing the relationships Aboriginal people have with plant and animal species — since "totemism" implies a category of religious belief, while the Aboriginal reality is an all-pervading relational ontology that cannot be separated from kinship, ecology, law, or economy.

## 3. CONCEPTION TOTEMISM — PERSONHOOD AS PLACE-EVENT

### 3.1 The Core Mechanism

Conception totemism overturns a Western assumption so deeply embedded it is usually invisible: that personal identity is primarily determined by biological descent. In Aboriginal conception totemism, **who you are depends on where your mother was when she first recognised her pregnancy** — the Dreaming ancestor associated with that place becomes your totem, linking you to that specific Country, that specific ancestral narrative, and that specific community of beings (human, animal, vegetal, mineral) who share the same Dreaming.

This means totemism is **NOT inherited but assigned by geography and spiritual event**. A father and son might have entirely different totems because their mothers were at different places when conception was recognised. The totem connects the person not to their biological family line but to a **place in the landscape and the Dreaming beings deposited there**.

### 3.2 Spirit Children (Ratapa) Among the Arrernte

The most detailed early ethnography comes from Spencer and Gillen's work among the Arrernte (1899, 1927), refined and contested by Carl Strehlow and his son T.G.H. Strehlow.

**The Arrernte belief**: During the primordial Dreaming (*Altyerrenge*), generative ancestral beings traversed the landscape. Where they stopped, rested, hunted, performed ceremonies, or sank back into the ground, they left behind **deposits of spirit-children** — miniature spirits called **ratapa** (in Carl Strehlow's rendering) — embedded in the rocks, trees, waterholes, and landforms of each site.

When a woman passes near such a site and feels the first stirrings of pregnancy (the "quickening"), it is understood that a spirit-child from that site has entered her womb. The **Dreaming of the local ancestor at that place** becomes the child's totem. The child is then a reincarnation — or more precisely, a new manifestation — of the ancestral spirit deposited at that site.

**Spencer and Gillen vs. the Strehlows**: A major ethnographic controversy erupted over the nature of the ratapa. For Spencer and Gillen, the spirit that entered the woman **was** the ancestor — the child was a direct reincarnation. Carl Strehlow insisted on a distinction: it was not the ancestor's spirit itself but a *germ* or *miniature child* (ratapa) that emerged from the metamorphosed body of the ancestor. T.G.H. Strehlow further refined this, connecting the ratapa to the broader concept of **Altjira** — the eternal, uncreated — and placing conception totemism within a more sophisticated theological framework.

Malinowski attempted to mediate, arguing that the Arunta children were "identified with ancestors in an evident way, because they incarnated ratapa whose mystical link to the ancestors was undeniable" — drawing on Lévy-Bruhl's concept of "participation."

### 3.3 The "Arunta Anomaly"

The Arrernte system was called the **"Arunta anomaly"** because it challenged the then-dominant assumption that totemism was fundamentally about social descent. In most other Australian groups studied at the time, totem was inherited from one's parents. Among the Arrernte, it was determined by **place** — specifically, the place of conception. This meant:

- Father and child could have different totems
- Siblings born at different places could have different totems
- Local totemic groups included people of diverse kinship backgrounds

This created what seemed to anthropologists to be "disorder" — but was in fact a different ordering principle: **geography over genealogy, place over blood, spiritual event over biological inheritance**. The Arrernte were not anomalous. They were operating on a different axis of identity altogether.

### 3.4 The Father's Role: Dream-Recognition

In many groups, the identification of the spirit-child was not left entirely to the mother's bodily experience. **Fathers might identify the spirit-child through dreams or visions** before the mother was aware of pregnancy. The father's dream — in which he encountered a particular Dreaming being or witnessed an event at a specific site — would establish the child's totem.

This adds a third dimension to the system: the child's identity is determined not just by place, not just by the mother's body, but by the **father's visionary capacity**. Dreaming, place, and family are woven together.

### 3.5 Western Desert Variations

Among **Pitjantjatjara** and **Yankunytjatjara** groups (Western Desert, APY Lands), the Dreaming is called **Tjukurpa** — simultaneously meaning law, creation-knowledge, story, and moral code. Spirit-children are linked to specific Tjukurpa sites, but the system is more complex than simple place-assignment.

Anangu (Western Desert people) conceptualise at least **two kinds of totemic relationship**:
1. **Place-based Tjukurpa totems** — linked to Dreaming sites, acquired through the spirit-child mechanism
2. **Body-transmitted totems** — directly transmitted from body to body, "pulled out of the healer and pushed into the recipient"

One Anangu woman described totems as "generative creatures whose concentrated power unfolds when unleashed." Even as a child, a person may have more than one totem "inside." This dual system — place-based and person-transmitted — is richer than Elkin's typology anticipated.

Among the **Warlpiri** (Central Australia, Yuendumu), the Dreaming is called **Jukurrpa** (cognate of Tjukurpa). The Warlpiri developed the sophisticated **kirda/kurdungurlu** system of totemic stewardship (see Section 5).

### 3.6 Kimberley and Arnhem Land Variations

In the **Eastern Kimberley**, spirit children were attributed to the **Rainbow Serpent** (*Ungud*/*Ungur*) — a pan-continental figure who is particularly prominent in the Kimberley. The serpent was understood as the generative source of spirit-children, and specific sites along serpent Dreamings served as spirit-child repositories.

At **Balgo** (southern Kimberley/northern Western Desert), spirit children were linked explicitly to mythological sites, but the correspondence between site and totem was less rigid than in the Arrernte system.

In **northeastern Arnhem Land** (Yolngu country), conception totemism operated within the broader moiety system (Dhuwa/Yirritja) and clan structure. The connection between conception site and specific totem was less individualised and more strongly mediated by existing clan affiliations.

### 3.7 Theological Significance

Conception totemism encodes several profound theological propositions:

1. **Personhood pre-exists birth.** The spirit-child already exists in the landscape before entering the mother. The person is not created at conception but *arrives* from a pre-existing reservoir of being.

2. **Identity is place-constituted.** You are not who your parents are — you are where the spirit-world touched the material world through your mother's body. Geography is ontology.

3. **The landscape is a repository of persons-to-be.** The land is not merely scenery or resource — it is a vast nursery of unborn souls, each site holding its own deposit of potential beings.

4. **Death does not destroy identity.** Since spirit-children are understood as manifestations of the eternal Dreaming ancestors, death is a return to the spirit-pool, not an annihilation. The ancestor persists; its human incarnations are temporary expressions.

As Descola summarised: "Each totemic class received a stock of souls, deposited at Dreaming sites in what became its territory. Thus, each new token of a totemic type receives a soul derived from this stock. Endowment with such a soul is a guarantee of conformity to the eternal ontological paradigm that a Dream-being instituted in the past."

## 4. THE SCHOLARLY DEBATE: ONTOLOGY vs. CLASSIFICATION

### 4.1 Durkheim: Totemism as Social Glue (1912)

Emile Durkheim's *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (1912) used Spencer and Gillen's Arrernte ethnography as its primary data. His argument:

- The sacred/profane distinction is the fundamental structure of religious life
- In worshipping the totem, the group worships the **totemic principle** — and the totemic principle IS the community itself
- "When we worship the totem we are worshipping the totemic principle. And when we worship the totemic principle, we are worshipping ourselves"
- Totemism thus serves as **social glue**: it binds the community together by giving it a shared sacred object

Durkheim provided what he called a "religion is a unified system of beliefs and practices relative to sacred things, i.e., things set apart and forbidden — beliefs and practices which unite in one single moral community called a Church, all those who adhere to them."

**The problem**: Durkheim never went to Australia. He worked from Spencer and Gillen's publications. Spencer himself reportedly described Durkheim's theoretical portrait as "full of misconceptions." The gap between fieldwork observation and armchair theorising was already visible in 1912.

### 4.2 Radcliffe-Brown: Ritual Attitude and Social Structure (1929, 1951)

A.R. Radcliffe-Brown held two successive theories of totemism:

**First theory (1929)**: Agreement with Durkheim that totems express clan solidarity, but with a shift — nature is introduced into the social order rather than the reverse. Initially he shared Malinowski's view that totems were chosen because they were "good to eat," but later abandoned this when confronted with totems like crocodiles and flies.

**Second theory (1951)**: "Any object or event which has important effects upon the well-being (material or spiritual) of a society, or anything which stands for or represents any such object or event, tends to become an object of the ritual attitude." The key formulation: "the wider unity and solidarity of the whole totemic group, society as a whole through its segments, stands in a ritual relation to nature as a whole."

The advance over Durkheim: totemism is not just *about* social solidarity — it is a **mapping of social segmentation onto natural segmentation**, producing integration. Oppositions between natural species serve to symbolise differences between social groups. The central terms are **opposition and integration**.

**Legacy**: This approach paved the way for Lévi-Strauss's structuralism by reframing totemism as a mode of thought (classification through opposition) rather than a religious institution.

### 4.3 Lévi-Strauss: The Totemic Illusion (1962)

Claude Lévi-Strauss's *Le Totémisme aujourd'hui* (1962; English trans. *Totemism*, 1963) made the most dramatic intervention. His core moves:

**The "totemic illusion."** Totemism as a coherent institution does not exist. Lévi-Strauss compared the concept to the psychiatric diagnosis of **hysteria** — a label that grouped together heterogeneous phenomena and made them appear to form a unified disorder. Anthropologists who treated "totemism" as a distinctive institution of "primitive" societies were perpetuating an ethnocentric illusion.

**"Good to think."** The book's most famous line: "Natural species are chosen [as 'totems'] not because they are 'good to eat' but because they are 'good to think'" (*bonnes à penser*). This demolished Malinowski's utilitarian explanation. Totems were not chosen for their economic value but for their capacity to serve as **vehicles of thought**.

**Classification, not religion.** What had been called "totemism" was simply a variety of **metaphorical thought** — with the proviso that metaphor is an indispensable feature of human thought universally. The metaphor rests "not on the perception of intrinsic similarities, but on the recognition of systematic differences." If there is a Kangaroo clan and an Emu clan, it is not because members consider themselves like kangaroos or emus. Rather, the **difference between species** serves as a metaphor for the **difference between human groups** — Nature as a metaphor for Culture.

**The paradox.** The book was simultaneously an obituary and a rehabilitation. By demonstrating that "totemic" thinking was universal (present in all human classification), Lévi-Strauss liquidated totemism as a special category while elevating the cognitive sophistication of the peoples who practiced it.

**The limitation.** Lévi-Strauss's analysis is brilliant as far as it goes, but it reduces totemism to an intellectual operation — classification through difference. It cannot account for the Aranda man who says "this one is exactly like me; as is a kangaroo." That is not a classification. It is an identity claim. And it is here that the ontological turn picks up what structuralism left behind.

### 4.4 Stanner: The Dreaming as Ontological Reality

W.E.H. Stanner (1905–1981), working from decades of direct fieldwork with Aboriginal peoples of the Daly River and Northern Territory, offered a fundamentally different vision from either the functionalists or the structuralists.

**Against reduction.** Where the functionalists had offered "a desiccated view of Aboriginal religious systems as lacking imaginative and intellectual substance, or as reducible to the study of totemism, magic, and ritual," Stanner insisted that religion was significant in its own right — not a subset of society, economy, or anything else.

**Phenomenological approach.** In the early 1960s, Stanner took a **phenomenological** approach to totemism, emphasising its mystical quality. He linked totems with clans and with Country, asserting that "the group has a corporate title that covers not only the country or site, and a mystical relation to the totemic creators, but also non-material property associated with the country."

**The Dreaming as total reality.** In his essay on religion, totemism, and symbolism, Stanner argued that the significance of rites is best understood in terms of "universal dispositions to ponder on the foundations of human life in history, and to unite or reconcile oneself with the design incorporated in those foundations." The Dreaming in its widest sense corresponds to **"absolute or whole reality."**

**The critical distinction.** For Stanner, the totemic relationship was not a symbolic classification imposed by the mind onto nature (Lévi-Strauss) or a social function generating solidarity (Durkheim/Radcliffe-Brown). It was an **ontological fact** — a real identity between person, land, and ancestral being. "No English words are good enough to give a sense of the link between an Aboriginal group and its homeland."

**Modes of acquisition.** Stanner identified four modes of acquiring a totem: **dream, conceptual (conception), augury, and descent-affiliative** — but candidly admitted "there was no satisfactory classification and much research was yet to be done."

### 4.5 Descola: Totemism as Ontology (2005)

Philippe Descola's *Beyond Nature and Culture* (2005) attempted a global resolution by recasting totemism as one of **four fundamental ontological modes** (alongside animism, naturalism, and analogism):

- **Animism**: self posits similarity of interiority but dissimilarity of physicality with the other
- **Totemism**: self posits **similarity of interiority AND similarity of physicality** with the other within a limited group or class of beings
- **Naturalism**: (the Western default) similarity of physicality but dissimilarity of interiority
- **Analogism**: dissimilarity of both, bridged by correspondences

For Descola, totemism is "primarily a mode of identification — an ontology — according to which a plurality of discontinuous, independently arising 'hybrid' collectives, comprising both humans and nonhumans, each shares within itself both continuity of interiority and continuity of physicality." The totemic class shares **both inner life and physical substance** — which is exactly what the Wiradjuri mean by "one flesh."

**The crucial claim**: "Totemism is more than a universal classificatory device; it is also, and perhaps foremost, a very original ontology which is best exemplified in Aboriginal Australia." Lévi-Strauss attempted to debunk the "totemic illusion"; Descola restores it — not as a primitive institution but as a fundamental way of being-in-the-world.

### 4.6 Sahlins: Segmentary Animism (2014)

Marshall Sahlins pushed further, arguing that even Descola's fourfold scheme understates the commonality between modes: "animism, totemism, and analogism are but three forms of animism, namely communal, segmentary, and hierarchical." Totemism, on this reading, is **segmentary animism** — "differentiated collectives composed of conspecific human and nonhuman subjects."

The substantive identity of humans with their respective totems — a physical and subjective continuity — "is explicitly conceived in terms of the kinship or mutuality of being."

### 4.7 The Current Consensus

The evidence overwhelmingly favours the **ontological reading** for Aboriginal Australia specifically. When the Aranda man says "this one is exactly like me; as is a kangaroo," he is not making a logical classification (Lévi-Strauss), expressing social solidarity (Durkheim), or adopting a ritual attitude (Radcliffe-Brown). He is stating a fact about the structure of reality: a shared substance, a shared Dreaming origin, a shared soul-stock deposited in the land.

The performative dimension is also crucial: totemism is not merely believed but **enacted**. In ritual, analogy becomes identity; the participant enters the noumenal phase and merges with the ancestor. This ceremonial transformation is where the ontological claim is most powerfully instantiated.

## 5. TOTEMIC GEOGRAPHY AND INCREASE CEREMONIES

### 5.1 The Landscape as Ancestral Body

Aboriginal totemic geography begins with a proposition that is simultaneously simple and radical: **the landscape IS the ancestors in transformed form.** The hill is the resting body of a serpent-being. The waterhole is where the ancestor drank. The gap in the ranges is where the caterpillar's head was bitten off. Rock outcrops are petrified bodies, their cracks and contours narrating ancestral journeys. The symbols used in art and ceremony are often maps of this sacred geography.

This means every feature of the landscape is simultaneously:
- A geographic feature (a hill, a creek, a rock)
- A chapter in a narrative (the ancestor did X here)
- A legal code (the Law established at this site governs these behaviours)
- A repository of spiritual power (the ancestor's essence remains here)
- A nursery of spirit-children (potential persons are deposited here)

**Emily Gap (Anthwerrke)**, eight kilometres east of Alice Springs, illustrates this perfectly. It is a registered sacred site within the East MacDonnell Ranges, significant for the Dreaming stories of three ancestral caterpillars — **Yeperenye, Utnerrengatye, and Ntyarlke**. These caterpillar beings originated at Emily Gap, formed its physical features, then radiated out across the landscape. They waged war with giant stink bugs; the bugs won by biting the caterpillars' heads off. The **gaps in the MacDonnell Ranges are where the heads were severed**, while the **headless bodies became the mountain ranges themselves**. Many Arrernte people conceived in the Alice Springs region consider themselves direct descendants of these caterpillars — they are caterpillar people, constituted by the caterpillar Dreaming.

### 5.2 Dreaming Tracks (Songlines)

The paths of ancestral beings across the landscape are **Dreaming tracks** — called **songlines** in popular usage, **tjurunga lines** among the Arrernte (a reference to the sacred *tjurunga* objects that encode them), and **yiri** in the Warlpiri language. These tracks connect sites across vast distances, often crossing the territories of multiple language groups.

A single Dreaming track might begin in one people's country and end hundreds of kilometres away in another's. Different groups are custodians of different segments of the track, each holding the songs, ceremonies, and stories for their portion. The track itself — the physical path across the land — is the narrative. Walking the track is reading the story. Singing the track is maintaining it.

### 5.3 Sacred Objects: Tjurunga/Churinga

Among the Arrernte, **tjurunga** (also spelled *churinga*) are sacred stone and wooden tablets, typically oval or elongated, engraved with concentric circles and totemic motifs. They represent the totemic ancestors and encode their trajectories across the landscape. T.G.H. Strehlow described them as the "proof" of an intact, circular system of belief.

Strehlow collected some 1,800 objects, most sacred, many of them tjurunga, from Arrernte groups — an act later condemned as a profound betrayal. He recorded and analysed the associated song-cycles in his magnum opus, *Songs of Central Australia* (1971), which Bruce Chatwin called "perhaps the only book in the world — the only real attempt since the Poetics of Aristotle, to define what song (and with song all language) is." Only 1,000 copies were published; due to restrictions on secret-sacred material, it will likely not be reprinted until copyright expires around 2044. Current copies sell for $3,500–$6,000.

**The ethical dimension**: Strehlow's case dramatises a fundamental tension in the study of Aboriginal religion. The knowledge he recorded was **restricted** — gender-specific, initiation-grade-specific, site-specific. His inheritance of the sacred through technological media (notes, recordings, film) was what one scholar called "a simulation of it. The simulacrum stands in for its origin, and is a fallen version of it." Strehlow's subsequent sharing of secret material with the press constituted what his critics call "irreparable, unforgivable betrayal of the Arrernte."

### 5.4 Intichiuma — Increase Ceremonies

**Definition.** The name **Intichiuma** (Arrernte) is applied to certain sacred ceremonies associated with the totems, the object of which is to secure the **increase** of the animal or plant that gives its name to the totem. Spencer and Gillen documented these extensively in *The Native Tribes of Central Australia* (1899).

**Core structure.** Each totem group has its own Intichiuma ceremony, and no two are alike in their specific performance. But all share a single purpose: **increasing the number of the totemic species**. Taking the tribe as a whole, the combined effect of all Intichiuma ceremonies is to increase the total food supply — each totemic group is responsible for maintaining the abundance of its own species, which feeds the rest of the community.

**Who performs it.** Only the men of a particular totemic group perform its Intichiuma ceremony. The lead must be taken by the **Alatunja** (headman/ceremonial leader), at a time he decides. No outsiders participate; no request or demand is made by other groups. "The mere performance of the ceremony is supposed to bring about the desired result."

**The witchetty grub ceremony (Udnirringita).** Spencer and Gillen's most detailed account:
- The men assembled in camp and slipped away quietly; women and non-totem men were not supposed to know they had gone
- All weapons and decorations were removed — participants went "quite unarmed and without any decoration of any kind; even the hair girdle had to be left behind"
- They walked in single file, the Alatunja sometimes leading, sometimes walking alongside
- Sacred objects used included: an **apmara** (small wooden trough), an **umbana** (long narrow bough shelter representing the chrysalis stage of the witchetty grub), and **unchima/churinga unchima** (small round stones representing grub eggs)
- The ceremony took place at the sacred site where the ancestral witchetty grub being was believed to have gone into the earth

**Post-ceremony taboo.** After Intichiuma, the totem species was **tabu** to the men who performed the ceremony. They could not eat it until it was abundant and fully grown — any infringement would undo the ceremony's effect. When Spencer and Gillen asked the Alatunja why he ate his own totem (which is "always regarded by the native as just the same as himself"), he replied: **"unless he did eat a little, he would not be able to perform properly the ceremony of Intichiuma."** Non-totem members could eat the species at any time, but it always had to be brought into camp to be cooked — eating it raw in the bush would anger the totem men and cause the species to vanish.

**The bidirectional relationship.** The Intichiuma reveals a crucial theological structure: the relationship between humans and totemic species is **not unidirectional**. The totem is "a fund of life force" that requires "sacred rituals that give strength and fecundity." The community sustains the totem's life force through ceremony; the totem sustains the community through increase. This is mutual maintenance — ecological-spiritual symbiosis.

**Distribution.** Intichiuma-type ceremonies were not limited to the Arrernte. Among the **Kakadu, Waduman, and Mudburra** (Northern Territory), equivalent ceremonies existed with the same purpose. The practice was widespread across Central and Northern Australia, though the specific forms varied enormously.

### 5.5 Ecological-Spiritual Maintenance

The Intichiuma system constitutes what might be called an **ecological theology**: each totemic group is responsible for the health and abundance of one species or natural phenomenon. Since the tribe as a whole contains groups responsible for every significant species in the ecosystem, the combined effect of all ceremonies is comprehensive environmental maintenance.

This is not "primitive magic" (as Frazer argued). It is an **integrated system of ecological stewardship with spiritual foundations**:
- Each group has **knowledge specialisation** in its totem species — behaviour, habitat, seasonal patterns
- Each group has **ceremonial responsibility** for its totem — the obligation to perform the increase ceremony at the right time
- Each group has **dietary restriction** around its totem — the taboo against eating ensures the group is not motivated by self-interest but by communal obligation
- The system creates **mutual dependence** — the kangaroo people feed the emu people, the emu people feed the kangaroo people, and each is sustained by the other's ceremonial labour

Frazer called this "not so much a religious as an economic system... a system of magic designed to supply a community with the necessities of life." This misses the point. The system is simultaneously religious AND economic AND ecological AND social — these distinctions, natural to Western thought, are exactly what Aboriginal ontology refuses to draw.

### 5.6 The Kirda/Kurdungurlu System (Warlpiri)

Among the **Warlpiri** of Central Australia, totemic stewardship is formalised in the **kirda/kurdungurlu** dual-responsibility system, first documented by M.J. Meggitt in *Desert People* (1962) and subsequently refined by later ethnographers.

**Kirda ("owner")**: A person who has inherited rights and responsibilities over a Jukurrpa (Dreaming) and its associated sites through their **father's line** (patrilineal, specifically from the father's father). Kirda are responsible for the maintenance of the well-being of the land and its people by performing ritual ceremonies.

**Kurdungurlu ("manager/guardian")**: A person who has inherited responsibilities through their **mother's line** (matrilateral, specifically from the mother's father). The kurdungurlu is etymologically related to the Warlpiri word *kurdu* ("child") and *ngurlu* ("from") — "from the child," referencing the mother's line.

**Complementary functions:**
- Kirda perform the ceremonies; kurdungurlu **supervise and correct them**
- Kurdungurlu provide "critical feedback to the kirda, with regard to country, sites and ceremonies, and ensure that key activities are carried out by kirda in accordance with the Law"
- The system creates "radical complementarity" — "an elaborate system of checks and balances"
- Neither kirda nor kurdungurlu can act alone; both are needed for legitimate ceremony

**Species stewardship example**: People of specific skin groups who are kirda for emu through patrilineal descent are **trustees for emu traditional knowledge** — "they have responsibilities for the dreaming song, story, ceremony, the country through which the ancestor traveled, and, to a certain extent, the ongoing health of the biophysical species." Through matrilineal descent, other groups are kurdungurlu for emu and "have a different set of responsibilities... that include making sure that the kirda undertake their responsibilities properly."

**Gender inclusivity**: Both men and women are identified as kirda or kurdungurlu for specific Dreamings. While certain segments of a Jukurrpa's geographic itinerary may be enacted only in men's or women's ceremonies, the structural roles themselves are not gender-exclusive.

The kirda/kurdungurlu system is one of the most sophisticated models of **distributed stewardship** in the ethnographic record. It encodes: dual accountability, separation of performance and oversight, intergenerational knowledge transfer through two distinct lines, and the principle that no one person or lineage has total control over any aspect of the Dreaming.

## 6. KEY SCHOLARS — BRIEF PROFILES

### 6.1 Sir Baldwin Spencer (1860–1929) & Francis James Gillen (1855–1912)

The foundational partnership. Spencer was a Melbourne zoologist; Gillen was the postmaster at Alice Springs and an amateur ethnographer with deep relationships with the Arrernte. Their *Native Tribes of Central Australia* (1899) was the first in-depth description of a totemic population and had "profound impact on the anthropological world, with deep and irreversible repercussions in discussions on totemism internationally." Their documentation of Intichiuma ceremonies, spirit-children, and the Alcheringa concept shaped every subsequent theorist's work — Durkheim, Frazer, Freud, Radcliffe-Brown, and Lévi-Strauss all depended on their fieldwork. Gillen's advocacy for the Arrernte community and his deep personal relationships enabled access to ceremonies that no outsider had previously witnessed. The Arrernte assigned totemic identities to both men — an act of agency and strategic engagement with European observers.

### 6.2 Carl Strehlow (1871–1922) & T.G.H. Strehlow (1908–1978)

Father and son. Carl was the Lutheran pastor at Hermannsburg Mission, who translated the New Testament into Arrernte and documented the *Altjira* concept. T.G.H. grew up among the Arrernte as a fluent speaker, giving him "primary" access to cultural knowledge that no outsider ethnographer could match. His magnum opus *Songs of Central Australia* (1971) recorded the patrilineal song-cycles of the Arrernte — what Chatwin called the only real attempt since Aristotle's *Poetics* to define what song is. Strehlow argued that Arrernte culture had "the profound depth of a culture that can be considered, like that of Ancient Greece, to be primary, even original." His legacy is permanently shadowed by his collection and mishandling of 1,800+ sacred objects and his disclosure of secret material to the German press — acts considered an unforgivable betrayal by many Arrernte people.

### 6.3 A.P. Elkin (1891–1979)

The great classifier. Elkin was a structural functionalist who produced the most systematic typology of Aboriginal totemism (1933, 1938). His three-class system (individual, social, cult) and eight structural forms provided the standard vocabulary for discussing totemism for decades. His insight that totemism expressed "a common life which man and nature share" and represented "a view of nature and life, of the universe and man, which unites them with nature's activities and species in a bond of mutual life-giving" remains foundational. His limitations are those of his era: the typological impulse sometimes forced diverse practices into rigid categories.

### 6.4 W.E.H. Stanner (1905–1981)

The phenomenologist. Stanner worked for decades among the Murrinh-pata and other peoples of the Daly River and Wadeye regions. His essays — collected in *White Man Got No Dreaming* (1979) — remain among the finest pieces of anthropological writing on Aboriginal religion. His coinage of **"Everywhen"** to describe the temporal nature of the Dreaming ("one cannot 'fix' The Dreaming in time: it was, and is, everywhen") entered common scholarly usage. His phenomenological insistence on the Dreaming as "absolute or whole reality" rather than mythology or classification pushed the discipline beyond both functionalism and structuralism. His identification of four modes of totemic acquisition (dream, conceptual, augury, descent-affiliative) acknowledged the complexity that resists typology.

### 6.5 Deborah Bird Rose (1946–2018)

The ecological philosopher. Rose lived for nearly two years (1980–82) among the **Yarralin** community (Victoria River Valley, Northern Territory), producing *Dingo Makes Us Human: Life and Land in an Australian Aboriginal Culture* (1992/2000), which won the Stanner Prize. She proposed that **kinship** was a more adequate concept than "totemism" for Aboriginal human-nature relations, since "totemism" implies a religious category while the Aboriginal reality is an all-pervading relational ontology. Her work extended into multi-species ethnography (*Wild Dog Dreaming: Love and Extinction*, 2011), environmental ethics (*Reports from a Wild Country*, 2004), and decolonisation. She demonstrated that Aboriginal "Country" is not property but relationship — a living, sentient, multi-species community of which humans are one part.

### 6.6 Howard Morphy (b. 1947)

The art-knowledge scholar. Morphy conducted over ten years of fieldwork among the **Yolngu** of Northeast Arnhem Land, producing *Ancestral Connections: Art and an Aboriginal System of Knowledge* (1991). His central contribution is showing how Yolngu bark painting simultaneously **represents and constitutes** relationships between things — it is both a picture of the Dreaming and an active participation in it. He documented the crucial **inside/outside knowledge dialectic**: paintings have both an external, public meaning and an internal, restricted meaning accessible only to initiated persons. The "inside" significance "retains its dual ability to represent and to constitute relationships between things." Morphy also wrote extensively on clan organisation, restricted knowledge, and how the Yolngu used art as "an instrument of cultural survival."

### 6.7 Fred Myers (b. 1948)

The Western Desert ethnographer. Myers' *Pintupi Country, Pintupi Self* (1986) won the Stanner Prize and remains foundational for understanding Western Desert Aboriginal life. His central insight: the Pintupi cosmos divides into **yuti** (visible) and **tjukurrpa** (Dreaming), with a crucial third term — *tjukurrtjanu mularrarringu*, meaning **"from the Dreaming, it becomes real"** — describing the passage between states. His second major work, *Painting Culture* (2002), traced the emergence of the Papunya Tula painting movement. Myers demonstrated that Pintupi political life is fundamentally concerned with rights to Country — when a painter chooses which sites to depict, they are making assertions about identity and belonging. For the Pintupi, "it is the moment of creation that is all-important — the act of painting, the re-engagement with the Dreaming."

### 6.8 M.J. Meggitt (1924–2004)

The Warlpiri ethnographer. Meggitt spent over 18 months (1953–58) with the Warlpiri (then spelled "Walbiri"), sent by his teacher Elkin. His *Desert People: A Study of the Walbiri Aborigines of Central Australia* (1962) covered social organisation, kinship, moieties, subsections, initiation, and religion/totemism. His documentation of the Warlpiri kinship and ceremonial system provided the foundation for subsequent work on the kirda/kurdungurlu dual stewardship model.

## 7. LINGUISTIC-THEOLOGICAL FEATURES OF TOTEMIC THOUGHT

### 7.1 Grammatical Ontology: The Imperfective Sacred

Among the **Pitjantjatjara** (Western Desert), a remarkable linguistic finding reveals the depth of totemic-Dreaming theology encoded in grammar: **all sacred events are narrated in the imperfective aspect**, while all secular events use the past tense.

In versions of the Eaglehawk myth, one hears "eaglehawk was falling off the branch" — never "eaglehawk fell off the branch." Not only is the past tense absent in mythic narrative, but its use is **not tolerated**. This is theology inscribed in grammar: the Dreaming is not past; it is ongoing. The imperfective aspect grammatically enacts the "Everywhen" — the ancestors are not beings who acted; they are beings who *are acting*. Ceremony, then, is not commemoration but participation in a still-unfolding event.

### 7.2 The Same Word for "Totem" and "Dream"

In the **Great Victoria Desert**, the same word designates both "totem" and "dream." This linguistic fusion suggests that the totemic relationship and the dream-state are phenomenologically inseparable — one's totem is what one sees when one sees truly, when ordinary perception gives way to Dreaming perception. The totem is not an abstract affiliation but a visionary reality.

### 7.3 "Flesh" as Totemic Category

Across multiple language groups, the word for totem translates as **"flesh"** or **"meat"** — not metaphorically but literally. The Wiradjuri *jarawaijewa* ("the meat that is within him"), the widespread use of "skin" for subsection identity, and the clan totem as "flesh" all point to a consistent ontological claim: **the totem is bodily**. It is not an idea about the body but a substance of the body. To eat one's totem would be auto-cannibalism.

### 7.4 Altjirerama: "To See God / To Dream"

The Upper Arrernte verb *altjirerama* glosses as both "to see God" and "to dream." This dual meaning encodes the proposition that dreaming IS vision of the divine — not in the weakened sense of "inspired dream" but in the strong sense that the dream-state is the perceptual mode appropriate to encountering the eternal. T.G.H. Strehlow emphasised this: the connection to dreaming reflects "the 'seeing' of eternal things during sleep."

### 8.2 Key Convergence Clusters

**1. Ontological identity (person-IS-totem) convergences:**
- Aboriginal "one flesh" / *jarawaijewa* (totem-as-body-substance) <-> Christian *homoousios* (of one substance with the Father) <-> Hindu *tat tvam asi* ("thou art that") <-> Sufi *fana* (annihilation of self into the divine)
- The structural parallel: each tradition has a term for the moment when apparent duality resolves into identity — but the Aboriginal version is distinctive because the identity is **with a specific non-human species and a specific place**, not with a universal divine principle.

**2. Ecological-spiritual maintenance convergences:**
- Intichiuma (increase ceremony for totem species) <-> Akkadian *sattukku* (regular cult-offerings maintaining cosmic order) <-> Vedic *yajna* (sacrifice sustaining *rta*, cosmic order) <-> Andean *ayni* (reciprocity with Pachamama)
- The structural parallel: ceremony-as-cosmic-maintenance — the idea that the spiritual and material world requires active human ritual participation to continue functioning.

**3. Place-constituted personhood convergences:**
- Conception totemism (identity determined by where spirit enters mother) <-> No direct parallel found in other excavated traditions — this appears genuinely unique to Aboriginal Australia. The closest analogues are weak: Hindu *janma-bhumi* (birth-land) carries emotional attachment but not ontological constitution; Jewish connection to the Land of Israel is collective rather than individual.

**4. Distributed stewardship convergences:**
- Kirda/kurdungurlu (dual-line responsibility with checks and balances) <-> Jewish *chavruta* (learning partnership where each corrects the other) <-> This is structurally distinctive. Most traditions concentrate ritual authority in a single line (priestly caste, hereditary chief, initiated elder). The Warlpiri system distributes it across TWO lines and makes the "manager" explicitly responsible for correcting the "owner." This is a governance theology.

### 8.3 Strongest YOUSPEAK Forge Candidates from Totemism

1. **kirda-kurdungurlu** as compound concept — dual stewardship, owner-and-guardian, the principle that custodianship requires both performer and corrector. No existing YOUSPEAK word names this governance principle. Potential compound: *kirdangurlu* or *kirdame* (with appropriate permissions considerations).

2. **ratapame** — Arrernte *ratapa* (spirit-child) + Sumerian *me*. The condition-of-being-a-person-deposited-in-landscape-awaiting-incarnation. Fills the gap for "personhood as geographical deposit" — a concept with no parallel in other excavated traditions.

3. **intichiuma** as loan-concept — the ceremony-of-sustaining-what-sustains-you. The bidirectional maintenance relationship. Could potentially combine with *me* or *qing* to form a YOUSPEAK compound naming reciprocal ecological-spiritual obligation.

### 8.4 Cautions

**Sacred knowledge restrictions.** Much of the deepest totemic knowledge is restricted — gender-specific, initiation-grade-specific, site-specific. Any YOUSPEAK forging from Aboriginal Australian donors must be scrupulously limited to publicly available, published material. The tradition itself teaches that knowledge is layered and access is earned. Violating this is not just ethically wrong; it is theologically incoherent — to use restricted material would be to betray the very principle of graduated knowledge that makes the tradition profound.

**The "totemism" label.** The concept of "totemism" is itself a Western analytical category that groups together phenomena Aboriginal people do not necessarily group together. Rose's caution — "the term 'totem' has proved to be a blunt instrument" — should inform any attempt to extract vocabulary. The Aboriginal reality is more diverse, more locally specific, and more integrated with other aspects of life than any single analytical frame can capture.

**Regional variation.** There is no single "Aboriginal totemism." There are hundreds of distinct systems operating across 250+ language groups. What is true of the Arrernte may not be true of the Yolngu, which may not be true of the Warlpiri, which may not be true of the Ngarrindjeri. Generalisations must be held lightly.
