## 1. THE MALE BIAS IN EARLY ETHNOGRAPHY

### 1.1 The Problem: How It Was Built

The foundational ethnographic record of Aboriginal Australian religion was created almost entirely by men, working with male informants, about male ceremonies. This was not mere oversight — it was a structural feature of the research context that produced a systematically distorted picture of Aboriginal religion as male-dominated.

The three most influential early ethnographers of Central Australian Aboriginal life were:

**Baldwin Spencer and Francis James Gillen** — Their collaboration (1896-1912) produced *The Native Tribes of Central Australia* (1899) and *The Northern Tribes of Central Australia* (1904), which became perhaps the most influential collection of Australian ethnographic material ever assembled. Emile Durkheim based *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* on their work; Sigmund Freud's *Totem and Taboo* was primarily inspired by it.

Their entry point was decisive: in 1896, Spencer joined Gillen to observe the **Engwura** — specifically a men's initiation ceremony complex among the Arrernte (Arunta). Spencer was given access because he was presented as Gillen's brother. This men's initiation ceremony shaped the entire foundation of their ethnographic record. Gillen never spoke Arrernte fluently; most communication with informants occurred through pidgin or interpreters.

**Carl Strehlow** (1871-1922) — Lutheran missionary at Hermannsburg who produced *Die Aranda- und Loritja-Stamme in Zentral-Australien* (1907-1920). He "worked with male elders writing down verbatim the songs and stories they recited for him, working on the 'verandah,' rather than in the field." His primary method was recording men's accounts rather than observing ceremonies or consulting women. Critically, although his wife Frieda Strehlow contributed significantly to ethnographic work — Anna Kenny has shown that part of the manuscript on birthing and associated ceremonies is in Frieda's handwriting — **her contributions were never acknowledged**. Information on women and women's knowledge was likely contributed by Frieda through her close association with Arrernte women at Hermannsburg, but it was subsumed under Carl's authorship.

**T.G.H. Strehlow** (1908-1978) — Carl's son, who cast himself as the only worthy "heir" to Arrernte traditions. He made extensive recordings of men's ceremonies during a period when access was less restricted. He remained "blinkered to the possibility of their ongoing relevance to Central Australian Aboriginal people." His later decision to share secret-sacred material with the press constituted what his critics called an "irreparable, unforgivable betrayal of the Arrernte."

### 1.2 The Informant Bias

The male bias operated at multiple interlocking levels:

1. **Male researchers with male informants.** All three major ethnographers were men who worked primarily with male elders. Women's ceremonial life was structurally invisible to them — not because it didn't exist, but because they could not access it.

2. **Gender-restricted access.** Aboriginal ceremonial knowledge is gender-restricted by design. Male researchers were literally barred from women's ceremonial spaces (the jilimi). They could not witness yawulyu, could not be told about women's Dreaming narratives, could not access women's sacred objects. This wasn't a failure of fieldwork method — it was the tradition working as intended.

3. **Language barriers.** Gillen communicated in pidgin. Carl Strehlow worked through translation. The subtleties of women's theological concepts, even when partially accessible, were filtered through imprecise linguistic channels.

4. **Artificial performance contexts.** Von Leonhardi observed that Spencer and Gillen "created an artificial context for the performance of ceremonies in 1896 and that the photographic equipment had interfered with the usual process." The ceremonies they documented were already distorted by the observation conditions.

5. **Generalization from narrow data.** T.G.H. Strehlow himself critiqued Spencer and Gillen: "I believe that Spencer and Gillen commit the same error... by generalising information and observations of individual culture traits and then by imputing the deduction to the blacks." Information from a few male informants was projected onto the entire society.

### 1.3 The Theoretical Amplification

The fieldwork bias was amplified by theoretical frameworks that enshrined it as universal truth:

**A.R. Radcliffe-Brown** — the first to be appointed to a Chair of Anthropology in Australia — "did not know of any of the business of the jilimi — for as a male he was barred from it." But rather than acknowledging ignorance, he interpreted Aboriginal society through Durkheim's sacred/profane binary: initiated men represented the sacred realm, Aboriginal women represented the uninitiated profane world. He classified women's spiritual work as "magic" — meaning it was "without power because unconnected to the Dreaming."

This became orthodoxy: **"Women were said to have no religion."**

Many anthropologists followed Radcliffe-Brown in dismissing women's ceremonial life. The result was a canonical picture of Aboriginal religion as exclusively male: male initiations, male Dreaming knowledge, male ceremonial authority, with women serving merely supporting roles (cooking for ceremonies, sexual exchange, reproduction). This picture was not merely incomplete — it was systematically wrong.

## 2. THE CORRECTION: KABERRY AND BELL

### 2.1 Phyllis Kaberry — *Aboriginal Woman: Sacred and Profane* (1939)

Phyllis Mary Kaberry (1910-1977) was the first anthropologist to examine and understand the fullness of Aboriginal women's lives. Her book arrived at a time when anthropology was widely male-dominated, and Aboriginal women were seen as "purely erotic beings or 'domesticated cows'... thought to have little influence in cultural development, devoid of a sacred life with their institutions defined as inferior to those of males."

**Fieldwork:** Kaberry researched in North-West Australia between 1935 and 1936. She attended the University of Sydney, graduating with a BA in English and philosophy (1933) and an MA in anthropology (1934) with first-class honors. She completed her PhD at the London School of Economics in 1939 under Bronislaw Malinowski, to whom she dedicated the book.

**Central contribution:** The book portrays "the Aboriginal woman as she really is — a complex social personality with her own prerogatives, duties, problems, beliefs, rituals and point of view." Where previous scholars had classified Aboriginal women's lives as "restricted and uninvolved with religious practice," Kaberry documented autonomous sacred activity, ritual knowledge, and social agency.

**Reception:** The book "received great amounts of criticism for suggesting that women were equal to men and possessed their own value of sacredness." Even Kaberry's mentor, Elkin, exhibited views that agreed with the common belief of women's inferiority, despite his interest in women's lives.

**Legacy:** Kaberry paved the way for a feminist approach in anthropological studies. Sandy Toussaint's preface to the reissue examines how the book came to be written, its original context, and its influence on the study of gender in anthropology. It remains a foundational text demonstrating how male-dominated fieldwork paradigms systematically missed women's sacred lives.

**Scholarly note:** Kaberry also co-authored with Malinowski. They had agreed to write a book together on culture contact, but Malinowski died before it was finished. With his notes in hand, Kaberry completed the project in 1945 as *The Dynamics of Cultural Change*.

### 2.2 Diane Bell — *Daughters of the Dreaming* (1983)

Diane Bell (b. 1943, Melbourne) is the most important scholar of Aboriginal women's ceremonial life. Her fieldwork at Warrabri (now Ali Curung) produced the landmark study that permanently changed the field.

**Education and career:**
- BA (Hons) Anthropology, Monash University (1975)
- PhD, Australian National University (1981)
- Research Fellow, ANU (1983-86)
- Chair of Australian Studies, Deakin University (first female professor on staff)
- Chair of Religion, Economic Development & Social Justice, College of the Holy Cross (1989)
- Director of Women's Studies and Professor of Anthropology, George Washington University (1999-2005)
- Emerita Professor at both ANU and GWU

**Fieldwork context:** Bell lived with her children at Warrabri from 1976 to 1978, conducting intensive fieldwork with Aboriginal women. The settlement, established as a government settlement 100 km south of Tennant Creek in 1956, housed multiple language groups: **Kaytej** (traditional owners of the area — the name Ali Curung derives from Alekarenge, related to the Dog/Dingo Dreaming in Kaytetye language), **Warlpiri** (approximately 40% of the settlement population), **Alyawarra**, and **Warumungu** (the settlement name "Warrabri" combined Warramunga + Wailbri).

**Methodology:** Bell's breakthrough was methodological as much as theoretical. She lived *within* women's spaces — specifically the **jilimi** (widows'/single women's camp) — and was accepted into gender-restricted ceremonial knowledge. Previous male researchers could not have accessed this material regardless of their intent.

### 2.3 Bell's Central Argument

The book's central argument challenged the prevailing view on multiple levels:

**1. Women have autonomous, independent ritual lives.** Aboriginal women "are autonomous, independent ritual actors who actively participate in the creation, transmission and maintenance of the values of their society." This was not a subsidiary or derivative tradition — it was a parallel sacred architecture with its own Dreaming boards, songs, body-paint designs, increase ceremonies, and theological knowledge.

**2. Women have direct access to the Jukurrpa (Dreaming).** Bell observed that "women are rarely mentioned in the literature as owners of country in their own right or as decision-making individuals — they appear as wives and mothers, their relationship to the jukurrpa always mediated through another." Her central finding was that women enjoyed **direct access to the Jukurrpa**, from which flowed rights and responsibilities in land, a power base as independent economic producers, and a high degree of control over their own lives.

**3. Gender complementarity, not hierarchy.** Bell presents a dynamic model of sexual relations suggesting that "the historical separation of knowledge bases between men and women ensured interdependence without privileging one sex over the other, requiring both sexes to work in tandem to maintain law, custom and community." Under the Law, men and women have distinctive roles, "but each has access to certain checks and balances which ensure that neither sex can enjoy an unrivalled supremacy over the other."

**4. Colonial disruption of gender balance.** Bell argued that colonial encroachment and the male dominance inherent to Anglo culture resulted in the privileging of male authority by settlers and, over time, an erosion of women's power base. The alleviation of Aboriginal women's subordination in contemporary settlement life was, controversially, a *return* to what she argued was greater pre-contact autonomy.

**5. Career "businesswomen."** Bell documented ritual specialists — women who, when freed of child-minding duties in middle age, would pursue the advancement of their knowledge of and ability to perform yawulyu ceremonies. These were not casual practitioners but dedicated specialists with decades of accumulated ceremonial knowledge.

### 2.4 Key Institutions Bell Documented

- **The jilimi** — widows'/single women's camp. A physically separate space where women conducted their ceremonial and social lives independently of men. The jilimi was not merely a residential arrangement but a ritual institution — the spatial anchor of women's autonomous sacred activity.
- **Yawulyu** — women's ritual, encompassing song, dance, body painting, and Dreaming narrative.
- **Jukurrpa** — the Dreaming, the land-based cosmological system, to which women had their own independent access and over which they held custodial authority for specific sites and narratives.

### 2.5 Reception and Controversy

The book "disturbed the entrenched doctrine of male dominance and required that gendered relations be seen as dynamic but grounded in space and time." It has been in continuous print since 1983, with revised editions in 1993 and 2002.

**Critiques:** *Daughters of the Dreaming* has been critiqued as an "Aboriginalist text" for its positioning of Bell as a knowledgeable expert with authority to "represent and document Indigenous women's secret and restricted knowledges in a public text." This raises the fundamental tension: the very act of making women's sacred knowledge visible to non-Aboriginal audiences risks violating the gender-restricted protocols that the tradition itself mandates.

**Underlying both:** Kaberry's and Bell's data are shared: "both [men and women] have sacred boards, both know songs and paint designs that encode the knowledge of the dreaming." The tradition was never male-only — the scholarly record was.

## 3. YAWULYU AND AWELYE — WOMEN'S CEREMONIES

### 3.1 Terminology

The ceremonies go by different names in different language groups:
- **Yawulyu** — used by the **Warlpiri** and **Warumungu** language groups
- **Awelye** — used by the more eastern **Alyawarre** and **Anmatyerre** people (especially of the Utopia region)
- **Inma** — used by the **Anangu** (Pitjantjatjara/Yankunytjatjara) women of Central Australia

The term can describe the entire ceremonial complex: songs, dances, totems, knowledge of country, Dreamtime stories, and body paint designs.

### 3.2 Structure of the Ceremony

**Gender restriction:** Yawulyu/Awelye are women's business and are never performed in the presence of men. Equivalent male ceremonies include *pujjarli* (also *yilpinji*).

**Body painting phase:** Women begin by painting each other's bodies in intricate designs that relate to each woman's Dreaming and her skin name. Pigments of ochre, clay, charcoal, and ash are collected from the earth and mixed with animal fats to create paint-like pastes. Women traditionally anoint their bodies in oil or animal fat before applying the ochre designs, either with their fingers or with a flat, padded stick. Designs are applied to breasts, arms, and thighs. The painting can take up to three hours.

**Singing during painting:** During the painting, women chant their Dreaming — songs relating to the ancestral travel narratives and totemic plants, animals, and natural forces. Each woman sings as she takes her turn to be "painted up." The songs are not accompaniment to the painting; they are integral to its efficacy.

**Dance phase:** Once all women have been painted, the ancestors' sacred dance and song begin. Dancing may last for several hours. The event concludes with women "meticulously performing site-specific dance cycles." The physical training for these dances often takes far longer than the performance itself.

### 3.3 Multi-Modal Integration

Yawulyu and Awelye are not a single art form but a collective multi-modal tradition. Teachings are expressed in multiple modalities simultaneously: song, rhythm, melody, gesture and dance, gathering, graphic imagery, totem objects, and spatial orientation. The body paint designs mirror the tracks made in sand during rituals — the body becomes a **ceremonial map**. The act of decorating the body in ochre transforms the individual, connecting them to the collective identity of their culture and to their roles as caretakers of the land and its Dreaming.

### 3.4 Themes and Functions

**Land and fertility:** Reflecting the woman's traditional role as nurturer, Awelye and Yawulyu connect with the fertility of the land, celebrating nutritious food, water, and healing sources. Flora and fauna, healing, and laws are among the Dreamings represented by the Awelye patterns.

**Health and healing:** When individuals are unwell, the older women sing these rites to heal girls and children. The ceremonies have both social and healing purposes.

**Community wellbeing:** Awelye demonstrates respect for the land. In performing these ceremonies, women ensure well-being and happiness within their communities.

**Love, land, and health:** Bell documented that "the major themes and purposes of women's rituals were for love, land and health." These three domains were the primary concerns of women's ceremonial activity.

### 3.5 Transmission and Authority

**Matrilineal kinship expression:** Awelye involves the sharing of land knowledge, customs, and Dreamtime stories. Older women dance to instruct younger women and pass on knowledge to granddaughters, ensuring continuity across generations. "Because the spirits of the land have given her the ritual, the elderly woman is boss."

**Career specialists:** As Bell documented, women who had passed child-rearing age could become dedicated ritual specialists — "career businesswomen" who devoted themselves to accumulating ceremonial knowledge and performance skill.

### 3.6 Contemporary Status

The practice of awelye is still actively performed throughout Central Australia for both social and healing purposes. However, rapid post-colonial changes — particularly the degeneration of clan family structures into smaller nuclear family units — have made intergenerational transmission more difficult.

## 4. THE STRUCTURE OF GENDER-RESTRICTED KNOWLEDGE

### 4.1 The Dual System

Aboriginal Australian ceremonial life operates through a dual system of "men's business" and "women's business." Sacred sites are designated for one or the other, with each restricted to members of the corresponding gender. Men's sacred sites are forbidden to women; women's sacred sites are prohibited to men.

The terminology used by Aboriginal people themselves is telling:
- **"Outside" or "open"** — public ceremonies accessible to the whole community
- **"Inside" or "closed"** — restricted ceremonies requiring specific qualifications (gender, age, initiation grade, kin status)

### 4.2 Complementarity, Not Hierarchy

The tradition's own theology is explicit: "Neither men nor women possess greater spiritual needs or responsibilities: they co-exist in different ways to ensure that sacred elements of Dreaming are practised and passed on." Both sexes are required to maintain the Law. Bell documented: "Under the Law, men and women have distinctive roles to play but each has access to certain checks and balances which ensure that neither sex can enjoy an unrivalled supremacy over the other." Underlying male and female practice is "a common purpose and a shared belief in the Dreamtime experience."

Even in moments of cross-gender sharing, something is always held back, kept for people of the same gender. In other circumstances, ceremonies apparently restricted to one gender may in fact require significant input from "law leaders" of the opposite gender — for example, the cooking of food for sacramental feasts.

### 4.3 What Women Know That Men Do Not

This question is, by its nature, impossible to answer fully in a public document — and that impossibility is itself the point. Women hold:
- Their own Dreaming boards (sacred objects)
- Their own songs encoding Dreaming knowledge
- Their own body-paint designs with ceremonial efficacy
- Their own increase ceremonies (for the fertility of land and species)
- Knowledge of women's sacred sites — locations, narratives, and protocols
- Birthing ceremonies at culturally significant women's sites
- Healing knowledge transmitted matrilineally
- Site-specific dance cycles with their own choreographic traditions

The critical insight is not *what* women know but the structural parallel: women's knowledge is a complete, self-sufficient sacred system that mirrors and complements men's knowledge. It is not a fragment of men's knowledge. It is an independent architecture.

## 5. THE HINDMARSH ISLAND BRIDGE CONTROVERSY

### 5.1 Background

The Hindmarsh Island bridge controversy (1994-2010) is the most significant and contested episode in Australian history concerning women's sacred sites and gender-restricted knowledge. It exposed, with painful clarity, the epistemological failure of Western legal systems when confronted with non-disclosable sacred knowledge.

**The development:** Tom and Wendy Chapman (trading as Binalong Pty Ltd) had purchased 30 hectares on Hindmarsh Island (Kumarangk) near Goolwa, South Australia, in 1977, proposing a 560-berth marina. Wendy Chapman served as Lord Mayor of Adelaide (1983-1985). A bridge was proposed to replace the cable ferry servicing the island. By 1994, Binalong faced liquidation owing $18.5 million.

### 5.2 The Sacred Knowledge Claim

In May 1994, a group of **Ngarrindjeri women elders** claimed Hindmarsh Island held sacred significance — specifically as a fertility site — for reasons that could not be publicly revealed. Anthropologist **Dr. Deane Fergie** prepared an assessment. Some of the cultural secrets were written down and sealed in two envelopes marked "Confidential: to be read by women only" and forwarded to Federal Minister **Robert Tickner**.

On July 10, 1994, Tickner placed a **25-year ban on the bridge construction**, putting the marina in doubt and bringing the Chapmans close to bankruptcy. Tickner had not personally read the sealed envelopes; Professor Cheryl Saunders's accompanying report argued the contents were unnecessary for decision-making.

The phrase **"secret women's business"** entered Australian public discourse — and quickly became, in many quarters, a term of derision.

### 5.3 The Counter-Claims

Some months later, a separate group of Indigenous women declared that the "secret women's business" had been fabricated. A Ngarrindjeri man, **Doug Milera**, who had originally supported the claim, publicly stated it was a fabrication. (Milera later retracted this retraction under circumstances that remain contested.)

### 5.4 The Sealed Envelopes Scandal

In March 1995, Shadow Minister **Ian McLachlan** tabled the confidential documents in Parliament, falsely claiming they lacked secrecy markings. The envelope had reached his office in error and was photocopied among staff despite clear restrictions. This disclosure of gender-restricted sacred material in a national parliament was itself a violation of the very protocols at issue.

### 5.5 The Royal Commission (1995)

Premier Dean Brown commissioned Justice **Iris Stevens** to investigate. Five "dissident" Ngarrindjeri women testified that the sacred knowledge must have been fabricated. Stevens concluded: **"the secret women's business was a fabrication"** created to obtain heritage protection.

The five Ngarrindjeri women who claimed knowledge of the sacred tradition did not give evidence at the Royal Commission because they considered doing so a violation of their religious freedoms. This created a fundamental epistemological paradox: the knowledge could not be disclosed to prove it existed, but its non-disclosure was taken as evidence of non-existence.

### 5.6 The Mathews Report (1996)

Senator Rosemary Crowley appointed **Justice Jane Mathews** as reporter to allow the women to present their restricted knowledge to a female adjudicator. The Howard Government (elected March 1996) refused to appoint a woman to receive the materials. On September 6, 1996, the High Court ruled Mathews's appointment invalid.

### 5.7 Federal Legislation and High Court Challenge (1997)

The **Hindmarsh Island Bridge Act 1997** removed heritage protections, allowing construction to proceed. **Doreen Kartinyeri** and Neville Gollan challenged the Act in the High Court. Five justices upheld it; Justice Kirby dissented.

### 5.8 The Federal Court Reversal: *Chapman v Luminis* (2001)

The Chapmans brought a damages claim against the South Australian government. Justice **John von Doussa** heard extensive testimony and, in August 2001, delivered a judgment that reversed the Royal Commission's conclusions:

**"Upon the evidence before this Court I am not satisfied that the restricted women's knowledge was fabricated or that it was not part of genuine Aboriginal tradition."**

Key findings:
- Late emergence of sacred knowledge is "expected in the case of genuine sacred information" — knowledge surfaces only when threatened, not on demand
- One expert witness (Philip Clarke from the South Australian Museum) had compromised objectivity by secretly assisting the dissident group's lawyers
- The various Seven Sisters accounts presented by the Ngarrindjeri women were "consistent"
- The court rejected the fabrication thesis and dismissed the Chapman damages claim

### 5.9 The 2010 Vindication

On July 7, 2010, the South Australian Government formally endorsed that "secret women's business" was genuine Aboriginal tradition. Ngarrindjeri elders led a symbolic walk across the completed bridge — which had been opened on March 4, 2001 — though they maintained cultural rejection of the structure.

By this time, 15 years had elapsed. The bridge was built. The sacred site was altered.

### 5.10 What the Controversy Reveals

The Hindmarsh Island case is not merely a legal episode. It is a case study in **epistemological incompatibility**:

1. **Western legal systems require disclosure to prove validity.** Aboriginal sacred knowledge systems require non-disclosure to maintain validity. These requirements are mutually exclusive.

2. **The paradox of gender-restricted knowledge.** Knowledge that women cannot reveal to men — including male judges, male parliamentarians, male anthropologists — becomes, in a male-dominated institutional context, knowledge that effectively does not exist.

3. **The Royal Commission's structural flaw.** Iris Stevens assessed the validity of women's sacred knowledge without the women testifying, because the women considered testimony a violation of their religious freedom. She concluded the knowledge was fabricated partly *because* it was not disclosed. This is the epistemological equivalent of concluding someone has no thoughts because they refuse to speak.

4. **The anthropologist Peter Sutton** — former South Australian Museum head — initially questioned the claims but by 2002 stated that additional evidence made "the overall fabrication theory insupportable."

5. **The racial and gender intersection.** Betty Fisher, a white historian, claimed that Ngarrindjeri women had told her of the "secret women's business" thirty years earlier. Several white male anthropologists refuted her testimony.

## 7. UNCERTAINTIES AND LIMITATIONS

1. **The content of restricted ceremonies.** By definition, publicly available sources cannot document the full content of women's gender-restricted ceremonies. What is recorded here is what Bell, Kaberry, and other scholars have published — which itself represents a negotiated selection from a larger body of knowledge.

2. **Kaberry's fieldwork details.** While her North-West Australian fieldwork (1935-36) is well-documented in its broad strokes, detailed accounts of specific ceremonies she witnessed are less accessible than Bell's work. The 1939 book itself, not widely digitized, remains the primary source.

3. **The Hindmarsh controversy's full resolution.** While the Federal Court and South Australian government vindicated the Ngarrindjeri women, the controversy continues to be contested in some quarters. The full scope of the sacred knowledge remains, appropriately, undisclosed.

4. **Bell's later work.** Bell's *Ngarrindjeri Wurruwarrin* (1998) documents her extensive work with Ngarrindjeri women during the Hindmarsh controversy, but its content involves restricted material that she chose to publish — a decision that itself remains debated.

5. **Regional variation.** Yawulyu/awelye as documented here pertains primarily to Central Australian groups (Warlpiri, Kaytej, Alyawarre, Anmatyerre, Anangu). Women's ceremonial life in other regions (Arnhem Land, Kimberley, southeast Australia) takes different forms that are less fully documented in the public scholarly record.
