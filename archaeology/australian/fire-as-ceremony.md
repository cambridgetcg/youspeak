## 1. FIRE-STICK FARMING: THE CONCEPT

### 1.1 Rhys Jones and the Coining of the Term (1969)

The term **"fire-stick farming"** was coined by Australian archaeologist **Rhys Jones** in a landmark essay published in *Australian Natural History* 16 (1969): 224-231.

Jones opened with a question that was radical for its time: **"We imagine that the country seen by the first colonists before they ringbarked their first tree was 'natural.' But was it?"** When he published, Australia still operated under the doctrine of *terra nullius* — the legal fiction that the continent was uninhabited at the time of European arrival.

Inspired by extensive fire management he witnessed practiced by Aboriginal communities across central Arnhem Land, and appreciating the antiquity of Aboriginal occupation, Jones answered his own question in the negative. His insight — that Aboriginal people had managed the Australian landscape through deliberate, systematic fire for millennia — was revolutionary.

### 1.2 The Intentional Use of "Farming"

Jones's word choice was deliberate. The use of "farming" simultaneously challenged two assumptions:
1. That Aboriginal people were "passive creatures eking out a rude existence in a wide brown land"
2. That fire was inherently destructive

By calling it "farming," Jones asserted that Aboriginal fire use was *productive* — a sophisticated technique that increased the land's carrying capacity for human needs.

### 1.3 What Fire-Stick Farming Is

Fire-stick farming — also called **cool burning** or **cultural burning** — is the practice of regularly using fire to burn vegetation as a management technique. More specifically, Jones described it as Aboriginal land management by the use of fire to **deliberately change the composition of plant and animal communities** as a way of:
- Facilitating hunting
- Converting scrub into grassland
- Encouraging game species (especially kangaroo)
- Promoting fire-tolerant and fire-resistant plants
- Extending favourable habitat zones

Jones went so far as to say that **"through firing over thousands of years, Aboriginal man has managed to extend his natural habitat zone."**

### 1.4 Fieldwork Basis

Jones drew from ethnographic fieldwork among Aboriginal groups in **Arnhem Land** and **Wardaman country**, where he documented frequent, intentional ignitions — often daily or every few days. The burning was not sporadic but constant, low-intensity, and carefully targeted.

### 1.5 Scientific Legacy

The original 1969 essay is described as having "herald[ed] a cultural and scientific revolution." The evidence of widespread, systematic, and careful application of Aboriginal fire across Australia is now considered "incontrovertible," and Aboriginal people drive major fire management projects across northern Australia.

## 2. BILL GAMMAGE — *THE BIGGEST ESTATE ON EARTH* (2011)

### 2.1 The Central Argument

Bill Gammage's *The Biggest Estate on Earth: How Aborigines Made Australia* (Allen & Unwin, 2011) takes Jones's insight and extends it to the entire continent. His thesis: **Aboriginal people managed all of Australia as a single, consciously designed estate** — not a wilderness, not even a series of local gardens, but a continent-wide integrated system.

The argument rests on a striking observation: across Australia, early European settlers commented again and again that **the land looked like a park** — "a gentleman's estate" with extensive grassy patches, pathways, open woodlands, and abundant wildlife. Gammage's conclusion is that this was no accident. Aboriginal people had produced this landscape through millennia of deliberate management.

### 2.2 Evidence Base

Gammage's book cites approximately **1,500 works** in its bibliography. His method was to:
1. Examine written and visual records of the Australian landscape from the first decades of European settlement
2. Compare these descriptions to what grows "naturally" in the same areas today
3. Use the difference — combined with our understanding of fire-stick farming — to reconstruct how Aboriginal people shaped the land

He found that "even the vegetation of what became Australia's capital cities was determined largely by persistent Aboriginal burnings — the positioning and type of trees, the pattern of grasses and shrubs, and whether countryside was park-like or heavily timbered were all influenced by Aboriginal practices."

### 2.3 The Template Concept

Gammage introduced the concept of **"templates"** — carefully designed associations of grass, bush, and trees that created **feed-shelter combinations** for target animals. These are perhaps the key analytical unit of his argument:

- Templates had to be carefully placed so as not to disrupt each other
- Given how long eucalypts live, **templates might take centuries to set up**
- Each needed several distinct fire regimes, continuously managed and integrated with neighbouring templates

This is what makes the continent-wide argument compelling: **"this system could hardly have land boundaries — there could not be a place where it was practised, and next to it a place where it wasn't."** Fire management was not local or sporadic but a coordinated Australia-wide estate.

### 2.4 The Gammage Quotation (Theology + Ecology)

The single most important sentence in the book for understanding the Aboriginal worldview:

> **"Aboriginal landscape awareness is rightly seen as drenched in religious sensibility, but equally the Dreaming is saturated with environmental consciousness. Theology and ecology are fused."**

This is the key: fire management is not a secular technique that happens to be practiced by religious people. It is religious practice. And religious practice is ecological management. The two are not parallel activities — they are the same activity perceived under different descriptions.

### 2.5 Totemic Coordination

Gammage found that **totems underwrote the ecological arrangement of Australia**, creating an entire continent managed under the same Law for similar biodiverse purposes regardless of vegetation type:

> "Despite vastly different plant communities, from spinifex to rainforest, from Tasmania to the Kimberleys, there were the same plant patterns."

The result was **"a single estate, albeit with many managers"** — coordination without centralisation, enabled by the shared totemic framework of the Dreaming.

### 2.6 Fire and Not-Fire

A critical Gammage insight: **the decision NOT to burn was as deliberate as the decision to burn.** Fire management included active fire exclusion — protecting certain areas from burning for decades or centuries to create specific ecological conditions. "Gammage stresses the importance of management by fire and 'not-fire' — with adjustments for seasons, latitude and vegetation types — as a constant practice."

### 2.7 Reception and Critique

**Geoffrey Blainey** called it "a bold book" with "lucid prose and vivid illustrations" that "takes an important idea to new heights."

**Academic critiques** (especially after the 2019-20 bushfires intensified interest): Critics have argued that Gammage's model is a "Grand Unified Theory" reliant on binaries that either underemphasise or overemphasise Aboriginal impact. A 2022 article in *History Australia* ("Rethinking *The Biggest Estate on Earth*") calls for re-examination of the model while acknowledging its influence.

The core critique: Gammage may attribute too much intentional design to what was in some cases more localised and adaptive practice. But even his critics acknowledge the fundamental insight that Aboriginal people managed the landscape at a scale far beyond what was previously understood.

## 3. ECOLOGICAL DIMENSIONS

### 3.1 Mosaic Burning

The signature technique of Aboriginal fire management is **mosaic burning** — creating a patchwork of burned and unburned areas across the landscape. The method:

- **Cool fires** — Early dry-season, low-intensity fires that trickle through the landscape, burning only some of the fuel
- **Spiral or strip patterns** — Aboriginal fire managers use a slower technique such as dragging a smouldering stick through the bush in deliberate patterns
- **Fine-scale heterogeneity** — The result is "pyrodiverse mosaics of open woody vegetation (ie low shrub abundance) and sparsely treed plains"

The contrast with modern fire management is stark: rural fire services conduct large-scale backburning and hazard reduction that burns inward, creating an inferno. Indigenous burning is cool — temperatures remain low so flames never reach the canopy.

### 3.2 Biodiversity Outcomes

**Habitat diversity:** The mosaic effect creates a variety of habitats that support diverse wildlife, including insects, reptiles, birds, and mammals. Different patches are at different successional stages, providing food and shelter for species with different requirements.

**Quantitative evidence (Bliege Bird et al., PNAS 2008):** This was the first quantitative test of the fire-stick farming hypothesis. Combining ethnographic observations of contemporary Aboriginal hunting and burning with satellite image analysis, they demonstrated that:
- Anthropogenic landscapes contain **a greater diversity of successional stages** than landscapes under a lightning fire regime
- Differences are of **scale, not of kind** — Aboriginal burning creates finer-grained mosaics than lightning alone
- Landscape-scale fire mosaics are directly linked to foraging for small, burrowed prey (monitor lizards), **a specialty of Aboriginal women**

**Plant diversity (Greenwood et al.):** Lead author Leanne Greenwood found that "by creating landscapes that have a diversity of historical fire patterns across time and space, Martu fire practitioners increase plant richness and diversity." Specifically, "high fire frequency diversity promoted higher diversity of those smaller short-lived herbaceous plant species."

### 3.3 Prevention of Catastrophic Wildfire

The primary mechanism: **mosaic burns create firebreaks.** A network of recently burned patches, each with reduced fuel loads, prevents the spread of hot, late-season fires. Without cultural burning, organic matter builds up, putting forests at risk of devastating wildfire.

**The consequences of disruption:** When Aboriginal fire management ceased after colonisation:
- Large, uncontrolled wildfires emerged, usually late in the dry season
- Shrub and heath cover increased dramatically in forests and woodlands (Mariani et al. 2022, *Frontiers in Ecology and the Environment*)
- Grassland openings steadily invaded by trees and shrubs — in the Klamath region, grassland area decreased from an estimated 44% in 1850 to a projected 4% by 2022

**Darwin Centre for Bushfire Research (2019):** Data from Charles Darwin University suggested that reintroduction of traditional burning on a large scale had "significantly reduced the area of land destroyed by wildfires" in northern Australia.

### 3.4 Different Fire Regimes for Different Ecologies

Aboriginal fire management was not a single technique but a portfolio of regimes adapted to local conditions:

- **Seasons:** Burns timed to specific seasons depending on vegetation type and moisture levels
- **Latitude:** Different approaches from tropical north to temperate south
- **Vegetation type:** From spinifex to rainforest, each required its own fire regime
- **Species management:** "Aboriginal people not only thought of kangaroos when laying out their burn patterns, but also of possums, wombats, birds, insects, reptiles and plants. Once you have started to lay out country to suit a species, you are on the way to an extraordinarily complex arrangement of the land" (Gammage)

Victor Steffensen emphasises this point: **"There is only one fire, and that is the right fire, and the fire for your country and your environment."** There are no set time frames — no five-to-seven-year periods or twenty-to-thirty-year intervals. "It was all about the knowledge to read the country correctly."

## 4. THEOLOGICAL DIMENSIONS

### 4.1 Fire as Dreaming Obligation

Aboriginal fire practice is not ecological management to which spiritual meaning has been attached. It is the fulfillment of **Dreaming Law** — obligations given by Ancestral Beings during the foundational time. The timing, patterns, and protocols of burning are governed by Dreaming Law: which areas to burn, when, and in what sequence are matters of sacred knowledge transmitted through ceremony, song, and initiation.

The Martu of Australia's Western Desert provide the clearest ethnographic case. Their foraging systems "have been structured around landscape-burning practices for millennia." These systems are mediated at one end by immediate-return foraging economy and at the other by "the way that burning transforms vegetative succession and habitat heterogeneity (pyrodiversity)."

Critically: **"The distinctive pyrodiversity of anthropogenic landscapes are where Martu insist that they and their estates are conceived."** The burned mosaic is not merely the landscape they live in — it is constitutive of their identity. The estates are "transgenerational storehouses of relational wealth that operate in a delayed-return ritual economy."

### 4.2 Care for Country as Theological Category

The phrase **"care for Country"** encodes a relational ontology where land is kin, not resource. Fire ceremonies maintain the health of Country in the same way one would care for a family member — because **Country IS family.** As Martu explain it, "the Dreaming is more than just a spiritual reality" — it constitutes the person-land-ancestor unity.

"Martu selves do not exist independently of the ecological relationships that make and signify family and homelands. Their notions of homeland and family are inherently relational, similar to notions captured in *oikos*, the Greek for home and the root for ecology."

This means fire management is not motivated by a "conservation ethic" in the Western sense. It is not about stewardship of an external resource. It is about maintaining the relationships that constitute one's own identity and the identity of one's kin — including non-human kin.

### 4.3 The Inseparability of Ecology and Theology

Gammage's formulation bears repeating: **"Theology and ecology are fused."** This is not a metaphorical claim. It means:

- Burning is ceremony
- Ceremony is burning
- The health of the land is the health of the Dreaming
- The health of the Dreaming is the health of the people
- To neglect fire is to neglect ancestral obligation
- To neglect ancestral obligation is to let the land sicken
- When the land sickens, the people sicken

There is no secular frame available for this practice. The person who burns Country is simultaneously: a fire manager, a ceremonial practitioner, a custodian of Dreaming narratives, an ecological scientist, and a kin-keeper. These are not multiple roles held by one person — they are one role perceived under different Western categories.

### 4.4 Fire as Healing

The restorative dimension is documented in contemporary cultural burning revival:

> "We're not just healing the land ecologically and doing good stewardship work. We're also healing ourselves as people, relearning our ways."

This is not metaphor. Aboriginal practitioners describe cultural burning as simultaneously healing the land, healing community relationships, and healing the connection to ancestors. The restoration of fire practice is experienced as the restoration of identity.

### 4.5 The Collapse Under Colonisation

When Aboriginal people were removed from traditional lands during colonisation, traditional burning largely stopped during the twentieth century. This was not merely an ecological disaster (though it was that — leading to large, uncontrolled wildfires and species extinction). It was a theological catastrophe: the ancestral obligations that constituted identity could not be fulfilled. The "transgenerational storehouses of relational wealth" that Martu fire management maintained were, as the scholarly literature puts it, **"ransacked by colonialism in the mid-twentieth century, precipitating a collapse in the anthropogenic fire regime and a decline and extinction of many endemic species."**

Since returning to their homelands in the 1980s, Martu "have reestablished a tight patchwork of vegetative succession and rescaled the landscape mosaic." The ecological recovery is also a ceremonial recovery is also an identity recovery. These are one thing.

## 5. VICTOR STEFFENSEN AND THE REVIVAL

### 5.1 Background

**Victor Steffensen** is a Tagalaka man — a descendant of the Tagalaka people through his mother's connections from the Gulf Country of north Queensland. His grandmother was one of the many Indigenous children taken from their families — part of the Stolen Generations: "My Granny was sent to be a housemaid, separated from her brothers, one was sent to Palm Island and one to work on the cattle stations, so we were separated and unable to pass on a lot of knowledge and language."

Steffensen grew up in an Aboriginal community in Kuranda, north-west of Cairns.

### 5.2 Learning from the Elders

In the 1990s, while living in Laura in far north Queensland, Steffensen met two culturally respected senior Aboriginal men: **Dr. George Musgrave ("Poppy")** and **Dr. Tommy George ("TG")** of the **Kuku-Thaypan people**. Already in their sixties and seventies, they were the last of their people to hold its ancient knowledge and the last speakers of the **Awu-Laya language**.

What started as a fishing trip became a life-long apprenticeship. Poppy and TG taught Steffensen "how to burn the country in the old traditional way to improve the land." They transmitted not only the practical techniques but the knowledge framework — the stories, the language, the way of reading Country.

### 5.3 The Unauthorised Burns

In the 1990s, Poppy, TG, and Steffensen began burning — **without official permits** — to heal damage to the land. This initially provoked strong opposition from local landowners and government rangers. But within two or three years, the benefits in terms of regeneration and cleansing became obvious. Coupled with Steffensen's efforts to explain through talks and workshops, this sparked a revival of interest that has become nationwide.

### 5.4 *Fire Country* (2020)

Published by Hardie Grant, *Fire Country: How Indigenous Fire Management Could Help Save Australia* is described as "not only a timely publication, it is a comprehensive guide to its subject, passionate and persuasive, wide in its scope and sure to be a classic."

The book combines:
- Personal narrative (growing up, meeting the elders, learning to burn)
- Practical instruction (how to read Country, how to burn safely)
- Advocacy (the case for reintroducing Aboriginal fire management)
- Critique (what is wrong with current Australian fire management)

An *Australian Story* episode (2020) documented the story: how two elders from Cape York entrusted Steffensen with ancient knowledge, and whether this method could answer Australia's bushfire crisis.

### 5.5 Professional Work and Advocacy

Over 30 years, Steffensen has:
- Co-founded the **National Indigenous Fire Workshops** (hosted in Queensland, New South Wales, and Victoria)
- Co-founded the **Firesticks Alliance**
- Held fire workshops across Australia for farmers, firefighters, and government agencies
- Connected with First Nations communities in **California, Canada, and among the Sami people of Scandinavia**
- Trained both Aboriginal communities and non-Indigenous Australians in cultural burning

### 5.6 Working with Emergency Services

Steffensen has worked with local, state, and federal government agencies, but is frank about systemic dysfunction: **"The biggest problem in this country is that everyone's confused... We have so many environmental agencies dealing with fire in this country and none of them working together. They all have different fire regimes."**

His vision: **"The future firefighter is not the firefighter we see today — he will evolve into a fire manager."** Not someone who fights fire but someone who understands fire — when to use it, when to withhold it, how to read the land's need.

Concrete engagement example: In April 2019, a Traditional Cultural Burning Workshop at a Land for Wildlife property in Grantham was presented by Steffensen, attended by private landowners, Queensland Fire and Emergency Services personnel, rural fire brigade members, Bunya Peoples' Aboriginal Rangers, and government officials.

### 5.7 The 2019-2020 Bushfire Crisis

The 2019-20 Australian bushfires — 35 fatalities, 3,093 homes destroyed, massive ecological damage — caused a seismic shift in public attitudes toward Aboriginal fire management. Steffensen noted: **"Since the 2019 wildfires there's been a huge shift and that is what we are all about, Aboriginal voices and Aboriginal knowledge being heard."**

Aboriginal peoples' landscape fires were central in discussions about bushfire responses. The crisis validated what Steffensen and his mentors had been demonstrating for decades: that the suppression-based fire regime inherited from colonialism was failing catastrophically.

### 5.8 The Elders' Legacy

Both Poppy and TG received honorary doctorates from **James Cook University** in recognition of their life's work. Both have since passed on, "but they left knowing that they had achieved something important in their lives. They were confident that Victor would carry this on for the rest of his life. They were very happy to see their knowledge being shared with other Mobs."

### 5.9 The Right Fire

Steffensen's core teaching:

> **"There is only one fire, and that is the right fire, and the fire for your country and your environment."**

There are no universal timetables, no set intervals. "It was all about the knowledge to read the country correctly, and with that knowledge comes safety." This is the theological dimension in practical form: the right fire requires knowledge of Country, and knowledge of Country is knowledge of the Dreaming. You cannot burn correctly without understanding the land, and you cannot understand the land without understanding its sacred history.

## 6. CONVERGENCES AND IMPLICATIONS

### 6.1 Fire as Maximal Functional Integration

Aboriginal fire management represents perhaps the most extreme case of functional integration documented in any tradition. In a single act — lighting a fire in the right place at the right time — the practitioner simultaneously:
- Manages ecology (promoting biodiversity, preventing catastrophic wildfire)
- Fulfills Dreaming obligation (maintaining ancestral Law)
- Practises ceremony (performing a sacred act with sacred knowledge)
- Maintains kinship (caring for Country-as-kin)
- Navigates the landscape (fire patterns follow songline routes)
- Produces food (encouraging game species and plant growth)
- Transmits knowledge (the act of burning teaches the young how to read Country)

These are not seven activities happening at once. They are one activity described in seven Western categories. The categories are artefacts of Western analysis; the unity is Aboriginal reality.

### 6.2 The Colonial Rupture as Theological Catastrophe

The cessation of burning under colonisation was not merely an ecological failure — it was a **spiritual crisis of the highest order**. If fire management IS Dreaming obligation, and you cannot fulfill your Dreaming obligation, then:
- The land sickens (ecological collapse)
- The ancestors' Law is broken (theological rupture)
- The person's identity disintegrates (because identity is constituted through ecological-spiritual relationship)
- The knowledge dies (because it is transmitted through practice, not text)

The revival of cultural burning is therefore a revival of everything simultaneously — ecology, theology, identity, knowledge, community.

### 6.3 Cross-Cultural Fire Traditions

The growing international network — Steffensen connecting with California First Nations, Canadian First Nations, and Sami people — suggests that fire-as-ceremony is not uniquely Australian but part of a broader pattern of Indigenous fire management worldwide. Each tradition has its own local knowledge, but the structural features converge: fire as sacred obligation, fire as ecological management, fire as identity practice.

## 8. UNCERTAINTIES AND LIMITATIONS

1. **Scale of Gammage's claims.** The degree to which Aboriginal fire management was truly continent-wide and coordinated (as opposed to locally adaptive and convergent) remains debated. Critics argue Gammage may overstate intentional design.

2. **Rhys Jones's later career.** Jones was subsequently involved in the controversial "blitzkrieg hypothesis" (that Aboriginal people caused megafauna extinction), which complicates his legacy. His fire-stick farming thesis is now widely accepted; his extinction thesis remains contested.

3. **Quantitative gaps.** While Bliege Bird et al. (2008) provided the first quantitative test, most evidence for pre-colonial fire management remains inferred from colonial records, vegetation analysis, and ethnographic analogy. Direct archaeological evidence of specific burning events 10,000+ years ago is inherently limited.

4. **Steffensen's elders.** The specific cultural burning knowledge transmitted by George Musgrave and Tommy George was from the Kuku-Thaypan tradition. Its applicability to other regions and language groups requires local adaptation — as Steffensen himself emphasises.

5. **The "right fire" problem.** If the right fire requires deep knowledge of specific Country, then scaling cultural burning across the continent requires not one program but hundreds of locally specific knowledge systems — many of which were disrupted or destroyed by colonisation. The knowledge gap is real.

6. **Climate change.** Traditional fire regimes evolved under pre-industrial climate conditions. Whether the same burning patterns remain optimal under accelerating climate change is an open question, though early evidence from northern Australia is encouraging.
