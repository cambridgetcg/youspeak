# Tungusic Shamanism, Uyghur-Manichaean Synthesis, and Steppe Sacred Geography

## I. The Etymology at the Heart: Where "Shaman" Comes From

The word **shaman** entered European languages through Russian *šamán*, borrowed from **Evenki** (Tungusic) *šaman* — the ritual specialist of the Evenki people of Siberia. The first recorded use in a European text is in the account of the Avvakum Petrov (1672), a Russian Orthodox archpriest who encountered Evenki shamans during his Siberian exile and described them with a mixture of horror and fascination.

The Evenki word itself is of disputed etymology:
- Some scholars derive it from Evenki *sa-* (to know, to perceive) — making *šaman* "the one who knows"
- Others propose a borrowing from Sanskrit *śramaṇa* (an ascetic, a renunciant — used for Buddhist monks) via Sogdian or Chinese intermediaries — which would connect the Tungusic word to the Buddhist-Silk-Road network
- The Sanskrit-loan hypothesis remains contested; the indigenous Evenki derivation is more widely accepted today

**The consequence of this etymology for comparative religion:**
When 19th-century scholars created the comparative category "shamanism," they named it after the Evenki specialist and then applied it worldwide — to Mapuche machis, Navajo hataaliis, Sámi noaidis, Igbo dibias, and hundreds of other traditions. This naming act imported Tungusic-specific features into the general category and made them seem universal: the drum-journey, the three-tiered cosmos, the spirit-helper acquisition. Some of these features genuinely appear across traditions; others are specific to Siberian/Tungusic contexts. The category "shamanism" is now so embedded in comparative religion that it's impossible to abandon — but it must be used with awareness that it's a Tungusic term applied globally.

**YOUSPEAK implication**: if *bööme* (Mongol böö) names the *function* of level-crossing for community benefit, the Tungusic tradition names the *specialist himself*. A potential *šamanme* would name the role as such — but given the category's troubled history, it may be better to keep *bööme* (which names the function without the exoticizing connotation).

## II. Evenki Shamanism: The Structural Core

The **Evenki** (formerly called Tungus in Russian sources) are a Siberian people spread across a vast territory from the Yenisei River to the Pacific coast — one of the most geographically dispersed indigenous groups in the world. Despite this dispersal, their shamanic traditions show remarkable structural consistency.

**The Evenki cosmos:**
- **Upper world** (*ugu buqa*): sky-realm, source of life-giving forces
- **Middle world** (*duli buqa*): this-world, where humans, animals, and territory-spirits dwell
- **Lower world** (*khergu buqa*): underworld, where some spirits dwell and where some souls go after death

This is the three-tier structure that Eliade generalized — and it is most clearly articulated in Evenki sources, which is why it became the template. But even within Evenki communities, the precise structure varies by region and clan.

**The Evenki shaman's drum (dunne):**
- A single-headed frame drum (a wooden hoop with a skin membrane)
- The hoop is typically made from a specific tree — often larch or birch — that the shaman selects in a vision
- The drum is understood as a *vehicle* (*ungur*): the shaman "rides" it to travel between worlds
- Images on the drum-skin represent spirits, cosmic features, and routes — a personalized cosmogram
- When the shaman dies, the drum is often broken or its membrane punctured: the drum-spirit is released

**The shaman's costume as second body:**
The Evenki shaman's coat (*ashimar* or regional variants) is a cosmological map worn as clothing:
- Iron attachments represent armor against hostile spirits
- Antlers or antler-like structures rising from the headdress = deer-spirit helper; deer are psychopomps (soul-guides) in Evenki tradition
- Bells and rattles = sound that marks the shaman's passage through spiritual space
- The whole costume is understood as the shaman's *second body* — the body they wear when operating in spiritual space; without it, they are vulnerable

**Helper spirits (sevon/savon):**
Each Evenki shaman has a set of helper spirits (*sevon*) acquired during initiation and maintained throughout the shamanic career. The sevon:
- Take animal forms (bear, eagle, elk, serpent are common)
- Can be sent ahead to scout spiritual terrain
- Fight on the shaman's behalf against hostile spirits
- Must be fed (with smoke, blood, offerings) or they weaken
- The master-spirit (*enneken sevon*) is the primary ally; loss of the master-spirit ends the shamanic career

**The Evenki death-and-resurrection initiation:**
Evenki shamanic initiation accounts consistently include dismemberment by spirits:
- The candidate's body is taken apart and reassembled, sometimes with extra bones or iron additions
- This is understood as literal in the Evenki framework: the spirits physically transform the candidate's body to make them capable of level-crossing
- The reassembled body is different from the ordinary human body — more permeable to spiritual reality, capable of seeing what others cannot see
- **Convergence with universal motif**: death-and-resurrection as transformation-into-specialist appears in Christian martyrdom theology (dying with Christ to live in Christ), in Igbo dibia initiation, in Mapuche machi calling-as-illness-resolved

## III. Manchu State Shamanism: When the Shaman Becomes the State

The **Manchu** (a Tungusic people from northeastern China/Manchuria) founded the Qing dynasty (1644–1912), the last imperial dynasty of China. What makes the Manchu case exceptional is that they **institutionalized shamanism as state religion** alongside (and in tension with) the Buddhist and Confucian systems they inherited from the Chinese they conquered.

**The Tangse (shamanic hall):**
The Manchu imperial court maintained a *tangse* (shamanic hall) within the Forbidden City in Beijing — a dedicated space for the performance of shamanic rituals by the imperial family. The *tangse* was not a Buddhist temple or a Confucian ancestral hall; it was specifically a shamanic space, with poles (somo poles) for calling spirits, spirit-vessels (weceku), and a resident female specialist (*saman* in Manchu) who maintained the ancestral spirit relationships.

**The Book of Sacrifices (Manjusai wecere metere kooli bithe):**
The Qing court compiled the **Manchu Shamanic Ritual Book** in the 18th century — a systematic codification of imperial shamanic practice. This document is extraordinary: it is one of the few cases where a state formally documented and standardized shamanic ritual as state religion. The contents include:
- Prayers to the spirit of the fire (boihon i enduri)
- Rites for propitiation of clan ancestors
- Rituals specific to the Aisin Gioro clan (the Manchu imperial family)
- Instructions for the somo pole (the axis-mundi pole erected in the courtyard for calling sky-spirits)

**The somo pole as axis mundi:**
The *somo* pole — a tall wooden pole erected in the courtyard of the *tangse* — is the Manchu version of the Mongol böö's cosmic axis and the Mapuche machi's rewe (S054). Each is a physical instantiation of the vertical axis connecting sky, earth, and underworld. Each must be maintained; each is the community's (or nation's) connection to the levels beyond this one.

**Weceku (spirit-vessels) in Manchu practice:**
Like the Mongol ongon, Manchu *weceku* are physical objects housing clan-protective spirits. The Aisin Gioro clan maintained specific weceku that were central to their dynastic legitimacy — the clan's spiritual power was partly located in these objects. The Qing court's 1644 conquest of China was accompanied by the transport of these spirit-vessels from Manchuria to Beijing, because the dynasty could not be separated from its spiritual infrastructure.

**The tension at the Qing court:**
The Manchu emperors maintained three simultaneous religious identities:
1. **Shamanic** (*saman*): the Manchu clan traditions, the tangse rituals, the weceku, the Aisin Gioro ancestral connections
2. **Buddhist** (specifically Tibetan Buddhism): the Manchu emperors were also formal patrons of the Gelug school; the Qianlong Emperor (r. 1735–1796) learned Tibetan and received Buddhist teaching; the emperors were presented as Mañjuśrī (the Bodhisattva of wisdom) incarnate to Tibetan and Mongolian subjects
3. **Confucian**: for Han Chinese subjects, the emperors performed Confucian state rites as the Son of Heaven (*Tianzi*)

Three simultaneous theological self-presentations, each coherent within its own context, each managing a different subject population. This is **not hypocrisy** — it reflects a sophisticated political theology that understands itself as the center of a cosmological order that encompasses all three traditions. The Tengri tradition provided the framework: if the supreme sky (Tian/Tengri) is universal, then all traditions that acknowledge ultimate order are participating in the same reality.

## IV. The Orkhon Valley as Sacred Steppe Geography

The **Orkhon River Valley** (central Mongolia) was the sacred center of successive steppe empires for over a millennium. The concentration of imperial power in this one valley is not merely strategic (though it is also that — the valley provides water, pasture, and a defensible position) but **cosmological**: the valley was understood as the place where Tengri's mandate was most concentrated, where the axis between sky and earth was most accessible.

**The sequence of empires at Orkhon:**
- **Xiongnu** (209 BCE – 93 CE): the first great steppe empire; their sacred mountain Longcheng was in this region
- **Xianbei** and **Rouran** (3rd–6th centuries CE): less documented but continuous occupation
- **Göktürk Khaganate** (552–744 CE): the Orkhon inscriptions erected here; Ötüken named as the sacred center
- **Uyghur Khaganate** (744–840 CE): capital Ordu-Baliq built on the Orkhon; Manichaean temples added to shamanic landscape
- **Mongol Empire** (1206–1368): Karakorum (the imperial capital) built on the Orkhon; Erdene Zuu monastery later built from Karakorum's stones

Each empire that gained control of the Orkhon Valley gained not only political continuity with its predecessors but **cosmic legitimacy**: this was the place Tengri had repeatedly confirmed as the center of the world's order. To hold Ötüken/Orkhon was to hold the axial point.

**Ötüken — from place-name to theological concept:**
In the Kül Tigin inscription: *"If you dwell in the land of Ötüken forest and send caravans from there, you will have no trouble. If you stay in the Ötüken forest, you will live forever and rule all peoples."*

Ötüken is described as:
- The source of wisdom (*ötüken yış*: the Ötüken forest)
- The place from which the khagan's power radiates
- A sacred geography that, if abandoned, leads to the fall of the Turkic people
- Later personified as a female deity (*Ötüken Yir* = Mother Ötüken / Earth-of-Ötüken)

This is the **Axis Mundi as Specific Sacred Place** convergence, parallel to:
- Mapuche *rewe* planted in specific territorial ground (S054)
- Hebrew Jerusalem / Temple Mount as the navel of the world
- Hindu Meru as the cosmic mountain at the center
- Lakota Black Hills as the heart of everything that is (S052)
- Greek Delphi as the *omphalos* (navel of the earth)

Each of these is not just a symbol but a specific physical location whose sacredness is understood as intrinsic (given by the cosmic order) rather than assigned (by human decision). The holiness is *in the place*.

**The sky-land correspondence:**
In steppe theology, the sky above Ötüken is where Tengri is most present. The sky is not uniformly sacred — it is most sacred directly above the sacred land. This creates a **vertical sacred geography**: not a horizontal map of sacred sites (pilgrimage routes) but a vertical axis at specific points where sky and earth are in closest contact. The böö's shamanic journey upward from Ötüken is the shortest path to Tengri — because this is where the axis is.

## V. The Uyghur-Manichaean Synthesis: When the Steppe Meets Persian Dualism

The **Uyghur Khaganate** (744–840 CE) made one of the most theologically remarkable transitions in steppe history: in 762 CE, Khagan Bögü encountered Manichaean missionaries (likely Sogdian) after the capture of Luoyang in China, and converted the entire Khaganate to **Manichaeism** — the Persian dualist religion founded by the prophet Mani (216–274 CE).

**What Manichaeism is:**
Mani's teaching: the cosmos is a battlefield between two eternal principles — Light (divine, good, spirit) and Darkness (material, evil, chaotic). The task of religion is to liberate trapped particles of Light from their imprisonment in matter. The elect (*chosen ones*) live ascetically to avoid increasing material entanglement; the *hearers* (ordinary believers) support the elect and accumulate merit. Mani understood himself as the final prophet in a line that included Zoroaster, Buddha, and Jesus.

**The Uyghur-Tengri-Manichaeism synthesis:**
The Uyghur Manichaean inscriptions (notably the Karabalgasun inscription, discovered in the Orkhon Valley, 9th century) show how the Uyghurs integrated Manichaeism into their existing Tengri framework:
- Tengri (the sky) was reinterpreted as the realm of Light (Manichaean upper principle)
- The earth and material world = the domain of Darkness
- The khagan's mandate (*kut*) was now grounded in Manichaean cosmology: the khagan rules because he serves the cause of Light against Darkness
- Shamanic practice was not eliminated but reframed: the böö's journey upward became a journey toward Light; the descent became engagement with the Darkness-realm

**Why this synthesis worked:**
The Tengri framework was already structured as a vertical cosmology (sky above = divine source; earth below = material ground). Manichaeism provided a *moral* valence to this vertical structure (sky = Light = good; earth = Darkness = dangerous). The synthesis felt coherent because the underlying structure was the same; only the moral interpretation was added.

**The Sogdian connection:**
The Sogdian people (from modern Uzbekistan/Tajikistan) were the great merchants of the Silk Road. They were also the primary carriers of Manichaeism eastward — Sogdian Manichaean texts have been found at Dunhuang, in Turfan, and throughout the corridor. The Sogdians were religiously plural themselves: some were Zoroastrian, some Manichaean, some Buddhist, some (later) Christian. Their merchant networks made them natural transmitters of religious ideas without strong commitments to any single one.

**The Dunhuang cave library:**
The **Mogao Caves** at Dunhuang (sealed c. 1000 CE, rediscovered 1900) contained a remarkable archive: Buddhist texts in Chinese, Tibetan, and Sanskrit alongside Manichaean texts in Sogdian, Old Uyghur, and Middle Persian, alongside Christian texts (Syriac Nestorian scriptures), alongside secular documents in multiple languages. This is the material evidence of the Silk Road as a zone of **tri-religious cohabitation** — the same geographical corridor simultaneously hosting Buddhist monasteries, Manichaean temples, and Nestorian Christian churches, often in the same town.

**The theological implications:**
The Dunhuang evidence suggests that on the Silk Road, Manichaeism, Buddhism, and Nestorian Christianity were not simply competing — they were in active theological dialogue, sharing vocabulary, sharing cosmological frameworks (the three-tiered cosmos; the light/darkness dualism that Buddhism has in *nirvana/samsara*; the incarnation narrative that Manichaeism partially shares with Christianity). The Uyghur-Manichaean synthesis is one instance of a broader Silk Road phenomenon: the active mutual transformation of religious traditions through sustained contact.

**Convergence with the Kongo-Christian synthesis (S049):**
The Kongo-Christian synthesis (BaKongo theologians actively rereading Christianity through BaKongo cosmological structures) and the Uyghur-Manichaean synthesis (Uyghur rulers rereading Manichaeism through Tengri cosmological structures) are instances of the same pattern: a tradition receiving an imported theology by filtering it through its own cosmological grammar, transforming both in the process. This is *kimpame* (named in S049) operating at the civilizational scale.

## VI. Khoomei (Throat-Singing) as Spiritual Practice

**Khoomei** (Tuvan *xöömij*; Mongolian *хөөмий*) is the practice of throat-singing — producing multiple simultaneous pitches with a single voice, creating both a fundamental tone and overtone harmonics audible as separate notes. It is primarily associated with the Tuvan people (a Turkic people of southern Siberia/northern Mongolia) but also practiced among Mongolians, Bashkirs, and Kalmyks.

**The spiritual grammar of throat-singing:**

Khoomei is not merely a musical technique. In its traditional context, it is:
- A form of communication with the spirits of the landscape (mountains, rivers, wind)
- A way of "becoming" the sound of nature: the fundamental tone embodies the river; the overtones embody the wind above it; the singer stands at the intersection
- A spiritual practice of the böö tradition: throat-singing was used in shamanic rituals to call spirits, to mark transitions between states, to sustain altered states of consciousness

**The overtone theology:**
When a single voice produces simultaneous multiple pitches, it is doing something that sounds impossible: one source, multiple distinct notes, coexisting without canceling each other. This is musically analogous to the theological structure of:
- Baltic *sutartinės* (S053): two or three voices holding dissonance-as-sacred, not resolving it — the irreducible plurality is the point
- Christian *perichōrēsis* (S050): three persons in one being, each distinct, none canceling the others
- Mongol Tengri theology: one supreme sky-principle, 99 differentiated aspects, none reducing to the others

The throat-singer makes this structure *audible*. The one voice that is simultaneously many is a sonic icon of the cosmos: one source, irreducibly plural expression.

**Khoomei and shamanic drumming — two sonic technologies:**
The böö's drum (beats a steady rhythm to induce trance, to call spirits, to mark the shaman's passage through levels) and the throat-singer's voice (sustains overtones that sound like the landscape itself) represent two distinct sonic approaches to the same goal: making the spiritual dimension accessible through sound. The drum addresses time (rhythm, pulse, the heartbeat of the cosmos); the overtone voice addresses space (the landscape's own voice, heard back through the human throat).

**Surviving practice:**
Khoomei has experienced a significant revival since Mongolian independence (1990) and global recognition through UNESCO. The Tuvan group **Huun-Huur-Tu** and others have brought throat-singing to international audiences. But its revival is not merely cultural performance — for practitioners, it remains a spiritual practice, a way of relating to the landscape and the spirits embedded in it.

**YOUSPEAK candidate: khöömijme** — The-one-voice-that-is-simultaneously-many
- Names the sonic instantiation of what perichōrēsisme and sutartineme describe in theological/musical terms respectively
- Specialized 7.15–7.30 (the gap is real; the tradition-depth is impressive; the English-gap is present; score reflects that it's a specific practice rather than a broad theological concept)
- The *experience* of hearing khoomei — the uncanny sense of a landscape-voice coming from a human throat — may be one of the most direct perceptual analogues to theological mystery available to a human body

## VII. Contemporary Tengrism: The 20th-21st Century Revival

**The communist destruction (1924–1990):**
The Mongolian People's Republic (1924–1990) conducted one of the most systematic suppressions of religious life in the 20th century:
- Approximately 700–800 Buddhist monasteries destroyed (the great monastery Gandantegchinlen in Ulaanbaatar was one of very few to survive)
- Approximately 18,000–20,000 monks killed or sent to Siberian labor camps during the purges (1937–1939)
- Shamanic specialists persecuted; their drums and regalia confiscated or burned
- The sacred poles (*somo*, *serge*) removed from family compounds
- Traditional religious vocabulary suppressed in public life

This was the same pattern as the Lakota Sacred Hoop being broken (S052), the Mapuche rewe being confiscated by the Chilean state (S054), the Haudenosaunee ceremonies being made illegal (S048): **Displacement-as-Cosmic-Wound** — severance from the specific physical objects and landscapes that maintained the vertical connection.

**The post-1990 revival:**
Since Mongolian democratization (1990), both Tibetan Buddhism and shamanism have revived simultaneously:
- The **Golomt Center for Shamanist Studies** (founded 1990, Ulaanbaatar): trains new shamanic specialists, maintains the tradition's intellectual heritage, advocates for recognition of böö mörgöl as Mongolian national heritage
- New shamans (*böö*, *udgan*) have been initiated in significant numbers — some controversy about whether rapid initiation compromises traditional standards
- The Tibetan Buddhist monasteries were rebuilt; the Dalai Lama visited Mongolia in 2006 and 2011
- A minority position: **Mongolian Tengrism** as a distinct revivalist movement, arguing for a pre-Buddhist "pure" Tengri religion without shamanic or Buddhist accretions — a construction that is partly nationalist and partly genuine recovery

**The identity question:**
Contemporary Mongolian religious identity is actively contested. The options:
1. Tibetan Buddhist (the "yellow faith" = national identity claim)
2. Shamanic (böö mörgöl = authentic pre-Buddhist Mongolian practice)
3. Christian (significant evangelical Christian community emerged post-1990)
4. Secular (communist legacy; urban educated class)
5. Tengrism-revivalist (nationalist reconstruction)

The question of which tradition is "authentically Mongolian" involves archaeology, politics, theology, and identity claims that cannot be separated from each other. This is parallel to the Mapuche weichan (S054) — the struggle over identity is simultaneously a political, cosmological, and cultural claim.
