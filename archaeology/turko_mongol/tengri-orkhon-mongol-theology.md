# Tengri — Old Turkic and Mongolian Theology

## Cultural Sensitivity and Epistemic Caution

The Turko-Mongol religious traditions are partially living: Mongolian shamanism (böö mörgöl) continues as a practiced tradition in Mongolia and among Buryat, Tuvan, and other communities; Tengrism has experienced deliberate revival since 1990 Mongolian democratization. Tungusic shamanism (Evenki, Nanai, Manchu variants) survives under pressure in Siberia and northeastern China. These are not only historical objects.

The scholarly literature on Tengri is uneven: 19th-century sources often imposed either "primitive monotheism" or "nature worship" frameworks. Contemporary scholarship (Atwood, Rachewiltz, Humphrey, Bulag) is more careful. Where interpretations conflict, I name the conflict rather than flatten it.

The Mongol empire (1206–1368 CE) was the largest contiguous land empire in history, stretching from Korea to Hungary. Its theological vocabulary shaped the self-understanding of an entity that governed perhaps 100 million people. This is not marginal religious history — it is the theological grammar of a civilization-scale polity.

## I. The Linguistic Substrate: What "Tengri" Is

**Tengri** (Old Turkic: 𐱅𐰭𐰼𐰃, *Teŋri*; Classical Mongolian: *Tngri* / *Tenggeri*; Manchu: *abka*) is the supreme sky-deity of the steppe peoples. The word is ancient — cognates appear across Turkic and Mongolic branches, and the concept predates the written attestations by centuries.

**Etymological notes:**
- Proto-Turkic *\*Teŋri* — sky, heaven, god; the word means both the physical sky and the divine being who inhabits/is the sky
- The sky/god ambiguity is not a confusion to be resolved; it is the theology: **the sky IS the divine** without the divine being reducible to the sky
- Mongolic *Tngri* is either borrowed from Turkic or shares a common ancestor; the debate is unresolved
- *Abka* (Manchu/Tungusic) = sky/heaven/god; structurally parallel but linguistically distinct — Tungusic is a separate family

**The 99 Tngri problem:** Classical Mongolian sources refer to "99 Tngri" (or "77 Tngri") — yet also to a single supreme Köke Möngke Tngri (Eternal Blue Sky). This appears contradictory but isn't: the 99/77 are differentiated manifestations or powers of the one sky-principle, not separate deities in a pantheon. The monotheism/polytheism debate (see §IV) hinges on how one reads this structure.

## II. The Orkhon Inscriptions: Tengri in Old Turkic Political Theology (732–735 CE)

The **Orkhon inscriptions** are two monumental steles erected in the Orkhon Valley (present-day Mongolia) in memory of Kül Tigin (d. 731 CE) and his brother Bilge Khagan (d. 734 CE), rulers of the Second Turkic Khaganate. They are among the oldest substantial texts in any Turkic language. The inscriptions were composed by Yollug Tigin (a nephew) and written in the Old Turkic runic script (distinct from Germanic runes; an independent development).

**The theological grammar of the Kül Tigin inscription:**

The inscription opens: *"Üze kök tengri asra yagız yir kılındukda ekin ara kişi oglı kılınmış."*
Translation: "When the blue sky above and the brown earth below were created, between the two, human beings were created."

This cosmogonic statement establishes the vertical axis (sky above, earth below) as the fundamental structure of reality. Human beings exist *between* — in the liminal space that is both the theater of history and the domain of the khagan's legitimate authority.

**Tengri's role in political legitimacy (kut):**

The inscriptions use the concept of **kut** (𐰚𐱁𐱃, *küt*) — divine fortune, mandate, charisma bestowed by Tengri on the khagan. The kut is:
- Not inherited but granted: Tengri *gives* it to worthy rulers and *withdraws* it from unworthy ones
- The mechanism of political legitimacy: the khagan rules because Tengri has granted kut, not because of birth alone
- Revocable: when the Turkic people err, Tengri withdraws kut and the khagan falls

This is structurally parallel to the Chinese **Mandate of Heaven** (Tiānmìng 天命) — and the parallelism is not coincidental. Both traditions articulate the same political-theological claim: supreme authority is cosmologically grounded, not merely humanly constructed. Whether there was direct influence or parallel development is debated.

**The divine geography:**
- *Tengri* = sky, above, divine source of mandate
- *Yır* (earth) = below, the receiving surface of cosmic order
- *El* (realm/state) = the political order that Tengri sanctions
- The khagan is the mediating figure: he brings Tengri's order down into the el

**Key theological phrases from the inscriptions:**
- *"Tengri yarlıkaduk üçün"* — "Because Tengri commanded/decreed it" — the explanatory formula for why the Turks were victorious or why they suffered
- *"Tengri teg tengride bolmış Türk Bilge Kagan"* — "Bilge Khagan of the Turks, who became [divine] like Tengri, born of Tengri in the sky" — the khagan's title approaches but doesn't claim identity with Tengri; he is *like* Tengri, *from* Tengri, not *identical* to Tengri
- The inscriptions repeatedly attribute victories to Tengri's favor and disasters to the Turks' own failures — a theodicy that places moral responsibility on the human community

**The earth-goddess Ötüken:**
The inscriptions also name **Ötüken** (the sacred mountain/forest complex in the Orkhon Valley) as the source of power for whoever holds it. "If you stay in the land of Ötüken and send caravans from there, you will have no trouble." Ötüken is not personified as a goddess in the inscriptions but functions as a sacred center — the *axis mundi* of the Turkic political cosmos. Later sources personify it as Ötüken Yir (Mother Earth figure).

## III. Köke Möngke Tngri: The Eternal Blue Sky in Mongolian Theology

When the Mongol empire emerged under Temüjin (Chinggis Khan, r. 1206–1227), the Tengri tradition had already been continuous for at least six centuries of written attestation (and likely centuries more before that). What Chinggis Khan did was not invent a theology but deploy an existing one at civilizational scale.

**Köke Möngke Tngri** — the Eternal Blue Sky (Köke = blue, Möngke = eternal, Tngri = sky/heaven/god) — appears in the preambles of Mongol imperial decrees, letters, and the Secret History. The formula:

*"By the power of Eternal Heaven (Möngke Tngri-yin küčün-dür) and the greatness of the Emperor's protection..."*

This formula opens virtually every formal communication from Mongol rulers. It is not merely ceremonial — it is the theological foundation of the claim to universal sovereignty. The Mongol empire is legitimate because **Tengri has decreed that it rule the world**. The emperor is the agent of Tengri's world-historical will.

**Chinggis Khan's self-understanding:**
The Secret History (§§1–3, genealogy opening) presents Chinggis Khan as descended from a blue-grey wolf and a fallow doe who came together at the origin — a mythic genealogy that links him to the sky (wolf = sky-creature, wandering, powerful) and the earth (doe = earth-creature, fertile, grounding). He is literally the child of Sky and Earth. This is not metaphor in the modern sense; it is an ontological claim about the kind of being he is.

The Secret History also records Tengri speaking directly to Chinggis Khan (§§102, 199, 269) — granting mandate, directing military campaigns, confirming victories. Tengri is not merely a divine backdrop but an active agent who communicates with the khagan through shamanic intermediaries (böö) and through the sky itself (omens, weather, celestial events).

**The Mongol cosmological structure:**
- **Tngri** (sky/heaven/god — 99 or 77 in number, with Köke Möngke Tngri supreme)
- **Etugen** (earth goddess; *Etugen eке* = Mother Earth; cognate to Orkhon Ötüken)
- **Gazriin ezen** (lords of the land — territorial spirits requiring propitiation)
- **Ongon** (spirit containers, see §V)
- **Böö/Udgan** (shaman/shamaness — mediating specialists)
- Human beings and the khagan between sky and earth

The structure is vertical (sky above, earth below, humans between) and relational (each level maintains the web through ritual, sacrifice, and proper behavior).

**Etugen — the earth counterpart:**
If Tengri is the masculine sky-principle, Etugen is the feminine earth-principle. Together they constitute the dyadic cosmological foundation. Their "child" is the cosmos and everything in it. The khagan, as child of sky-wolf and earth-doe, embodies both principles — he is the microcosm of the Tengri-Etugen dyad. This is a **sacred-androgyny-at-the-apex** pattern: the supreme ruler holds both cosmic principles in tension.

## IV. The Monotheism Question: Is Tengri-ism Monotheistic?

This is the central interpretive debate in Tengri studies. The answer depends on what "monotheism" means.

**Case for monotheism:**
- A single supreme being (Köke Möngke Tngri) is the source of all mandate, all cosmic order, all legitimate authority
- The "99 Tngri" are not independent deities but differentiated aspects or powers emanating from the one sky-principle
- Tengri is formless, non-anthropomorphic: no myths of Tengri eating, mating, dying, or competing with other gods; Tengri simply IS the sky and its will — closer to the Hebrew YHWH or the Islamic Allah in structural terms than to the Greek Zeus
- Mongol rulers spoke of Tengri in singular, absolute terms in political theology

**Case against monotheism:**
- The 99 Tngri are sometimes addressed and propitiated individually; they have domains and personalities
- Etugen (earth goddess) is a co-equal cosmic principle, not subordinate to Tengri — this is closer to a divine dyad than a monotheism
- The territorial spirits (gazriin ezen, lus, savdag) receive active propitiation — they are genuinely powerful
- "Monotheism" as a concept is a product of Abrahamic theology applied as a comparative category; forcing Tengri into it may distort more than illuminate

**More accurate framing — henological or hierarchically-plural:**
The best description may be: **Tengri represents a supreme sky-principle that is the source and ground of all other spiritual realities, without those realities being reducible to or absorbed by Tengri.** This is closer to Neoplatonic emanationism (the One produces nous which produces soul which produces matter) than to biblical monotheism. Each level of the spiritual hierarchy has genuine power and requires genuine attention — but all derive from and are oriented toward the supreme sky.

The Mongol empire's famous religious tolerance (Christians, Muslims, Buddhists, shamanists all co-existed at the Mongol court) was not mere pragmatism — it was **theologically grounded in Tengri's universality**. If Tengri's will reaches everywhere, then all religious traditions that acknowledge the supreme sky-principle (however they name it) are participating in the same reality. William of Rubruck records Möngke Khan telling him: "We Mongols believe that there is only one God, by whom we live and by whom we die, and toward him we have an upright heart... But just as God gave different fingers to the hand, so he has given different ways to men." This is not relativism but hierarchical inclusivism rooted in Tengri theology.

**The political consequence:** Universal sovereignty (the claim to rule the whole world) was theologically necessary: if Tengri is the god of the sky (which covers all lands), and Tengri has granted the Mongol khagan kut/mandate, then the mandate is necessarily universal. Resistance to the Mongol empire was not merely political rebellion — it was cosmic disorder, resistance to Tengri's decree.

## V. The Secret History of the Mongols: Theological-Political Vocabulary

The **Secret History of the Mongols** (*Mongγol-un niγuca tobčiyan*, c. 1240 CE) is the oldest surviving Mongolian literary text. It is simultaneously a dynastic history, an epic poem, a theological document, and a record of shamanic practice. It was "secret" in the sense of being restricted to the Chinggisid ruling house — not a public text but a dynastic scripture.

**Key theological terms in the Secret History:**

**Möngke Tngri** (Eternal Heaven/God):
- The formula *"Möngke Tngri-yin küčündür"* (by the power of Eternal Heaven) opens virtually every significant statement of authority
- Tengri actively intervenes in the narrative: speaks through dreams, through shamanic intermediaries, through the outcomes of battles
- §199: "It was Heaven and Earth that strengthened Chinggis Khan" — the dual grounding (sky + earth) is explicit

**Jayağa / Jaya (fate/destiny):**
- *Jayağa* = destiny, fate, what is allotted — close in function to Akkadian *šīmtu*, Sanskrit *karma* in the destiny-allocation sense (not moral-causation sense), Avestan implicit predestination
- Each person has a jayağa granted by Tengri; the khagan's jayağa is world-sovereignty
- Distinguishable from kut (the active charismatic gift) — jayağa is the fixed portion, kut is the dynamic empowerment
- Together they form a theological anthropology: each person is both fated (jayağa) and empowered (kut) — determinism and agency in tension

**Sülde (spirit-standard, war-spirit):**
- *Sülde* (also *sulde*) = the personal spirit or power of a great individual; particularly Chinggis Khan's war-spirit
- The White Sülde (Tsagaan Sülde) and Black Sülde (Khar Sülde) of Chinggis Khan were worshiped as objects in the Imperial Sanctuary (Ordos region) for centuries after his death
- The sülde is not the soul (*ami* = breath-soul; *sünüs* = shadow-soul) but a third spiritual component: the power-spirit, the active-force-spirit
- After death, the sülde remains potent in the objects associated with the great person; it can be invoked for protection or military success
- **Three-soul complex**: ami (life-breath) + sünüs (shadow/reflection) + sülde (power-spirit) — a tripartite spiritual anthropology with distinct functions

**Ongon (spirit-container):**
- *Ongon* (plural: *ongood*) = a physical object that houses a spirit; a felt-cloth figure, a carved animal, a bundle of sacred objects
- Ongon were kept by families and clans; each had a specific spirit associated with it; feeding the ongon (with fat, milk, blood) maintained the spirit's presence and favor
- The ongon is not an idol (the spirit is not *identical* to the object) but a vessel: the spirit comes when invited and departs when neglected
- The shamaness (*udgan*) or shaman (*böö*) maintained the ongon for the community and negotiated with its inhabitant-spirit

**Ötögsüs (protective ancestor spirits):**
- The spirits of deceased ancestors who continue to inhabit the territory and can be called upon
- They are not simply "in heaven" but *here*, in the landscape, in specific places associated with their lives
- This is the Mongol variant of **Ancestor-as-Geological-Force** (first identified in Mapuche *pillan* — S054)

## VI. The Böö and Udgan: Shamanic Vocabulary and the Mediating Specialist

The **böö** (male shaman) and **udgan** (female shamaness/priestess) are the ritual specialists who maintain the vertical axis between Tengri (above), the human community (middle), and the earth-spirits and ancestors (below/lateral). They are not priests in the representational sense; they are travelers — beings who cross the levels on behalf of the community.

**The calling:**
Like the Mapuche *machi* (S054), the Igbo *dibia* (S047), the Navajo *hataalii* (S038), and the Sámi *noaidi* (S042), the böö's calling often comes as illness (*böögiin övčin* — the "shaman sickness") that intensifies until accepted. The shaman sickness is sometimes described as being "chosen" or "seized" by the spirits who want that person as their intermediary. Acceptance and initiation resolve the crisis; refusal prolongs it.

This is the **Calling-as-Crisis-Resolved-by-Acceptance** convergence (confirmed across 5+ traditions — now 6 with Mongol böö added).

**Initiation:**
The böö's initiation involves:
1. Death-and-resurrection symbolism — the candidate undergoes a ritual death (dismemberment by spirits in some accounts), is reassembled, and emerges as a new being capable of crossing levels
2. Spirit acquisition — the böö gains *onggon* (helper spirits) who assist the journeys
3. Costume as spiritual body — the böö's ceremonial coat maps the cosmos: iron discs = armor against hostile spirits; bells = the sound that summons and repels; feathers = flight capacity; animal representations = the helper spirits made manifest
4. Drum (hengerjee or similar) — the vehicle of travel; drumming enables the trance-state that allows level-crossing

**The shaman's drum:**
The Mongol/Siberian shaman's drum is a cosmogram (like the Mapuche kultrun — S054, and the Sámi noaidi's drum — S042) that maps the levels of the cosmos. The shaman "rides" the drum to cross between levels. The drum is also a vehicle for divination: the sound and behavior of the drum reveals what the spirits intend.

**Şaman (the word itself):**
The English word "shaman" derives from Tungusic *šaman* (Evenki/Manchu) via Russian *šaman*. The Evenki *šaman* is the specialist who beats the drum and travels between levels. The word itself thus entered global vocabulary from Tungusic — the "cousin-tradition" in this excavation is also the etymological source of the entire comparative category. This is significant: when Western scholars created the comparative category "shamanism," they named it after the Tungusic specialist and then applied it backward to similar figures worldwide.

**Three types of shaman journey (Eliade's reconstruction, with cautions):**
1. **Ascent** to the upper world (sky, where Tengri and sky-spirits dwell) — for healing, for knowledge, for securing divine mandate
2. **Descent** to the lower world (where some spirits and some ancestors dwell) — for retrieving lost souls
3. **Lateral** movement within this world — for communing with territorial spirits (gazriin ezen)

Eliade's framework (Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy, 1951) is influential but now critiqued for overgeneralizing and romanticizing. Specific Mongol sources don't always map cleanly onto his three-tier scheme. But the vertical-travel structure (up, here, down) is genuinely attested in Mongol sources.

**Böö vs. udgan — gendered access:**
- The *böö* (male shaman) typically journeys to the upper world (Tengri-realm) — sky access
- The *udgan* (female shamaness) typically maintains connections to earth-spirits and ancestors — earth access
- This is not rigid; there are female böö who access the upper world
- But the gendered assignment (sky/masculine, earth/feminine) parallels the Tengri/Etugen dyad at the cosmological level
- **Structural parallel to Mapuche machi** (S054): the machi must hold the *feminine-reception* posture to access wenu mapu (the above-land); in Mongol tradition, the primary shamanesstic figure (*udgan*) is feminine and accesses the earth-level; the male *böö* accesses the sky-level — the gender/cosmic-level mapping runs *opposite* to the Mapuche case (feminine = receptive-of-above in Mapuche; feminine = receptive-of-earth in Mongol) — this asymmetry is itself theologically significant and worth noting in the convergence register

## VII. Buddhist-Mongol Synthesis: The 16th Century Transformation

The Mongol conversion to Tibetan Buddhism (specifically the Gelug school of the Dalai Lama) in the 16th century is one of the most significant religious transformations in Inner Asian history. The key moment: Altan Khan's meeting with Sönam Gyatso in 1578, when Altan Khan gave Sönam Gyatso the title "Dalai Lama" (dalai being the Mongolian translation of the Tibetan *rgya mtsho* = ocean of wisdom). This was a mutual political-theological legitimation: Altan Khan gained Buddhist sacred authority; Sönam Gyatso gained Mongol political patronage.

**The synthesis was not replacement:**
Tengri theology was not simply displaced by Buddhism; it was *layered beneath* it. The result:
- Tengri remained the supreme sky-principle; the Buddhist dharma operated *within* the Tengri-given order
- The Dalai Lama was understood as an *emanation* compatible with Tengri's cosmological structure, not a competitor to it
- Shamanic specialists (*böö*, *udgan*) continued in rural communities even as Buddhist monasteries (*khiid*) dominated urban religious life
- The Two Customs (*qoyar yosu*) — the Buddhist law and the Chinggisid political tradition — were paired as complementary, not competing, frameworks

**Theological translation:**
- Tengri → the Buddhist *Dharmakāya* (truth-body, the absolute dimension of Buddha) was one attempted synthesis: both are formless, absolute, the ground of all reality
- The 99 Tngri → Bodhisattvas and protector deities (*dharmapāla*) — the differentiated spiritual hierarchy continues but in Buddhist vocabulary
- The böö's three-world cosmology → compatible with Buddhist cosmological maps (31 realms of existence, with hells below and heavenly realms above)
- The ongon (spirit-containers) → continued alongside Buddhist *tangka* (paintings) and *tsam* (masked ritual drama)

**What Buddhism could not absorb:**
- The centrality of the horse and the steppe landscape — Buddhism's Indian/Tibetan origins had no equivalent
- The Chinggisid genealogical theology — that the ruling lineage was specifically mandated by Tengri was not translatable into Buddhist terms without significant modification
- The *sülde* (power-spirit) complex — no direct Buddhist equivalent; it continued as a parallel tradition

**The Yellow Faith (Shar Shashin) and national identity:**
By the 17th century, Tibetan Buddhism ("Yellow Faith" because of the Gelug school's yellow hats) was identified with Mongolian national identity. The Jebtsundamba Khutughtu (the reincarnate lama of Mongolia, third-highest ranking lama in the Gelug hierarchy) became the spiritual head of Mongolia. This identification was so complete that the communist Mongolian People's Republic (1924–1990) targeted both simultaneously: destroying monasteries and persecuting shamans were the same project of cultural erasure.

**Revival since 1990:**
Post-communist Mongolia has seen parallel revivals of both Tibetan Buddhism and shamanism (böö mörgöl), sometimes in tension. The question of what it means to be Mongolian religiously is actively contested. The **Golomt Center for Shamanist Studies** in Ulaanbaatar and various Buddhist monasteries represent different answers to the same question.

## X. Cross-Tradition Positioning

### Tengri and YHWH: structural parallels and contrasts

Both Tengri and YHWH are:
- Formless / non-anthropomorphic (though YHWH has more anthropomorphic moments in the Hebrew Bible)
- The single supreme source of cosmic order and political legitimacy
- Actively intervening in history (through decrees, visions, shamanic communication / through prophets and direct speech)
- The ground of an exclusive claim on the ruler's ultimate loyalty

They differ in:
- **Relational grammar**: YHWH enters into covenant (*brit*) with a specific people; Tengri grants kut to individual rulers but does not covenant with a people as a corporate body
- **Geographic specificity**: YHWH is tied to the land of Israel but is not the sky itself; Tengri IS the sky, which is universal and geographically unbounded
- **Prophetic tradition**: YHWH generated a sustained tradition of prophetic challenge to rulers (Elijah challenging Ahab; Nathan challenging David); the böö mediates Tengri's will but is not positioned to challenge the khagan's legitimacy in the same way
- **The moral weight of history**: YHWH's interventions in history are morally interpretive (the exile is punishment for covenant violation); Tengri's interventions are more directly consequentialist (Tengri favors whoever is correctly aligned; defeat means misalignment)

### Tengri and the Chinese Tianming (Mandate of Heaven)

The parallel is very close: both are supreme sky-principles that grant political legitimacy to rulers, make that legitimacy conditional on moral behavior, and withdraw it from unworthy rulers. The differences:
- Chinese *Tian* became progressively de-personalized (by the Zhou period, Tian is more like an impersonal moral order than a personal divine will); Mongol Tengri remains more directly personal
- The *Tianming* revocation mechanism is retrospective (we know a ruler has lost the mandate when they fall); *kut* can be withdrawn more immediately and is tracked through real-time outcomes (victories and defeats)
- Chinese ritual specialists (the emperor's astronomers, diviners) mediate Tian's will through celestial observation; Mongol böö mediate Tengri's will through shamanic journey — a structural difference in how divine communication flows

### Tengri and Mana (Polynesian)

Both *kut* and *mana* describe a divinely-sourced power that makes leaders effective — the charismatic dimension of political authority. Both can be depleted or augmented by behavior. Both are more dynamic than a static grant of legitimacy. The difference: Polynesian *mana* is more closely tied to social hierarchy (inherited, accumulated, vulnerable to ritual pollution) while *kut* is more directly granted by the supreme sky-being and operates at the political-historical scale rather than the social-ritual scale.
