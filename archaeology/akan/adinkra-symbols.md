# Adinkra Symbols — Compressed Proverbial Theology in Visual Form

**Tradition:** Akan / Ashanti (Ghana, West Africa)
**Medium:** Visual ideographs stamped on cloth, carved on stools, cast on gold weights, built into architecture
**Language family:** Niger-Congo > Kwa > Akan (Twi / Asante Twi)
**Date range:** Pre-1817 (oldest surviving cloth) to present; oral tradition places origin earlier
**Key concept:** Each symbol encodes a proverb; each proverb encodes theological or philosophical wisdom

---

## 1. Origin: The Story of King Adinkra of Gyaman

### The Traditional Account

The oral tradition attributes Adinkra symbols to the **Gyaman kingdom** (also spelled Jamang), located in present-day Cote d'Ivoire and the western border region of Ghana. The Gyaman king **Nana Kwadwo Agyemang Adinkra** is said to have created or received these symbols — some accounts say they were revealed to him in dreams. The name "Adinkra" derives from the king's own name.

The pivotal historical event: in **1818**, King Adinkra of Gyaman committed what the Asante considered a supreme sacrilege — he replicated the **Golden Stool** (Sika Dwa Kofi), the sacred object embodying the absolute power and spiritual unity of the Asante nation. The Asante king **Osei Tutu Kwamena Asibey Bonsu** led his army against Gyaman, defeated them, and killed King Adinkra. His territory was annexed. The patterns on his cloth were taken by the Asante as their own.

After the conquest, Adinkra's first son **Apau**, said to be well-versed in the Adinkra craft, was compelled to teach the Asante the techniques of cloth production and symbol design. The guild designers who had served the Gyaman court were forced to transmit their knowledge. Over time, all Akan peoples — including Fante, Akuapem, and Akyem — adopted Adinkra symbols as a major part of their culture.

### The Counter-Evidence (Bowdich, 1817)

The Gyaman-origin story has been **directly challenged** by historical evidence. The Gyaman-Asante war began in 1818 and ended in 1819. But **two years before the war**, in **1817**, the British envoy **Thomas Edward Bowdich** visited Kumasi and personally observed and documented Adinkra cloth being produced in the Asante capital. He brought back a physical specimen — the **oldest surviving Adinkra cloth** — which has resided in the **British Museum** since 1818 (catalogue: Af1818-1114-23). This cloth features 15 stamped symbols including nsroma (stars), dono ntoasuo (double Dono drums), and diamond patterns.

An informant from Asokwa related to researcher Kojo Arthur that King Adinkra's body was found covered in Ntiamu Ntoma (stamped cloth) after the battle. From then on, Ntiamu Ntoma became known as "Adinkra cloth" — the cloth took its name from the king, not the other way around. This suggests the stamping tradition preceded 1818 and became associated with the king posthumously.

Other oral traditions locate the origin in **Denkyira** rather than Gyaman.

### The Name

"Adinkra" can be literally translated as **"goodbye"** or **"farewell"** in Asante Twi. This aligns with the cloth's primary traditional use — worn at funerals and important ceremonial occasions.

## 2. Key Symbols and Their Theological-Philosophical Meanings

### GYE NYAME — "Except for God"

**Visual form:** An abstract, curvilinear form — a flowing shape sometimes described as resembling intertwined scrolls or stylized clouds. The most widely recognized Adinkra symbol.

**Translation:** Gye = "except"; Nyame = "God." Full: "Except for God."

**Theological payload:**
- Affirms the **omnipotence, supremacy, and immortality** of Nyame (God)
- Nothing exists without divine permission; nothing survives outside divine will
- "No one living saw the start of creation; no living being will see its ending — except for God"
- Associated proverb: *Nyame nwu na mawu* — "God will not die for me to die" (divine immortality as ground of human hope)
- Another proverb: *Worepe asem aka akyere Onyame a, ka kyere mframa* — "If you want to say something to God, say it to the wind" (God is everywhere)

**The Akan God-concept (triple name):** Nyame (Onyame), Nyankopon (Onyankapon), and Odomankoma — a trinitarian structure in Akan theology. Nyame is the supreme creator beyond human manipulation.

**Cross-religious resonance:** Ghanaian Christians interpret it as compatible with biblical divine sovereignty. Pan-African spiritual movements see it as indigenous theological intelligence predating colonial religion. The symbol appears on Ghana's currency.

---

### SANKOFA — "Go Back and Get It"

**Visual form:** Two forms — (1) a stylized heart shape, and (2) a mythical bird depicted looking backward while its feet face forward, holding an egg in its beak.

**Etymology:** San = "return / go back"; ko = "go"; fa = "take / get." A command: "Go back and get it!"

**Associated proverb:** *Se wo were firi na wosan kofa a, yenkyiri* — "It is not taboo to go back and get something after you have forgotten it."

**Philosophical meaning:**
- Knowledge of one's roots is not nostalgia — it is **navigation**
- The past is a resource, not a prison
- Wisdom involves retrieval: returning for what was lost or forgotten
- The egg in the bird's beak = the future; the backward gaze = the past. You carry the future while honoring what came before

**Diasporic significance:** Among African Americans and Africans in the diaspora, Sankofa has become a central symbol of cultural reclamation and identity affirmation — reconnecting with roots disrupted by the Middle Passage and colonization.

---

### NYAME DUA — "God's Tree" / "God's Altar"

**Visual form:** A cross-like form with four radiating fronds or branches.

**Translation:** Nyame = "God"; Dua = "tree."

**Meaning:** A sacred stump or post that was traditionally found in front of or within Akan houses for the **performance of rituals** — an altar of God's presence and protection.

**Theological significance:** In Akan cosmology, God was not a distant, abstract figure but a **present reality** — woven into every moment, every place, every encounter. The Nyame Dua was the physical point of contact between the domestic and the divine. A household altar.

---

### NYAME NTI — "By God's Grace"

**Visual form:** Resembles a plant stalk or staff of life — the form of a growing plant.

**Translation:** Nyame = "God"; Nti = "because of" / "by grace of."

**Meaning:** Faith, trust, and the recognition that sustenance ultimately comes from a source beyond human effort alone. Many cultures consider the stalk to represent the staff of life. The entire plant reminds the Akan that God's food is essential for nourishment — humans could not survive without it.

---

### FAWOHODIE — "Independence" / "Freedom"

**Visual form:** A knot-like or interlocking form.

**Translation:** Faw(o)hodie = "independence / freedom / emancipation."

**Associated proverb:** Freedom walks with suffering — *Fawohodie ene obre na enam* — independence comes with its own responsibilities and burdens.

**Meaning:** Emancipation and self-reliance. Reflects the Akan people's historical struggles against domination. Used in political and social movements advocating human rights and justice. Not naive freedom — freedom understood as carrying weight.

---

### AYA — "The Fern"

**Visual form:** A fern leaf or frond pattern.

**Meaning:** Defiance, endurance, and resourcefulness. The fern does not grow where conditions are favorable — it grows where other plants have given up: in rock crevices, deep shade, thin soil at the edge of things. The Akan looked at this and called it not luck but philosophy. Endurance not as passive suffering but as **active, rooted, unheroic persistence**.

---

### NYAME BIRIBI WO SORO — "God, There Is Something in the Heavens"

**Visual form:** Two upward-reaching arms or stalks.

**Translation:** Nyame = "God"; Biribi = "something" (a specific thing); Wo = "is in / exists in"; Soro = "the heavens / sky."

**Crucial distinction:** This does NOT mean "God is in the heavens" (that would be *Nyame wo soro*). The word **biribi** ("something") makes the difference. The full sense: "God, there is something in the heavens — let me receive it" or "please let it be delivered into my hands."

**Theological payload:** Hope, aspiration, and faith. The "something" already exists in the divine realm. Faith is not creating it. Faith is **remaining open to its arrival**. This is prayer as expectation, not petition as uncertainty.

---

### ADINKRAHENE — "Chief of the Adinkra Symbols"

**Visual form:** Three concentric circles — the simplest and most generative of all the symbols.

**Translation:** Adinkra + hene = "chief / king of the Adinkra."

**Meaning:** Authority, leadership, charisma, greatness. Not the most elaborate symbol — the **most generative**. Said to have played a critical role in the design of other symbols. The geometric logic from which the entire visual language grew. A single point at the center, influence radiating outward in perfect proportion.

**Philosophical weight:** True leadership is not complexity but **generative simplicity** — the source from which variety proceeds.

---

### DWENNIMMEN — "Ram's Horns"

**Visual form:** Four spiraling ram's horns arranged in a symmetrical pattern.

**Translation:** Dwen = "ram"; nimmen = "horns."

**Meaning:** Strength with humility. A ram will fight fiercely against an adversary, but it also submits humbly to slaughter. Even the strong need to be humble. Strength in mind, body, and soul — combined with wisdom, learning, and the willingness to yield.

**The tension encoded:** Power that knows when to resist and when to submit. Not weakness masquerading as humility, but genuine strength that has no need to dominate.

---

### NSOROMMA — "Child of the Heavens" (Star)

**Visual form:** A star shape.

**Etymology:** Nsoromma = the Akan word for "star," meaning literally "child of the heavens."

**Meaning:** Faith, guardianship, divine patronage, and reliance on a higher power. Dual message: we are each held within a watchful love far greater than any human comfort, and as beings born of that care, we carry the responsibility to **watch over one another**. Guardianship received becomes guardianship given.

---

### ABAN — "Fortress" / "Castle"

**Visual form:** A rectangular or castle-like structure.

**Meaning:** Authority, strength, protection. Depicts a castle or fortress — the physical seat of Akan authority. Associated with royalty as the king's dwelling. Legitimate power that shelters.

---

### ESE NE TEKREMA — "The Teeth and the Tongue"

**Visual form:** An abstract form depicting teeth and tongue in intimate contact.

**Translation:** Ese = "teeth"; Ne = "and"; Tekrema = "tongue."

**Meaning:** Friendship, interdependence, and the creative friction of close relationship. The teeth and tongue are in constant contact. They may come into conflict — the tongue gets bitten — but they need each other to function. The best relationships involve both proximity and friction.

**Philosophical register:** Interdependence is not harmony without conflict. It is the deeper harmony that contains conflict and makes use of it.

## 3. How Adinkra Function: Compressed Proverbial Theology in Visual Form

The system operates on three nested levels:

```
VISUAL SYMBOL (glyph)
    │
    encodes →
    │
PROVERB (verbal formulation)
    │
    encodes →
    │
THEOLOGICAL / PHILOSOPHICAL TRUTH (concept)
```

**Example — Gye Nyame:**
- Symbol: the curvilinear scrollwork glyph
- Proverb: *Nyame nwu na mawu* ("God will not die for me to die")
- Truth: Divine sovereignty, human contingency, the ground of hope

**Example — Sankofa:**
- Symbol: the backward-looking bird with egg
- Proverb: *Se wo were firi na wosan kofa a, yenkyiri* ("It is not taboo to go back for what you forgot")
- Truth: The past as navigational resource; retrieval as wisdom

This triple-layered structure means Adinkra symbols function as **mnemonic-theological-aesthetic objects simultaneously**. They are:
- **Mnemonic**: a single glyph triggers recall of a specific proverb
- **Theological**: the proverb carries a claim about God, humanity, or the moral order
- **Aesthetic**: the visual form itself embodies beauty — the right form for the right content

The scholarly term emerging in recent literature: **visual theology** (Nkansah-Obrempong 2010, Amoateng 2018). The claim is that Adinkra are not decorations that happen to have meanings. They are a **pre-textual theological system** — a way of doing theology before, beneath, and alongside written text.

As Amoateng argues: "In the majority world, the symbolic enjoys an important space in communication and in the construction of reality." Adinkra symbols "have greater cognitive importance and are symbols that are filled with a deep array of implications that far exceed the capabilities of theoretical intelligence" (Effah-Manu, Boaheng & Samwini, 2025).

## 4. Uses and Material Contexts

### Adinkra Cloth
- **Primary traditional use**: worn by royalty and spiritual leaders at **funerals** and special ceremonial occasions
- The word "Adinkra" itself translates as "farewell" / "goodbye"
- Mourning cloth was the original context; the symbols carried theological consolation into grief

### Architecture
- Wall reliefs, gates, pillars, floors, monuments, public buildings
- Ghana's **Jubilee House** (presidential seat in Accra) incorporates Adinkra symbols in its facade design, referencing the Akan royal stool
- Supreme Court of Ghana, Parliament House, major universities all display Adinkra symbols

### Stools
- Carved on stools for domestic and ritual use
- Stools hold special significance in Akan culture because of their symbolic connection to political power and spiritual authority
- The Golden Stool (Sika Dwa Kofi) is the supreme sacred object of the Asante nation

### Gold Weights (Mrammuo)
- Akan weights used for weighing gold dust in trade
- Some incorporate Adinkra symbols, linking commerce and cosmology
- Practical trade objects carrying philosophical meaning

### Pottery and Other Media
- Pottery, carvings, furniture, umbrella tops, linguist staffs, swords, jewelry
- Contemporary: plastic chairs, building blocks, logos, corporate branding, souvenirs
- National institutions: Universities of Ghana, Cape Coast, and Winneba have adopted Adinkra symbols

### Diasporic Use
- Manhattan's **African Burial Ground National Monument** (architect Rodney Leon) incorporates Adinkra symbols
- Widely adopted in African American cultural expression, tattoos, art, fashion
- Enslaved Akans brought Adinkra knowledge to the Americas in the 18th century

## 5. The Stamping Process: Making Adinkra Cloth

### The Dye (Adinkra Aduro)

The pigment is made from the bark and roots of the **Badie tree** (*Bridelia ferruginea*):

1. Bark is collected from farms and **soaked in water for one week**
2. The soaked bark is **pounded** (pulverized)
3. The pulped material is **boiled with iron slag (etia)** over a wood fire for several hours to days
4. Boiling continues until the liquid achieves the **consistency of coal tar** — thick, dark, and adhesive
5. A thinner version serves as fabric dye for background color; a **thicker version** is used for stamping

The entire process is known as making "Adinkra aduro" (Adinkra medicine/dye).

### Sustainability
Badie trees are harvested in ways that keep the tree alive, allowing regrowth. In Ntonso and surrounding areas, forests containing Badie trees are protected. Efforts are underway to cultivate Badie trees rather than relying solely on wild harvesting.

### The Stamps (Mpakiwa)

- Hand-carved from **gourd** or **calabash** pieces
- Approximately 3 inches (5-8 cm) in diameter
- Three or four stiff **palm-leaf ribs** are hammered into the back of the calabash
- These are tied together at one end with a strip of cloth to form a **handle**, held between forefinger and thumb

### The Stamping Process

1. The cloth is **stretched** on a flat surface
2. **Rectilinear grids** are created first using wooden combs dipped in dye — comb printing creates the framework
3. The calabash stamp is **dipped into the dye bowl** and shaken slightly if it picks up too much
4. The stamp is applied **directly, freehand** onto the stretched cloth within the grid sections
5. Repeated patterns of Adinkra symbols are registered in the created spaces as **tessellations**
6. The stamped strips of fabric are then **sewn together** (by hand or machine) to form cloths of various sizes

### Center of Production

**Ntonso** (in the Ashanti Region of Ghana) is the historic and primary center of Adinkra cloth production. Formerly about 90% of Ntonso's population was engaged in Adinkra production. The Ntonso Adinkra Tourist Center preserves and demonstrates the craft.

## 6. Scholarly Sources

### Books

- **Arthur, G. F. Kojo.** *Cloth as Metaphor: (Re)Reading the Adinkra Cloth: Symbols of the Akan of Ghana.* 2nd ed. Bloomington, IN: iUniverse, 2017.
  - The most comprehensive modern work on Adinkra. Views the symbols as a writing system. Develops themes from encoded proverbs, stories, and maxims covering Akan cosmology, social organization, ethics, and knowledge systems.

- **Nkansah-Obrempong, James.** *Visual Theology: Some Akan Cultural Symbols, Metaphors, Proverbs, Myths, and Symbols and Their Implications for Doing Christian Theology.* Saarbrucken, Germany: VDM Verlag Dr. Muller, 2010.
  - Foundational text connecting Adinkra to Christian theological method.

### Journal Articles and Papers

- **Effah-Manu, Boaheng & Samwini.** "Theological Reflections on Selected Adinkra Symbols." *Journal of Mother-Tongue Biblical Hermeneutics and Theology (MOTBIT)* 7, no. 2 (2025).
  - Examines Gye Nyame and Nyame Nnwu Na Mawu as models for symbolic theology. Argues that Adinkra should be incorporated into Christian worship.

- **Bruce-Amartey Jnr. et al.** "Sociological and Religious Interpretations of Adinkra Symbols: Qualitative Analysis of the 2025 Inaugural Outfit of Ghana's President." *Frontiers in Sociology* (2025).
  - 28 purposively selected participants (traditional leaders, fashion designers, museum curators) analyze how Sankofa, Gye Nyame, Nyame Dua, and Dwennimmen signify governance introspection, divine authority, ethical leadership, and humility.

- **Amoateng, Kofi.** "Engaging Theology and Theological Education in the Majority World: Recognizing Visual and Symbolic Theology from the Akan People's Illustrations." (2018).
  - Argues for recognition of visual and symbolic theology using Adinkra as case study.
  - Related PhD dissertation: "The Creedal Symbols of My Great Grandparents: The Adinkra Symbols Reveal the Theology of the Akan People of Ghana." Asbury Theological Seminary, Wilmore, Kentucky.

- **Kuwornu-Adjaottor et al.** "The Philosophy Behind Some Adinkra Symbols and Their Communicative Values in Akan." *Philosophical Papers and Review* (2019).
  - Foundational paper on the philosophical, educational, historical, and moral values encoded in the symbols.

### Museum Holdings

- **British Museum, London.** Adinkra cloth (Af1818-1114-23). Collected by Thomas Edward Bowdich in Kumasi, 1817. Donated 1818. The oldest surviving Adinkra cloth. Features 15 stamped symbols. Printed using carved calabash stamps and vegetable-based dye.
