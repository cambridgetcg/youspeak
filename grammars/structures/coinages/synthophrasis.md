# structures-005 — synthophrasis

_The together-utterance; the speech-act that holds all its relations inside itself. Named from Greek *syn* (together-with) + *phrasis* (utterance/speaking). Synthophrasis names the structural-theological principle embedded in polysynthetic languages: the sentence is a single word because the self is never truly alone; being is always being-with; isolation is not the base-state of existence. The relational constitution of the self is encoded as grammar — not asserted as theology but required as structure._

---

## The Gap

English is what linguists call an *analytic* or *isolating* language: words are relatively stable units, and grammatical relations are expressed by word-order and separate grammatical words ("I give it to him" — four words for four elements). Polysynthetic languages go to the opposite extreme: all four elements (I, give, it, him) are incorporated into one phonological word, along with aspect, evidential, and sometimes setting.

The theological import of this difference is not merely aesthetic. Synthophrasis names what polysynthetic languages encode at the structural level: **the relational constitution of beings is prior to their individuation**.

In English, "I" exists as a free-standing word before entering relation with "him" through "give." The grammar treats individuation as primary and relation as secondary. In polysynthetic languages, the word that means "I-give-to-him" does not contain a separable "I" that precedes the relation — the I is constituted by the relational-act of giving. The grammar encodes relational ontology.

This gap matters for YOUSPEAK because:

1. **The CREATOR-CREATED relationship (YOUSPEAK's foundational vocation)** is relational at its core: the CREATED only is what it is in relation to the CREATOR; the CREATOR's nature includes creating-and-sustaining-relation; neither pole of the relationship is intelligible in isolation. Synthophrasis names the structural-theological principle that makes this primary.

2. **Multiple YOUSPEAK coinages are implicitly synthophrastic**: mitakuyame (Lakota *mitákuye oyásʼiŋ*, "all my relations" / "we are all related") — the Lakota phrase is itself a synthophrastic utterance: the speaker, all beings, the relationship of relatedness, are incorporated in one breath. Synthophrasis names the structural principle that makes mitakuyame possible.

3. **The theological tradition of perichoresis** (the mutual-indwelling of the Trinitarian persons — Father, Son, Spirit in each other without dissolving) is the closest Greek-theological analog to synthophrasis. The Trinitarian persons cannot be spoken of in isolation; they are constituted by their mutual relations. Synthophrasis is what that looks like as grammar.

## The Phenomenon

### Polysynthesis in Yupik

Central Alaskan Yupik is frequently cited as the most polysynthetic language documented:

*Tuntussuqatiariyaaqhavsi* = "you're going to have to go caribou-hunting again"

One word, containing:
- *tuntu* — caribou (noun incorporated into verb)
- *-ssur* — to hunt (activity)
- *-qatar* — to be going to (prospective aspect)
- *-iariy* — it is necessary (necessitative mood)
- *-aaq* — still/again (iteration)
- *-hav* — second person (you)
- *-si* — plural (multiple addressees)

The caribou, the hunting, the future, the necessity, the repetition, the "you," the plurality — all are inside one word. You cannot separate the caribou from the hunting or the necessity from the person without destroying the word. The relations are constitutive of the utterance.

### Mohawk and Noun-Incorporation

In Mohawk (Iroquoian), the object of an action can be incorporated into the verb:

*Wash-a-r-u-hstat* = "to make oneself beautiful / to fix one's face"

The word incorporates: action (make/fix) + body-part (face) + reflexive (oneself) + completive aspect. "Face" is not a separate object the action applies to; the face is inside the verb, constituting the action from within.

Mohawk religious texts (especially those related to the Longhouse ceremonial tradition) use noun-incorporation extensively. To pray is to speak a word that contains the sacred being prayed to — the prayer and the presence are grammatically co-constitutive.

### Cree and the Animate Distinction

Algonquian languages (Cree, Ojibwe, Innu, Passamaquoddy) have a grammatically primary distinction between animate and inanimate beings. All nouns are either animate or inanimate — not as a biological category (trees are animate, pebbles are not, but a feather used in ceremony is animate and a feather not-in-ceremony is inanimate) but as an ontological-relational category: is this being in relation with the sacred-alive world, or is it not?

Polysynthetic verb-forms agree with the animacy of their arguments. When a healer names a sacred plant (animate) versus an ordinary material (inanimate), the grammar of the entire sentence shifts. The ontological status of the being spoken about is encoded in the verb's morphology — not separately declared (as in classifiers) but incorporated throughout the sentence structure.

### The Theological Principle: Relational Ontology

Synthophrasis encodes: **being is always being-with**. The word that contains its relations is not a word that has been stuffed with extra elements; it is a word that is honest about what a person, action, or situation actually is — which is: always already embedded in relation.

The English sentence "I gave it to him" treats "I," "give," "it," and "him" as four ontologically independent units entering a temporary relation. Synthophrasis says: no — "I-gave-it-to-him" is one event, one ontological unit, and the "I" in it is only what it is because of the giving and the him.

This is the grammar of perichoresis: the Father is who he is only in the relation-to-Son; the Son is who he is only in the relation-to-Father; they do not precede their mutual relation and then enter it. They are constituted by it. Synthophrasis names this as a structural principle visible in polysynthetic languages and honored by YOUSPEAK even when YOUSPEAK does not adopt polysynthetic grammar.

### YOUSPEAK and Synthophrasis

The -me suffix's ability to compound with any donor-root is itself a minimal form of synthophrasis: the compound contains both the donor-reality and the received-quality in one word.

## Form and Etymology

**synthophrasis** — from Greek *syn* (σύν, together-with; the preposition of co-presence and conjunction; the prefix of *synthesis*, *synchrony*, *symphony*, *synergy*) + *phrasis* (φράσις, utterance, way of speaking, articulated expression; from *phrazō* = to indicate, declare, point out; the root of "phrase," "paraphrase," "periphrasis").

Synthophrasis: the together-utterance; the speech-act that keeps everything together inside itself; the word that does not disperse its relations.

Phonology: sin-THOF-rah-sis (four syllables, stress on second). The *synthesis* + *phrasis* reading is transparent.
