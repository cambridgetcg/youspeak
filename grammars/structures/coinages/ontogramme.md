# structures-004 — ontogramme

_The grammatical mark that declares the ontological class of what is spoken about. Named from Greek *ontos* (being/in-respect-of-being) + *gramma* (written-mark / grammatical-unit). Ontogramme names the structural-theological operation in which the grammar of a language requires the speaker to make an ontological commitment before they can refer to anything. In classifier-languages, you cannot count or invoke a deity without the grammar first declaring what kind of being the deity is. The grammar forces the acknowledgment._

---

## The Gap

English has no grammatical mechanism that forces ontological commitment before reference. "Three gods" and "three rocks" use the same grammatical form ("three" + noun). In English, no grammatical mark distinguishes the ontological category of what you count, name, or invoke. The classifier traditions of Mayan, Chinese, Japanese, and Swahili do precisely this — and the theological implications are significant.

Ontogramme names the structural-theological operation embedded in this system: **the grammar that forces you to declare what kind of being something is before you can speak about it**.

The gap matters for YOUSPEAK because:

1. **YOUSPEAK's THEOBASIS.md and CREATOR-CREATED.md establish that the CREATOR-CREATED distinction is foundational**. But YOUSPEAK currently has no grammatical mechanism for marking ontological class. Ontogramme names the principle from traditions that do — and positions it as a goal for YOUSPEAK's determinatives/ chapter.

2. **Theological misclassification is a recurring problem in religious language**: treating the divine as merely human-great (a category error in classical theology), treating the created as divine (idolatry), treating the sacred object as mere craft (desacralization). Classifier-traditions have a structural solution: the wrong classifier is grammatically wrong, not just theologically wrong. Ontogramme names this structural-correctness.

3. **The determinatives/ chapter in grammars/ is an existing YOUSPEAK organ that operates on the same principle**: semantic class-markers that accompany words to declare their domain. Ontogramme is the named theological principle that determinatives/ is building toward.

## The Phenomenon

### Yucatec Maya Classifiers

Yucatec Maya uses numeral classifiers that must precede any counting act. The classifier declares the ontological category of what is being counted before the numeral is given. Different classifiers apply to:

- Animate beings (including deities, humans, animals, sacred plants)
- Inanimate objects (stones, buildings, manufactured items)
- Ritual-sacred objects (items that may be physically inanimate but are ritually-animate)
- Round objects (fruit, seeds)
- Long-thin objects (arrows, paths)

When a Maya shaman counts sacred objects for a ritual, the grammar requires the animate-ritual classifier. Using the inanimate classifier would be grammatically wrong AND theologically wrong — the error is one act, not two. The grammar enforces the theological acknowledgment.

This means: to speak about the divine in Yucatec Maya is to engage in an act of ontological acknowledgment with every sentence. The classifier system ensures you cannot address a deity while simultaneously treating them as inanimate. The grammar catches the theological error.

### Japanese Counters and Divine-Pillars

Classical Japanese uses counter-words (助数詞, *josūshi*) for different categories of entity. Among the most theologically striking: the counter-word for deities (kami) is 柱 (*hashira*, pillar/post).

神一柱 (*kami hito-hashira*): "one kami" — literally "one deity-pillar." The god is counted as a pillar. This is not merely a grammatical convention; it encodes a cosmological-theology: the kami stands in the world as a pillar-support, a structural-sustaining presence. The counter-word is a compressed cosmological claim. To count a kami correctly is to declare their ontological function.

Using the wrong counter for a deity in Japanese is not merely grammatically awkward; it fails to acknowledge the deity's ontological character. Ontogramme is the structural principle naming why this matters.

### Classical Chinese Measure-Words

Classical and Modern Chinese uses 量词 (*liàngcí*, measure-words / classifiers) for all counting and many nominal references. The system distinguishes:

- 一條河 (one-*tiáo*-river): *tiáo* for long-flexible things
- 一座廟 (one-*zuò*-temple): *zuò* for large-imposing-stable structures
- 一位神 (one-*wèi*-deity): *wèi* is the classifier for respected persons (teachers, elders, officials, and deities)

To address a deity with the wrong classifier is to misclassify their ontological dignity. The *wèi* classifier declares: "this is a being deserving of respect-acknowledgment." Using a lower-register classifier for a deity is both grammatical error and theological disrespect. The grammar enforces a theology of appropriate-acknowledgment.

### Swahili and Bantu Noun-Classes

Swahili (and Bantu languages generally) have a noun-class system where every noun belongs to a class, and verbs, adjectives, and pronouns must agree with that class. The classes encode rough ontological categories: animate-human class, animate-non-human-large class, plants class, abstract-concepts class, small-objects class.

When Swahili is used in Christian liturgy (as it is widely in East African churches), the noun-class of sacred objects and divine persons is carried through all the agreement-marking in the sentence. Every verb in a sentence about God must agree with God's noun-class. The theology is structurally encoded in every sentence by the agreement-system. You cannot speak about God without every grammatical element in the sentence declaring what class God belongs to.

### The Theological Principle

Ontogramme encodes the idea that **ontological acknowledgment precedes and enables reference**. You cannot speak about a being correctly without first declaring what kind of being it is. The classifier is not an afterthought; it is the prior condition of accurate speech about the being.

Applied to YOUSPEAK and its worship-vocation: every time YOUSPEAK names or invokes the DIVINE, the question of the DIVINE's ontological class is already embedded in the act of naming. Ontogramme names this structural principle — and points toward YOUSPEAK's determinatives/ organ as the place where this principle is being operationalized.

### YOUSPEAK's Determinatives/ Organ

The grammars/determinatives/ chapter is a YOUSPEAK organ operating on the same structural logic as classifier-systems: determinatives are semantic class-markers that accompany words to declare their domain. An ontogramme is a determinative operating specifically at the level of ontological-being-category.

The principle Ontogramme names is the theological aspiration of the determinatives/ chapter: to give YOUSPEAK a mechanism by which the ontological class of divine persons, sacred objects, and created beings is grammatically declared when they are spoken about — not left to context, not assumed, but structurally required.

## Form and Etymology

**ontogramme** — from Greek *ontos* (ὄντος, genitive of *ōn* = being; the root of *ontology*, *onto-*, the study of being itself) + *gramma* (γράμμα, written-mark, grammatical-unit, inscription).

Parallel to *stratigramme* (stacking-written-mark) and *programme* (written-in-advance). The *-gramme* suffix names the grammatical-unit aspect; the *onto-* prefix names what this grammatical unit is doing: marking being-category.

Phonology: on-toh-GRAM (three syllables, stress on final following the *programme* analog). The *onto-* prefix is immediately familiar from *ontology*.
