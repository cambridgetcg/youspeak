# Anastrophance — the head-inversion mechanism

_The diplosemic operation of swapping the head and modifier positions in a two-morpheme compound to yield a correlated-but-distinct partner word._

---

## The mechanism

Greek-and-Latin compounding rules (inherited by YOUSPEAK) treat the **second** morpheme of a compound as the semantic head. The first morpheme modifies. When the two are swapped, the head relocates, and the compound's meaning shifts from "A-quality-X" to "X-quality-A."

```
  M₁M₂  →  the X of A       (M₂ is head; M₁ modifies)
  M₂M₁  →  the A of X       (M₁ is head; M₂ modifies)
```

The two readings are structurally correlated (same morphemes, same domain) and distinct (different head → different thing-named). When both coinages are canonical, they form a **diplosemic pair** — a single morphological unit that the reader can invert to access the sibling-meaning.

## Engineering rules

1. **Both morphemes must be independently substantival-capable.** If only one can serve as head (the other being strictly adjectival or adverbial), inversion produces ungrammatical output.
2. **The two meanings must name two genuinely distinct things in the domain.** If the head-swap produces a mere synonym or a meaningless phrase, the pair is not diplosemic — it is a false duality.
3. **Both coinages must pass the individual 5-axis assessment.** A pair where one member fails canon-threshold is not a diplosemic pair; it is a canon-word and a refine-word in the same morphological family.
4. **The pair must pass the 3-axis correlation assessment** (structural-cohesion, depth-yield, register-stability).

## The canonical exemplar

**doxakallos ↔ kallodoxa** (both Greek: δόξα + κάλλος):

- *doxakallos* (M₁=doxa, M₂=kallos, head=kallos) — "the beauty-quality of glory." The beauty-in-GoD as ontological attribute. **Canon 8.25.**
- *kallodoxa* (M₁=kallos, M₂=doxa, head=doxa) — "the glory-quality of beauty." The specific glory-bearing-ness of beauty-when-it-is-divine. **Canon 8.00** (forged 2026-04-23).

The two together: doxakallos names the beauty-side of divine glory; kallodoxa names the glory-side of divine beauty. Holding both: divine glory and divine beauty are two attributes of the single divine Being, and each is ordered toward the other — glory is specifically beautiful, beauty is specifically glory-bearing. The head-inversion pair expresses what the prose would require a paragraph to articulate.

## Partial exemplar — phainō-family

**orthophanes ↔ kallophanes** is a partial Anastrophance exemplar:

- *orthophanes* (M₁=orthos "right", M₂=phanēs "appearing"): the rightness-made-visible.
- *kallophanes* (M₁=kallos "beauty", M₂=phanēs "appearing"): the beauty-made-visible.

These are not a pure Anastrophance pair because the M₂ is shared (phanēs) and only M₁ varies. This is a different mechanism — *companion-on-a-common-suffix* — which deserves its own name (tentatively: **homocaudance**, "same-tail-quality," for future formalization). True Anastrophance requires the full head-inversion of two morphemes that both appear in both words.

## Use cases

### Dual-delivery

When both coinages are canonical and the reader knows both, a sentence using one silently-invokes the other. "Her gesture was a doxakallos of simple attention" — the reader aware of kallodoxa hears: *and therefore the attention is receiving the glory-quality of beauty*. The sentence is doing double-work.

### Encryption (weak)

A sentence deploying only one member of the pair reads straightforwardly to a surface-reader; a depth-reader checks for the inverted partner. If the text's claim requires the inverted reading to be consistent, the inverted reading is the encrypted depth.

### Joke

An Anastrophance joke exploits the unexpected inversion: "Kallodoxa is not doxakallos, but when you are not the kind of person who knows, you cannot tell." The sentence is a joke-at-the-expense-of-the-uninitiated, which is the characteristic shape of technical-humor.

## Failure modes

- **Meaningless inversion** — the morphemes do not both support substantival head use. Example: attempting to invert *orthophanes* to *phanestorthos* produces nothing; *-phanes* cannot become the modifier with *ortho-* as head under YOUSPEAK morphology.
- **Synonymy collapse** — the inversion yields the same meaning. Example: *theokallos* ("divine beauty") vs *kallotheo*- ... which still reads "divine beauty" because *theos* is never a modifier in this register. Not a diplosemic pair.
- **Accidental recall** — the inversion lands on an existing word with an unrelated meaning. Requires forge-discipline to verify no collision before the pair is declared.

## Failure-mode remediation

When attempting an Anastrophance forge and one of these failure modes obtains, the forger has three paths:

1. **Reject the pair** — the candidate is not suited to Anastrophance; try another mechanism (Enkalyptance, Parallaxance) for the same domain.
2. **Alter the modifier** — try different M₁ with the same M₂-head to find a head-inversion-capable sibling.
3. **Alter the head** — try different M₂ with the same M₁-modifier; different phonology may admit inversion.

## Correlation validation for the canonical exemplar

**doxakallos ↔ kallodoxa** correlation assessment (per manifesto's 3-axis rubric):

- **C1 structural-cohesion: 9/10** — head-inversion of identical morphemes; morphologically maximally cohesive.

- **C3 register-stability: 9/10** — the pair reads stable in liturgical, theological, and technical register equally.

**Correlation mean: 8.67.** Pair canonized as the first exemplar in the organ.
