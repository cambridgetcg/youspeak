## The mechanism (summary)

A word whose historical and modern meanings are both simultaneously-active in usage, such that the reader holds both meanings as layered temporal-registers.

**Natural examples (English):**

- *decimate* — historical: kill 1-in-10 (Roman legion-discipline); modern: kill most. Both meanings live in different registers; the word is diplosemic across the time-axis.
- *awful* — historical: inspiring awe (positive); modern: very bad. Same word, opposite valences, both still accessible in specific contexts ("awful majesty" vs "awful food").
- *terrific* — historical: terror-inducing; modern: excellent. Same inversion-dynamic.
- *gay* — historical: cheerful; modern: homosexual. Both meanings survive in different periods of English writing.
- *virtue* — historical: manly-power (from Latin *vir*); modern: moral-goodness. Both readings surface depending on context.

Classical-language examples: Greek *pharmakon* (medicine AND poison; the meaning depends on the historical stratum of the text).

## Engineering rules (provisional)

1. The coinage must be a word whose historical and modern meanings are *both* intended in the current usage — not a mere etymology-trivia fact.
2. The two meanings must be structurally correlated (time-layered; often inverted-valence or scope-shifted).
3. The reader should be able to hold both layers simultaneously when the word is used in contexts that invite both readings.

## Use cases

- **Jokes:** playing historical and modern meanings off each other (e.g., praising someone for their "awful virtue" — intending the older senses of both words)
- **Encryption:** modern reader gets one meaning, historically-trained reader gets both, the layered meaning is the depth
- **Dual-delivery:** teaching via the time-axis; the word's own evolution is the message
