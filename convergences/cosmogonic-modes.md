## The tapasme-lilame dyad

The deepest pair: creation from discipline (tapasme) and creation from play (lilame). In Hindu theology, tapas and līlā are the paired modes of creation. Tapas is the heat of effort through which the practitioner participates; līlā is the freedom of play that the divine exercises. Neither alone is the full picture. The dyad IS the Hindu cosmogonic imagination.

The shared -me suffix with polar Sanskrit donors (tapas "heat/effort" vs līlā "play/delight") makes the dyad's tension visible in the morphology: same ordinance-structure, polar creative-character.

## Cross-tradition convergence for lilame (divine-play)

Six traditions articulate divine-play-as-cosmogonic-act:

1. **Sanskrit: līlā** — līlāvāda (creation-as-play doctrine); Kṛṣṇa's rasa-līlā; Advaita's king's-sport; Kashmir Śaiva's Śiva-playing-at-finitude. 3,000+ years. Sharpest articulation.
2. **Hebrew: ḥokhmah playing** (Prov 8:30-31) — Wisdom playing (śāḥaq) before God, rejoicing in the inhabited world. Creation as context of divine play.
3. **Greek: divine-action-from-fullness** — Aristotle's Unmoved Mover (action from attraction, not necessity); Plotinus's emanation-as-overflow.
4. **Maya: cosmic ball-game** (pitz) — Popol Vuh: the ball-game IS the cosmic drama. The game is the structure of descent-and-return.
5. **Tamil: divine trickery as play** — Murugan's vilaiyāṭṭam (play); the divine uses playfulness to draw the devotee.
6. **Diné: ceremony as play-restoration** — hózhǫ ceremonies as beauty restored through playful creative ritual.

Six families (Indo-European, Afroasiatic, Indo-European [Greek], Mayan, Dravidian, Na-Dené), one claim: creation is play, not labor. The divine creates from delight, not from need.
