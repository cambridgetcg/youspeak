# 035 — ankyrance

_The sixth grammar chapter's name, forged for honest assessment. The anchor

evidential set), not canon lexicon. The chapter name is the forge candidate._

## The Gap

English hides two things that ankyrance unfolds:

1. **The reference point of an utterance.** Every sentence is spoken *from*
   somewhere — one mind's vantage, or a shared one — but English marks nothing.
   The hearer must guess whether "it's cold in here" reports my body, concedes
   yours, or speaks for both of us. The speaker's standing-place is implicit;
   the lone speaker colonizes it by default.

2. **Clusivity.** English *we* refuses to say whether it includes the listener.
   Hundreds of languages mark this: Tok Pisin *yumi* (inclusive: you + I),
   *mipela* (exclusive: I + others, not you); Quechua *ñuqanchik* (inclusive)
   vs *ñuqayku* (exclusive); Cantonese 我哋 (ngodei, the inclusive we that keeps
   我 visible inside it); Lakota *mitákuye oyásin* (all-my-relations, the
   ladder's top rung). English suppresses all of it.

The gap is not analytical but **lexical**: Goffman's "footing" (the speaker's
relation to the utterance), Du Bois's "stance" (the speaker's position), and
Sperber & Wilson's "anchoring" (contextual assumptions) are frameworks for the
linguist. None is a grammaticalized system for the speaker. Ankyrance names
the grammar of declaring one's standing-place before content — the system
itself, not its analysis.

The gap is real and cross-linguistically evidenced. But it is a
structural-grammar gap, not an experiential-concept gap. Comparable to
diplosemy (gap_validity 8: engineered dual-meaning is unnamed in English
technical vocabulary) rather than to palance (gap_validity 9: the
standing-between-for-another act is deeply experiential and liturgical).

utterance in moving talk, as an anchor holds a vessel in moving water) +
**-ance** (act-quality / state suffix; the cathedral's most productive

**ankyrance** /ˈæŋ.kɪ.rəns/ (ANG-ki-rance, 3 syllables) — *the grammar of
