# 003 — Metastrophesis

_The turning-kept. Plato's metastrophē (the soul's full about-face in Republic VII) nominalized with -sis. The permanent reorientation that persists after the encounter._

## The Gap

After orthophanes and doxalgia, the beholder is not merely moved and then restored. Something remains — a permanent reorientation. The axis of the mind has been shifted. This altered-root-state persists. It is not a memory of the encounter; it is the beholder now living along a different line.

*Metanoia* (Christian theology) comes close but has been narrowed to moral repentance. *Conversion* is too religious-institutional. *Transformation* is too generic. *Epiphany* names the arrival, not the persistence. What is missing: the word for the *standing condition of having-been-turned-and-remaining-turned*.

## The Forge

**metastrophē** (μεταστροφή — Plato's term in Republic VII for the soul's complete turning-about toward the Good, a full reorientation of the axis of attention) + **-sis** (abstract-noun suffix, indicating the state or condition resulting from the verb).

**metastrophesis:** the state of having been completely turned and now remaining in that turned orientation. The persistent axis-shift. The turned-and-kept condition.

## Archaeology considered

The Tocharian archaeology (`archaeology/tocharian/crossing.md`) offered **klautk-** — turning, transforming, converting — as a distinctive donor. The compound *klautkoterēsis* (klautk + Greek tērēsis, keeping) was considered. Rejected for memorability: the mixed-register compound imposes too much learning cost for a word that must be used often. The concept Tocharian contributed — conversion-as-persistent-kept-state — is retained in the word's sense. The archaeology fed the meaning without shaping the morphology.

This is a methodological note: **archaeology feeds the concept; the best donor for the form may be different from the best donor for the meaning.** YOUSPEAK 1.0 should formalize this distinction.

## Companion

Fourth term in the Liturgy community:

1. **orthophanes** — the beauty appears
2. **anagnoristasis** — recognition stands still
3. **doxalgia** — the ache arrives
4. **metastrophesis** — the beholder remains turned

The encounter becomes a sentence that is also a grammar of transformation.

## Near-neighbours

- **metanoia** — mind-changing, repentance; narrowed by Christian usage to moral conversion; lacks aesthetic register
- **conversion** — institutional, religious or ideological; lacks the aesthetic specificity
- **transformation** — too generic, not necessarily persistent, not necessarily axial
- **epiphany** — names the moment, not the state
- **awakening** — Buddhist-inflected; names the content, not the standing condition
- **reorientation** — too clinical, lacks the depth of axis-shift

## Trial sentences

1. After the encounter he was in **metastrophesis** — not changed in one thing, but living along a different axis.
2. The community could tell which of them had been to the mountain: a certain **metastrophesis** showed in how they stood.
3. **Metastrophesis** is not a memory of beholding; it is the shape the beholder now takes.

## Honest assessment

Gap narrower than doxalgia's or orthophanes's. Some overlap with metanoia, which has been colonized by Christian usage but never fully occupied the aesthetic-beholding case. Memorability moderate. Verdict `refine` at 6.70.
