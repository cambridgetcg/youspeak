# 028 — hallance

_The act of praise-that-shines-greatness-out — the luminous declaration that something is so exceeding-great that not declaring it is impossible. Named from Hebrew **hallel** (הָלַל, root h-l-l = to shine, to be radiant; the dominant OT praise-word; source of hallelujah) and confirmed by five cross-tradition parallels: Greek doxazō (glory-made-visible), Sanskrit stuti (formal excellence-declaration), Arabic tasbih (incomparability-praised), Yoruba oríkì (praise-poetry as character-revelation). Hallance names the worship-act that is always already testimony: when it is done well, the praised one is made present in the praising._

---

## The Gap

English in the vicinity:

- **"Praise"** — covers it but broadly; "praise" can be mild (praising a child for tying their shoes); hallance is specifically the explosive-acknowledgment that greatness is so exceeding it overflows into declaration
- **"Glorify"** — closer; but glorify can be neutral (to make famous, to add glory to); hallance is specifically the worshipper's act of shining out what they have recognized
- **"Worship"** — too broad; encompasses all acts of orientation-to-GoD; hallance is specifically the luminous-declaration subset
- **"Laud"** — archaic and thin; no theology in it
- **"Acclaim"** — secular; no sacred-recognition quality

Hallance names the specific act: **the praise that shines the recognized greatness out, the declaration that makes invisible glory visible, the overflow of recognized brightness into vocalized testimony**. When the Psalmist says "Hallelujah" (hallelu-Yah = shine-the-LORD!), they are performing hallance. When the angels in Isaiah's vision cry "Holy, holy, holy!" they are performing hallance. When the Muslim says "Subhanallah" at encountering something sublime, they are performing hallance. All instances of praise in which the praiser cannot NOT declare it — that is hallance.

---

## The Phenomenon

### Hebrew: hallel — the shine-root of praise

The root **h-l-l** means to shine, to be radiant, to be clear. The verb *hālal* = to praise radiantly, to declare someone's brilliance, to shine-their-light-out.

The Psalter is structured around this root. Psalms 113-118 are called the **Great Hallel** — the luminous-praise sequence sung at Passover (Jesus and the disciples sang the Hallel at the Last Supper before going to Gethsemane — Matthew 26:30). Psalm 150 is the climactic hallance-instruction: "Praise [hallalu] him in his sanctuary... praise him with trumpet... with harp... let everything that has breath praise [hallalu] the LORD."

**Hallelujah** (הַלְלוּיָהּ) = *hallelu* (plural imperative of hālal = shine! declare the radiance!) + *Yah* (the compressed divine name). "Shine the LORD! Let the LORD's brightness be declared!" It is the most universal worship-word across Christian traditions, appearing in Hebrew, Aramaic, Greek, Latin, and every vernacular tradition. Hallelujah is the pure hallance-exclamation: the overflow of recognized divine brightness into vocalized declaration.

The key insight: praise in the hallal-tradition is not polite commendation — it is the testimony of one who has seen something bright and cannot stay silent about it. The brightness demands declaration. Hallance is that demand-met.

### Greek: doxazō — making-glory-visible

Greek approaches from **doxa** (δόξα — glory, brightness, reputation, the radiance of genuine excellence). The verb **doxazō** = to cause-doxa to be seen, to make the glory visible.

Matthew 5:16: *"Let your light shine before others that they may see your good works and glorify [doxazō] your Father."* The structure: you live well → light shines → others see → they make the Father's glory visible (doxazō). The Father's glory is already there; hallance is the act of making it seeable by naming it.

John 17 is Jesus's extended doxazō: "I have glorified [doxaza] you on earth by completing the work you gave me." Jesus's mission is described as doxa-work — making the Father's glory visible in the world. The ministry of the incarnation is hallance.

### Arabic: tasbih — praise as incomparability-acknowledgment

**Tasbih** (تَسْبِيح) comes from **subhāna** (سُبْحَانَ — "glory be to" / "how far above all imperfection/limitation"). *Subhānallāh* (Glory be to God) is the first tasbih — the spontaneous overflow of recognition that God exceeds every category.

After each of the five daily prayers, Muslim tradition prescribes: 33 repetitions of *Subhānallāh* (tasbih — praise-of-incomparability), 33 of *Alhamdulillāh* (tahmid — praise-of-all-good), 33 of *Allāhu Akbar* (takbir — praise-of-greatness). This praise-sequence is hallance structured into daily practice: the overflow of divine-recognition shaped into disciplined form.

The word *tasbih* also names the prayer-beads used to count these repetitions — the material form of organized hallance.

### Sanskrit: stuti — the formal excellence-declaration

**Stuti** (स्तुति) is from root *stu* = to praise. It names the formal praise-poetry tradition in Sanskrit — the systematic declaration of a deity's names and attributes through hymn.

The greatest stotras are systematic hallance: the *Lalitā Sahasranāma* (1000 Names of the Divine Mother) names every attribute in sequence; each name is a hallance-act, declaring one facet of divine excellence. The Vishnu Sahasranāma does the same for Vishnu. The cumulative stuti: every attribute seen, every name declared, the entire being praised through exhaustive naming. This is hallance in its most structured form — the determination to leave nothing of the divine's greatness unsaid.

### The theological core

Hallance encodes: **praise is testimony to recognized brightness**. When the praiser hallances, they are not adding glory to the praised — the glory is already there. They are becoming transparent to it, making it visible in their praise-act. 

The Psalms understand this: "The heavens declare [sefer] the glory of God" (Psalm 19:1) — the heavens perform hallance by being what they are; their existence is luminous declaration. The worshipper performing hallance participates in what creation is always already doing.

## Form and Etymology

**hallance** — from Hebrew *hallel* (הָלַל: h-l-l = to shine, to be radiant, to praise-brilliantly; the dominant OT praise-root, source of hallelujah) + *-ance* (act-quality suffix).

The h-l-l root is preserved in the first syllable; -ance nominalizes. Hallance = the act-quality of shining-out recognized greatness. Two syllables: HAL-lance (stress on first; the double-l echo of the Hebrew h-l-l root is preserved in the -ll- of hallance; the word sounds like what it means — it has energy in the first syllable).

## Liturgical triad: qorvance + epiclance + hallance

Three worship-acts, each necessary:
1. **Qorvance** — creature draws near through what is brought
2. **Epiclance** — creature calls the divine to draw near in return  
3. **Hallance** — the encounter declared radiantly out; the recognized greatness made visible

The triad describes every act of worship that reaches completion: approach (qorvance), mutual-encounter (epiclance), and declaration (hallance). The Psalm-structure: the Psalmist comes before GoD (qorvance), calls on GoD to be present (epiclance), and fills the temple with praise (hallance). The Eucharist: the bread brought forward (qorvance), the Spirit called down (epiclance), the congregation's "Holy, holy, holy!" (hallance).
