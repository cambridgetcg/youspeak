# 163 — mathemame

## The Gap

English and its neighbours handle "learning" (the process), "knowledge" (the state), "mastery" (the achievement-register), "wisdom" (the matured-knowing-state), "discipline" (the practice). What none compactly handles is **the settled state of having-received what discipline has yielded, registered as cosmic-gift rather than personal achievement**.

The gap has a precise shape — five features that must combine:

1. **Discipline-mediated**: the content arrives not in a single event but as the yield of long sustained practice
2. **Received, not produced**: even when the practitioner has worked hard, the content registers as given
3. **Abiding state**: the perfect-aspect register — "I am in the state of having-learned" — names a permanent possession, not a momentary acquisition
4. **Tradition-implicated**: the discipline itself is often received from teachers and lineages; the chain of receiving is structurally constitutive
5. **Worship-grade**: the yield is held as cosmic-ordinance, not personal credit

Candidates ruled out:

- "Mastery" — emphasizes achievement; misses the receiving-register
- "Expertise" — cognitive-state; lacks the gift-structure
- "Wisdom" — too general; not specifically discipline-yielded
- "Skill" — practical, lacks the worship-register
- "Knowledge" — too generic; could be momentary information
- "Learning" — the process; not the yielded state
- "Tradition-share" — phrasal; awkward; not quite the experience-name

Ten traditions confirmed the gap: Greek mathēma + memathēka, Hindu śāstra, Confucian xué, Sufi taʿallum + silsila, Buddhist śikṣā, Jewish talmud Torah, Egyptian sebau, Christian discipulus + paradosis, mathematical theorem-as-gift, artisanal form-as-received. See `convergences/learned-truth-as-received.md`.

## The Forge

**mathēma** (μάθημα):

Greek noun for "that-which-is-learned," from μανθάνω (*manthanō*), "to learn." PIE root *\*mendh-* "to direct one's mind toward something" (cognate with Sanskrit *medhā* "wisdom"; Latin *mens* "mind" via related root).

The Greek verbal family articulates the discipline-yields-content structure with grammatical precision:

- *manthanō* — the act of learning (verb)
- *emathon* — punctual learning-event (aorist)
- *memathēka* — abiding state of having-learned (perfect; THE theological nerve)
- *mathēsis* — the act-as-named
- *mathēma* — the content of having-learned (result-noun)
- *mathētēs* — the disciple-learner

The perfect-aspect *memathēka* is structurally identical to *heurēka*: both encode the abiding-state-of-having-received. The Greek vocabulary itself testifies that disciplined learning is reception-with-persistence.

Plato narrowed *mathēmata* to the quadrivium (arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, harmonics). The narrowing dominated subsequent technical usage, but the broader pre-Platonic sense (anything-learnable-by-discipline) remained available. The cathedral receives both: mathemame is paradigmatically about mathematical-style learned-truth but extends to any discipline that yields receivable content (contemplation, art, craft, doctrine, music).

The patristic period developed *mathēsis* as the Christian discipleship-register: the disciple receives (*paralambanō*) and hands-on (*paradidōmi*); the chain of *mathēsis* is the church's tradition. Augustine's *discere* (learn) paired with *invenire* (find) maps to the cathedral's mathemame paired with eurekame.

**me** (𒈨):

Sumerian *me*: divine-ordinance qualities held in the divine keep and bestowed as cosmic gift. The cathedral's standard suffix for received-ordinance. Twelfth member of the -me family.

The compound **mathemame** = the learned-truth understood as received-ordinance: the abiding state of having-received what discipline has yielded; the content of having-learned, registered as cosmic-gift.

**Pronunciation**: /ˈmæθ.ə.mə.meɪ/ — MATH-uh-muh-meh (4 syllables; English pronunciation of "mathema" preserved; -me as light suffix). Primary stress on first syllable (following the English "mathema-" pattern in "mathematics"); secondary stress on the fourth syllable. This stress-pattern differs from eurekame/yonedame/noemame (which have second-syllable primary stress); the variation is appropriate because the English donor-pronunciation places first-syllable stress.

**Morphological analysis**:

- *math-* (Greek manthanō root; the learning-stem)
- *-ema-* (Greek result-noun ending; preserving the mathēma form)
- *-me* (Sumerian received-ordinance suffix; YOUSPEAK standard cosmic-gift marker)

The compound's etymological grace: "math" is universally recognizable in English (cognate-chain mathematics → mathēma → manthanō → PIE mendh-), giving the word a built-in mnemonic anchor. The reader who knows "math" already has the discipline-stem; the -me suffix supplies the worship-register; the compound is learnable in one explanation.

## Distinction from Neighbours

**vs. eurekame**: Eurekame is the *event of joy* when evidence makes truth conclusively visible (discovery-arc threshold). Mathemame is the *abiding state* of disciplined receiving (cuts across arcs as a separate axis). Eurekame is moment-event; mathemame is settled-state. Both are received-ordinance, different temporal structures.

**vs. noemame**: Noemame is the *entry-event* into a realm-region of meaning (noetic-entry arc threshold; momentary). Mathemame is the *abiding state* of having-received-what-discipline-yields (state, not event). A single mathemame-state may contain many noemame-events (every theorem in a mathematician's repertoire was once a noemame; the mathematician's relation to the field is mathemame). Noemame is door; mathemame is the room one has long inhabited.

**vs. yonedame**: Yonedame is the *abiding state of being-fully-known-through-relation* (relational-ontological). Mathemame is the *abiding state of disciplined-receiving* (epistemic-disciplinary). Both are state-pole concepts but on different axes: yonedame is relational; mathemame is disciplinary. A person can be in yonedame with their spouse and mathemame with their craft; the two states co-exist in different domains of the same life.

**vs. kimme**: Kimme is *attention as received-ordinance* — the capacity-of-attending received as gift. Mathemame is *learned-truth as received-ordinance* — the yield-of-attending-over-time received as gift. Kimme is capacity; mathemame is its long-cultivated fruit. The two are sequential in worship-register: kimme makes the discipline possible; mathemame is what the discipline yields.

**vs. shemme**: Shemme is *receptive-hearing as ordinance* — the specific auditory-Shema register of being-shaped-by-what-is-heard. Mathemame is sensorily-neutral and discipline-general. Shemme is one mode in which mathemame can be cultivated (hearing-the-tradition); mathemame is the broader fruit of any disciplined receiving.

**vs. doxomme**: Doxomme is *thanksgiving-as-received-ordinance* — the affective response to having-received. Mathemame is the content-state of having-received. Doxomme is the natural response from inside mathemame: one is in mathemame (settled-discipline-yield) and from that state doxomme arises (the thanksgiving for what was given).

**vs. "expertise" / "mastery" / "wisdom" / "tradition"**: Expertise is the cognitive-state of competence (lacks gift-structure). Mastery emphasizes achievement (misses the receiving-register). Wisdom is too general (not specifically discipline-yielded). Tradition is a body of content held by a community (not the individual's state). Each English candidate names a partial-feature; mathemame compact-names the full combination.

## Liturgy — mathemame in use

The cathedral's three worship-arcs (discovery, relational-ontological, noetic-entry) are event-or-state-structured at their threshold-pole. Mathemame complements them as a **discipline-yielded** state that may contain many threshold-events of all three arcs across a long practice.

A mathematician's relation to their field is mathemame. The field contains hundreds of theorems each of which was once a noemame-event and many of which were eurekame-events. The mathematician's state of being-able-to-work-fluently-in-the-field is mathemame — the long-accumulated discipline-yield.

A contemplative's relation to their tradition is mathemame. The tradition contains many specific teachings each of which was once received as a noemame; the contemplative's matured inhabitation of the whole is mathemame.

An artisan's relation to their craft is mathemame. The craft contains specific techniques and forms each of which was once received as a learned-piece; the artisan's mature state of being-a-vessel-for-the-craft is mathemame.

A worshipper's relation to the cathedral's vocabulary is mathemame in the making. Each canonical word is a noemame-target; the worshipper who has inhabited many of them across years is approaching mathemame with respect to YOUSPEAK itself.

mathemame is what the cathedral's vocabulary, faithfully studied, eventually yields: a settled state of receiving-the-discipline-as-cosmic-gift, in which subsequent encounters with new words and new convergences are received-from-within the long study's frame.
