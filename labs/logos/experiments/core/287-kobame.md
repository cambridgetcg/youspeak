## The Gap — found-divine-space, the sacred-space concept the cathedral has not named

The cathedral has words touching sacred space:

- **skename** (Core 7.90): the tension between the built dwelling and the not-built — the booth that was not built, Peter's arrest at the Transfiguration. skename names the TENSION, the arrest, the desire to fix what moves. It does not name the positive concept: the space that IS already divine, found not built.
- **asemе** (Core 7.80): àṣẹ — the activating sacred power that makes invocation effective. asemе names the POWER that fills a space; the space itself (the terreiro) is built and then axé arrives. asemе is about what FILLS; kobame is about what the space already IS before anything fills it.
- **fulgurme** (Core 7.85): the world as inscribed for reading — the Etruscan cosmic-divination concept. fulgurme names the world as READABLE; kobame names a space as already-DIVINE. Reading is an activity; found-divine-ness is a condition.
- **myesime** (Core 8.07): the threshold-crossing that changes who you are. myesime names the CROSSING; kobame names the SPACE being crossed INTO. The threshold exists because the space on the other side is already sacred — kobame is the condition that makes myesime possible.

None of these names what kobame names: **sacred space that is found, not built — the condition of a space being already divine, already open, already inviting entrance, without human construction, consecration, or maintenance.**

The Basque cave-shrine tradition is the most theologically developed instance of this concept in any tradition the cathedral has excavated. In Basque cosmology:

- The earth (Ama Lur) is a living body, ontologically not metaphorically.
- Caves are entrances to the earth's interior — passages into the divine body.
- The cave IS Mari's body opened. The mountain is her body; the cave is the passage into her interiority.
- **No built temples.** The absence of built temples is the theology, not a deficiency. You do not build a house for a being whose body is the mountain.
- The sacred space simply IS — it was there before humans and will be there after. The human relationship is approach, protocol, and departure — not construction, maintenance, or ownership.

This is architecturally radical and unique in the cathedral. Every other sacred-space tradition presupposes human activity:

- Akan: the sacred grove is maintained by human action.
- Zoroastrian: the fire temple is built and tended.
- Australian Aboriginal: sacred sites are maintained through ceremony (though the sacredness itself precedes the ceremony — this is the closest parallel, see below).
- Shinto: the shrine is built around the shintai (the natural feature that is the kami's body).
- Hindu: temples are built AT tirthas (the tirtha precedes the temple, but the temple is still built).

Basque alone built NOTHING. The cave alone suffices. The sacred space was never built and never needed to be.

The gap is real and unfilled. No canon word names found-divine-space as a positive concept.

## The Donor — Basque koba

**Basque *koba*** (cave) — from *kobazulo* (*koba* + *zulo* [hole]). In Basque cosmology, the cave is not a geological feature but a theological one: the entrance to the earth's interior, the passage into Ama Lur's (Mother Earth's) body.

The theological structure is precise and unprecedented in the cathedral:

1. **The earth is a living body** — ontologically, not metaphorically. The earth (Lurra/Ama Lur) is the mother of the sun (Ekhi) and the moon (Ilazki). They enter and exit her body daily.

2. **Caves are entrances to the earth's interior** — not symbols of depth but actual passages into the divine body. The cave IS the goddess's body opened.

3. **Mari does not live IN the cave; the cave IS Mari's body** — the mountain is her body and the cave is the passage into her interiority. The cave-shrine is architecturally radical: the sacred space is not constructed but discovered. It already exists. You enter it; you do not create it.

4. **No built temples** — the absence of built temples is the theology, not a deficiency. You do not build a house for a being whose body is the mountain. The cave-shrine tradition is the one case in the cathedral where the sacred space requires NO human construction, NO consecration, NO maintenance.

5. **Weather originates underground** — storms, lightning, thunder, and wind are produced beneath the earth's crust and emerge through caves and chasms. Mari controls weather because she inhabits the place where weather is made. The cave is not merely a dwelling; it is the generative interior of the cosmos.

6. **The cave protocol** encodes the ethics of approach: enter and leave the same way (no deception), do not sit in Mari's presence (maintain alertness), do not take anything (nothing belongs to you here), never turn your back on Mari (face truth directly). The protocol is ethical training encoded as spatial behavior — but the sacredness of the space does not depend on the protocol being followed. The space is already divine before you arrive and remains divine after you leave.

The cave-shrine network spans the Basque Country with NO built structures: Anboto (the primary dwelling, 1,330m), Aketegi (with beds of gold), Aralar (where the church was built OVER the cave, incorporating the cave-mouth into the altar wall — the Christian building acknowledges the pre-Christian sacred space it could not replace), Murumendi, Txindoki, Supelegor, Balzola, and dozens more. The seven-year migration pattern (Mari moving between caves) prevents any single community from owning the sacred — the divine itinerates across the already-divine network.

**Basque** (Euskara) is a language isolate — the only surviving pre-Indo-European language in Western Europe. It has NO demonstrated relationship to Indo-European, Afro-Asiatic, Sino-Tibetan, Niger-Congo, or any other language family. This makes Basque the cathedral's second isolate donor (after Sumerian), and the Basque-Sumerian compound an unprecedented double-isolate pathway: no Indo-European, no Afro-Asiatic, no Sino-Tibetan component. Two unrelated isolates from different millennia and different continents.

The Basque cave-shrine tradition is documented through:
- José Miguel de Barandiarán's *Mitología Vasca* (1960/1979) and *Diccionario Ilustrado de Mitología Vasca* (1972) — the indispensable ethnographic fieldwork
- Julio Caro Baroja's *Los Vascos* (1949/1971) — the comparative-historical placement
- The *Eusko-Folklore* series (1921–1975) — decades of field-collected oral traditions
- Resurrección María de Azkue's *Euskalerriaren Yakintza* (1935–1947) — independent corroboration
- The cave-shrine network itself — physically extant sites across the Basque Country, many still visited

**Sumerian *me*** (𒈨) — divine-ordinance, received-gift-quality. The established YOUSPEAK morpheme. Its contribution: the already-divine character of the found space is received-ordinance, not human projection. The cave's sacredness is not something humans assign to nature (animism, nature mysticism, romantic projection). It is a structural feature of how the earth IS — the world is structured such that some spaces are already open, already divine, already inviting. Humans discover this; they do not produce it. The -me marks the found-divine character as given: humans did not invent the fact that caves are sacred; they discovered it.

**Compound**: koba + me → **kobame** (KO-ba-may; 3 syllables)

Pronunciation: /ko.ˈba.meɪ/ — KO-ba-may. The Basque *koba* is intuitive (sounds like English "cove" — a natural opening in rock). The *-me* suffix follows the established YOUSPEAK pattern. The compound is three syllables, highly manageable.

## Cross-tradition grounding — found-divine-space

1. **Basque: koba** — the central concept; the cave as Mari's body opened; the mountain IS the goddess; no built temples, no consecration, no maintenance; the most theologically developed instance of found-divine-space in any excavated tradition; the cave-shrine network spans the Basque Country with no built structures

2. **Australian Aboriginal: sacred sites as Ancestral-Being's body** — the site IS the ancestor (not a memorial, not a symbol); ceremony maintains the relationship but does not CREATE the sacredness; the sacredness precedes all human activity; the deepest convergence with Basque: both are ontological, not metaphorical — the land does not represent the divine, it IS the divine; distinction: Aboriginal system is radically spatial (every rock, waterhole, ridge is a specific ancestor's body — the whole landscape is sacred); Basque system is concentrated (specific caves are divine, the landscape between is not ontologically identical with the goddess — the koba is the opened interior, not the whole mountain's surface)

3. **Shinto: shintai (神体 — "god-body")** — a natural feature (mountain, waterfall, ancient tree, unusual rock formation) that IS the kami's body, not the kami's house; the shrine (jinja) is built AROUND the shintai but the shintai itself is not built — it is found and recognized; distinction: Shinto eventually built shrines around the shintai (the shrine marks and protects the already-divine); Basque built NOTHING — the cave alone suffices; the Shinto trajectory is from found-divine to built-around-found-divine; the Basque trajectory stays at found-divine

4. **Hindu: tirtha (तीर्थ — sacred crossing-place)** — a riverbank, a confluence of rivers, a mountain pass; the place is already sacred because the divine already crossed there; temples are built AT tirthas but the tirtha precedes the temple; distinction: tirtha is about CROSSING (the sacredness is in the transition, the ford, the pass); kobame is about DWELLING (the sacredness is in the interior, the cave as body opened); tirtha is liminal; kobame is interior

5. **Navajo: the four sacred mountains** — Dibé Nitsaa, Sisnaajiní, Tsoodził, Dook'o'oosłííd; fixed by the Holy People as the boundaries of Diné homeland; the mountains are already sacred, already divine, not built or consecrated by humans; the hogan (dwelling) is built but the mountains are found; distinction: Navajo sacred mountains define a people's BOUNDARIES (cosmic-cartographic — the mountains frame the world the people live within); kobame is about the INTERIOR (the cave as body opened — the passage INTO, not the boundary AROUND)

6. **Greek: the chasm (χάσμα — chasma) as natural mouth of the underworld** — the cleft in the earth at Delphi (the Omphalos), at Eleusis, at the nekuomanteia; the chasm is already open, already a passage, before any temple is built above it; the oracle sits BESIDE the chasm, not ON it; distinction: Greek chasm is about PASSAGE to the underworld (vertical descent, the way down); Basque koba is about DWELLING as divine body (interior, the goddess's body opened — not a passage TO somewhere else but a passage INTO the divine that IS here)

Six traditions converge on the concept of sacred space that is found, not built — already divine before any human construction. The Basque cave-shrine is the most theologically radical: NO built structure at all, the cave IS the divine body. The Australian Aboriginal tradition is the deepest convergence (both ontological, both pre-human sacredness) but is distributed across the whole landscape. Basque alone concentrates found-divine-space in the specific opened-interior: the cave.

## Why this is Core, not Specialized

The archaeology file estimated Core 7.60–7.85 and recommended kobame as "the strongest Basque coinage candidate." I score it Core for four reasons:

1. **The concept is everyday-relevant, not domain-specific.** The experience of entering a space that is already sacred — a forest clearing, a mountain summit, a sea cave, a desert canyon — is not confined to Basque cave religion. It is a universal human experience: the encounter with a place whose sacredness is recognized, not created. The cathedral needs a word for the general pattern, not only the Basque instance.

2. **The found-vs-built distinction is architecturally fundamental.** Every other sacred-space concept in the cathedral presupposes building. kobame names the alternative: the space that was never built and never needed to be. This is not a minor variation; it is a fundamentally different theology of sacred space. The cathedral cannot have a complete sacred-space vocabulary without the found-divine-space pole.

3. **The 6-tradition convergence is strong.** Basque, Australian Aboriginal, Shinto, Hindu, Navajo, and Greek all recognize found-divine-space in some form. The POLYPHONE foundation mandates etymological breadth; 6 traditions from 5 language families (isolate, Australian, Japonic, Indo-Aryan, Athabaskan, Hellenic) converging on one concept is strong evidence of Core relevance.

4. **The double-isolate pathway is unprecedented.** Basque + Sumerian is the cathedral's first compound from two language isolates — no Indo-European, no Afro-Asiatic, no Sino-Tibetan component. This is the maximum possible family diversity for a two-morpheme compound. The POLYPHONE foundation is satisfied at the highest level.

The concept clears threshold (7.88 ≥ 7.5) with margin. Core is honest.

- **eternalme** (Core 7.85) — time-as-received-temporal-ordinance. eternalme is about TIME; kobame is about SPACE. The two are complementary dimensions of the received cosmic order.
- **nyamame** (Core 8.00) — vital-force-as-cosmic-given (the world's ambient aliveness). nyamame pervades all things generally; kobame names specific spaces where the pervasion is already concentrated, already open, already inviting. nyamame is the diffuse field; kobame is the opened interior.

## Retirement conditions

This word would be superseded if:
- A more precise donor compound were found that captures the found-divine-space concept with greater etymological clarity
- The concept were absorbed into a broader sacred-space word that includes found-divine-space as one of its sub-types
- The Basque cave theology were shown to be entirely a modern reconstruction (the evidence problem is real — no pre-Christian Basque texts exist — but the cave-shrine network itself is physically extant and the oral traditions are independently corroborated by Barandiarán and Azkue)
