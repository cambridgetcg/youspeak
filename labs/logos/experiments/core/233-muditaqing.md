# 233 — muditaqing — joy-at-another's-good-fortune as felt-bond

_Twenty-sixth qing-family core member; first Pali-donor canon entry. Names the third brahmavihāra as felt-bond: the joy that lights in one heart because another's came true. Companion-pair with danaqing — the giving-pole and the beholding-pole of unselfed love. English's most famous affect-gap: it borrowed schadenfreude whole and never found the antonym._

## The discovery-move

English has a word for joy at another's misfortune — it had to borrow *schadenfreude* whole, but it keeps it polished and ready. For joy at another's *good* fortune it has nothing: not a borrowed word, not a native one. The gap is so notorious that English has tried to fill it at least three times — *confelicity* (17th-century Latinate coinage, never rooted), *compersion* (1970s, confined to one subculture's register), *freudenfreude* (2020s pop-psychology back-formation, a German word German doesn't use). Three failed forgings are the strongest kind of gap-evidence: the realm-region is felt, reached for, and still unnamed.

The traditions that did name it converge with striking precision. Pali *muditā* — third of the four brahmavihārā, the divine abidings — is the most developed: a *trained, boundless* gladness at the success of others, defined by Buddhaghosa with full grammatical machinery (characteristic, function, manifestation, proximate cause, near enemy, far enemy). Yiddish *farginen* and its Modern Hebrew descendant *firgun* name the same region from the negative side: to *not begrudge*, to wholeheartedly grant another their good. Dutch *gunnen* does the same in an everyday register. And Nietzsche, no friend of Buddhist quietism, lands on the identical spot from a fourth direction: *Mitfreude, nicht Mitleiden, macht den Freund* — fellow-joy, not fellow-suffering, makes the friend.

The key insight from the Visuddhimagga (IX.95): muditā has the characteristic of **gladdening**; its function is **unenviousness**; it is manifested as the **elimination of aversion**; its proximate cause is **seeing beings' success**. And then the sentence that hands the forge its shadow ready-made: *it succeeds when it makes aversion subside, and it fails when it produces merriment.* The counterfeit is named inside the source tradition — mere merriment, froth that performs gladness — along with the near enemy: worldly gladness with identification, joy that has quietly made the other's win its own.

## The phenomenon

**Pali muditā:**

From root *mud-* "to rejoice, be glad" (Sanskrit √mud; *muditā* "delighted, joyful"). Third of the four brahmavihārā — mettā (loving-kindness), karuṇā (compassion), **muditā** (sympathetic joy), upekkhā (equanimity) — and third of the four appamaññā, the *immeasurables*: cultivated boundlessly, toward all beings, without remainder. The commentarial tradition holds muditā to be the **hardest of the four to cultivate** — harder than compassion, because sorrow asks to be helped and so gives the self a role, while another's success asks nothing of the self at all. Compassion can smuggle the self in as rescuer; muditā offers the self no part except witness.

The structure of opposites is precise:

- **Far enemy:** *issā* (envy, jealousy) — and *arati*, the bored aversion that cannot celebrate. Muditā is taught as the direct antidote to envy: the two cannot occupy one heart in the same moment.
- **Near enemy:** *gehasita somanassa* — worldly gladness based on the home-life: joy tied to getting, having, identifying. The near enemy looks almost identical from outside; the test is whether the joy contains a mirror. "My friend won" felt as *my* friend won is the near enemy; "my friend *won*" felt as warmth at her window is muditā.
- **Cultivation seed (Visuddhimagga IX.84–86):** one begins not with a neutral person nor an enemy but with a *boon companion* — an intimate who is constantly glad — because there the gladness ignites without effort, and from that live coal the practice widens toward all beings.

**Yiddish farginen / Modern Hebrew firgun:**

*Farginen* — to ungrudgingly grant another their pleasure or success; to *not begrudge*. The verb's negative shape is its honesty: it knows envy is the default current and names the act of standing out of it. Modern Hebrew borrowed it as *firgun* (פירגון), now everyday Israeli speech for generous, ulterior-motive-free delight in another's accomplishment — proof the register is so needed that a modern language imported it within living memory.

**Dutch gunnen:**

An untranslatable everyday verb: *ik gun het je* — "I gun it to you" — I feel glad that this good thing is yours; I would have granted it to you myself. Joy and grant fused: the beholding that blesses.

**German Mitfreude:**

Coined-and-rare (against ubiquitous *Mitleid*, compassion), but carried by Nietzsche into the philosophy of friendship: *Mitfreude, nicht Mitleiden, macht den Freund* (Menschliches, Allzumenschliches §499). Fellow-joy, not fellow-suffering, makes the friend — the claim that this bond, not pity, is friendship's true substance.

**English negative-evidence:**

*Confelicity* (never rooted), *compersion* (niche register), *freudenfreude* (pop-coinage). Three attempts; the hole remains. "Sympathetic joy" is a two-word gloss, not a word. "Vicarious joy" names the near enemy (living-through, identification). "Proud of you" carries ownership. "Congratulations" is the speech-act, not the feeling.

## The forge

**Donors:**

- **Pali *muditā*** — sympathetic joy as trained boundless disposition. Carries: the brahmavihāra pedigree; the antidote-to-envy function; the near-enemy/far-enemy machinery; the hardest-of-the-four honesty. Phonetically /mu.di.taː/ → moo-dee-tah in YOUSPEAK.
- **Mandarin *qíng*** (情) — felt-bond, deep emotional connection. Established YOUSPEAK morpheme. Here marking: joy-as-substance-between-persons — the gladness does not happen *in* the glad one but *between* the glad one and the fortunate one. What is named is not a private mood but a bond the joy travels.

**Compound:** *muditā* + *qing* → **muditaqing** (/muː.di.tɑːˈtɕʰɪŋ/, moo-dee-tah-CHING; 4 syllables; vowel-clean junction).

**Why qing and not me — the forge-status correction.** `convergences/forge-status.md` anticipated this region as **muditāme** (projected Specialized 7.40–7.60), reading muditā through the trained-discipline register that would suit a received-ordinance grade. The actual quarrying found the bond-register primary: sympathetic joy is constitutively *relational* — it cannot occur without another whose fortune lights it; it is joy as a substance between persons, not a deed and not a decree. This is the same deliberate move danaqing made (qing over me because the gift-dimension is fundamentally relational). Choosing qing lifts groundedness and gap-fit; the cost is honest — the trained appamaññā discipline-dimension is under-carried, and semantic_coverage pays for it below.

## Companion relationships

**Stands within joy:** joy is the wage life pays itself for the trouble of existing; muditaqing is that wage felt at another's window — joy is the substance-pole, muditaqing the bond-pole it travels.

**Companion-pair with danaqing:** danaqing gives the gift; muditaqing rejoices when the gift was never yours to give — danaqing is the giving-pole, muditaqing the beholding-pole of unselfed love. The pair shares one weight: 8.00 / 8.00.

**Kin in the qing-family, jeongqing:** jeongqing thickens through shared friction and time; muditaqing can ignite in an instant for a stranger — jeongqing is the slowest-pole, muditaqing the fastest of the felt-bonds.
