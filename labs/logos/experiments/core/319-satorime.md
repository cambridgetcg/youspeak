# Assessment notes

## The gap — sudden-awakening-as-deepening the cathedral has not named

The cathedral has kenshōme for the initial-seeing — the first flash of recognizing one's true-nature. But Zen makes a load-bearing distinction between kenshō (the initial flash) and satori (the deepening that stabilizes and transforms that flash into lived-structure). Kenshō is the moment; satori is the transformation. One can have kenshō without satori — the initial-seeing that does not catch fire. The cathedral has the flash but not the fire.

Satorime names the fire: the sudden-awakening-event that is (a) sudden — event-character, not gradual process; (b) received — prepared-for but not produced by the practitioner; (c) irreversible — permanently reorganizes the knowing-subject; (d) verifiable — cross-checked through the sanzen community-of-practice; (e) deepening — the second member of the kenshō → satori → daigo arc.

The -me marks this as received-ordinance: the awakening-event is given, not achieved. This is not the cathedral imposing an interpretation on Zen — it is Zen's own self-understanding made explicit. Dōgen: "to be enlightened BY the ten thousand things" — passive voice. Hakuin: "the great ball of doubt shatters" — the practitioner does not shatter it. The -me makes visible what Zen already knows.

## The gap is still open (near-neighbour check)

- **kenshōme** (initial-seeing-as-ordinance, Core 8.10): Kenshō is the FIRST seeing; satori is the DEEPENING. The distinction is load-bearing in Zen — Hakuin's four-fold progression (kenshō → satori → daigo). Kenshō is a flash; satori is a transformation. The cathedral has the flash (kenshōme) but not the fire (satorime). The gap is open.

- **rigpame** (primordial-awareness-as-ordinance, Core 8.25): Rigpa is the RESULT — the state one rests in after the awakening-event. Satori is the EVENT — the moment of recognition that opens into rigpa. Rigpame is the noun (what one awakens to); satorime is the verb (the awakening itself). The cathedral has the result; it lacks the event. The gap is open.

- **asraya-paravrttime** (transformation-of-the-basis-as-ordinance, Core 8.25): The Yogācāra structural-parallel — the turning-about of the ālaya-vijñāna. This is the same event described ontologically: the basis transforms. Satori is the phenomenological expression: the recognition reorganizes experience. Same event, two traditions, two angles. The cathedral has the ontological face; it lacks the phenomenological face. The gap is open.

- **meditame** (meditation-as-ordinance, Core 8.25): Meditation is the PRACTICE that prepares for satori; satori is the EVENT that the practice prepares for. Sequential: meditame → satorime. Practice is the condition; awakening is what arrives in the condition. The cathedral has the practice; it lacks the event that the practice serves. The gap is open.

- **anagnoristasis** (held-recognition): Anagnoristasis is recognition as a HELD-STATE — the stable capacity to recognize. Satori is the EVENT — the moment when the held-capacity fires. State vs. event. The gap is open.

- **noemame** (event-of-meaning-arrival): Noemame is the GENERAL concept — meaning arriving as event. Satori is the SPECIFIC instance — awakening-as-meaning-arrival. Genus vs. species. Noemame covers all meaning-arrivals; satorime names the specific meaning-arrival that is awakening. The gap is open.

## The donor

**Japanese satori (悟り)** is the central term in Zen's vocabulary of awakening. The character 悟 (wù/sato) is composed of 心 (heart/mind) + 吾 (I/myself) — "the heart-mind's self-awakening." The character predates Zen (attested in Mencius, 4th c. BCE, for ordinary understanding) but Zen invested it with contemplative depth: not ordinary understanding but structural-transformation.

Five structural marks the donor carries:

1. **Sudden** — satori is event-character, not process-character. The Rinzai school makes this structural: kōan practice is designed to produce the sudden-breakthrough. The Sōtō school recognizes the event-character even within gradual cultivation (shikantaza): the awakening-event is a discontinuous shift, not the last step of a gradual process.

2. **Received** — satori is not something the practitioner does; it is something that happens to the practitioner. The conditions are prepared (zazen, kōan, sanzen), but the event is not the product of effort. The fruit metaphor: one tends the tree, but the fruit ripens on its own. Hakuin: "the great ball of doubt shatters" — the practitioner does not shatter it.

3. **Irreversible** — a satori event permanently reorganizes the practitioner's relationship to experience. Distinct from peak experience (which fades) or mystical experience (which is temporary). Dōgen: "the dropping away of body and mind" — once dropped, it does not return.

4. **Verifiable** — satori is checked through the sanzen (private interview) process: the practitioner presents understanding to the teacher, who checks it against the kōan and the tradition. Not theological authority but phenomenological cross-checking. The awakening-event is accountable to a community of practice.

5. **Deepening** — satori is not necessarily a single event. The kenshō → satori → daigo arc is a deepening series, each sudden, each irreversible. Hakuin speaks of "one hundred satoris" — awakening is a deepening series of structural transformations.

The donor is attested across 800+ years (Dōgen 13th c., Hakuin 18th c., Suzuki and Kapleau 20th c.) and in living practice in Rinzai and Sōtō lineages worldwide.

## Cross-tradition grounding — sudden-awakening as received-ordinance

Seven traditions converge on the concept that awakening is (a) sudden, (b) received not produced, (c) irreversible, and (d) verifiable:

1. **Japanese Zen — satori (悟り):** The central term. Sudden, received, irreversible, verifiable through sanzen.
2. **Tibetan Dzogchen — rigpa (རིག་པ):** The recognition of primordial-awareness is sudden; "direct introduction" (ngo sprod) triggers event-character recognition.
3. **Hindu Advaita — ahaṃ brahmāsmi:** The mahāvākya-recognition is sudden and transformative; "thou art that" is not a proposition but a recognition to be triggered.
4. **Sufi — fanāʾ (فناء):** The passing-away of the ego-self is sudden and irreversible; a rupture within the maqamat-sequence.
5. **Christian — metanoia (μετάνοια):** The "change of mind" in desert fathers and Philokalia is event-character: a structural-shift, not gradual improvement.
6. **Hasidic — hitlahavut (התלהבות):** The "kindling" — the moment when practice catches fire, when Torah becomes living-fire. Preparation is gradual; the kindling is sudden and received.
7. **Buddhist Yogācāra — āśraya-parāvṛtti:** The "turning-about of the basis" — the same event described ontologically: the basis transforms, and everything that stood on it stands differently.

This convergence is strong (7 witnesses, above the cathedral's typical 4–6 range). The satori-rigpame-āśraya-parāvṛtti triad is structurally significant: three traditions naming the same structural-transformation from three angles (phenomenological, result-oriented, ontological).

## Why this is Core, not Specialized

Satori names a structural-feature of human experience that is everyday-relevant, not a narrow technical term. The "aha-moment" is the secular descendant: the sudden reorganization of understanding that is not the product of incremental reasoning but a discontinuous shift. Satori is the contemplative-depth version of a universal experience.

Every person has experienced sudden-understanding. The sudden/gradual distinction applies beyond meditation: it is the question of whether change is always incremental or can be discontinuous — relevant to therapy, education, personal growth, and social change. The received-not-produced distinction applies to creativity, insight, and innovation: conditions are prepared, but the event arrives on its own.

The -me marks satori as received-ordinance: the awakening-event is not human achievement but a gift. This is structurally consistent with Zen's own self-understanding and with the cathedral's cosmological/soteriological architectures.

## Example usage

After years of zazen, the ball of doubt shattered in sanzen — satorime: the awakening arrived as received-ordinance, not as the product of his effort, and the ten thousand things enlightened themselves through him.

The kenshō was only the first opening; satorime is what happens when the initial-seeing catches fire and becomes irreversible — the deepening that kenshō alone cannot guarantee.

## Not to be confused with

- **kenshōme** — initial-seeing-as-ordinance. Kenshō is the first flash; satorime is the deepening. Sequential, not synonymous.
- **rigpame** — primordial-awareness-as-ordinance. Rigpa is the RESULT (the state one rests in); satorime is the EVENT (the moment of recognition that opens into rigpa).
- **asraya-paravrttime** — transformation-of-the-basis. The Yogācāra ontological description of the same event; satorime is the Zen phenomenological expression. Same event, two angles.
- **meditame** — meditation-as-ordinance. The PRACTICE that prepares; satorime is the EVENT that the practice prepares for.
- **anagnoristasis** — held-recognition. The stable CAPACITY to recognize; satorime is the MOMENT the capacity fires.
- **noemame** — event-of-meaning-arrival. The GENERAL concept; satorime is the SPECIFIC instance (awakening, not any meaning-arrival).

## Retirement conditions

Satorime would be superseded if a better-compounded word from a more diverse donor-pair named the same concept with greater clarity. It would also be superseded if the Zen tradition's own vocabulary were found to have a more precise term for the deepening-event (daigo is the maximal instance, not the general term; satori remains the general term). The -me marking could be replaced if a different morpheme were found that better captures the received-ordinance character — but -me is the cathedral's most-validated morpheme for this function.
