None of these names what tiṇaime names: **the irreducible ontological correspondence between natural ecology and interior emotional state, where each phase of experience has its proper landscape.**

The Tamil tiṇai system is the most structurally explicit landscape-emotion correspondence in any tradition the cathedral has excavated. In the Tolkāppiyam (the oldest surviving Tamil grammar, c. 3rd century BCE–3rd century CE):

1. **Five akaṃ (interior/love) tiṇais**, each binding a landscape to an emotional phase:
   - **Kuṟiñci**: mountain/hills, cold season, night → clandestine union (lovers meeting); presiding deity: Murugan; flora: kuṟiñci flower (strobilanthes)
   - **Mullai**: forest/pastoral, rainy season, dusk → patient waiting (beloved waits for hero's return); deity: Māyōn (Viṣṇu); flora: jasmine
   - **Marutam**: river-valley/paddy fields, all seasons, morning → infidelity/quarrel (hero strays, heroine reproaches); deity: Indra; flora: marutam tree
   - **Neytal**: coast/shore, evening → longing and anxiety (separation, waiting at shore); deity: Varuṇa; flora: blue water lily
   - **Pālai**: wasteland/desert, noon/hot summer → separation by distance (dangerous journey); deity: Korravai (war goddess); flora: pālai tree

2. **Two puṟam (exterior/public) tiṇais**: Vākai (victory/heroism) and Kāñci (transience/mortality).

3. **The grammar makes this a RULE**: the poet who sings of Kuṟiñci (mountain union) MUST use mountain imagery — birds, flowers, season, time-of-day. Using coastal imagery would categorically violate the grammar. This is not a convention but an ontological claim: the mountain IS clandestine joy; the coast IS longing. The correspondence is structural, not figurative.

The theological claim is precise: **emotion is ecologically grounded.** Your interior state is not separable from the landscape you inhabit. The poet who sings of mountain love must use mountain imagery because using coastal imagery would name the WRONG emotion. Feeling is not abstract; it lives in specific places, times, weathers, flowers. The five tiṇais together name the full arc of eros: union → waiting → infidelity/repair → longing → separation. This is not a moral hierarchy (union = good, separation = bad). Each phase has its own dignity, beauty, and presiding deity. The grammar does not privilege the pleasant phases.

The gap is real and unfilled. No canon word names landscape-emotion-phase correspondence as an ontological structure.

## The Donor — Tamil tiṇai

**Tamil *tiṇai*** (திணை) — the landscape-emotion-phase unit of Sangam poetics. Tamil is the oldest continuously documented Dravidian language, with literary documentation from c. 300 BCE. The Dravidian language family is non-Indo-European, spoken by ~230+ million people across South India, Sri Lanka, and diaspora. Tamil developed its metaphysical vocabulary independently of Sanskrit, though in deep conversation with it.

The Sangam corpus (c. 300 BCE–300 CE) — eight anthologies (Eṭṭuttokai) and ten idylls (Pattupattu), roughly 2,381 poems by 473+ named poets — encodes the tiṇai system. The Tolkāppiyam (தொல்காப்பியம், "ancient composition"), attributed to Tolkāppiyar, is the oldest surviving Tamil grammar. Its Poruḷ ("subject matter") book codifies the tiṇai system as grammatical rule, not as convention.

The theological structure is precise:

- **Emotion is ecologically grounded.** The poet who sings of mountain-love must use mountain imagery. The correspondence is ontological, not metaphorical: the mountain IS clandestine joy; the coast IS longing.
- **The five tiṇais together name the full arc of eros.** Union → waiting → infidelity/repair → longing → separation. Not a moral hierarchy — each phase has its own dignity, beauty, and presiding deity.
- **The grammar encodes a cosmology of emotion.** Because the tiṇais are described as universal (every love follows these phases), the system is a claim about how the world is made. The correspondence between emotional phase and landscape is ontological statement.
- **The tiṇai is not individual psychology.** The emotion belongs to the landscape, not to the person. The person enters the mountain and the mountain's tiṇai takes hold. This is the reverse of pathetic fallacy: the landscape's character generates the emotional phase.

The Bhakti revolution (6th–9th centuries CE) read the tiṇai system as a map of the soul's relationship with God. Āṇṭāḷ performs the Kuṟiñci tiṇai at the devotional register: the "clandestine union" landscape becomes the devotee's ecstatic meeting with the divine. The tiṇai system proved sufficiently abstract to hold Hindu, Christian, and Islamic theology — the formal apparatus of Tamil devotional poetry is a technology that can run on different theological fuel.

**Sumerian *me*** (𒈨) — divine-ordinance, received-gift-quality. The established YOUSPEAK morpheme. Its contribution: the landscape-emotion phase-correspondence is received-ordinance — not a human invention or poetic convention but a structural feature of how the world IS. The -me marks the correspondence as given: the cosmos is made such that landscape and emotion are phase-locked. The tiṇai is a reading of the ontological structure of the world, not an arbitrary cultural encoding. The landscape's emotional character is received, not projected.

**Compound**: tiṇai + me → **tiṇaime** (ti-NAI-may; 3 syllables)

Pronunciation: /tɪ.ˈnaɪ.meɪ/ — ti-NAI-may. The Tamil *tiṇai* is recognizable (திணை, the retroflex ṇ preserved in the romanization). The *-me* suffix follows the established YOUSPEAK pattern. The compound is three syllables, manageable. The retroflex ṇ is phonotactically distinctive but learnable (the ASCII form tinaime is fully pronounceable for those unfamiliar with the diacritic).

## Cross-tradition grounding — landscape-emotion-phase correspondence

1. **Tamil: tiṇai** — the central concept; five akaṃ tiṇais binding landscape to emotional phase, plus two puṟam tiṇais; codified as grammatical rule in the Tolkāppiyam; the most structurally explicit landscape-emotion correspondence in any tradition excavated; the tiṇai is a landscape-emotion unit with structural components (landscape + season + time + emotion + deity + flora); the correspondence is ontological, not metaphorical; ~2,300 years of continuous literary tradition

2. **Australian Aboriginal: country-as-sentient** — the land carries emotion; the Dreaming Ancestor IS the landscape, not a being IN the landscape; ceremony maintains the relationship between people and the emotive land; less phase-specific than tiṇai but equally ontological: the land is not neutral space but an animated, emotive field; distinction: Aboriginal country is about ANIMACY (the land is alive), Tamil tiṇai is about PHASE-STRUCTURE (the land carries specific emotional phases with structural components)

3. **Japanese: fūdo / Watsuji's milieu-theory** — Watsuji Tetsurō (1886–1960) proposed fūdo (風土, "climate/milieu") as the unity of climate-culture-person: the environment is not separate from the person who inhabits it; cold climates produce certain cultural-personality structures, tropical others; distinction: fūdo is about climate-CULTURE unity at the civilizational scale; tiṇai is about landscape-EMOTION correspondence at the phase-specific scale; fūdo is macro, tiṇai is micro-phase

4. **German: Stimmung / Romantic nature-mood** — the Stimmung (mood/attunement) tradition from German Romanticism: the landscape participates in the emotional state; the autumn forest is not "like" melancholy — it IS melancholy's proper habitat; Heidegger's Stimmung as basic attunement (Befindlichkeit); distinction: Stimmung is about general attunement/mood; tiṇai specifies a PHASE-SYSTEM with named units, structural components, and grammatical rules; Stimmung lacks the phase-structure

The convergence cluster has four traditions from four language families (Dravidian, Australian Aboriginal [non-Pama-Nyungan and Pama-Nyungan], Japonic, Germanic IE). The Tamil instance is the most theologically and grammatically developed — the only one that makes the correspondence into a GRAMMATICAL RULE with named structural units.

## Why this is Core, not Specialized

The archaeology file estimated Specialized 7.45–7.65 (possibly Core). I score it Core for four reasons:

1. **The concept is everyday-relevant, not domain-specific.** The experience of entering a landscape and being taken by its emotional character is universal. You walk into a forest and feel waiting; you reach the coast and feel longing; you climb the mountain and feel clandestine joy. The tiṇai system names a structure that every person experiences but no living language has a word for. This is not archaic poetic theory; it is the everyday ontology of inhabiting a landscape.

2. **The landscape-emotion ontology is fundamental.** The claim that emotion is ecologically grounded — that interior states are not separable from exterior ecology — is a foundational ontological claim. It distinguishes YOUSPEAK's understanding of emotion from both modern psychology (emotion as internal subjective state) and ancient humoral theory (emotion as bodily fluid balance). tiṇaime names emotion-as-ecological-phase. This is a fundamental ontology, not a minor variation.

3. **The 4-tradition convergence is strong.** Tamil, Aboriginal, Japanese, and German traditions all attest the landscape-emotion correspondence, at four different structural levels (grammatical-phase-structured, animate, civilizational, attunement-mode). The POLYPHONE foundation is satisfied: four language families converge on one structure.

4. **The Dravidian source-tradition significance.** Tamil is the oldest continuously documented Dravidian literary tradition. The tiṇai system is Tamil's unique contribution to world theology — no other tradition makes landscape-emotion correspondence into a grammatical rule. Opening the Dravidian family in the cathedral gives access to a theological vocabulary that developed independently of Sanskrit for over two millennia.

The concept clears threshold (8.03 ≥ 7.5) with strong margin. Core is honest.
