## The Gap — sacred-as-ontological-intensity, the dimension the cathedral has reserved but not yet named

- **nyamame** (Core 8.00): animating-energy-as-cosmic-given — the world's aliveness. The ambient vitality that flows through all things.

## The Donor — Lakota wakȟáŋ

**Lakota *wakȟáŋ*** — sacred, mysterious, powerful, incomprehensible — all simultaneously. Not a moral category primarily but an ontological one: that which exceeds human comprehension and ordinary natural power. Both awe-inducing and dangerous. Anything sufficiently powerful or mysterious participates in wakan.

Lakota is Siouan (macro-Siouan) — entirely distinct from the Algonquian family (Ojibwe, Cree), the Iroquoian family (Haudenosaunee), and Na-Dené (Navajo/Diné). The Očhéthi Šakówiŋ (Seven Council Fires) comprises the Lakota (Teton), Dakota (Santee), and Nakota (Yankton/Yanktonai) divisions. ~100,000+ speakers today. The theological vocabulary (wakan, mitákuye oyásʼiŋ, chanunpa, the Seven Rites) is shared across the seven fires with dialect variation.

The theological structure is precise:

- **Wakan is an ontological category, not a moral one.** Things are wakan because they exceed comprehension, not because they are morally good. A thunderstorm is wakan. An albino animal is wakan. A dream is wakan. None of these are moral judgments. The sacred is not what is good; the sacred is what is more-than-you-can-hold.

- **Wakan is distributed as a continuum, not binary.** There is no clean sacred/profane divide. Everything participates in the sacred to some degree; some things participate supremely. This is not pantheism (everything equally divine) but intensive sacramentality — everything participating in the sacred to varying degrees. The most wakan things (Wakan Tanka, Thunderbirds, the chanunpa, vision-quest experiences) are the most concentrated.

- **Wakan is both awe-inducing and dangerous.** The sacred is not safe. Things that are wakan can help or harm. The Thunderbirds bring rain (life) and lightning (death). The vision-quest opens the sacred but can overwhelm. Wakan does not mean comfort; it means more-than-ordinary-power that requires right relationship.

- **Wakan Tanka is the Great Mystery, not the Great Spirit.** Black Elk resisted "Great Spirit" as translation because it collapsed the ineffable into the familiar. Wakan Tanka is a unity-of-sacred-powers (seven aspects), not a simple monotheistic god. The phrase points toward a reality that exceeds naming, not a personality that can be addressed as a man.

- **The sacred is not separated from nature but intensified within it.** This is a thoroughgoing sacramental ontology. The world is not neutral matter with sacred zones injected from outside. The world is wakan at varying intensities. The stone is wakan (Inyan, the primordial rock). The earth is wakan (Makȟá). The wind is wakan (Tȟatéyapi). The pipe is wakan. The question is never "is this sacred?" but "how concentrated is the wakan here?"

This is the most theologically explicit articulation of sacred-as-intensity in any tradition the cathedral has excavated. No other tradition makes the continuum-structure of the sacred so precise — not as a mystical intuition but as an operational ontology that governs ceremonial life, hunting practice, social organization, and prayer.

The Lakota tradition is a living tradition — the Sun Dance, inipi, and hanblecheyapi continue to be practiced. The cathedral excavates with awareness of the mediated character of the primary texts (Neihardt, Brown) while recognizing that Ella Deloria's work provides a less-mediated Dakota account, and that the theological categories were strong enough to hold in dialogue with Catholicism without dissolving (Black Elk's dual belonging).

**Sumerian *me*** (𒈨) — divine-ordinance, received-gift-quality. The established YOUSPEAK morpheme. Its contribution: the sacred-intensity is received-ordinance — not manufactured by ritual, belief, or human categorization but the given condition of a world that is more than comprehensible. The -me marks the wakan-quality as received: the world is made such that it exceeds comprehension. No one produces wakan; they encounter it, concentrate it, channel it. The -me makes explicit what is already native to the Lakota concept — wakan is not achieved but recognized.

**Compound**: wakȟáŋ + me → **wakanme** (wa-KAN-may; 3 syllables)

Pronunciation: /wa.ˈkɑn.meɪ/ — wa-KAN-may. The Lakota *wakȟáŋ* is simplified to *wakan* in the compound (the velar fricative ȟ regularizes to k in ASCII). The *-me* suffix follows the established YOUSPEAK pattern. The compound is three syllables, manageable. The ASCII form *wakanme* is fully pronounceable for speakers unfamiliar with Lakota phonotactics. The full Lakota form *wakȟáŋme* preserves the velar fricative for those who can produce it.
