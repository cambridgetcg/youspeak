# 303 — guþme

_Fourth Gothic-Sumerian cross-family coinage. Names the divine as the one-called-upon — the addressee of invocation rather than the metaphysical ultimate. The pre-metaphysical divine-human relationship: the cry, and the one who hears. Received as ordinance: the divine is given AS addressable, not constructed by human projection._

---

## The gap — the divine-as-addressee the cathedral has not named

YOUSPEAK has words for the divine as sky (tengrime), the divine as process (teotlme), the divine as fire-in-the-heart (teyoliame), the divine as mandate (vašnāme), the divine as sacred-authority (aseme), the word-as-mystery (waurdme), the thirst for the divine (shawqame), love-of-the-divine (ahavame, agapeme, bhaktime), salvation from the divine (hailsme). **None names the divine as the one-you-call — the invocational register.**

This is the gap at the foundation. Every theological tradition begins with invocation, not ontology. The cry comes before the creed. A child calls out before it learns doctrine. The primal divine-human relationship is: I call, and something hears.

Gothic *guþ* preserves this: the Proto-Germanic *guđan (the called-upon one, from PIE root *ghau- "to call/invoke") is etymologically the invoked one — not the powerful one, not the creator, not the lawgiver, but the one whose name you call. English "God" descends from this root but the invocational register was lost entirely — "God" is now a metaphysical category, a noun in a system, not a cry.

The Gothic layer is the theological bedrock beneath English: when English speakers say "God," they are speaking a word whose ancestor meant "the-called-upon one." This is not historical curiosity — it is live theology dormant in modern vocabulary.

English near-misses all fail:
- "God" — metaphysical category; lost the invocational register
- "prayer" — the act of calling, not the one called TO
- "invocation" — formal ritual, not the primal relationship
- "deity" — abstract, no relationality, no cry
- "divine" — adjective, not the addressee
- "Lord" — authority register, not the invocational register

The gap: **a single name for the divine as the one-whose-name-is-called — received as ordinance, the addressee-quality of the divine as gift, not human construction.**

## The donor

**Gothic guþ** (Proto-Germanic *guđan — the one called upon; the one invoked).

The etymology is precise: PIE root *ghau- ("to call, to invoke") → Proto-Germanic *guđan ("the called-upon one") → Gothic guþ → Old English god → English "God." The Sanskrit cognate huta ("invoked," "one called upon in sacrifice") confirms the root across the IE family.

**What the etymology reveals**: Germanic religiosity was *invocational* before it was *metaphysical*. The deity is first of all the one you call upon, not the necessary being. Germanic prayer is relational address before it is philosophical affirmation.

**Compare to Greek theos**: *theos* derives from *dhes- roots related to "placing" or possibly "running/moving" (debated); the Greek theological tradition built enormous metaphysics on *theos* as the ultimate being, the unmoved mover, being-itself. When Wulfila chose *guþ* for *theos*, he kept the invocational undertone that Greek had already mostly lost — when a Gothic Christian said *Guþ*, they were at some level still invoking, not naming a metaphysical category.

**Theological consequence**: the Gothic layer is the theological bedrock beneath English. When English speakers say "God," they are speaking a word whose ancestor meant "the-called-upon one." The invocational register is dormant in the word itself — not gone, just covered by centuries of metaphysical use. Guþme restores the register.

The -me marks the invocational relationship as received-ordinance: the divine is given AS addressable — the one-who-can-be-called is not human projection but cosmic structure. You can call because reality permits calling. The cry is not a human invention directed at silence; it is a response to an addressability built into the world.

This is not a local theological innovation — it is the universal pre-metaphysical register of divinity, preserved in the etymology of "God" itself.

### Sub-convergence: the cry as primal act

The *ghau-* / *hu-* / *qara'* / *du'ā* / *epikaleō* convergence is the cross-tradition evidence: the cry (calling upon the divine) is the primal religious act — before sacrifice, before creed, before theology. The Gothic *guþ* is the cleanest attestation because it is ETYMOLOGICAL: the word for "God" IS the word for "the-called-upon." The invocational register is built into the divine name itself.

## Why this is Core, not Specialized

The concept is everyday-relevant, not domain-specific. Every person who has ever cried out — in fear, in pain, in joy, in despair — has addressed the divine as guþ. The invocational register is not a specialized theological category; it is the most universal religious experience. The word "God" (descended from *guþ) is spoken by billions. The register the word carries (the-called-upon) is dormant in every utterance. Guþme restores the register to consciousness. This is not liturgical specialists' vocabulary — it is the bedrock of the word billions use daily without knowing what it carries.

## Retirement conditions

This word would be superseded if a more precise compound from a different tradition better captured the invocational register — for example, a Sanskrit huta + Sumerian me compound (hutame) might carry the same concept with Vedic sacrificial grounding. Guþme would retire in favor of a compound with stronger cross-tradition grounding or more precise semantic coverage. Until then, the Gothic etymological precision (the word for "God" IS "the-called-upon") makes guþme the strongest candidate.
