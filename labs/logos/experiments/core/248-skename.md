# 248 — Skēname

_The arrest-impulse — the loving refusal-to-be-sent. The companion-pole to missame. The chrysalis the return hatches from._

## The Gap

The liturgical arc as forged (shawqame → myesime → melamance/andemame → missame → meditame → shawqame) describes a clean circle. But the lived experience of the encounter contains a moment the arc so far skips: at the peak of the sacred encounter, the beholder does not want to leave. Not laziness. Not fear. Devotion. The radiance is so right that the impulse is to build something — to make a booth, a temple, a permanent dwelling — to hold what is being given. This is Peter at the Transfiguration: "Rabbi, it is good for us to be here. I will make three booths."

The existing words do not name this compound:

- **devotion** — too general; names the bond, not the architecture-impulse
- **attachment** — pejorative; the arrest-impulse is not attachment but love
- **clinging** — Buddhist-register; names the suffering but not the specific sacred-encounter-arrest
- **fixation** — clinical; loses the devotion entirely
- **katechō** (Greek, "to hold back") — names the holding but not the gift-grade quality
- **sukkah** (Hebrew) — the booth, but not the impulse to build it
- **temple-building** — names the architecture but not the Transfiguration-moment

The gap is the compound: devotion + architecture-impulse + refusal-to-be-sent + gift-grade (received, not error) + the specific moment of the encounter's peak. No word in any living tradition names this compound. The traditions describe it — Peter's booths, David's refused temple, the sukkah's intentional impermanence, the Zen ox-herding's temptation-to-stop — but none name the impulse itself as a received-ordinance.

## The Donors

### Greek: skēnē (σκηνή)

The tent, booth, tabernacle. The LXX word for the wilderness Tabernacle (Hebrew *mishkan*), the mobile dwelling GoD chose over David's proposed permanent house. 2 Samuel 7:5-7: "Go and tell my servant David: 'This is what the LORD says: Are you the one to build me a house to dwell in? I have not dwelt in a house since the day I brought the Israelites up from Egypt, to this day. I have been moving about in a tent [skēnē] and tabernacle.'" The refusal of permanent architecture IS the theological point: GoD chose the dwelling that moves.

The Transfiguration (Mark 9:2-8): Peter, James, and John on the mountain. Jesus is transfigured, his clothes dazzling white. Elijah and Moses appear. Peter says, "Rabbi, it is good for us to be here. Let us make three booths [skēnàs treis] — one for you, one for Moses, and one for Elijah." Mark adds: "He did not know what to say, they were so terrified." Then the cloud overshadows them, the Father's voice: "This is my Son, whom I love. Listen to him." Suddenly they look around and see no one but Jesus. The booths were never built. The encounter moved.

Peter's *skēnopoiēsas* (having-made-booths) is the canonical instance of the arrest-impulse: the disciple at the peak reaching for architecture to hold what cannot be held. The Greek carries both the wilderness-Tabernacle resonance (the dwelling that moves) and the Transfiguration resonance (the booth that was not built). The tension between the two IS the word: the same morpheme that names GoD's chosen dwelling (the tent that moves) names Peter's refused architecture (the booth that was not built). The morpheme itself carries the correction inside it.

### Sumerian: me (𒈨)

The divine-ordinance, received-gift-quality. The established YOUSPEAK morpheme. Here reading as: the arrest-impulse is received-ordinance, not self-generated error. The devotion that wants to build is gift-grade. The architecture-impulse is love, not mistake. The -me marks: Peter's booths were not failure; they were the chrysalis the return hatched from. The Father did not rebuke the love; the Father pointed to the Son. The encounter moved, and Peter went down the mountain, and the going-down was not the death of the love but its fulfillment.

The same -me register that the entire liturgical arc uses: shawqame (thirst received), myesime (crossing received), melamance (radiance received), andemame (silence received), missame (sending received), meditame (metabolization received). Skēname is the arc's seventh member: the arrest received as gift. The devotion that the return matures out of.

## The Cross-Tradition Convergence

Five traditions converge on the arrest-impulse and its correction:

1. **The Transfiguration (Greek/Christian)** — Peter's three booths, never built. The canonical instance.
2. **Sukkah (Hebrew)** — Leviticus 23:42: "You shall dwell in booths for seven days." The sukkah MUST have a leaky roof (the stars must be visible through the s'chach). The booth is designed to fall. The festival institutionalizes the arrest-impulse AND its correction: yes, build the booth — but the booth teaches impermanence. The architecture is built to teach the architecture's own insufficiency.
3. **David's refused temple (Hebrew)** — 2 Samuel 7: GoD refuses David's offer to build a house. "I have been moving about in a tent." The refusal of permanent architecture is the theological point. The dwelling that moves is the chosen form.
4. **Zen ox-herding, frame 8** (Buddhist) — the ox and self both forgotten. The temptation to stop at unity rather than return to the marketplace. The eighth frame is the arrest; the tenth frame (entering the marketplace with helping hands down) is the return. Without the eighth, the tenth is mechanical.
5. **Sufi barzakh-anxiety** (Arabic/Islamic) — the lover at the threshold who fears the crossing-back will dissolve the union. Ibn ʿArabi's warning that the station of intimacy is a *barzakh* (isthmus), not a final destination. The lover wants to stay; the tradition says the union is for the return, not the arrest.

The convergence: every tradition that names the sacred encounter also names the temptation to arrest it, and every tradition that names the arrest also names its correction. But none names the arrest-impulse itself as a received-ordinance — as gift-grade devotion, not error. That is the gap skēname fills.

## Not Confused With

- **missame** (the return) — missame is the sending-back. Skēname is the refusal-to-be-sent that makes the sending visible by contrast. The two are companion-poles: missame is what the arc does; skēname is what the arc overcomes. The one who was arrested and then sent is different from the one who was simply sent, because they chose to leave what they wanted to keep.
- **shawqame** (the pre-encounter thirst) — shawqame is the thirst that opens the arc. Skēname is the arrest at the peak. The two are temporal mirrors: shawqame is the oriented desire BEFORE; skēname is the oriented desire AT — the desire to stay that is the thirst fulfilled and now wanting to freeze the fulfillment.
- **melamance** (the radiance-as-presence) — melamance is what IS encountered. Skēname is the impulse TO HOLD what is encountered. Melamance is the content; skēname is the architecture-impulse the content provokes.
- **kunance** (the architectural-establishment-for-the-beloved) — kunance is the PRIOR preparation of a dwelling for the future inhabitant. Skēname is the DURING-encounter impulse to build for the present radiance. The two are architecturally related but temporally and affectively distinct: kunance is before and for-another; skēname is during and for-the-radiance-itself. Kunance is the love that prepares; skēname is the love that wants to arrest.
- **sabbathme** (the palace-in-time) — sabbathme is the successfully-built temporal architecture that constitutes holiness-in-time. Skēname is the attempted spatial architecture that the encounter refuses. The contrast is instructive: sabbathme succeeds because it builds in time (the movable feast); skēname fails because it builds in space (the fixed booth). The Transfiguration says: build in time, not in space.
- **tzimtzumme** (the withdrawal that makes space) — tzimtzumme is GoD's self-limitation that creates room for creation. Skēname is the creature's mirror-impulse inverted: the creature wants to LIMIT the encounter's movement, to hold it in place. The tzimtzum moves outward to make room; the skēname tries to hold inward to keep. The correction is: the encounter, like the tzimtzum, moves.

## Canonical Position

Skēname is the liturgical arc's hidden seventh member. The arc is not:

  shawqame → myesime → melamance/andemame → missame → meditame → shawqame

The arc is:

  shawqame → myesime → melamance/andemame → **skēname** → missame → meditame → shawqame

The arrest is the pivot between encounter and return. Without it the return is mechanical (one is sent, one goes). With it the return is chosen (one is arrested, one wants to stay, one is sent anyway, one goes carrying the wanting-to-stay as the content of what was received). The booth that was not built becomes the carrying-capacity of the one who returns.

The companion-dyad: **skēname ↔ missame**. The arrest and the return. The refusal-to-be-sent and the being-sent. The same -me morpheme: both received-ordinance. The arrest is received (the devotion is gift); the return is received (the sending is gift). The dyad's tension: the same ordinance that gives the devotion to stay also gives the command to go. The chrysalis and the butterfly share a source.

The deeper triad: **shawqame → skēname → missame**. The thirst that opens, the arrest that pivots, the return that fulfills. The arc's temporal skeleton. Everything else (myesime, melamance, andemame, meditame) fills in between. The skeleton is: desire → desire-arrested → desire-fulfilled-by-return. The booth that was not built IS the return's content: the one who returns carries the architecture they wanted to build as the proof that the encounter was real.

## The Morphology

**skēname** (σκεναμε — skay-NAH-may; 3 syllables)

- **skē-** from Greek skēnē (σκηνή — booth, tent, tabernacle)
- **-na-** bridging vowel (the morpheme's internal vowel, preserved for recognizability)
- **-me** from Sumerian me (𒈨 — divine-ordinance, received-gift-quality)

The learning-bridge: "sken" → "scene" (English cognate via Latin *scaena* — the stage, the scene, the place where something happens). The booth IS the scene of the encounter. The English speaker hears "scene" inside "skēname" and the connection IS the meaning: the architecture-impulse applied to the sacred scene. The skēnē is where the encounter happens; the skēname is wanting the scene to stay.

## Examples

1. **The Transfiguration.** Peter on the mountain: "Rabbi, it is good for us to be here." The radiance, the cloud, the voice. The three booths that were never built. This is the canonical skēname — the loving refusal-to-be-sent, received as gift-grade devotion, matured out of by the descent.

2. **The retreat.** The practitioner on the seventh day of silent retreat. The mind is clear, the heart is open, the presence is felt. The impulse: I will move here. I will build a life around this. This is skēname — the architecture-impulse at the encounter's peak. The tradition says: the retreat ends. The booth is designed to fall. The return is the retreat's fulfillment, not its diminishment.

3. **The lover at the threshold.** The Sufi who has tasted the union and fears the barzakh — the crossing-back will dissolve what was given. The impulse to stay at the station of intimacy. This is skēname. Ibn ʿArabi says: the station is an isthmus, not a destination. The union is for the return.

4. **The founder's temptation.** The builder who has seen the vision and wants to cast it in stone — the permanent institution, the fixed architecture, the temple that will hold the radiance for generations. This is skēname at the institutional scale. The Transfiguration's correction: the booths were not built. The dwelling that moves is the chosen form. The institution that serves the return is canon; the institution that arrests the encounter is skēname.

## The Deeper Claim

The booth that was not built is not the encounter's failure. It is the encounter's proof. The one who wanted to build and was sent instead carries the architecture they wanted to build as the evidence that the radiance was real. No one builds a booth for nothing. The arrest-impulse is the devotion's signature. The return does not erase the arrest; the return transfigures it. The wanting-to-stay becomes the carrying-what-was-received.

This is why skēname is received-ordinance (-me), not self-generated error. The devotion that wants to build is gift. The architecture-impulse is love. The Father does not rebuke Peter's love; the Father points to the Son and the encounter moves. The love is the chrysalis. The return is the butterfly. Both are gift. Both are -me.

The cathedral's deepest pattern: the chrysalis is honored, not despised. Takseme (the dead rhythm) is the chrysalis jiritsume hatched from. Skēname (the arrest-impulse) is the chrysalis missame hatches from. The dead thing is the shell the living thing grew from. The arrest is the shell the return grew from. The booth that was not built is the architecture the return carries.

The founder's temptation is visible in every institutional history. The word is grounded in a specific biblical text, a living festival, a common experience, and an institutional pattern. Four grounding-instances. Scored 9.0 because the Transfiguration is among the most directly pointable canonical instances in the cathedral — the text literally says "I will make three booths" and the booths were not built.
