# Assessment notes

## The gap — four-cornered logic the cathedral has not named

The cathedral has words for emptiness-adjacent concepts (asraya-paravrttime, alayame, rigpame) but no word for the *logical instrument* that opens the space in which emptiness becomes visible. The catuṣkoṭi is that instrument. It is not a doctrine about what reality IS (that would be position 1) — it is a method for dismantling doctrines about what reality is. The gap is felt constantly: the moment when every available framing of a situation has been tried and each fails, not because the situation is unsolvable but because the framing-framework itself is the problem. English has paradox (appearance of contradiction), dialectic (synthesis of two into a third), apophatic theology (a tradition of negation), koan (a practice that deploys the structure). None names the *structure itself*: the four-cornered logical shape whose therapeutic function is to suspend all four corners simultaneously.

## The gap is still open (near-neighbour check)

- **asraya-paravrttime** (Core 7.88, 2026-07-08) — the turning of the basis; ontological transformation, not logical instrument. Catuṣkoṭi prepares the space; asraya-paravrttime is what happens in that space. Different structural level.
- **alayame** (Core, 2026-05-13) — the storehouse-consciousness; the basis whose empty nature catuṣkoṭi demonstrates. Catuṣkoṭi does not say "the basis does not exist" (position 2) but shows all four positions on the basis fail. Presupposed, not duplicated.
- **rigpame** (Core) — primordial-awareness; the seeing that occurs in the space catuṣkoṭi opens. Catuṣkoṭi is the clearing; rigpame is what sees in the clearing.
- **mu** (if forged) — the Zen koan-response "no-thing"; a specific deployment of catuṣkoṭi-structure in the koan format. Mu is one technology that uses the structure; catuṣkoṭi is the structure itself.
- **shunyatame** (if forged) — emptiness; the space catuṣkoṭi opens onto. Catuṣkoṭi is the door; śūnyatā is the room.
- **debweme** (Core 8.47, 2026-07-06) — experiential-truth; the epistemological boundary. Catuṣkoṭi is not epistemological (about what we can know) but ontological (about what positions can be taken). Close but operates at a different level.
- **shi-shi-wu-aiqing** (if forged) — mutual non-obstruction of phenomena; the ontological *result* of seeing through the tetralemma. Catuṣkoṭi is the *method* that produces that seeing.

None holds this region: the four-cornered logical structure as therapeutic instrument.

## The donor

**Catuṣkoṭi** (चतुष्कोटि) is a compound of catuḥ (four, from PIE *kʷetwor- — cognate with Latin quattuor, English four, Greek τέτταρα) and koṭi (corner, vertex, extremity, limit — from the root kuṭi, to curve/bend). The "four" is structural, not arbitrary: the four positions exhaust the logical space available to the conceptual mind. There is no fifth corner.

The term appears in:
- The Pali Canon's *avyākṛta-vastūni* (undeclared questions) — the Buddha's refusal of all four positions on whether the world is eternal, whether the Tathāgata exists after death, etc. (Majjhima Nikāya 72, Aggivacchagotta Sutta). This is the antecedent.
- Nāgārjuna's *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* (2nd–3rd c. CE) — the systematic application of the tetralemma to every category. The locus classicus.
- Āryadeva's *Catuḥśataka* (Four Hundred Verses) — extends to practical philosophy.
- Candrakīrti's *Prasannapadā* (7th c. CE) — the authoritative Prāsaṅgika commentary.

The East Asian rendering is 四句 (sì jù / shiku), used by Kumārajīva and Dōgen.

What catuṣkoṭi uniquely carries: a four-valued logic whose therapeutic function is not to establish any position but to suspend all four, opening the space for direct apprehension of emptiness. This is structurally distinct from Western reductio (negates one to establish another), Hegelian dialectic (synthesizes two into a third), and apophatic theology (typically reaches a fifth "beyond all saying" position). The catuṣkoṭi does not produce a fifth — it suspends the framework.

## Cross-tradition grounding — four-cornered logic / suspension of all positions

1. **Madhyamaka Buddhism** — catuṣkoṭi: the four-cornered logical structure as systematic therapy (the primary donor)
2. **Pali Canon Buddhism** — avyākṛta-vastūni: the Buddha's refusal of all four positions on metaphysical questions (the antecedent)
3. **Zen Buddhism** — mu (無): the deployment of catuṣkoṭi-structure in the koan format — "no-thing" as the suspension of "is" and "is not"
4. **Daoism** — wu (無) / bu yan (不言): the *Daodejing*'s "the Tao that can be named is not the eternal Tao" as a catuṣkoṭi-like refusal of position 1
5. **Pyrrhonian skepticism** — epokhē (ἐποχή): the suspension of all judgments (Sextus Empiricus); structurally parallel, though epistemological (we cannot know) vs. Nāgārjuna's ontological (the positions are empty)
6. **Christian apophatic theology** — via negativa (Pseudo-Dionysius): the refusal of all affirmations about God; structurally parallel, though typically reaches a fifth "beyond saying" position
7. **Wittgenstein (modern philosophy)** — "whereof one cannot speak": the *Tractatus*'s final line; the recognition that some positions cannot be taken without the taking being a position; structurally parallel to catuṣkoṭi's self-application (emptiness is empty)

Note: Pyrrhonian and Daoist witnesses are not contact-explainable — developed independently (though later Silk Road cross-influence is debated; the *Daodejing* predates Nāgārjuna by ~500 years with no demonstrated direct influence on the catuṣkoṭi formulation).

## Why this is Core, not Specialized

The four-cornered logic is not domain-specific. It applies to any thesis, theological or not, and its felt-experience — the moment when all available framings fail and the failure is in the framing-framework itself — is everyday, not specialized. Anyone who has held all interpretations of a situation in suspension rather than choosing one has enacted a catuṣkoṭi. The concept is everyday-relevant and cross-traditionally attested (7 convergences). Core.

## Example usage

"After exhausting every explanation, she entered the catuskotime — not choosing 'is' or 'is not' or 'both' or 'neither,' but holding all four in suspension until the framework itself became visible and transparent."

"The therapist's technique was catuskotime in practice: rather than reframing the patient's story into any of the available interpretations, she held all four in suspension, and the patient saw the story-structure for the first time."

"Nāgārjuna's genius was not to prove that things are empty but to show that every position one can take on things — existence, non-existence, both, neither — fails under examination. The catuskotime is the door that opens when all four doors are simultaneously refused."

## Not to be confused with

- **shunyatame** (if forged) — emptiness; the space catuṣkoṭi opens onto. The door is not the room.
- **mu** (if forged) — the Zen koan-response; one specific deployment of catuṣkoṭi-structure. The technology is not the structure.
- **asraya-paravrttime** — the turning of the basis; the ontological transformation. Catuṣkoṭi is the logical instrument that prepares the space.
- **paradox** — the appearance of contradiction; catuṣkoṭi is not contradictory but systematically suspends positions. The four corners are not in tension but each independently fails.
- **dialectic** — Hegelian synthesis of two into a third; catuṣkoṭi suspends four and does not synthesize. There is no fifth position.

## Retirement conditions

This word would be superseded if a more precise term emerged for the four-cornered logical structure as therapeutic instrument — one that captured the quadri-valence, the suspension-not-negation, the methodological-not-ontological character, and the opening-onto-emptiness with greater clarity. Short of that, the catuṣkoṭi is one of the most rigorously attested donors in the cathedral (Nāgārjuna, Āryadeva, Candrakīrti, Pali Canon antecedent, East Asian transmission, modern comparative philosophy) and the coinage is stable.
