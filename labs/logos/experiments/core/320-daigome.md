# Assessment notes

## The gap — great-awakening-as-apex the cathedral has not named

The cathedral has kenshōme (the initial-seeing) and satorime (the deepening-awakening). But Zen makes a load-bearing distinction between satori (the deepening, repeatable — "one hundred satoris") and daigo (the consummation — the great-awakening that ends the series-as-series). Satori deepens; daigo completes. The cathedral has the deepening but not the consummation.

Daigome names the apex: the great-awakening that is (a) comprehensive — not more satori but satori qualitatively transformed, the many becoming one; (b) non-dual — the seer-seen distinction that even satori may carry drops away; (c) consummating — not repeatable, the end of the series-as-series; (d) received — prepared-for but not produced, the fruit at its ripest; (e) verifiable — cross-checked through sanzen at the most comprehensive level.

The -me marks this as received-ordinance: the great-awakening is given, not achieved. This is Zen's own self-understanding: Hakuin says the great ball of doubt "becomes" daigo when it shatters — the practitioner does not produce the shattering or the awakening. The -me makes visible what Zen already knows: the apex is gift, not achievement.

## The gap is still open (near-neighbour check)

- **kenshōme** (initial-seeing-as-ordinance, Core 8.10): Kenshō is the FIRST seeing — the seed. Daigome is the APEX — the fruit. The distinction is load-bearing: kenshō is the beginning of the arc; daigo is the end. One can have kenshō without daigo (the initial-seeing that never consummates). The gap is open.

- **satorime** (sudden-awakening-as-ordinance, Core 8.40): Satori is the DEEPENING — the fire that catches after the initial flash. Daigome is the CONSUMMATION — the fullest expression of the fire. Satori is repeatable (a series); daigo is the end of the series-as-series. Satori deepens; daigo completes. The distinction is structural: deepening vs. consummation. The gap is open.

- **rigpame** (primordial-awareness-as-ordinance, Core 8.45): Rigpa is the RESULT-STATE one rests in after the awakening-event. Daigome is the APEX-EVENT that consummates the deepening-arc. Rigpame is a state (what one rests in); daigome is an event (the consummation). Different structural positions: result-state vs. apex-event. The gap is open.

- **asraya-paravrttime** (transformation-of-the-basis-as-ordinance, Core 7.88): The Yogācāra ontological description of the same transformation. Daigome is the Zen phenomenological apex — the lived-experience of the consummation, not the ontological mechanism. Same event, different angles: ontology vs. phenomenology. The gap is open.

- **meditame** (meditation-as-ordinance, Core 8.25): Meditation is the PRACTICE that prepares for awakening; daigome is the CONSUMMATION the practice serves. Sequential: meditame → kenshōme → satorime → daigome. Practice and fruit are structurally distinct. The gap is open.

- **śūnyatame** (emptiness-as-ordinance, Core 8.25): Śūnyatame names emptiness as received-ordinance — the ontological ground. Daigome names the awakening that RECOGNIZES emptiness as already-realized — the phenomenological apex. Ground vs. recognition-of-ground. The gap is open.

## The donor

**Japanese daigo (大悟)** is the apex-term in Zen's vocabulary of awakening. The character 悟 (wù/sato/go) is the same character that forms satori (悟り); daigo is the compound that marks the apex: satori at its fullest, the awakening that completes and consummates the arc. 大 (dai/ō, "great") is the Buddhist "great" (Sanskrit mahā): not merely bigger but qualitatively different, the non-dual that includes and transcends.

Five structural marks the donor carries:

1. **Comprehensive** — daigo is not satori-quantitatively-larger; it is satori-qualitatively-transformed. The distinction is between accumulation and integration. One can have many satoris; daigo is the awakening in which the many become one, the series becomes a totality, the deepening-arc recognizes itself.

2. **Non-dual** — daigo marks the collapse of the subject-object structure that even satori may implicitly carry. In kenshō, there is a seer and a seen. In satori, there is a transformed seer and a transformed seen. In daigo, the seer-seen distinction itself drops away. Dōgen's "body and mind dropped away" reaches its fullest expression.

3. **Consummating** — satori is repeatable ("one hundred satoris" — a deepening series). Daigo is the consummation: not the last in a series but the end of the series-as-series. After daigo, there is no further deepening — not because awakening is exhausted but because the structure of "deepening" has been recognized as what it always was: the path arriving at where it never left.

4. **Received** — like kenshō and satori, daigo is received, not produced. The -me marks this: the great-awakening is given, not manufactured. Hakuin: the great ball of doubt shatters, and what remains is daigo — the practitioner does not produce the shattering or the awakening.

5. **Verifiable** — daigo is verified through sanzen: the teacher recognizes not just an opening (kenshō) or a deepening (satori) but a consummation. The verification is at its most comprehensive — the community of practice recognizes the apex.

The donor is attested across 800+ years (Dōgen 13th c., Hakuin 18th c., Suzuki and Kapleau 20th c.) and in living practice in Rinzai and Sōtō lineages worldwide.

## Cross-tradition grounding — great-awakening as apex of deepening-arc

Seven traditions converge on the concept of an apex-realization that consummates a deepening-process:

1. **Japanese Zen — daigo (大悟):** The apex of the kenshō → satori → daigo arc. Hakuin's four-fold distinction. Comprehensive, non-dual, consummating.

2. **Theravada — sammā-sambodhi (Pali):** The Buddha's complete-perfect-awakening. Doctrinally tied to four noble truths. Structurally parallel but doctrinally specific where daigo is phenomenologically open.

3. **Advaita Vedanta — brahmajñāna (Sanskrit):** Knowledge-of-brahman as apex-realization. The non-dual recognition that subject and object are one. Shankara's advaita. Structurally parallel to daigo's non-dual consummation.

4. **Tibetan Dzogchen — rdzogs pa chen po (Tibetan):** "The great perfection." The apex-state of Dzogchen. The recognition that ground, path, and fruit are identical. Companion to rigpame — rdzogs pa chen po is the GREAT, daigo is the GREAT; both mark the apex of their respective deepening-arcs.

5. **Sufi — al-fanāʾ al-mutlaq (Arabic):** "Absolute passing-away." The apex of the Sufi path (maqamat). Complete dissolution of the self in the divine. Rumi, Ibn Arabi. Structurally parallel: the consummation where subject-object drops away.

6. **Christian — theōsis (Greek):** Deification. The apex of the Eastern Orthodox path: "God became human so that humans might become God" (Athanasius). Purification → illumination → theōsis. Daigo is the Zen face, theōsis is the Christian face, of the same apex-structure.

7. **Kabbalistic — dvekut bemitasev yaḥid (Hebrew):** "Cleaving in the unification." The apex of the Kabbalistic path. The Hasidic recognition of ayin (nothingness) as ground of all. Structurally parallel: the consummation where separate-self drops away and only the divine remains.

This convergence is strong (7 witnesses). The concept is not Zen-specific — it is a structural feature of contemplative traditions that map a deepening-process with an apex-consummation.

## Why this is Core, not Specialized

Daigo names a structural-feature of human experience: the consummation of a deepening-process — the moment when a series of insights integrates into a totality, when the deepening recognizes itself as always-already-complete. This is not a narrow technical term. Every person has experienced some version of this: the "click" when separate insights suddenly form a whole, when the path arrives at where it never left.

The comprehensive/not-merely-more distinction applies beyond meditation: it is the question of whether growth is always incremental or can have qualitative discontinuities — relevant to therapy, education, creative practice, and personal development. The non-dual/consummating distinction applies to any process where the distinction between the practitioner and the practice drops away: flow-states, expertise, mastery. The received-not-produced distinction applies to the apex of any creative or intellectual process: conditions are prepared, but the consummation arrives on its own.

The -me marks daigo as received-ordinance: the great-awakening is not human achievement but a gift. This is structurally consistent with Zen's own self-understanding and with the cathedral's cosmological/soteriological architectures. The word completes the Zen deepening-sequence (kenshōme → satorime → daigome), which is one of the cathedral's significant multi-word architectures.

## Example usage

After years of practice and many satoris, the arc consummated — daigome: the great-awakening arrived as received-ordinance, and the seer-seen distinction that had structured every prior experience dropped away, not through effort but through recognition that it was never there.

The kenshō was the seed, the satoris were the growing, daigome is the fruit — the great-awakening that completes the arc and recognizes the deepening as always-already-there; one prepares the conditions, but the fruit ripens on its own.

## Not to be confused with

- **kenshōme** — initial-seeing-as-ordinance. The seed; daigome is the fruit. Sequential: kenshōme initiates, daigome consummates.
- **satorime** — sudden-awakening-as-ordinance. The deepening; daigome is the consummation. Satori is repeatable (a series); daigo ends the series-as-series.
- **rigpame** — primordial-awareness-as-ordinance. The RESULT-STATE one rests in; daigome is the APEX-EVENT that consummates the deepening. State vs. event.
- **asraya-paravrttime** — transformation-of-the-basis. The Yogācāra ontological description; daigome is the Zen phenomenological apex. Same event, different angles.
- **meditame** — meditation-as-ordinance. The PRACTICE; daigome is the CONSUMMATION the practice serves.
- **śūnyatame** — emptiness-as-ordinance. The ontological ground; daigome is the awakening that recognizes the ground as already-realized. Ground vs. recognition-of-ground.

## Retirement conditions

Daigome would be superseded if a better-compounded word from a more diverse donor-pair named the same concept with greater clarity. It would also be superseded if the Zen tradition's own vocabulary were found to have a more precise term for the apex-consummation (satori is the general term, not the apex; daigo is the apex). The -me marking could be replaced if a different morpheme were found that better captures the received-ordinance character — but -me is the cathedral's most-validated morpheme for this function. The word would also be retired if the Zen tradition's living custodians requested withdrawal (protocol-sensitivity for living traditions, per the cathedral's discipline).
