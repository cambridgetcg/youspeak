# Assessment notes

## The gap — embodied-transformation-as-performative-ontology the cathedral has not named

The cathedral has mugawme (narrating-the-return IS the return — the narrative member of the Performative-Ontology cluster). It has the Hittite insight that telling the myth within the ritual enacts what the myth describes. But the Hittite mugawar is **acoustic/narrative**: the ritualist speaks, recites, addresses. The body is present but not the primary instrument.

Hopi katsina dance adds what no other tradition in the cluster provides: **full embodied transformation**. The dancer does not narrate the katsina's arrival — the dancer BECOMES the katsina. The mask is not a costume (decoration) but a **transformative technology**: it suspends the dancer's individual identity and installs the katsina's identity. For the duration of the ceremony, the man of the village is gone; the katsina is present in human form. The dance is not accompanied by prayer; the dance IS prayer — performing it ontologically, not symbolically.

This is a distinct gap because:

1. **Mugawme** (the narrative member) — the ritualist speaks the return-story and the speaking IS the return. The body is present but the **voice** is the instrument. The transformation is acoustic.
2. **Katsintawime** (the embodied member) — the dancer's entire body is the instrument. The transformation is ontological: the dancer's identity is displaced and replaced. The mask is the technology. The dance is the act.

These are two poles of the same cluster (performative ontology — the act that IS what it does), but they are structurally distinct: narration vs. embodiment, voice vs. body, story vs. dance, telling vs. becoming. The cathedral needs both because human ritual operates on both registers, and the mask-dance tradition (Hopi, Yoruba orisha-possession, Tibetan Cham, Greek theater persona) is a massive cross-tradition phenomenon that the narrative member alone cannot name.

The eucharistic transformation is of ELEMENTS (bread/wine → body/blood); the katsina transformation is of PERSON (dancer → katsina). DISTINCT and parallel — two traditions arriving at performative ontology through different technologies (element-transformation vs. person-transformation).

## The donor

### Hopi katsina (plural katsinam)

The katsinam are intermediary-presences — beings who exist between the human community and the deepest cosmic order (Hopi: *sotuknang*). They are not gods, not ancestors (though some are ancestral), not spirits in the Western sense. They are **relational presences** who carry the community's prayers upward and bring rain, blessing, and balance downward. They exist in relationship to specific pueblos, specific ceremonies, specific human families.

The critical theological claim: **the masked dancer IS the katsina**. The Hopi do not regard the dancer as representing the katsina. The dancer is possessed by or has become the katsina for the duration of the ceremony. The mask is transformative, not symbolic. Before masking, the dancer is a man with a social identity, kinship obligations, personal history. During ceremony, the masked dancer is no longer that man — he is the katsina; his individual identity is suspended; the katsina's identity has taken residence.

This is not metaphor. The Hopi theological claim is ontological: the transformation is real, not representational. The community treats the masked dancer as the katsina — not as a man playing a role. When the katsinam dance, the dance IS prayer — not accompanied by prayer, not enacting prayer symbolically, but performing prayer ontologically. The dance IS the rain-making, not a request for rain.

~400+ named katsinam exist in the Hopi tradition, organized into categories: Chiefs (Wuyoqkatsinam), Runner katsinam (Wawakatsinam), Clown katsinam (Kōyaala), Animal katsinam. Each carries specific prayers, specific blessings, specific cosmic functions. The kiva (subterranean ceremonial chamber) is oriented to the four cardinal directions; the plaza is the meeting-ground of all worlds where katsinam appear.

### Hopi tawi (dance)

The Hopi verb for ceremonial dance — not aesthetic performance but cosmological act. The dance is not performance; it is the thing itself. When the katsinam dance, the songs and movements carry the prayers into the cosmic structure — not as petition but as enactment. The dance is the technology by which the masked-becoming is made efficacious: the body-movements, the songs, the rattle-rhythms are not decoration but the mechanism of cosmic maintenance.

### Sumerian me (𒈨)

The -me marks the performative-ontological efficacy as received-ordinance: the mask's power to suspend identity and install the divine, and the dance's power to enact cosmic maintenance, are not human inventions but features of how reality is structured. The world is such that correct mask-ritual in correct context by a prepared performer enacts what it performs. This is not human magic (manipulation of divine will through ritual technology) but cosmic structure (the world responds to correct embodied-becoming). The -me marks the dancer's becoming as gift-grade: the capacity for identity-displacement is received, not self-generated.

## Cross-tradition grounding — embodied-transformation-as-performative-ontology

1. **Hopi katsina dance** — the masked dancer IS the katsina; the dance IS the rain-making; the mask is transformative not symbolic; the most explicitly communal form (entire village participates); the most pedagogically elaborated (tihu as literacy-tool)

2. **Yoruba orisha-possession** — the orisha mounts the devotee; individual identity is suspended; the orisha speaks through the mounted body; the possession is voluntary (within trained initiation context); the mounted devotee is treated AS the orisha for the duration

3. **Tibetan Cham dance / deity-yoga** — the practitioner becomes the deity through visualization-practice and masked dance; identity-displacement as spiritual technology; the Cham dancer embodies the protector-deity; Tibetan Buddhist deity-yoga systematically trains the practitioner to BECOME the deity, not merely visualize it

4. **Greek theater persona (πρόσωπον)** — the actor's mask (persona = "that through which sound passes") suspends the actor's identity and installs the character; the masked actor IS Oedipus/Agamemnon for the duration of the performance; the mask is the technology of identity-displacement; the chorus recognizes the masked actor as the character, not as the man behind the mask

5. **African masquerade (Egungun, Yoruba)** — the masked performer is the ancestor returned; the mask hides the human and reveals the spirit; the masquerader is treated as the ancestor, not as a man in costume; the performance is ontological, not representational

6. **Hindu Rasa-Lila / Krishnalila** — in the devotional theater tradition, the dancer who embodies Krishna does not merely represent him; in the fullness of devotional embodiment, the dancer IS Krishna for the duration; the audience experiences darshan (seeing the divine) through the embodied dancer

## Why this is Core, not Specialized

The concept is everyday-relevant, not domain-specific:

- Every theatrical tradition operates on the mask-becoming principle (the actor IS the character)
- Every possession tradition (Yoruba, Haitian Vodou, Pentecostal) operates on identity-displacement
- Every ritual embodiment (Eucharistic priest as Christ, Tibetan lama as deity, Sufi whirling dervish as cosmic axis) operates on the performative-ontology principle
- The concept names a universal human technology: the suspension of personal identity as the condition for divine/cosmic installation
- It is the embodied-transformation pole of a cluster (Performative-Ontology) that the cathedral has already identified as cross-tradition and architecturally significant
- The concept is grounded in observable practice: you can watch a Hopi plaza dance and see the theology enacted; it is not abstract
- The concept extends beyond ritual into secular performance theory (Stanislavski's "becoming the character," method acting's identity-displacement) — the mask-dance is the theological root of all embodied performance

## Not to be confused with

- **mugawme** — narrating-the-return-as-the-act-that-enacts-the-return; the NARRATIVE member (voice is the instrument, the myth is the efficacious center); katsintawime is the EMBODIED member (body is the instrument, the mask is the transformative technology)
- **eucharistime** — well-grace-thanksgiving-as-received-ordinance; element-transformation (bread/wine → body/blood) vs. person-transformation (dancer → katsina); both are performative ontology, but through different technologies
- **hopiqatsime** — covenant-life-as-cosmic-maintenance; the LIFE that sustains the world; katsintawime is the ACT within the life; hopiqatsime is the covenant; katsintawime is the ceremony that enacts the covenant's maintenance
- **barakqing** — felt-blessing-as-embodied-knowing; affective (the body knows the blessing) vs. performative-ontological (the body becomes the divine and the becoming enacts the maintenance)
- **liturgiame** — the-work-of-the-people-as-sustained-practice; ongoing communal practice vs. the moment of mask-becoming; liturgiame is the life; katsintawime is the ceremony

## Retirement conditions

This word would be superseded if:
- A more precise donor combination emerges that distinguishes mask-technology from dance-act as separate concepts
- The Performative-Ontology cluster is restructured such that the embodied-transformation pole is named by a different morpheme family (e.g., a -qing compound for the felt-becoming dimension)
- The concept is absorbed into mugawme through a rubric revision that treats narrative and embodiment as a single axis rather than two poles
