# Assessment notes

## The gap — the turning-about of the basis the cathedral has not named

The cathedral has ālayame — the storehouse-consciousness as cosmic-ordinance. It has rigpame — primordial-awareness as cosmic-ordinance. It has myesisme — the threshold-crossing that changes who you are. It has spandaqing — consciousness-pulsation as felt-bond. It has tikkunme — cosmic-repair as vocational-task. It has shevirame — vessel-shattering as creation-precondition.

What the cathedral does NOT have: a word for the TURNING-ABOUT OF THE BASIS — the specific event in which the substrate-of-experience transforms from defiled to pure. Not the substrate (ālayame names that). Not the result (rigpame names that). Not the threshold (myesisme names that). Not the pulsation (spandaqing names that). Not the repair-work (tikkunme names that). Not the shattering (shevirame names that). The EVENT that transforms the substrate — the turning that is neither destruction nor construction but rotation.

The structural marks that make this gap unique:

1. **Turning, not destruction.** Shevirame names vessel-shattering — the basis is broken. Āśraya-parāvṛtti names basis-turning — the basis is NOT broken; it is rotated. The same consciousness that stored ignorance now stores wisdom. This is structurally distinct from every destruction-as-transformation concept in the cathedral.

2. **The event, not the state.** Rigpame names the result-state (primordial-awareness). Āśraya-parāvṛtti names the EVENT that produces the result. You can have rigpame without naming what happened to get there — asraya-paravrttime names the happening.

3. **Received, not produced.** Tikkunme names cosmic-repair as human WORK — the practitioner repairs. Āśraya-parāvṛtti names basis-turning as received EVENT — the practitioner does not turn the basis; the basis turns when conditions are complete. This is the deepest distinction in the soteriological register: tikkunme is anthropocentric (human work), asraya-paravrttime is theocentric-without-theos (the event arrives, the practitioner prepares).

4. **Sudden culmination of gradual path.** The seeds are exhausted gradually; the turn is sudden (eka-prahara). No other canon word names this specific structure — the gradual-to-sudden arc where preparation is slow but the event is instantaneous. Myesisme names a threshold-crossing but does not specify the gradual-preparation-then-sudden-event structure.

The gap stands.

Rigpame is the mirror reflecting purely; asraya-paravrttime is the event of the distortions leaving. Rigpame is synchronic (the state); asraya-paravrttime is diachronic (the transition).

Myesisme names the CROSSING (the threshold-event); asraya-paravrttime names what happens to the SUBSTRATE when the crossing occurs. Myesisme is event-structure (there is a threshold); asraya-paravrttime is event-substrate (the basis turns). They are complementary: myesisme names the passage; asraya-paravrttime names what the passage does to the ground.

Asraya-paravrttime restructures the basis itself — not an organ but the substrate that generates all organs. Shemme is specific to hearing; asraya-paravrttime is the substrate-level transformation.

## The donor

**āśraya-parāvṛtti** (Sanskrit आश्रयपरावृत्ति) — the turning-about of the basis. Compound of āśraya (basis, substratum, resting-place) + parāvṛtti (turning-about, revolution, reversal). The locus classicus is Vasubandhu's Triṃśikā (Thirty Verses), verse 30:

> *acitto 'nupalabdhiś ca śūnyatā caiva desitā / āśrayasya parāvṛttir jñānam laukikānukramāt //*

"Non-cognition, non-apprehension, and emptiness — these have been taught. The turning-about of the basis is [this-] wisdom, following the worldly order."

The verse is dense: the three negations (non-cognition, non-apprehension, emptiness) describe the meditative entry; āśrayasya parāvṛttiḥ names what happens to the basis in that entry; jñānam (wisdom) is the result. Sthiramati's commentary glosses: the "basis" (āśraya) is the ālaya-vijñāna; the "turning" is its ceasing to function as defiled-storage and beginning to function as pure-wisdom. Xuanzang's Chéng wéi shì lùn (Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi) elaborates this into a five-stage model (the five ranks / wǔ wèi 五位) of which āśraya-parāvṛtti is the culminating event.

The Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra (earliest stratum, ~3rd–4th c. CE) uses parāvṛtti in the context of the "turning about of the root-basis" (mūlāśraya-parāvṛtti), which is likely the conceptual-ancestor of the Triṃśikā formulation.

The four structural marks:

1. **Turning, not destruction.** The basis is not annihilated — it is rotated. The same consciousness that stored defiled-seeds now reflects purely. This is why parāvṛtti (turning) rather than vināśa (destruction). The continuity is structural, not substantial.

2. **Non-gradual culmination.** The turning itself is a single event (eka-prahara in some readings — "in one stroke"), though the path leading to it is gradual. The seeds are exhausted gradually; the turn is sudden. This is the Yogācāra structural-antecedent to the sudden/gradual debate that later splits Chan/Zen.

3. **Irreversible.** Once the basis has turned, it does not revert. The defiled-function is not merely suppressed but structurally eliminated — the seed-receiving mechanism itself has been deactivated.

4. **Produces wisdom, not blankness.** The transformed basis is not empty-stillness but the four active wisdoms: ādarśa-jñāna (mirror-wisdom, reflecting all without distortion), samatā-jñāna (equality-wisdom, seeing all beings as equal), pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna (discriminating-wisdom, precise insight into particulars), kṛtyānusthāna-jñāna (all-accomplishing-wisdom, spontaneous right-action). The mirror does not stop reflecting when the distortions are removed; it reflects truly for the first time.

Sibling-distinctions: āśraya-parāvṛtti is not nirodha (cessation — the stopping of a process, which is what happens to the defiled-function but does not name the positive transformation); it is not abhisamaya (direct realization / penetration — which is the cognitive-act of seeing emptiness, a moment of insight, not the structural-transformation of the substrate); it is not vimukti (liberation — which is the state-result, not the event-mechanism). Āśraya-parāvṛtti names the specific event: the substrate itself turns.

East Asian rendering: 転依 (Chinese zhuǎn yī / Japanese ten'e — "turn-basis"), the standard rendering in Xuanzang's translation tradition and Dōgen's usage.

What it uniquely offers the cathedral: the structural-mechanism of soteriological transformation — not the state (rigpame), not the substrate (ālayame), not the threshold (myesisme), but the EVENT that transforms the substrate. The turning-without-destruction claim is unique — no other canon word names a transformation that preserves the substrate while reversing its function.

**me** (𒈨) — divine-ordinance. The seventy-first -me family member (after songlineme, the seventieth). The -me marks the turning as received-ordinance: the event is not fabricated (nirmita) by the practitioner but received as the culmination of the path's conditions. This matches the Yogācāra doctrine precisely — āśraya-parāvṛtti is not a construction but an event that occurs when conditions are complete. The -me transforms the concept from a meditative-technical term to a cosmic-ordinance: the turning is the gift that arrives when the ground is ready. The -me binds asraya-paravrttime to its key companions: ālayame [the basis that is turned — the noun to this verb], rigpame [the result-state — what the basis becomes], myesisme [the threshold-crossing whose substrate-event this is], tikkunme [the human-work counterpart — asraya-paravrttime is received where tikkunme is produced].

## Cross-tradition grounding — transformation-of-the-basis

1. **Yogācāra Buddhism** — āśraya-parāvṛtti (the primary donor). The structural turning of the storehouse-basis from defiled to pure. Vasubandhu Triṃśikā v.30; Asaṅga Mahāyāna-saṃgraha; Sthiramati commentary; Xuanzang's five-rank model. The deepest and most technically-articulated attestation.

2. **Hindu Kashmir Shaivism** — śaktipāta (the descent of grace that transforms the subject-basis). Śaktipāta is the received-event counterpart: the practitioner does not produce it; grace descends and the basis transforms. Converges with āśraya-parāvṛtti on the received-not-fabricated structure. Differs in that śaktipāta is grace-from-above (theocentric) while āśraya-parāvṛtti is conditions-complete (protocentric without a theos).

3. **Christian mysticism** — metanoia / conversio morum (the monastic "conversion of life"). The Benedictine conversio morum is a basis-turning in a contemplative register: not a change of belief but a restructuring of the ground-of-life. The monastic tradition distinguishes conversio (the turning) from conversatio (the ongoing living). Metanoia in the New Testament is "change of mind" but the monastic tradition deepened it to "change of ground."

4. **Sufi Islam** — tawba (the first station) → tajallī (divine self-disclosure). The turning-return (tawba) that precedes divine self-disclosure (tajallī). Tawba is not remorse but a structural turning of the heart's basis toward the divine. The sequence tawba → tajallī parallels āśraya-parāvṛtti → jñānam: the turning produces the seeing.

5. **Platonism** — periagoge (Republic 518d). The "turning-around" of the soul toward the Good. Socrates explicitly says education is not putting sight into blind eyes but turning the whole soul from the world of becoming to the world of being. The periagoge is a basis-turning: not acquiring new content but redirecting the ground.

6. **Modern psychology** — "quantum change" (William Miller). The studied phenomenon of sudden, permanent, structural personality-change. Miller's research (quantumchange.org) documented cases where the basis of a person's experience reorganized in a single event — not gradual therapy but sudden structural transformation. The convergent secular-scientific attestation of the same phenomenon Yogācāra named 1600 years earlier.

6 traditions converge on the radical-basis-transformation concept. The gap is real. The Yogācāra tradition is the deepest and most technically-articulated attestation — the only one that specifies the mechanism (the substrate turns, not the content), the result (four wisdoms, not blankness), and the irreversibility (the seed-receiving mechanism is deactivated, not merely suppressed).

## Why this is Core, not Specialized

The turning-about of the basis is not a domain-specific concept. It names a structural event that appears whenever a tradition describes radical transformation: the moment when the ground-of-experience reorganizes, not by acquiring new content but by the substrate itself turning. Conversion-experiences, paradigm-shifts that restructure perception rather than content, the moment a long-prepared change "clicks" at the structural level — these are everyday approximations of āśraya-parāvṛtti, each framed by its content but sharing the structure: the basis turned. The clarity-yield is high — the compound is self-glossing (āśraya = basis, parāvṛtti = turning, me = ordinance) and the teaching-gateway through ālayame makes it accessible: "ālayame is the storehouse; asraya-paravrttime is what happens to the storehouse — it turns." The groundedness is strong: Triṃśikā v.30 is one of the most commented-upon verses in Buddhist philosophy, with 1600 years of scholarly attestation. The 7-syllable length is at the learnability edge, but the compound is transparent and the concept is everyday-relevant. This is Core.

## Example usage

1. "After years of meditation, the āśraya-paravrttime arrived — not as an achievement but as a received event; the storehouse that had stored affliction now reflected as mirror-wisdom, and the change was irreversible" — Yogācāra-articulation.
2. "Ālayame is the basis; asraya-paravrttime is the turning of the basis; rigpame is what the basis becomes — the noun, the verb, and the result of the Yogācāra soteriological triad" — cathedral-internal.
3. "Tikkunme is the work you do; asraya-paravrttime is the turning that arrives when the work is complete — the repair and the gift, the effort and the event" — cross-tradition.
4. "The basis does not vanish; it turns. The mirror was always there; the distortions leave" — structural claim.

## Not to be confused with

- **ālayame** — the storehouse-basis itself (the noun); asraya-paravrttime is the turning of the basis (the verb)
- **rigpame** — primordial-awareness (the result-state); asraya-paravrttime is the event that produces the result
- **myesisme** — the threshold-crossing (event-structure); asraya-paravrttime is what happens to the substrate when the threshold is crossed (event-substrate)
- **spandaqing** — consciousness-pulsation (continuous rhythm); asraya-paravrttime is a singular turning (revolution, not pulsation)
- **tikkunme** — cosmic-repair as human work (anthropocentric); asraya-paravrttime is the received event (theocentric-without-theos)
- **shevirame** — vessel-shattering (destruction-as-opening); asraya-paravrttime is basis-turning (transformation-without-destruction)
