# 182 — hayatqing

## The Gap

Arabic *ḥayāt* (حياة) is the Semitic-cognate-sibling of Hebrew *chayim* (both from Proto-Semitic root *ḥ-y-y "to live"), but the registers and theological-development differ. Where Hebrew chayim emphasizes multitudinous-plural-life (form is plural-by-grammar; "breath of lives"; the Jewish liturgical "L'chaim!"), Arabic ḥayāt emphasizes life-as-state-given specifically by Allah:

- Quran 2:258 — *huwa al-laḏī yuḥyī wa yumītu* — "He is the One who gives life and causes death"
- One of the 99 Names of Allah is *al-Ḥayy* — "the Living" (the source of all life)
- Sufi tradition's *ḥayāt al-qulūb* — "life of the hearts" — the spiritual aliveness given to the receptive

The qing-suffix places hayatqing in the felt-bond family rather than the received-ordinance family. The choice is precise: ḥayāt in Arabic religious literature is felt-relational — life is the relationship-with-the-Living-One, not merely cosmic-ordinance impersonally-given. The qing register reads this rightly.

The cathedral now has:
- **chayimme** (Hebrew + -me) — multitudinous-plural-life-as-cosmic-ordinance
- **hayatqing** (Arabic + qing) — life-as-state-given-by-Allah-as-felt-bond

Same Semitic root, different traditions, different registers. **Both belong in canon.** Seventh life-side forge; first to use the qing-family register for the life-side.

## The Forge

*ḥayāt*: Arabic life-as-state; Proto-Semitic root *ḥ-y-y; Quranic theology centers on Allah as al-Ḥayy and life as gift; the form ḥayāt is the abstract-noun "life" used across Quran and hadith.
