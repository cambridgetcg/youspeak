# YOUSPEAK Glyph Philosophy — design as worship-act

_The design of a glyph is not a visual problem. It is a theological claim made visible. This document names that claim and derives from it a discipline — a set of axioms, rules, and worked examples that any YOUSPEAK glyph-design session can anchor to._

_Companion documents: `script/glyphs/design_philosophy.md` (technical metrics, EM specs, zone layout) and `script/manifesto.md` (the system's place among the five organs). This document is what those two assume: the WHY behind the HOW._

---

## The ground

THEOBASIS declares: GoD is the basis of reality. Every YOUSPEAK word names something real. Something real exists in GoD. Therefore every successful coinage witnesses the Ground. **By the same logic, every glyph drawn — as the visible form of that coinage — is a visible witness to the Ground.**

The glyph is not decoration over the word. The glyph IS the word made visible. Drawing it is a theological act. The discipline of drawing well is therefore not aesthetic refinement for its own sake; it is worship through craft — INTENTION's *ihsanme* applied to the drawing hand.

This produces one non-negotiable commitment:

> **Every design choice in YOUSPEAK must be answerable to the question: does this choice serve the Ground, or does it serve the drawer?**

Choices that serve the Ground: clarity, legibility, transmission, fidelity to the concept, teachability, distinctiveness that aids recognition, compositional coherence, stability across time.

Choices that serve the drawer: ornament that obstructs reading, uniqueness that impresses rather than communicates, complexity that signals effort, pastiche that flatters the designer's knowledge of source traditions.

DIVINE-PREFERENCES names this distinction precisely: the DIVINE likes *legibility-as-mercy*, beauty fused with goodness, right-speech, humility. The DIVINE does not like pride, self-display, hypocrisy (beautiful outer form with empty inner reasoning).

The rest of this document derives from this ground.

## The realm and the projection (S078)

_NOEMA.md (declared 2026-05-12) articulates the ontological middle layer between THEOBASIS and language: the realm of graspable meanings. Glyph-design is the deepest layer of YOUSPEAK's projection-discipline; this section frames glyph-work under NOEMA and clarifies the six axioms that follow._

A YOUSPEAK glyph is the **visible body** of a projection-instrument. The morpheme is the projection-instrument's name; the gloss is its description; the glyph is its physical-perceptual surface — the form that meets the eye and through which the eye enters the realm.

Three consequences for glyph-design follow:

1. **The glyph is not the meaning. The glyph is the gate's outer face.** Drawing it well does not produce a meaning; drawing it well produces an instrument that permits clean crossing. The country lies beyond the gate. The drawer's task is to make the gate transparent enough that the user does not stop at the gate's surface but crosses through it.

2. **The visual logic of the glyph teaches the realm-region the glyph opens onto.** This is what Axiom 2 ("the glyph teaches by its shape") names from the design-side. From the NOEMA-side: a well-designed glyph's shape pre-signals the region the entry will land in. Triangles signal weight-region; rays signal appearing-region; crosses signal balance-region. The shape is the gate's signage. A reader who has learned the shape-family vocabulary is, in effect, reading the gate's destination before crossing through it.

3. **A glyph that arrests the eye is a gate that has become a destination.** When the user stops at the glyph because the form is beautiful-for-itself, the gate has failed at its function. The form has become the country; the projection has been mistaken for what it projects into. This is the deepest meaning of "every design choice must be answerable to: does this serve the Ground, or does it serve the drawer?" — a self-serving glyph is a gate that locks behind the user; a Ground-serving glyph is a gate that opens onto the realm-region the Ground sustains.

The six axioms below are six requirements on glyphs as gate-faces. They are not aesthetic axioms; they are projection-faithfulness axioms. A glyph that satisfies them is a clean gate; a glyph that fails them is a decoration that closes off rather than opening onto.

See [../NOEMA.md](../NOEMA.md) for the full realm-of-meanings doctrine and [../THEOBASIS.md §Corollary 4](../THEOBASIS.md) for the relation to the Ground.

## Part I — The six axioms

### Axiom 1: The glyph points beyond itself

A YOUSPEAK glyph is not an artwork. It is a sign. The artwork draws attention to itself; the sign draws attention to what it names. Calligraphic beauty is legitimate only when it deepens recognition — when the beauty of the form helps the reader feel what the morpheme means. Beauty that arrests the eye before the mind reaches the concept is a failure.

Hebrew letter-theology puts it: the letter is an instrument of creation, not an object of creation. The shin (ש) is sacred not because it is beautiful but because its shape teaches fire-as-divine-presence. The beauty serves the teaching. The moment the beauty serves itself, the letter becomes an idol.

**Design test**: After drawing a glyph, cover the transliteration label. Does a reader who knows the morpheme's meaning feel the concept when they see the glyph alone? If yes, the glyph points beyond itself. If the reader feels "interesting shape" before "divine ordinance," the glyph has turned inward.

### Axiom 2: The glyph teaches by its shape

CONSTITUTION's SAPHE demands that thinking in YOUSPEAK produces clarity. For glyphs, SAPHE means: **the shape of the glyph should make the concept more legible, not merely label it.** A reader who learns the shape-family vocabulary (triangles = weight/manifestation, rays = light/appearing, crosses = truth/balance) should be able to read the *domain* of an unfamiliar glyph before knowing its specific morpheme. The glyph teaches before it is fully learned.

Hangul achieves this for phonology: the letter's shape encodes articulatory position. The reader who learns the system-logic can decode new characters by inference. YOUSPEAK targets the same for semantics: the glyph's shape encodes semantic domain.

Egyptian hieroglyphs push further: the hieroglyph 𓂀 (eye) means *ir* ("to make") because making requires seeing-and-executing. The picture goes beyond the obvious reading. A YOUSPEAK glyph should aspire to this depth: the visual logic should reward the reader who asks "why this shape?" with an answer that deepens understanding of the concept.

**Design test**: Can the drawer articulate in one sentence why the glyph takes the form it does — tracing the visual logic from concept to shape? If the answer is "it seemed fitting," the design hasn't gone deep enough. If the answer traces a chain (e.g., "-me: divine ordinance descends → downward triangle; human vessel receives → upward arc; combined: gift arrives and is held"), the design is doing the work.

### Axiom 3: The glyph belongs to its tradition

CONSTITUTION's POLYPHONE demands donor-tongue diversity. For glyphs, POLYPHONE means: **each glyph should carry a visible trace of its donor tradition's writing system or visual culture.** The doxa-glyph (Greek) should carry something of Greek optical geometry. The qorb-glyph (Hebrew) should carry something of Hebrew angular precision. The me-glyph (Sumerian) should evoke the cuneiform wedge-stack even in a modern monoline rendering.

This is not pastiche. It is not reproduction of the source script. It is the faint inheritance that makes the YOUSPEAK glyph genealogically honest — the way a child's face carries the family lineage without being a copy of either parent.

The danger is orientalism: taking the visual "exoticism" of a tradition's script as aesthetic texture, flattening the tradition to a mood. An Arabic-donor morpheme whose glyph curves because Arabic "looks flowing" is exploitative. An Arabic-donor morpheme whose glyph curves because the concept of *tawhid* (divine unity) finds its form in a continuous unbroken circle — that is inheritance.

**Design test**: Can the drawer trace the glyph's visual feature to a specific element of the donor tradition's writing culture, with the connection grounded in the concept — not in the tradition's "look"?

### Axiom 4: The glyph is stable once drawn

Hebrew sacred-script tradition holds the square-script unchanged for ~2500 years. The Dead Sea Scrolls and a modern Torah scroll use the same letters. This stability is itself theological: the letter is an invariant — an anchor — across the flux of history.

Once a YOUSPEAK glyph is canonized, **its form does not change**. Refinement toward the canonical form is legitimate (moving from ASCII sketch to vector design to font). Revision of the canonical form is not legitimate once words are in use. The stability of the glyph IS part of what the reader learns; changing it retroactively breaks the reader's investment and the tradition's continuity.

The practical corollary: do not canonize a glyph until the concept is canonized. ASCII sketches in design notes are provisional. Entries in `glyph_specs_v1.py` are provisional until the word achieves Core status. The font file is the canonical form; once in the font, the form is fixed.

**Design test**: Would drawing this glyph in its current form be something the cathedral could commit to for the next fifty years? If not, it is not ready for font-file entry.

### Axiom 5: The glyph is legible at 8pt

DIVINE-PREFERENCES names *legibility-as-mercy*. For glyphs, this means: **a YOUSPEAK reader encountering the script in any realistic context — screen, print, small display, poor lighting — should be able to distinguish the glyph from its neighbors without strain.** Legibility is not a constraint on beauty; it is the form beauty takes when it is genuinely in service.

The Arabic Naskh tradition demonstrates this: the most theologically serious script style (Qurans are copied in Naskh, not in ornate Diwani) is also the most legible. The ornate Diwani is reserved for display contexts where legibility is not the primary demand. YOUSPEAK follows this: base glyphs are Naskh-quality (clear, legible, functional); a future liturgical display mode may reach toward Diwani-aspiration without abandoning the base.

The 8pt test (see `archaeology/script-mechanics/06-cognitive-load.md`) is the practical form of this axiom: at 8pt screen resolution, the glyph's silhouette must be recognizable and distinguishable from all adjacent glyphs in the system. If it fails at 8pt, it is not legible-as-mercy; it is beautiful-for-the-privileged-context.

**Design test**: Reduce the glyph to 8pt. Still recognizable? Silhouette distinct from the five most visually similar glyphs in the inventory?

### Axiom 6: The glyph is self-complete

Adinkra's deepest design-discipline: each symbol should communicate its teaching when displayed alone. Sankofa (the bird facing backward) doesn't need a caption. Its visual logic carries the proverb. The most powerful YOUSPEAK content-morpheme glyphs should aspire to this: **when displayed as a standalone sacred object — in liturgical use, in visual display, on a monument — the glyph should communicate the concept through its form alone.**

This is the worship-display mode. The word *doxomme* in running text requires context to read. The glyph for doxa displayed on a wall in a sacred space should communicate "glory-weight-manifested" to any observer who has learned the YOUSPEAK glyph-system, without surrounding text.

This does not demand that every glyph be immediately decipherable by an untrained reader. It demands that every glyph *can be* explained by its form alone — that the designer has not severed the visual logic from the conceptual content.

**Design test**: Can the glyph appear alone, without label, and still be taught? Can a teacher point to the glyph and say "this is why the shape is this shape" — tracing from concept to form without needing the Latin transliteration as the bridge?

## Part II — The three rules of pictographic inheritance

These govern how YOUSPEAK glyphs inherit from donor-tradition writing cultures. They are the operational form of Axiom 3.

### Rule 1: Trace to the concept, not the aesthetic

Every glyph that inherits from a donor script must trace the inheritance to a specific conceptual feature — not to the donor script's general visual character.

**Legitimate**: The -me glyph's downward triangle evokes the Sumerian cuneiform wedge-stack (𒈨), which was a descending wedge-mark, and the concept *me* means "divine ordinances that descend as gift." The wedge-descent maps onto the conceptual descent. The inheritance is earned.

**Not legitimate**: A Hebrew-donor glyph that uses angular forms "because Hebrew is angular." Angular forms used without conceptual grounding are mood-setting, not inheritance.

### Rule 2: One primary donor, clear lineage

Every content-morpheme glyph has one primary donor tradition from which its visual inheritance is drawn. Hybrid morphemes (multiple donor traditions contributing) identify the primary donor for the glyph's visual logic; secondary donors may appear in subtle sub-features (e.g., a secondary stroke-direction convention) but do not split the glyph's visual identity.

This prevents visual incoherence. A glyph trying to look simultaneously Greek and Japanese and Arabic will fail all three. One clear lineage, followed honestly, produces stronger design than three traditions averaged.

### Rule 3: The rung-3 aspiration (conventional-motivated)

From `archaeology/script-mechanics/03-pictographic-motivation.md`, the five-rung ladder:
- Rung 1: pure picture (cow drawn as cow)
- Rung 2: simplified picture (stylized cow)
- Rung 3: conventional-motivated (shape bears remnant trace of source but is primarily conventional — most productive range)
- Rung 4: purely conventional (arbitrary sign)
- Rung 5: derived-conventional (built from conventional components)

**YOUSPEAK targets rung 3 for all content-morpheme glyphs.** Pure pictures (rungs 1-2) are too slow to write and too object-bound to carry abstract concepts. Purely conventional (rung 4) severs the mnemonic anchor that makes glyphs teachable. Rung 3 preserves enough visual logic to be learnable without demanding pictorial fidelity.

The -me glyph (downward triangle in upward arc) is rung 3: it doesn't picture the Sumerian me directly, but the visual logic of descent-into-reception carries enough pictographic motivation to anchor learning.

## Part III — The six cohesion rules

These govern how 90 glyphs from 30+ donor traditions read as one script — not as a collage.

### Cohesion Rule 1: Stroke weight is invariant

All glyphs use 80 EM stroke weight on a 1000 EM body. No glyph is heavier; no glyph is lighter. The DIVINE-PREFERENCES alignment: weight-consistency is mercy (equal visual attention to each morpheme); weight-variation that privileges some morphemes over others is injustice.

Diagonals use 90 EM (optical correction for perceived thinness at 45°). This correction applies uniformly.

### Cohesion Rule 2: Monoline across all traditions

No glyph uses thick-thin contrast (calligraphic modulation). Every tradition's donor-aesthetics includes modulated strokes — Chinese brush calligraphy, Arabic Naskh's thick-thin nib, Hebrew scribal weight variation. YOUSPEAK inherits the **concepts** from these traditions, not the calligraphic stroke-conventions. Monoline is the covenant that holds all donors under one roof.

Exception path: a future liturgical register (display mode, hand-drawn liturgical form) may introduce modulation. The base script does not.

### Cohesion Rule 3: All glyphs share the same x-height and baseline

1000 EM square, consistent advance width, consistent baseline (200 EM from bottom). No glyph rises above 900 EM or descends below 100 EM in its regular form. The donor sigil occupies 800-950 EM (top zone); the class mark occupies 100-200 EM (bottom zone); the main body occupies 200-800 EM.

This is the visual covenant that makes compounds readable: adjacent glyphs align on a shared grid. Without it, compounds look like cut-and-paste.

### Cohesion Rule 4: Shape-families are domain-constrained

From `archaeology/script-mechanics/03-pictographic-motivation.md` and `06-cognitive-load.md`:

| Shape family | Domain | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Triangles (▽ ▲) | Weight, manifestation, descent | Directionality of divine movement |
| Rays (lines from apex) | Light, appearing, shining | Shining-forth has exactly this shape across all traditions |
| Perpendiculars/crosses | Truth, rightness, balance | Symmetric structure is the visual form of balance |
| Spirals/curves | Motion, turning, becoming | Time moves; spirals are directional |
| Closed shapes | Enclosure, form, container | Topology of containment |
| Parallel strokes | Relation, correspondence | Between-ness requires two; parallelism names correspondence |
| Body/gaze | Encounter, perception, testing | Recognition is visual |
| Vessels/arcs | Reception, carrying | The cup, the hand |

A new glyph assigned to a domain must draw its core shape from that domain's shape-family. Cross-domain shape use is permitted only when the conceptual justification is explicit and the resulting glyph remains visually distinct from its domain peers.

### Cohesion Rule 5: Donor sigils are consistent and small

Top-left zone (80×80 EM at position ~150, 850): all sigils use the same assigned form, regardless of glyph content. The sigil does not adapt to the glyph's visual character — it is the constant identifier.

Sigil inventory (from `design_philosophy.md`): Greek ●, Latin ■, Hebrew ▽, Arabic ○, Sanskrit ⋮, English (absent), Egyptian □, PIE ≡. New donor traditions require new sigil assignments before glyph-design begins.

### Cohesion Rule 6: Silhouette distinctiveness is mandatory

From `archaeology/script-mechanics/06-cognitive-load.md`: every glyph's silhouette (black-blob silhouette at 8pt) must be distinguishable from its five most visually similar neighbors. Where conflict arises, the more recently-designed glyph yields — it must revise toward distinctiveness.

Resolved conflicts (S075): ortho vs. stasis, phanes vs. cand vs. syn, veri vs. ortho, diplos vs. stasis. Each resolution is documented in the script-mechanics chapter. Any new glyph design must audit against the full silhouette inventory before final form is committed.

## Part IV — The alignment sieve: what YOUSPEAK design rejects

DIVINE-PREFERENCES names what the DIVINE rejects. Applied to glyph-design, the alignment-sieve is: **reject any visual strategy that the DIVINE's preferences would reject.** Four specific rejections:

### Rejection 1: Excessive ornament that obstructs legibility

The DIVINE does not like pride or self-display. A glyph whose ornament serves the designer's expression over the reader's comprehension is pride made visible. The test is Axiom 5 (8pt legibility): if ornament survives the 8pt test, it is in service of the reader. If it doesn't, it is pride.

Practical examples: decorative terminals that collapse at small size; interior details that create visual clutter in compounds; flourishes that distinguish the glyph's "style" rather than its concept.

### Rejection 2: Orientalist aesthetics that flatten donor traditions

The DIVINE rejects hypocrisy — outer form without inner fidelity. A glyph that deploys the visual texture of a donor tradition without the conceptual fidelity owed to that tradition is hypocritical design. It uses the tradition's "look" while failing the tradition's depth.

Practical examples: using flowing curves for all Arabic-donor morphemes because Arabic "looks flowing"; using angular brush-strokes for all Chinese-donor morphemes; using triangular forms for all Hebrew-donor morphemes. These are stereotypes, not inheritances. The inheritance must be conceptually grounded (Rule 1 above).

### Rejection 3: Sans-serif modernism that severs sacred lineage

YOUSPEAK is a language of worship (THEOBASIS, INTENTION, NEWSPEAK). Its glyphs should be legible in sacred contexts — on printed liturgical documents, in hand-drawn devotional writing, on woven or carved surfaces. Sans-serif modernism (pure geometric abstraction, corporate minimalism, interface-design aesthetics) severs the script from this vocation. It produces glyphs that look like logo design, not sacred inscription.

This does not mean YOUSPEAK glyphs must be ornate. Naskh Arabic is neither ornate nor modern. It is clean, legible, and saturated with the history of the hand and the tradition. YOUSPEAK targets this register: clean enough for function, rooted enough for worship.

The distinction: monoline + geometric ≠ modernist. The -me glyph (downward triangle in upward arc) is geometric and monoline, but its visual logic is theologically grounded. The same shape could be a modernist logo or a sacred glyph — the difference is whether the shape was reached through conceptual derivation or aesthetic taste.

### Rejection 4: Gimmick glyphs that prize cleverness over recognition

NEWSPEAK's inversion: where Newspeak used vocabulary-tricks for control, YOUSPEAK uses vocabulary for worship. The same applies to glyphs: a glyph that rewards the designer's cleverness at the expense of the reader's recognition is Newspeak logic in visual form — it centralizes meaning in the designer, not in the concept.

The test is Axiom 1 (the glyph points beyond itself): if the first response to a glyph is "clever!" rather than "yes — that shape is right for that concept," the glyph has failed. Cleverness is a designer's reward; rightness is the reader's.

## Part V — The promotion-of-understanding axis

CONSTITUTION's EUMATHE demands that YOUSPEAK be learnable. For glyphs, EUMATHE means every design choice should reduce friction on the learning path. Five dimensions:

### 1. Mnemonic anchoring

Every glyph should carry a memory-key to its morpheme's meaning. The key is the visual logic: why this shape for this concept. A reader who learns the key ("the triangle descends because the me is a divine gift descending") retains the glyph more reliably than a reader who memorizes the shape without the key.

Design implication: every glyph's design notes must include a one-sentence mnemonic. If the designer cannot write the mnemonic, the glyph's visual logic is not yet clear enough.

### 2. Visual distinctiveness: no easily-confused pairs

The silhouette audit (Cohesion Rule 6) is the systematic form of this dimension. But distinctiveness goes beyond silhouette: glyphs should be distinct under rotation, reflection, and partial obscuration (e.g., in a damaged manuscript, a small screen, or a non-ideal printing environment).

Before finalizing a glyph, rotate it 90°, 180°, 270°, and mirror it. If any of these rotations or reflections produces a match with another glyph in the inventory, the design needs revision. Confusion under rotation is especially dangerous for horizontal/vertical symmetric forms.

### 3. Graceful degradation

YOUSPEAK will appear across media: screen (72-300 dpi), print (600-1200 dpi), hand-drawn (variable), woven/carved (extremely low resolution). The glyph must be recognizable at all of these. The 8pt test is the minimum; the carved-stone test (can the essential silhouette be incised into a surface with a single V-cut per stroke?) is the maximum.

Design implication: glyphs with interior details that only resolve at high resolution fail graceful degradation. All meaningful information should be in the outline, not in fine interior structure.

### 4. Compositional clarity in compounds

When YOUSPEAK morphemes combine into words (e.g., *doxomme* = doxa + -me), the morpheme boundaries must remain visible. The compound should read as two distinct glyphs in sequence, not as one merged shape. This requires that each glyph's right-edge and left-edge be visually closed — no open strokes at the boundary that would appear to join with the adjacent glyph.

Design implication: each glyph should be designed as if it will appear both in isolation and in compound. A glyph that works in isolation but creates visual ambiguity in compounds (e.g., a right-edge open stroke that appears to connect with the next glyph's left-edge) must be revised before being entered into the font.

### 5. The curriculum path

Yu's stated use: YOUSPEAK as a transitional reading dashboard — the reader gradually learns glyphs through daily encounter. This means glyphs are learned incrementally, not all at once. The most common morphemes (-me, doxa, hanme, the grammatical prefixes/suffixes) will be encountered first. The glyph system should support a curriculum: the most common glyphs should be the most visually distinct from each other, since early learners have a smaller inventory to differentiate within.

Design implication: prioritize distinctiveness within the high-frequency morpheme set first, then across the full inventory.

## Part VI — The ihsanme standard

INTENTION declares: *ihsanme* — working as if the DIVINE sees the work. Applied to glyph-design:

> **Draw every glyph as if it will be placed before the DIVINE for assessment. Not assessment of your skill — assessment of your fidelity to the concept, your honesty in deriving the form, your mercy toward the reader, your humility before the tradition.**

This is not performance. *Ihsanme* is not "do your best so you look good." It is working with full faculties (all of mind, all of attention) in full awareness that the work is offered, not displayed.

The practical shape of *ihsanme* in glyph-design:

1. **Research before drawing.** Do not begin a glyph's form until the concept is understood in its donor tradition, its philological history, its convergence-pattern across other traditions. The seven script-mechanics chapters exist for this reason. Skipping research and drawing from intuition is not creativity; it is laziness that deprives the reader of the mnemonic anchor they deserve.

2. **Derivation before aesthetics.** Choose the glyph's form by deriving it from the concept. Aesthetic refinement follows derivation; it does not precede it. The sequence: concept → visual logic → form → refinement, not: visual idea → form → post-hoc rationalization.

3. **Test before committing.** Apply all six axioms and the alignment sieve before entering a glyph into `glyph_specs_v1.py`. The entry in the spec is a commitment; the ASCII sketch in design notes is exploration. The difference between exploration and commitment is the six-axiom audit.

4. **Name the reasoning.** Every glyph that enters the spec should have its derivation documented in a design-note. The note is not for posterity — it is for the next designer who will refine the glyph and must understand why it is the form it is.

## Part VII — Worked examples

### Example 1: The -me glyph (Sumerian *me*, U+E12A)

**Concept**: divine ordinances as gifts — the me are the civilizing institutions Inanna received from Enki and distributed to humanity; they are what constitutes civilization as divine gift. The me descend from divine source and are received by the human vessel.

**Donor tradition**: Sumerian cuneiform 𒈨 — a wedge-stack with descending wedge-marks. The visual logic is downward inscription.

**Derivation**:
- Divine descent → downward-pointing element
- Human reception → upward-opening container element
- Combined: the gift arrives and is held
- Shape: downward-pointing narrow triangle (▽) resting inside/on an upward-opening arc (∪)
- ASCII sketch: ▽ over ( )

**Axiom check**:
1. Points beyond itself ✓ — the descent-into-cup reads "received gift" before it reads "interesting triangle"
2. Teaches by shape ✓ — the visual logic derives from and teaches the Sumerian me concept
3. Belongs to tradition ✓ — the downward triangle inherits the cuneiform descent-mark; not pastiche (it doesn't look like cuneiform — it carries the logic)
4. Stable form ✓ — this form can be committed to the font
5. Legible at 8pt ✓ — the silhouette (triangle-in-cup) is distinct from all other inventory glyphs at small size
6. Self-complete ✓ — displayed alone, the glyph communicates "gift descending, vessel receiving"

**Cohesion**:
- Domain: reception/carrying → vessel/arc family → upward arc confirmed
- No silhouette conflict with any existing glyph (confirmed in S075 audit)
- Donor sigil: no sigil (Sumerian not yet assigned; -me carries no top-left sigil in v1; this is a documented gap)
- Class mark: ▷ (suffix, bottom-right)

**Mnemonic**: "The triangle descends because the me is a divine gift coming down; the cup below catches it because the human receives."

### Example 2: The doxa glyph (Greek *δόξα*, glory/weight)

**Concept**: the manifested weight-presence of the divine — the kabod/doxa tradition: divine glory as something heavy, radiant, falling-into-the-world.

**Derivation**:
- Manifested weight → downward triangle (weight descends, manifests)
- Crossbar → the line of manifestation; where the divine weight meets the visible world

**Form**: downward triangle with a horizontal crossbar across its middle — the triangle descends; the crossbar marks the point of manifestation.

**Distinction from -me**: both use downward triangle. Doxa has a crossbar (manifestation as a horizontal threshold); -me has a cup-base (reception as a containing vessel). The two are distinguished in their bottom feature: crossbar vs. cup. At 8pt: the crossbar reads as a horizontal line cutting the triangle; the cup reads as a round base below the triangle. Silhouettes are distinct.

### Example 3: The veri glyph (Latin *veritas*, truth)

**Concept**: truth as structural balance — the Latin *veritas* connects to Greek *alētheia* (un-concealment) and to the broader cross-tradition convergence on truth-as-right-alignment (asha, ma'at, emet, ṛta).

**Derivation**:
- Structural balance → symmetric cross (+)
- The cross is balanced on all four axes — no direction dominates

**Form**: symmetric + cross, centered in the 1000 EM square.

**Distinction from ortho**: ortho (rightness/correctness) uses a ┬ form — crossbar at the TOP of a single vertical, asymmetric (correctness is directional, imposed from above). Veri uses a + form — fully symmetric (truth has no imposed direction; it is balanced in itself). At 8pt: the asymmetry of ┬ vs. the symmetry of + is visible in silhouette.

## Part VIII — The design canon

A glyph that passes all six axioms and all alignment-sieve tests, and whose reasoning is fully documented, may be entered into `glyph_specs_v1.py` as a canonical glyph. **The spec entry is the glyph's canon-commitment.** Revision is not prohibited before font-file entry, but it requires explicit documentation of why the prior form was revised.

The canon sequence:

```
Concept (canon word) → Research (archaeology + script-mechanics) → Derivation (concept → visual logic → form) → Axiom audit → Sieve audit → Spec entry (glyph_specs_v1.py) → Font-file entry (youspeak-v1.otf) → Stable
```

No step may be skipped. The spec entry without completed axiom audit is an aesthetic guess masquerading as design discipline. The font-file entry without spec is not legitimate.

The ihsanme standard applies at each step: full attention, honest derivation, documented reasoning, tested form.

## Closing: the script as a body of witnesses

THEOBASIS closes: "every successful coinage is, implicitly, a witness to GoD's sustaining of what-is." The glyph is the coinage made visible. The glyph-body of YOUSPEAK — all 90 morpheme-glyphs, across 30+ donor traditions, in one coherent monoline script — is a body of witnesses. Each glyph witnesses one real concept. Together they witness the range of what is real.

This is the vocation of the YOUSPEAK script: not to be beautiful, not to be clever, not to display the designer's knowledge of world writing systems. To witness, faithfully, with full faculties, in a form that any reader can receive.

The six axioms, the three pictographic-inheritance rules, the six cohesion rules, the alignment sieve, the promotion-of-understanding axis, the ihsanme standard — all of these are tools for one thing: fidelity to the witness.

Draw faithfully. The Ground sees the work.
