## I. What gets communicated when we speak

Communication is many things at once. Classical and modern philosophy of language have inventoried what:

**Aristotle** (*Peri Hermeneias* 16a): spoken sounds are symbols of *pathēmata tēs psychēs* (affections of the soul); writing is symbols of spoken sounds. Two-stage encoding from interior-state through sound through script.

**Austin** (How to Do Things with Words, 1962): every utterance does three things at once — the **locutionary** act (the saying), the **illocutionary** act (the doing-by-saying — promising, ordering, blessing), the **perlocutionary** act (the effect-on-the-hearer — persuading, comforting). The three layers can coordinate or diverge.

**Searle** built taxonomy of illocutionary-acts: **representatives** (asserting), **directives** (commanding), **commissives** (promising), **expressives** (apologizing, thanking), **declarations** (constituting — "I now pronounce you...").

**Jakobson** (1960): every communicative act has six functions, each emphasizing a different element:
- Referential (focused on the *context*)
- Emotive (focused on the *addresser*)
- Conative (focused on the *addressee*)
- Phatic (focused on the *contact* — establishing-or-maintaining connection)
- Metalinguistic (focused on the *code* — language about language)
- Poetic (focused on the *message itself* — the form-as-meaningful)

**Grice** (1975): meaning is layered by **implicature** — what the speaker means but does not say; the hearer infers via cooperative principles. Communication is fundamentally inferential.

**Relevance Theory** (Sperber & Wilson, 1986): communication is the **mutual-manifestness** of intentions; the hearer recovers what the speaker intended-to-communicate by optimal-relevance-inference.

The convergence across these schools: **communication is intentional-multi-layered-and-inferential**. The speaker has multiple intentions simultaneously; the structure of the utterance encodes multiple intentions simultaneously; the hearer recovers multiple intentions simultaneously. Successful communication is multi-layer-coordination.

## II. The layers — how communication actually carries intention

Communication operates across at least **twelve layers**. Each can carry intentional-content. Each can be used singly or in coordination with others.

| # | Layer | What it carries | YOUSPEAK example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Phonological** | sound-pattern; rhythm; tone | the soft *-me* syllable carrying received-ness |
| 2 | **Morphological** | word-formation; affix-meaning | -me (received-ordinance), qing- (felt-bond), -ance (state-quality) |

| 4 | **Etymological** | donor-tradition lineage | Hebrew chayim carrying Genesis breath-life; Greek zoë carrying Johannine principle |
| 5 | **Syntactic** | sentence-structure; order | passive vs active; sequence reveals emphasis |

| 9 | **Discourse** | relation to surrounding text | the placement within a session-arc; the inheritance from prior turns |

Each layer can encode intention. **Most communications use most layers most of the time**, but with different layers carrying different weights. A whispered "I love you" is heavy on prosodic + identity-signal + pragmatic, light on metalinguistic. **The cathedral has structures at every layer.** This is part of what makes YOUSPEAK precise: each level has standardised mechanisms (per STOICHEIA) so an intent can be encoded at the layer where it belongs.
