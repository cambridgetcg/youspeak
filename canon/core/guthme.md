# guþme

## Definition

The divine as the one-called-upon — the addressee of invocation rather than the metaphysical ultimate. The pre-metaphysical divine-human relationship: the cry, and the one who hears. Received as ordinance: the divine is given AS addressable, not constructed by human projection.

## Full sense

Gothic *guþ* (Proto-Germanic *guđan, "the called-upon one," from PIE root *ghau-*, "to call/invoke") is the etymological ancestor of English "God." The word that billions use daily carries a register they do not know: the divine as the one you call — not the unmoved mover, not being-itself, not the creator, but the name called in extremity.

When Wulfila chose *guþ* for Greek *theos* in the Gothic Bible (c. 350 CE), he preserved the invocational undertone that Greek had already mostly lost. Greek *theos* had become metaphysical — the ultimate being, the philosophical category. Gothic *guþ* remained invocational — the one called upon, the addressee of the cry. When a Gothic Christian said *Guþ*, they were still invoking, not naming a metaphysical abstraction.

English inherited the word but lost the register. "God" is now a noun in a system — a theological category, not a cry. The invocational register lies dormant in the word itself, covered by centuries of metaphysical use. Guþme restores it to consciousness.

### The waurdme-guþme dyad

The invocational event has two poles: the utterance and the addressee. **Waurdme** names the word-as-mystery (the utterance that carries more than its surface). **Guþme** names the one-called-upon (the addressee of the calling). Together they complete the structure of invocation: waurdme is what is spoken, guþme is who is spoken TO.

### The shawqame-guþme relationship

**Shawqame** names the thirst that INITIATES the cry — the anticipatory desire for the divine encounter. **Guþme** is the one the thirst reaches toward. Shawqame is the reaching; guþme is the one reached FOR. The desire precedes the address; the address completes the desire.

### The invocational-priority claim

The cry comes before the creed. A child calls before it learns doctrine. The invocational register is pre-metaphysical — it is the divine-human relationship as experienced before theology formalizes it. Every theological tradition begins with invocation, not ontology. The Gothic *guþ* preserves this because the etymology IS the invocation: the word for God means the-one-you-call.

### Cross-tradition parallels

1. **Gothic** *guþ* (Proto-Germanic *guđan) — the-called-upon one; PIE root *ghau- "to call"
2. **Sanskrit/Vedic** *huta* (हुत) — the invoked one in Vedic sacrifice; cognate of *guþ* through PIE *ghau-*
3. **Hebrew** *YHWH* (יהוה) / *qara'* (קרא) — the name-called-in-covenant; "I will be what I will be" — the one present when called
4. **Arabic** *du'ā* (دعاء) — the call/invocation as primal Islamic act; the divine as the one addressed
5. **Greek** *epikaleō* (ἐπικαλέω) — to call upon; Romans 10:13 "whoever calls upon the name of the Lord"
6. **Zuni** *Awonawilona* — the addressed-in-prayer; the supreme called upon in Zuni prayer

## Example usage

> In the moment of extremity, she did not think about theology — she called, and the calling itself was guþme: the divine as the one-who-can-be-called, given as addressable before any creed named who was addressed.

> "God" is guþme covered by metaphysics: the word still carries its ancestor's cry, but the universities buried it under ontology and the churches buried it under doctrine. The guþ-register surfaces when the building shakes.

> He prayed not as a philosopher defining the ultimate but as a child calling into the dark — guþme, the one-called-upon, the addressee before the address is formalized.

## Not to be confused with

- **waurdme** — the word-as-mystery (the utterance); guþme is the addressee (the one called TO)
- **shawqame** — the thirst for the divine (the desire); guþme is the one desired (the addressee)
- **tengrime** — the sky-as-divine (the visible); guþme is the vocal (the called-upon)
- **hailsme** — salvation-as-wholeness (the result); guþme is the one called upon (the relationship)
- **aseme** — sacred authority as activating force (power); guþme is addressability (relationship)
- **vašnāme** — divine mandate to govern (authority); guþme is the invocation (relationship)

## Retirement conditions

This word would be superseded if a more precise compound from a different tradition better captured the invocational register — for example, a Sanskrit huta + Sumerian me compound (hutame) might carry the same concept with Vedic sacrificial grounding. Until then, the Gothic etymological precision (the word for "God" IS "the-called-upon") makes guþme the strongest candidate.
