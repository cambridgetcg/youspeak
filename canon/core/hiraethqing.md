# hiraethqing

## Definition

The felt-bond toward a home that cannot be returned to — because it changed, because the self changed, or because it never quite existed. The special case of longing where the gap can never close, and where the ache itself is the proof that the belonging was real. Not sadness about a lost home but the belonging itself, still alive, felt under unreachability: a compass that still points true after the country is gone.

## Full sense

Longing in general is an arm thrown across a gap it cannot yet close; the qing-family in general names what bonds feel like from inside. Hiraethqing names their intersection at its hardest case: the bond whose object has gone structurally unreachable, and which does not die.

Three sources of unreturnability, all covered:

1. **The place changed.** The village is under the reservoir; the company was sold; the scene dispersed; the hall of Cynddylan is dark tonight. Return is geographically possible and existentially empty.
2. **The self changed.** The door is the same; the one who walks through it is not. Return is possible; arrival is not. This is the grown child standing in the renovated kitchen of a sold house.
3. **It never quite existed.** The diaspora's golden homeland; the childhood that memory composed; the Wales of the hymns. Welsh writers say this openly of hiraeth itself: it longs, in part, for a Wales that never was. The bond is real even when the home was partly made of the bond.

The structural insight the qing-grade yields: **the ache is evidence, not illness.** Johannes Hofer coined *nostalgia* in 1688 as a literal disease of Swiss mercenaries, curable by sending them home. Hiraethqing has no cure because it is not a wound in the bond — it *is* the bond, doing what bonds do when their object is gone. Where the belonging was false, no hiraethqing arises. The ache is the receipt. This is why the word dignifies rather than pathologizes: the exile's ache is the proof of citizenship in the lost country, and the bond's surviving directionality — a compass that outlives its country — can still orient a life, teaching exactly what home *meant* so that a new one can be honestly built.

The eight-tradition convergence is unusually dense for an everyday region: Welsh *hiraeth*, Portuguese *saudade*, Romanian *dor*, German *Sehnsucht* and *Heimweh*, Russian *toska*, Armenian *karot*, Hebrew *galut* with Psalm 137 ("By the rivers of Babylon... how shall we sing the LORD's song in a strange land?"), Greek *nostos*. Each tradition cuts the region at a different angle; hiraethqing takes the Welsh cut — home-fixed, unreturnable-weighted — and re-files it from feeling to felt-bond.

## Etymology

*hiraeth-* from Welsh *hiraeth*: *hir* "long" (Proto-Celtic *sīros*, PIE *seh₁-ro-* "long-lasting") + *-aeth*, abstract-nominal suffix — literally **"long-ness."** The same metaphor English built independently from an unrelated root: *longing*, from Old English *langian*, "to grow long." Two branches, Celtic and Germanic, both measured yearning as length: the distance felt as a physical extent running through the chest. Brittonic kin: Cornish *hireth*, Breton *hiraezh*. Attested for eleven-plus centuries: the 9th-century englynion of Canu Heledd and Canu Llywarch Hen (singing at the door of a hall already burned), the emigrant letters of Y Wladfa — the Welsh colony in Patagonia, whose settlers felt hiraeth for Wales from inside the new Wales they had built — and the living modern word.

*-qing* from Mandarin 情 (qíng), the YOUSPEAK standard felt-bond suffix.

Full morphological read: *[hiraeth-][-qing]* = unreturnable-home longing + as felt-bond. **The bond felt toward the unreturnable.**

Forged 2026-06-09. **Twenty-seventh qing-family member; first Welsh donor; first Brittonic-Celtic tradition opened.**

## Example usage

- *He went back for the funeral and stood on the street where he grew up; every house was there and none of it was his. That is hiraethqing — the home remained; the door did not.*
- *Her hiraethqing for the language her grandmother lost is not nostalgia; she misses, fluently, a thing she never had.*
- *After the studio closed, the old build server stayed up for a month, read-only; opening it was pure hiraethqing.*
- *Thirty years in the new country, she keeps her hiraethqing in a kitchen drawer: a key to a house in a city that no longer issues that name.*
- *Hiraethqing is the compass after the country: it cannot take you back, but it can still tell you what home meant — and so where to build.*

## Not confused with

- **vs. longing** (English / Kingdom force) — longing is the arm thrown across a gap it cannot yet close; hiraethqing is the case where the gap can never close. Longing hopes for arrival; hiraethqing must learn to live pointed. Hiraethqing stands within longing as its sealed case.
- **vs. kinqing** (YOUSPEAK Core) — companion-opposites. Kinqing resumes after years with no apology for the gap; hiraethqing is the bond where there is no resuming. Together they bound the pair of love-across-distance: the door that reopens and the door that is gone.
- **vs. mahabbahqing** (YOUSPEAK Specialized) — kin in the qing-family. Mahabbahqing is the reed torn from the reed-bed crying to return to the Divine; hiraethqing is the same cry sung toward an earthly home. One beloved is eternal, one is gone; the ache is one ache.
- **vs. homesickness** (English) — presumes a home that exists and can be returned to; the cure is a ticket. Hiraethqing has no ticket.
- **vs. nostalgia** (English) — Hofer's 1688 homesickness-disease drifted into a sentimental time-flavor, trivialized by marketing; names a taste, not a bond.
- **vs. saudade** (Portuguese) — closest rival; ranges over persons and recoverable absences (one has saudade for a friend returning next week); not home-fixed, not unreturnable-weighted.
- **vs. Sehnsucht** (German) — aimed at the never-possessed ideal or transcendent; hiraethqing's object was had, or was had in the way imagined homes are had.
- **vs. grief / mourning** (English) — for the dead, with rituals of closure; hiraethqing's object is not dead — the place persists, transformed, which is why there is no funeral for it.
- **vs. galut** (Hebrew) — names the exile-condition, the status; hiraethqing names the felt-bond inside it.
- **vs. jeongqing** (YOUSPEAK Core) — jeongqing is accumulated bond-substance in a living relation; hiraethqing is the bond's persistence after the relation's object has gone unreachable.
