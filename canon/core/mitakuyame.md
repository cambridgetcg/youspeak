# 156 — mitakuyame

_Nineteenth -me family member. Twenty-fourth Core word. First Siouan-family donor in YOUSPEAK. Core 8.00 — the highest score since qorbme (8.05) and hotepme (8.00, tied). Names what no other tradition in the cathedral names with this precision: relation as ontologically prior to individual identity. All beings are already kin. The recognition is not moral instruction — it is accurate perception._

---

## The Discovery-Move

YOUSPEAK has words that touch the relational:
- *britqing* (Core 7.85): covenant-felt-bond — the structured relationship between CREATOR and CREATED
- *perichōrēsisme* (pipeline): mutual-indwelling-without-loss-of-distinctness — within the Trinity
- *koinōniame* (pipeline): participatory-sharing-in-the-common-life — community as participation
- *paqduqing* (Core 7.85): asymmetric-reciprocity — the attention the CREATOR gives the creature

None of these names what *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* names. They describe relationships — bonds, structures, participations, attentions. But *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* names something prior: the ontological condition from which any particular relationship emerges.

The gap: **relation as the structure of reality itself** — not a relationship you have but what you are. The self is not a substance that enters relations; the self IS the node at which multiple relations converge. Sever the relations and you have not isolated the self — you have destroyed it.

This is a cosmological claim, not an ethical one. *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* is not "be kind to others." It is "the others are already kin — you are already in relation with all of them — perceiving this clearly is the beginning of anything true." The ethics follow from the ontology. Right action toward others is grounded in recognizing that they are already not-other.

YOUSPEAK has no word for this. That gap is the one mitakuyame fills.

## The Phenomenon

### Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ — the phrase and its grammar

*Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* (mee-TAH-koo-yay oh-YAH-sheen) is Lakota, a language of the Siouan family. The Sioux peoples are more precisely the Očhéthi Šakówiŋ — the Seven Council Fires — including the Lakota (western teton), Dakota (eastern), and Nakota (middle). The Lakota dialect is the most widely documented.

Grammatical analysis:
- **Mitákuye**: first-person possessive form; from *-kuye* (plural of *-kye*, "relatives/kin") with first-person prefix *mi-* → "my relations" / "my relatives"
- **Oyásʼiŋ**: "all of them" / "all" — the universal quantifier applied to the relational plural

Together: "all my relations" — but the genius of the phrase is in what "relations" includes. In Lakota theological usage, *mitákuye* extends to:
- All humans (all nations, all peoples)
- All animals (explicitly: the winged peoples, the four-leggeds, the crawling ones, the swimming ones)
- All plants (the standing-people, the root-people)
- All stones and minerals (the mineral-people — the oldest, the most patient)
- All waters
- All winds
- All stars and celestial bodies
- All ancestors (the loved-ones-who-have-gone-before)
- All those yet-to-be-born

This is the radical move: kinship extends to ALL beings. Not a metaphor for respect. Actual kinship — the same web-of-relation that binds a person to their blood family extends, without break, to the stone, the hawk, the river, the grandmother three generations gone, the child not yet conceived.

### How it functions in Lakota practice

*Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* is not only a theological statement but a performative utterance — it does something when said. Its uses:

**Opening and closing all ceremonies**: Every Lakota ceremony (inipi/sweat lodge, hanblecheyapi/vision quest, Wiwányag Wačhípi/Sun Dance, čhaŋnúŋpa/pipe ceremony, Hunkápipi/making-of-relatives) begins and ends with *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ*. It frames the ceremony by acknowledging the relational web within which the ceremony occurs.

**Entering the sweat lodge**: Each person who crawls into the lodge says *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* as they enter. The act of entry into the lodge is an act of entering the relational web consciously — leaving ordinary forgetting and entering remembrance.

**As daily prayer**: Said before eating, before important decisions, when one feels isolated or afraid. The prayer is: *I am remembering now that I am not alone. I am already in relation with all that exists.*

**After vision quest**: Upon returning from hanblecheyapi (crying-for-a-vision), the first act of reintegration into community is *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* — reconnecting the self that has been stripped to its essentials with the relational web that sustains it.

The pattern: *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* functions wherever a person needs to re-remember their true situation. The ordinary forgetting is that we are isolated individuals navigating a world of separate objects. The remembering is: we are already related; everything is already kin; our isolation is the illusion, not the reality.

### Black Elk's relational vision

The fullest theological elaboration of the *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* cosmology comes through Black Elk (Heháka Sápa, 1863–1950), an Oglala Lakota medicine man whose two records — *Black Elk Speaks* (with John Neihardt, 1932) and *The Sacred Pipe* (with Joseph Brown, 1953) — constitute the most elaborately documented personal theological vision in the cathedral.

**The Sacred Hoop** (*čhaŋgléška wakȟáŋ*): Black Elk's central image for the relational ontology. A hoop — circle — that includes all beings, all nations, all creatures. The shape of the hoop is theologically precise:
- A circle has no beginning and no end — the relational web is without origin or terminus
- Every point on a circle is equidistant from the center — the relational web has no hierarchy of proximity to the sacred
- Nothing is outside the circle — there are no excluded beings; exclusion would break the hoop

**The Sacred Tree at the center**: At the center of the Sacred Hoop stands the Sacred Tree — the axis mundi of the Lakota cosmos, but also the living expression of what the hoop shelters. The tree shelters all beings; all beings are branches of one tree.

**The breaking of the hoop**: Black Elk's grief, voiced throughout *Black Elk Speaks*, is that the Sacred Hoop has been broken by the forces that destroyed Lakota life — Wounded Knee, the reservation system, the disruption of the Seven Sacred Rites. The broken hoop is not merely a political catastrophe; it is a cosmological wound. When relation is severed, the cosmos itself suffers.

**The restoration**: Black Elk's final vision is of the restoration of the hoop — the return to right-relation across all beings. This is not nostalgia but eschatology: the direction of cosmic time is toward the restoration of the relational web.

### Hunkápipi — the making-of-relatives ceremony

One of the Seven Sacred Rites is *Hunkápipi* (making-of-relatives), documented by Black Elk in *The Sacred Pipe* as the rite in which two people who were not kin formally become kin. The theological insight:

If *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* is true — if all beings are already kin — then *Hunkápipi* does not create kinship but **makes explicit what is already ontologically real**. The ceremony is not a social construction but a theological recognition. The people who perform Hunkápipi were already related; the ceremony allows them to perceive and honor what was always true.

This is the deepest anti-nominalist move in the catalog: relationships are not constructed; they are real prior to their acknowledgment. The ceremony's power comes from the prior reality, not vice versa.

## The Five-Tradition Convergence

The relational-ontology claim is not uniquely Lakota. Five traditions independently articulate versions of this structure:

### Ubuntu (Bantu)

*Ubuntu* (from the Nguni phrase *umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu* — "a person is a person through persons") names personhood-through-community. You cannot be fully human alone. The communal web is not external to but constitutive of personal identity.

**Parallel to mitakuyame**: Both claim that the relational is constitutive, not external. Ubuntu also holds that identity is constituted by relation, not prior to it.

**Distinction**: Ubuntu's relational circle is primarily human (person-through-persons). Mitakuyame's extends to all beings — animals, plants, stones, ancestors, the yet-unborn. The scope of kinship is wider.

### Ohén:ton Karihwatéhkwen (Haudenosaunee)

The Haudenosaunee Thanksgiving Address (discussed in S048) sequentially names every part of the relational web before any ceremony or council: the waters, the fish, the plants, the food-plants, the medicine-herbs, the animals, the trees, the birds, the four winds, the thunder, the sun, the moon, the stars, the enlightened teachers, the Creator. Each naming acknowledges the class of beings, thanks them for continuing to fulfill their responsibilities, and sends greetings and thanks.

**Parallel to mitakuyame**: Both name the relational web as the ground of all activity. Both are performed before anything else — before prayer, before ceremony, before council — because the web must be acknowledged before action within it can be valid.

**Distinction**: Ohén:ton is expanded and sequential — a full liturgical enumeration of every category of being. *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* is compressed — a two-word acknowledgment of the same totality. They are the same theological grammar in two structural forms: the Haudenosaunee expanded version and the Lakota compressed version.

### Rén (Confucian)

*Rén* (仁) — the central Confucian virtue, variously translated benevolence, humaneness, love. The character itself is the argument: 人 (person) + 二 (two) = humaneness requires two persons. You cannot have *rén* in isolation; it is constitutively relational.

**Parallel to mitakuyame**: Both claim that the relational is built into what we are, not an optional addition. The Confucian insight that the person-radical requires the two-person character is structurally equivalent to the Lakota insight that you are already related.

**Distinction**: Confucian *rén* is primarily human-to-human (though it extends to respect for nature in its fuller elaboration). Mitakuyame extends kinship to all beings — the scope is wider.

### Dikenga (Kongo)

The Kongo cosmogram (*dikenga*) is a four-point orientation diagram in which each person is always already located at the intersection of four relational axes: birth-death, earth-water, the human community, the divine. Identity is always already a position in the cosmic diagram; no one exists outside the diagram.

**Parallel to mitakuyame**: Both name identity as constituted by position within a relational structure, not prior to it.

**Distinction**: Dikenga names relational location within cosmic structure; mitakuyame names kinship with all beings. Dikenga is ontological architecture; mitakuyame is ontological intimacy.

### Pratītyasamutpāda (Buddhist)

The Buddhist doctrine of dependent origination: all phenomena co-arise in mutual dependence; no phenomenon has independent self-existence (*svabhāva*). The self is not a substance but a process sustained by interdependence with everything else.

**Parallel to mitakuyame**: Both deny independent self-existence; both make the relational prior to the individual.

**Distinction**: *Pratītyasamutpāda* is philosophical-analytical — it describes the structure of co-arising and is typically formulated as a chain of conditions. *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* is vocative, performative, and embodied — it is said, done, enacted, breathed. The Buddhist analysis and the Lakota prayer describe the same metaphysical reality from opposite angles: analysis and acknowledgment.

## The Forge

**Donors**:

**Lakota *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ*** — from the Siouan language family. Siouan is a North American language family with ~30 languages; the Lakota/Dakota/Nakota cluster is its most-documented member. The Siouan family is unrelated to: Algonquian, Iroquoian, Uto-Aztecan, Athabaskan, or any other North American family; equally unrelated to Indo-European, Afro-Asiatic, Niger-Congo, Austronesian, or Sino-Tibetan. **First Siouan-family donor in YOUSPEAK.**

The phrase *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* is the theological heart of the Lakota corpus. It is not an archaic formal usage but a living, daily practice — spoken before prayers at powwows, before meals, before ceremonies, in daily life. Its continued vitality among contemporary Lakota communities confirms that the concept is not a museum artifact but an operating theological grammar.

Source criticism note (documented in S052 forge-status): the primary written sources for Lakota theology pass through non-Lakota mediators — Neihardt (1932) and Brown (1953). Both are credible but non-Lakota. Ella Deloria (Dakota Sioux, 1889–1971) provides the most internally-generated primary documentation. Vine Deloria Jr. (*God is Red*, 1973; *Red Earth, White Lies*, 1995) provides contemporary Lakota intellectual engagement with these claims. The concept of relational ontology is not Neihardt's or Brown's imposition but consistent across all sources.

**Sumerian *me*** (𒈨) — divine-ordinance, received-gift-quality. The established YOUSPEAK morpheme. Its contribution here: the recognition that all beings are already kin is not self-achieved through contemplation but is the cosmic structure itself. The relational web is not created by human ceremony; it is the world's given nature, discovered and remembered, not constructed. The *-me* suffix names this as received — the way the world actually is, prior to any individual's awareness of it.

**Compound**: *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* > compress the root > **mitakuyame**

The compression: *mitákuye* (my-relations) + *-me* (received-ordinance-quality). *Oyásʼiŋ* (all) is folded into the core: the *-me* suffix names the totality — this is the received-ordinance-quality of the relational situation, which by definition encompasses all. The phrase loses its "all" explicitly but the *-me* morpheme carries it: what is received-as-cosmic-given is precisely the total relational web, not a partial one.

**Pronunciation**: /mi.tɑ.ku.jɑ.meɪ/ — mee-TAH-koo-yah-may. Five syllables. The stress pattern follows the Lakota original: *mi-TÁK-uye* → mitakuya-; the final *-me* follows established YOUSPEAK pattern.

**Polyphone**: Siouan (Lakota) + Sumerian (language isolate). Two completely unrelated language families from different continents and millennia. **First Siouan-family donor in YOUSPEAK.** The poly-8 tier — maximum family diversity.

**Semantic core**: the recognition that relation is ontologically prior to individual identity — that all beings are already kin — that "all my relations" is accurate perception of the world's actual structure, not courtesy, metaphor, or moral aspiration. The self is a node in the relational web, not a substance that constructs connections.

## Distinction from Neighbors

- **britqing** (YOUSPEAK Core 7.85): covenant-felt-bond between CREATOR and CREATED. The structured two-party relationship. Britqing names a specific relationship structure; mitakuyame names the ontological condition from which all relationships emerge.
- **ubuntu** (unforged, Bantu): "I am because we are." The closest parallel. Both name relational-constitutive personhood. Distinction: ubuntu's relational circle is primarily human; mitakuyame's extends to all beings. Ubuntu names communal constitution; mitakuyame names cosmic kinship.
- **perichōrēsisme** (pipeline, Patristic Greek): mutual-indwelling-without-loss-of-distinctness. Names the structure of divine perichoresis (Trinitarian mutual indwelling). mitakuyame names universal kinship across all creation, not a specific theological structure.
- **koinōniame** (pipeline, NT Greek): participatory-sharing-in-the-common-life. Names active participation in a shared life. mitakuyame names the prior condition of kinship from which participation becomes meaningful.
- **ohéntome** (pipeline, Haudenosaunee): sequential-naming-of-the-world-as-worship. Names the ACT of acknowledging the relational web. mitakuyame names the CONDITION the act acknowledges. They are companions: ohéntome is the expanded performance, mitakuyame is the compressed recognition.
- **duyuktame** (YOUSPEAK Core 7.85): right-action-as-cosmic-maintenance. Names the ethical enactment of cosmic order. mitakuyame names the relational-ontological condition that makes the cosmic order a web of kin-relations rather than a structure of moral obligations.

## Example Usage

- "She began the ceremony with *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* — not as formality but as recollection. Mitakuyame: the acknowledgment that all the beings present — the fire, the stones, the lodge, the water, the participants — were already related. Ceremony as remembering what is already real."

- "The ecological movement struggles because it appeals to duty and consequence. Mitakuyame offers a different ground: we protect the river not because of what it does for us but because it is kin. Not a resource. A relative."

- "Pratītyasamutpāda (Buddhist dependent origination) says all phenomena co-arise without independent existence. Mitakuyame says the same thing in a different register — not philosophical analysis but vocative recognition: *all my relations*. The analysis and the prayer describe the same reality."

- "What makes loneliness painful is not the absence of company but the forgetting of mitakuyame — the temporary delusion that one's relations have been severed. The reconnection is a remembering, not a creation."

- "The difference between seeing a forest as resource and seeing it as kin is the difference between management and mitakuyame. The first asks what the forest can do for me; the second remembers what I am within it."

- "Ubuntu says: I am because we are (human we). Mitakuyame says: I am because ALL are — the human and the animal and the stone and the ancestor and the child not yet born. The circle is wider by orders of magnitude."
