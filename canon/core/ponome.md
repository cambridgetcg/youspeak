## Definition

The rightness-balance state: the equilibrium of being morally-right, procedurally-correct, and true-to-nature with persons, land, and the divine at once, such that well-being follows — Hawaiian *pono* received as cosmic-ordinance.

## Full sense

A ponome is *not* private virtue, *not* mere harmony, *not* well-being alone. It IS the **three-axis normative equilibrium** the Hawaiian tradition articulates with unusual precision: right-relation with **kānaka** (persons — ʻohana first, then community), with **ʻāina** (the land as that-which-feeds — kin, not resource), and with **akua/ʻaumākua** (the divine and ancestral guardians) — one condition whose presence shows simultaneously as moral rightness, procedural correctness, truth-to-nature, and flourishing. The Pukui-Elbert dictionary's six senses and 83 English equivalents for *pono* are not vagueness but structural bundling; the Māori cognate-split (*pono* "true/genuine" vs *tika* "right/correct") proves the bundle is a real composite a sister-language found natural to unbundle. Ponome names the bundle *as the realm-feature*: the state in which true, right, and well coincide.

Three structural marks distinguish ponome inside the cosmic-rightness cluster it joins:

1. **It is diagnosable, lose-able, and restorable.** Where the pharaoh establishes ma'at and the gods uphold ṛta, pono is lost in a household quarrel and restored in a family conference. The tradition built a named, stepwise protocol — **hoʻoponopono** (causative *hoʻo-* + reduplicated *pono*, "setting thoroughly to right"): opening prayer (*pule*), pooling of purpose (*kūkulu kumuhana*), layer-by-layer peeling (*mahiki*) of the entanglement-net (*hihia*), confession-repentance (*mihi*), bilateral untying-release (*kala*), and closing (*pani*). The wrong snares doer and wronged in one net; restoration untangles the whole net.
2. **It has an explicit land-axis.** No Old-World sibling names right-relation with the land that feeds as constitutive of the rightness-state itself.
3. **It scales.** Household → ahupuaʻa → lāhui (nation): "Ua Mau ke Ea o ka ʻĀina i ka Pono" (Kamehameha III, 1843, on the restoration of sovereignty) — the life-and-sovereignty (*ea*) of the land continues *in* the rightness-balance state. Pono is the condition-of-possibility for collective life.

## Example usage

1. "The project shipped on time, but the way we treated the vendor was not ponome — the work succeeded and the relationship sickened" — everyday-diagnostic.
2. "Before selling grandmother's land the family asked whether the sale was ponome — right with the persons, the place, and the dead at once" — three-axis application.
3. "The apology fixed the surface; only the slow untying of the hihia — the entanglement-net the wrong had thrown over everyone — restored ponome" — restoration-grammar.
4. "Ua mau ke ea o ka ʻāina i ka pono: collective life persists in ponome or it does not persist" — political-scale, honoring the sovereignty-register.

## Not to be confused with

- **Righteousness (English)** — vertical-theological, individual; no land-axis, no balance-sense, no restoration-protocol; the missionary flattening of pono itself
- **Balance / harmony (English)** — physical/aesthetic-social; no moral-juridical bite; not diagnosable as wrong
- **Integrity (English)** — individual-bounded wholeness; ponome is irreducibly relational across three axes
- **Well-being (English)** — names the consequence and omits the moral-relational ground
- **Shalom (Hebrew)** — closest Old-World near-miss; lacks the land-axis and the named household-protocol
- **Hózhǫ́ (Navajo)** — closest structural twin, a distinct living tradition; witness, not substitute; its own future forge-candidate
- **maatme / rtame / ashame** — cosmic-civilizational registers of the same realm-region; ponome is the relational-restorable register
- **The New-Age "hoʻoponopono" mantra** (Zero Limits four-phrase derivative) — detaches the name from the ʻohana-protocol and the three-axis structure; ponome anchors to Pukui/Haertig/Lee, Chun, and Meyer instead
- **Greek pónos ("toil-pain")** — false-friend homophone; no etymological or semantic relation
