# balagme

## Definition

Formal-grief-deployed-as-liturgical-mechanism-for-divine-return-as-received-ordinance — the ongoing public practice of lament, performed by trained specialists in a designated register, maintained across centuries as living worship, that constitutes the relationship with the divine in the mode of appeal after rupture.

## Full sense

The cathedral has named the individual sacred lament (voghbme — the Narekatsi devotional cry), the inner compunction-mourning (penthosme — the Hesychast gift), the accumulated wound (hanme — the Korean social-historical grief), and the divine withdrawal (telipme — the pulling away). balagme names what none of these reach: the formal communal grief that IS the mechanism of divine return.

The structure balagme names has five constitutive dimensions:

1. **Formal** — the grief is not spontaneous expression but structured practice. The balag has a recognizable form: opening praise, the rupture, the question of duration, intercession, resolution-or-absence. The form is not constraint; it is what makes the grief liturgical rather than merely emotional.

2. **Communal** — the lament is performed by trained specialists (gala-priests) on behalf of a community. It is not the individual cry (that is voghbme) but the public practice that maintains the divine-human relationship across a whole people.

3. **Designated register** — the balag was performed in eme-sal, the "women's tongue" or "fine tongue" dialect of Sumerian. Grief has its own tongue. The lament is not in the administrative language or the royal inscription language; it is in the grief-language, a distinct linguistic register that marks the practice as ontologically distinct from ordinary speech.

4. **Centuries-maintained** — the canonical balag *Uru amma-irabi* was performed continuously for approximately 1500 years. The 1800-year span of the corpus is theological data: the lament is not about a specific historical event but IS the ongoing form the divine-human relationship takes in a world where the divine can withdraw.

5. **Mode of appeal** — many balag laments end without resolution, in ongoing grief, ongoing address, ongoing question. The liturgical function of the unresolved lament is to maintain the relationship in the mode of appeal. As long as we are asking, we are still in relationship.

The -me marks the mechanism as received-ordinance: the lament does not coerce the deity. The formal-grief-as-appeal IS the cosmic structure of how a relationship that has ruptured is held open. The balag drum was itself a bearer of divine ME — the instrument that carries the lament carries the ordinance.

### The 1800-year span as theological data

The fact that balag laments were performed continuously from approximately 2100 BCE to 200 BCE is not merely historical. It is theological data. What does it mean to perform a lament for the destruction of Ur in 400 BCE, 1600 years after Ur's destruction, in a Babylonian temple under Persian imperial administration?

It means: the lament is not about the historical event. The lament IS the relationship. The grief is not a reaction to a specific catastrophe; it is the ongoing form the divine-human relationship takes in a world where the divine can withdraw. The 1500-year continuous performance is the deepest possible witness to the claim that the lament is the mechanism — the formal practice that maintains the relationship in the mode of appeal.

### The balag drum as bearer of divine ME

The balag drum was not merely an instrument. It was a bearer of divine ME — the ordinance-quality that makes sacred practice possible. The instrument that carries the lament carries the ordinance. This is the deepest justification for the -me suffix: the balag is not just a drum; it is a cosmic mechanism. The grief it carries is not just emotion; it is ordinance.

### Cross-tradition parallels

1. Sumerian — balag corpus: 1800-year continuous liturgical grief as divine-return mechanism; the prototype
2. Jewish — Tisha B'Av: annual communal lament for the destruction of the Temple, maintained across 2000 years of diaspora
3. Christian — Good Friday: the liturgical lament for the death of Christ, performed annually as the mechanism of awaiting resurrection
4. Islamic — Ashura: communal lamentation for the death of Husayn at Karbala, maintained across centuries as the mechanism of communal identity and divine appeal
5. Armenian — Genocide commemorative liturgy: annual formal grief as communal practice of memory, appeal, and the maintenance of relationship with the dead and the divine

Each tradition holds: the formal grief is not merely a reaction to loss. It is the PRACTICE — the ongoing liturgical act that maintains the relationship in the mode of appeal. The lament is the mechanism. The grief is the worship.

## Not to be confused with

- **voghbme** — sacred-lament-as-received-ordinance (Armenian, the Narekatsi register). Voghbme is the INDIVIDUAL devotional cry — I address GoD with my grief. Balagme is the COMMUNAL liturgical mechanism — the formal practice that maintains the divine-human relationship across time and across a whole people. The Narekatsi *Matean* is 95 prayers of one monk; the balag corpus is 1800 years of communal performance.
- **penthosme** — the sacramental gift of compunction-mourning (Hesychast, Climacus). Penthosme is the INNER gift — compunction-mourning that cleanses the heart, the "joyful-sorrow" (charmolypē). Balagme is the OUTER practice — the public, performed, transmitted liturgical act. Penthosme is sacramental and personal; balagme is liturgical and communal.
- **hanme** — accumulated social-historical sorrow as generative ground (Korean). Hanme is the WOUND — the accumulated grief that becomes creative energy. Balagme is the PRACTICE — the formal grief that addresses the wound to the divine as the mechanism of return. Hanme is what is carried; balagme is what is done with it.
- **ehanme** — beautiful longing, the ache-toward. Ehanme reaches forward in longing. Balagme reaches toward the divine in formal grief after rupture. Different temporal structure: ehanme is desire; balagme is appeal.
- **telipme** — the divine pulling away — the withdrawal. Telipme is the divine-side of the rupture. Balagme is the human-side response — the formal grief that maintains the relationship despite telipme.
- **nepsisme** — disciplined-watchful-sobriety (Hesychast). Nepsisme is the vigilance that catches afflicted thoughts at their first stirring. Balagme is the formal grief that addresses the rupture after it has occurred. Nepsisme is preventive; balagme is restorative.

## Retirement conditions

This word would be superseded if a more learnable or more broadly-attested donor were found for the formal-grief-as-liturgical-mechanism concept. The balag is uniquely well-documented (1800-year continuous manuscript transmission) but both donors are Sumerian — a companion forge from a different tradition (e.g., a Jewish qinah-me or an Arabic nuhā-me) could strengthen the polyphone balance. The companion forge candidates galame (the practitioner-pole) and ersemame (the daily-rhythm-pole) remain in the queue; they would complement rather than replace balagme.
