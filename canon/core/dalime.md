# dalime

## Definition

The sacred-encounter-in-wildness-that-constitutes-a-claim-on-you — the divine who comes to you in the wild, externally, unilaterally, and the coming itself is a relational claim with conditions.

## Full sense

The cathedral has named the sacred-seeing (darshanqing), the thirst for the encounter (shawqame), the threshold-crossing (myesime), the messenger (agnime), the activating power (aseme). dalime names what none of these reach: the encounter that CLAIMS you.

The structure dalime names has six constitutive dimensions:

1. **Unsought** — the sacred comes to you; you did not seek it. This distinguishes dalime from hanbléčeyapi (the vision-quest where the seeker goes out) and from shawqame (the thirst that initiates the path).
2. **External** — the sacred meets you from outside, not as indwelling. This distinguishes dalime from enthousiasmos (the divine enters you) and from bittulme (the self-nullification that makes room for the divine within).
3. **Wilderness-mediated** — the encounter happens in ungoverned space, not in temple, not in domesticated sacred architecture. The wild is the medium because the wild is where the structures that mediate ordinary sacred access do not reach.
4. **Constitutive** — the encounter IS the relationship, not a precursor to one. This distinguishes dalime from vocation (a calling to a role — dalime is a claiming through encounter, not an assignment to a task).
5. **Unilateral** — the claim precedes consent. The sacred claims before you choose. This distinguishes dalime from covenant (berith — a mutual agreement; dalime is the sacred claiming before the mutual agreement is possible).
6. **Conditional** — the claim demands something back: fidelity, devotion, non-disclosure. Dali punishes the unfaithful and the boastful. The encounter's gift IS the demand.

The -me marks the claim as received-ordinance: the encounter's demand is not a human projection onto the wild. The claiming-quality is the cosmic gift of how the sacred meets the human in ungoverned space. The -me refuses to treat the claim as psychological interpretation — it is the structure of a world where the sacred encounters as claimant, not as spectacle.

### The numinous is not enough

Rudolph Otto's *fascinans et tremendum* (the numinous as "mysterium tremendum et fascinans") names the awe-and-terror before the sacred. But Otto's structure is a STATE of the beholder — the human feels awe and attraction. dalime names a RELATIONSHIP constituted by the encounter. The sacred does not merely inspire awe; it arrives, and the arrival is a claim. The numinous is what the sacred LOOKS LIKE from the human side; dalime is what the sacred DOES when it comes to you in the wild.

This distinction matters because the numinous, as a state, can be observed, recorded, and left behind. dalime, as a claim, cannot — the encounter follows you. Dali's hunter cannot return to his ordinary life as if the mountain did not claim him. The claim is constitutive: the relationship exists because the encounter happened, and it persists because the encounter's demand persists.

### Cross-tradition parallels

1. Georgian/Svan — Dali: the goddess who appears to hunters in the high Caucasus; the encounter constitutes a conditional relationship
2. Lakota — hanbléčeyapi: the vision-quest as calling-forth; but in hanbléčeyapi the seeker goes out, in Dali the sacred comes to you
3. Mongolian — böö-calling: spirits claim specific persons; the shaman is not self-selected but spirit-claimed; the claim precedes the role
4. Celtic — anamchara (now anamcharame): the soul-friend constituted by encounter; but anamchara is horizontal (friend-to-friend), Dali is vertical (sacred-to-human)
5. Hebrew — Jacob at Peniel, Moses at the bush: the encounter constitutes a claim — "I will not let you go until you bless me"
6. Greek — Artemis to Actaeon: the goddess who appears in the wild; but Actaeon's encounter is destructive because he SEES, Dali's is constitutive because she COMES

"She went into the wilderness to be alone. The mountain had other plans. The encounter was dalime: unsought, external, constitutive — the wild claimed her before she could choose."

## Not to be confused with

- **darshanqing** — reciprocal sacred-seeing (Sanskrit). Darshanqing is mutual — both see. Dalime is unilateral — the sacred claims first; the beholder's seeing is response, not co-constitution.
- **shawqame** — the thirst for the divine encounter (Arabic). Shawqame is the longing that precedes. Dalime is the encounter itself — the sacred arrives unsought.
- **myesime** — the threshold-crossing. Myesime is the crossing. Dalime is the claim that constitutes the threshold by arriving — the crossing happens BECAUSE the sacred came.
- **agnime** — the messenger who IS the message (Vedic). Agnime carries a word. Dali carries a claim — the medium is the relationship, not the message.
- **aseme** — the activating sacred power (Yoruba). Aseme is invoked by the human. Dalime invokes the human — the sacred activates the claim.
- **hanme** — accumulated grief as creative source (Korean). Hanme is the wound that becomes energy. Dalime is the encounter that becomes obligation.
- **anamcharame** — the soul-friend (Old Irish). Anamcharame is horizontal (friend-to-friend). Dalime is vertical (sacred-to-human) — the claim is asymmetric.

## Retirement conditions

This word would be superseded if a more learnable or more widely-attested donor were found for the sacred-encounter-as-relational-claim concept (Dali is specific to one tradition; a more broadly-attested donor would strengthen groundedness). A potential companion could name the condition-fulfillment side — the fidelity the claim demands — but that would be a companion, not a replacement. The Dali tradition is unique in combining all six dimensions (unsought, external, wilderness-mediated, unilateral, constitutive, conditional); no other tradition provides a more accessible donor for this specific combination.
