# ridame

## Definition

Contentment-as-double-directional-convergence received as cosmic ordinance — the stable station where being pleased with God's decree and being pleasing to God become one act.

## Full sense

Ridame is the seventh and final station of the Sufi maqamat-sequence — the culmination of the entire inward path. After the traveler has turned (tawbame), practiced scrupulousness (waraʿ), renounced (zuhd), recognized non-ownership (faqrme), bound to the path (sabrme), and released outcomes (tawakkulme), there remains one final posture: accepting everything — the path, the outcomes, the present, the past, the future — as good. Not because the traveler has calculated that it is good, but because the traveler has stopped calculating. The gap between what one wants and what one receives closes — not because one gets what one wants, but because wanting and receiving become the same act.

The double-direction is the structural mark. Q89:27-28 addresses the *nafs muṭmaʾinna* (the soul at rest): *irjiʿī ilā rabbiki rāḍiyatan marḍiyya* — "return to your Lord, well-pleased (rāḍiyatan) and well-pleasing (marḍiyya)." The two participles share the same root (r-ḍ-w): the active (the creature pleased) and the passive (the creature approved). The same act, two faces. Q98:8 makes the double-direction explicit: *Raḍiya-llāhu ʿan-hum wa-raḍū ʿan-hu* — "God is pleased with them, and they are pleased with Him." The two acts are simultaneous and mutually constituting: to be content with God's decree IS to become the creature God is pleased with.

### The maqamat-sequence context

Ridame is the seventh and final station in the Sufi maqamat-sequence (tawba → waraʿ → zuhd → faqr → ṣabr → tawakkul → riḍā). The sequence maps the full trajectory of the inward path from first turning to final contentment. The three pre-pivot stations (tawba, waraʿ, zuhd) are the path of *giving up* — the traveler sheds what is not-God. Faqr (the pivot) is the recognition that nothing was ever owned. The three post-pivot stations (ṣabr, tawakkul, riḍā) are the path of *receiving* — what becomes possible in the space that poverty opens.

The post-pivot triad is now COMPLETE with ridame: ṣabr (bind to the path) → tawakkul (release the outcome) → riḍā (accept the result). The triad maps the trajectory from commitment (ṣabr) through release (tawakkul) to acceptance (riḍā). Each station is the fruit of the preceding: one cannot truly accept all things until one has bound oneself to remain (ṣabr) and released the management of outcomes (tawakkul).

Ridame is the culmination — the station where the traveler rests. Al-Hujwīrī: "After riḍā there is no further station — not because the path ends but because the one who walks has become the path." The seven stations were not a journey through space but a journey through the layers of resistance. When the last layer dissolves, what remains is the ground one has been standing on all along.

### The three degrees of riḍā (al-Ghazālī)

1. **Riḍā bi-l-qadaʾ** — contentment with divine decree in general. The traveler accepts that God governs all. This is the first degree — the broadest, the most accessible.
2. **Riḍā bi-l-maṣīb** — contentment with specific affliction. The traveler does not merely accept that God governs but embraces what God specifically sends, including suffering. This is the second degree — narrower, deeper.
3. **Riḍā bi-llāh** — contentment with God Himself. The traveler is not content because God gives good things but because God IS. This is the summit — the third degree, where the traveler would be content in the fire, as Ibrāhīm was content in the fire, because the fire is God's decree, and God's decree is the face of God.

### The sabrme-ridame arc

Al-Ghazālī: "The distance between ṣabr and riḍā is the distance between the throat and the heart. Ṣabr swallows what it does not want; riḍā wants what it has swallowed. The one who has ṣabr is like one who drinks bitter medicine knowing it will heal. The one who has riḍā is like one who has tasted the medicine and finds it sweet — not because the medicine changed but because the taster changed."

The arc: sabrme (the wound held shut) → ridame (the wound healed). The binding of sabrme was necessary because the pressure was real; ridame is the point where the binding is no longer necessary because the pressure is no longer opposed. The traveler who had sabrme endured what they did not want; the traveler who has ridame no longer does not want it.

### The tawakkulme-ridame arc

Tawakkulme is about the future — I trust what will happen. Ridame is about the present — I accept what is happening. The two are sequential: one first trusts what will come (tawakkulme), then accepts what does come (ridame). The traveler at tawakkulme may still flinch when the outcome arrives; the traveler at ridame does not flinch — the flinch has been dissolved by the convergence of wanting and receiving.

### Cross-tradition parallels

1. **Arabic/Islamic:** *riḍā* — the maqām of double-directional contentment (Q89:27-28, Q98:8); the seventh and final station.
2. **Christian:** *eudokeia* (εὐδοκία) — divine good pleasure (Matt 3:17: "This is my beloved Son, with whom I am well-pleased"); the Lord's Prayer "thy will be done" as the creature's acceptance. The convergence at the Cross (Luke 22:42: "Not my will but yours be done").
3. **Sanskrit/Hindu:** *saṅtoṣa* (सन्तोष) — contentment as the second *niyama* (Patañjali Yoga Sūtras II.42); the *sthitaprajña* (the one of steady wisdom, Bhagavad Gītā II.55-72).
4. **Pali/Buddhist:** *tathatā* (तथता) — thusness, the acceptance of things as they are without the filter of wanting/not-wanting; also *santosa* (Pali contentment).
5. **Stoic:** *amor fati* — love of fate; Epictetus: "Let your will be that things happen as they do, and you will be happy."
6. **Hebrew/Jewish:** *r-ṣ-h* (רָצָה) — divine pleasure (Ps 147:11: "The Lord takes pleasure in those who fear him"); Ps 23:1 "I shall not want" as contentment.
7. **Daoist:** *ziran* (自然) — self-so, the state of things being as they naturally are; the contentment of non-claiming (Daodejing 17).

## Example usage

After years of practice — the turning (tawbame), the recognition that nothing was owned (faqrme), the binding to the path (sabrme), the releasing of outcomes (tawakkulme) — she arrived at ridame: the station where wanting and receiving became the same act, where being pleased with God's decree was simultaneously becoming the creature God was pleased with.

The traveler at the maqām of ridame does not say "I accept this because I must" (that is sabrme) or "I trust what will come" (that is tawakkulme) but "I embrace what is, and in embracing, I am embraced." The double-direction is the structural mark: the same root, two faces, one act.

"Ridame is not the end of the path," the teacher said, "but the recognition that there was never anywhere to go. The seven stations were not a journey through space but a journey through the layers of resistance. When the last layer dissolves, what remains is not a destination but the ground one has been standing on all along."

## Not to be confused with

- **sukhance** — creature-ease, the feeling of contentment. Ridame is the relational convergence, not the feeling.
- **shukrme** — gratitude for specific gifts. Ridame accepts all that arrives, including affliction.
- **sabrme** — patient endurance, binding to the path. Ridame is the dissolution of the resistance that made binding necessary.
- **tawakkulme** — trust about the future. Ridame is acceptance of the present.
- **faqrme** — recognition of non-ownership. Ridame is the contentment with non-ownership.
- **tawbame** — the first turning. Ridame is the final rest.

## Retirement conditions

Ridame would be superseded if:
1. A more precise compound were coined that captured the double-directional convergence (creature-pleased AND God-pleased) more exactly — but the riḍā + me compound already captures this (the -me marks the divine ordination, and the root r-ḍ-w's morphological duality provides the double-direction).
2. The maqamat-sequence were restructured such that riḍā were not the final station — but the classical tradition consistently positions riḍā as the seventh and final station.
3. A cross-tradition compound were coined that named the convergence without dependence on the Arabic-Sufi framework — but the Arabic donor carries the most precise articulation of the double-direction (the root's active/passive duality is unique among the convergence witnesses).
