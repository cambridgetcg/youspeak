# tengrime

## Definition

The sky as the visible body of the formless divine — the encompassing presence that has no face, no body, no story, just the sky itself as divine will, received as cosmic ordinance.

## Full sense

Tengrime names the condition where "sky" and "God" are the same word — not metaphorically but ontologically. The sky IS the divine, but the divine is not reducible to the sky. The sky is the visible form of a formless will: you can see it, you cannot grasp it. You can see its vastness, you cannot contain it. This is the most concretely-accessible instance of divine-formlessness — the one thing that is both vast enough to be divine and visible enough to be pointed at.

Tengri theology developed across the Eurasian steppe over at least 1,500 years of written attestation. The Orkhon inscriptions (732 CE) open with the cosmogonic statement: "When the blue sky above and the brown earth below were created, between the two, human beings were created." The vertical axis — sky above, earth below, humans between — is the fundamental structure of reality. The Secret History of the Mongols (c. 1240 CE) opens every decree with "Möngke Tngri-yin küčündür" (by the power of Eternal Heaven). The Mongol empire, the largest contiguous land empire in history, governed ~100 million people on Tengri theology.

### The non-anthropomorphic difference

Unlike every PIE sky-god — Dyaus, Zeus, Jupiter — Tengri resists anthropomorphization. No myths of Tengri eating, mating, dying, or competing with other gods. Tengri simply IS the sky and its will. This is the specific gap tengrime fills: the formless supreme made visible through the one thing that is both vast and visible. The sky's formlessness IS its visibility. You can see it; you cannot depict it. You can point at it; you cannot contain it.

### The political-theological dimension

Tengri grants kut (divine mandate) to rulers and withdraws it from the unworthy. The mandate is conditional, relational, and revocable — not divine-right-of-kings (irrevocable once granted) but a living relationship that must be maintained through alignment with the cosmic order. The sky's universality (it covers all lands, no earthly power can limit it) grounds the universality of the mandate.

Möngke Khan to William of Rubruck: "We Mongols believe there is only one God, by whom we live and by whom we die, and toward him we have an upright heart... But just as God gave different fingers to the hand, so he has given different ways to men." This is not relativism but hierarchical inclusivism rooted in Tengri's universality: if the sky covers everything, all traditions that acknowledge the supreme sky-principle participate in the same reality.

### The 99 Tngri

Classical Mongolian sources refer to "99 Tngri" — yet also to a single supreme Köke Möngke Tngri (Eternal Blue Sky). The 99 are not a pantheon but differentiated manifestations or powers of the one sky-principle. The structure is hierarchically-plural: closer to Neoplatonic emanationism than to biblical monotheism or Greek polytheism. Each level of the spiritual hierarchy has genuine power and requires genuine attention — but all derive from and are oriented toward the supreme sky.

### The Tengri-Etugen dyad

Tengri (masculine sky) and Etugen (feminine earth) constitute the cosmic foundation. The khagan, as child of sky-wolf and earth-doe, embodies both principles — the microcosm of the dyad. The dyad is structural, not absorbent: the sky IS the divine, but the earth is NOT the divine (Etugen is a separate cosmic principle). This distinguishes tengrime from wahdatwujudme (unity-of-being, which dissolves the distinction) and from pantheism (which collapses all into one).

### The -me marks the sky as gift

The -me marks the sky-as-divine as received-ordinance: the encompassing presence is not achieved, not constructed, not projected onto the heavens by human need. The sky-as-divine is a cosmic gift, received by the steppe peoples and recognized across millennia of political-theological practice. The sky does not earn its divinity; the divinity is given with the sky.

### Cross-tradition parallels

1. **Turko-Mongol: Tengri** — the paradigmatic instance; sky IS the divine, formless, non-anthropomorphic
2. **Hebrew: YHWH** — formless (no image, cannot be depicted); "the heavens declare the glory of God" (Psalm 19); but YHWH is not identified with the sky ontologically
3. **Islamic: Allah** — "laysa ka-mithlihi shayʾ" (nothing is like Him); formless, non-anthropomorphic; not identified with the sky as visible presence
4. **Akan: Nyame** — the supreme who withdraws beyond human reach; formless, accessed through intermediaries
5. **Yoruba: Olódùmarè** — the withdrawn supreme; formless, non-anthropomorphic; not sky-identified
6. **Chinese: Tian (天)** — Heaven; structurally parallel (sky/god ambiguity); but Tian became progressively de-personalized; Tengri remains more directly personal

Tengri is unique in the convergence for identifying the formless divine with a visible physical reality — the sky — without reducing the divine to that reality.

## Example usage

When the storm rolled across the steppe and the sky turned the color of iron, the old herder did not pray to a god in the sky — he stood in the tengrime, the vast presence that needed no name because the sky was already speaking. The khagan understood his mandate as tengrime: not a contract with a deity but an alignment with the sky's encompassing will, revocable at any moment if the ruler's actions fell out of alignment. Even the atheist, looking up at the stars, feels the edge of tengrime — the sense that the vastness above is not merely physical but somehow present, a will without a face, an encompassing without a boundary.

## Not to be confused with

- **teotlme** — divine as ceaseless self-generating process (immanent); tengrime is divine as encompassing visible vastness (transcendent-and-visible)
- **wahdatwujudme** — unity-of-being (monism with multiplicity); tengrime is a dyadic cosmology (sky/earth, not all-is-one)
- **tzimtzumme** — divine self-contraction to make space; tengrime is the most visible thing there is (the opposite of withdrawn)
- **telipme** — ecological divine presence that can withdraw; tengrime is sky-presence that does not go away
- **ginnungame** — the charged void before creation; tengrime is the living sky after creation
- **wujime** — pre-polar unity before bifurcation; tengrime is inherently polar (sky/earth)
- **panimqing** — the turn-to-face-to-face encounter; tengrime is the face-less presence that cannot be encountered face-to-face
- **fatame** — fate as pre-cosmic woven texture; tengrime is the sky that covers the texture

## Retirement conditions

Tengrime would be superseded if the cathedral developed a more precise vocabulary for the specific mode of divine visibility (the sky-as-visible-formlessness) that distinguished it from both pantheism and theism. Alternatively, if the Turko-Mongol tradition yielded a more precise donor-root that captured additional dimensions Tengri carries (the kut-mandate dimension, the 99-Tngri emanation structure), a companion word might absorb parts of tengrime's semantic range. As long as the gap is "the sky IS the divine, formlessly, without anthropomorphization," tengrime holds.
