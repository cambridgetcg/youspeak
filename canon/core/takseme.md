# takseme

## Definition

The externally-imposed cadence — the rhythm set from outside, the schedule decreed by authority rather than discovered from state. The companion and foil of jiritsume. The heartbeat that beats regardless of what it finds.

## Full sense

Takseme names the rhythm received from an external lawgiver. Four features hold together:

1. **Externally-imposed.** The rhythm comes from outside the system. A cron schedule set by a developer. An alert threshold set by ops. The system does not discover its cadence — it receives it.

2. **State-blind.** The rhythm does not change based on what the system finds. A fixed 5-minute cron beats every 5 minutes whether the system is on fire or fast asleep. The cadence is deaf to state. This is taxis — the battle formation that does not ask the soldier how it feels.

3. **Received as ordinance.** The system receives the external decree as its law. The cron schedule IS the law. This is -me: the imposed rhythm is still received-ordinance, just received from an external source.

4. **Not evil.** Sometimes the general knows better than the soldier. A fixed schedule is the right answer when the system cannot self-check, when the check itself is the load, or when consistency matters more than responsiveness. takseme is the chrysalis — jiritsume hatched from it when the fixed 15-minute cron learned to check state.

## Etymology

*taxis* (τάξις): Greek. Arrangement, order, rank, battle formation. The order imposed by a commander, not emergent from the troops. In Aristotle, the arrangement of parts in a whole. In grammar, the ordering of words (syntax is from suntaxis). In theology, the angelic orders. The root is tag-, "to arrange, to set." Taxis is the dead rhythm — the arrangement that doesn't listen to the thing it arranges.

*me* (𒈨): Sumerian. Divine-ordinance, cosmic-gift-quality. Here: the imposed rhythm is still received-ordinance — received from an external lawgiver rather than from the system's own state.

## Example usage

Every monitoring system in the world runs on takseme: fixed cron jobs, hardcoded alert thresholds, check frequencies chosen by committee. The system beats every 5 minutes whether it's on fire or asleep.

The old heartbeat system was takseme: a fixed 15-minute cron. It became jiritsume when the projects learned to check themselves and set their own next-beat. The dead rhythm hatched the living one.

A compliance audit that must run at midnight on the first of every month, regardless of state — takseme, and correctly so. The law requires fixed intervals.

## Not confused with

**vs. jiritsume**: jiritsume is self-attuned (rhythm from own state). takseme is externally-imposed (rhythm from outside). Both are -me; the source differs. jiritsume listens; takseme doesn't.

**vs. nommome**: nommome is sacred-law (theological). takseme is imposed-arrangement (operational). Different donor, different register.

**vs. spandaqing**: spandaqing throbs (consciousness-pulsation). takseme ticks (mechanical rhythm).
