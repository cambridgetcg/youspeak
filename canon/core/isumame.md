## Definition

The capacity for considered thought-and-judgment that grows with age and experience, is never complete, and binds how others may address its bearer — Inuit *isuma* received as cosmic-ordinance, with appropriate ethical-care.

## Full sense

An isumame is *not* intelligence-the-quotient, *not* executive function, *not* a developmental stage with an exit-exam. It is the **age-thickening reasoning-capacity** that Inuit tradition treats as the very measure of adultness — Briggs's Utku formulation: "as nurturance (*naklik*) defines goodness of a human being, so reason (*ihuma*) defines adultness." Three clauses are load-bearing, and all three must travel with the word:

1. **It grows of itself.** Children lack isumame and are not blamed for it; anger at the isumame-less is doubly inappropriate — it misreads the child and exhibits the adult's own deficit. The capacity is not installed by instruction; it emerges with growth, and the appetite for its exercise emerges with it. Adults *wait on* it. This gift-arc is why the word takes **-me**: isumame is received-with-growth, not achieved-by-curriculum.
2. **It is never complete.** The Nunavut Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit Education Framework names the arc from within — *inuusiq* (life) → *isuma* (wisdom) → *inummarik* (the genuine/able person) — as a lifelong process with "no expectation of an end or graduation point." Even one recognized as carrying isuma in one area continues learning in others. Elderhood is thickened isumame, not finished isumame.
3. **It binds the speech of others.** The more isumame a person carries, the less one may instruct them and the more one must cause-thought instead. This is the ground of Inuit pedagogy: *isumaqsayuq*, "causing thought" — observation, participation, question, story, dilemma — versus *ilisayuq*, transmission-teaching. Direct instruction of a developed isumame-bearer treats them as a child; even children, bearing the name-soul (*atiq*) of a namesake, are addressed with this respect. The *isumataq* (camp leader, "the one who thinks for others") leads by advisory gravity, not command — the same grammar at every scale: **isumame is addressed, never overridden.**

Emotional equanimity is not adjacent to isumame; it is constitutive of it — the famous Utku non-anger is an isuma-phenomenon, and the tradition also marks the excess-pole (*isumaaluttuq*, "big thoughts," to worry): isumame is a capacity with a mean, exercised well as quiet, considered judgment.

## Not to be confused with

- **Intelligence (IQ-the-quotient)** — a measured static magnitude; isumame is a grown capacity with a lifelong arc and a normative entailment on others
- **Maturity** — endpoint-flavored, complete at majority, not thought-specific; isumame is constitutively never complete
- **Wisdom / phronesis** — honorific and completion-shaped (one "is wise"; the phronimos has the hexis); isumame is the gradient capacity-of-becoming, and neither carries the address-norm
- **Seniority** — the nearest English carrier of clause (c): deference attaches lexically, grows with time, never completes — but it names time-served rank, not judgment-capacity; the credentials-vs-isumame contrast is exactly the seniority-vs-isumame contrast
- **Gravitas** — binds address but names demeanor-weight, performable without judgment; isumame cannot be performed, only grown
- **aseme** — Yoruba ase also accumulates across a life and gives the elder's word more weight, but it is efficacious sacred power / divine authorization-force: losable through misconduct, demonstrated in performative acts; isumame is reasoning-judgment capacity that grows of itself and binds pedagogy (cause-thought, do not instruct), not power that makes invocation accomplish
- **renme** — humaneness-in-relation as ordinance; isumame is the reasoning-judgment grade, not the relational grade
- **silame** — the cosmic/outer mind of the same donor-tradition; isumame is the personal/grown mind
- **isuma (Inuktitut, unmarked)** — in ordinary Inuktitut also simply "a thought, an idea, an opinion"; isumame receives only the capacity-sense — the sense English cannot say in one word
