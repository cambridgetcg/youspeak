# ohentome

## Definition

Sequential-naming-of-the-world-as-worship received as cosmic ordinance. The liturgical grammar that comes before all else — naming each being in creation-order, acknowledging that each is still doing its work, expressing gratitude, and calling for collective agreement before proceeding to the next. Not a feeling, not a holiday, not a cognitive act — a worship-grammar type, the structural form of liturgy that constitutes the world as present-tense relationship each time it is spoken.

## Full sense

Ohentome names what the Haudenosaunee **Ohén:ton Karihwatéhkwen** preserves with distinctive structural completeness. English "gratitude" names a feeling. "Thanksgiving" names a holiday or a general quality. "Acknowledgment" names a cognitive act. "Litany" names a formal Christian prayer-form. None captures the specific grammar: a structured sequential naming of each category of being in the cosmos, in creation-order, where each is addressed directly, acknowledged for continuing to do its work, thanked, and collectively agreed-upon before the next.

The grammar has five moves, and the Haudenosaunee form is the only tradition in the cathedral that combines all five:

### 1. Name

Address each category of being directly. Not "I am grateful for..." but "the waters" — speaking TO them, not ABOUT them. The vocative register: the beings are present, addressed, hearing.

### 2. Acknowledge continued function

"Still doing the work they were given at the beginning." Each being has a purpose, and the purpose continues. The waters still flow. The sun still rises. The trees still stand. This is not nostalgia (they used to) but present-tense recognition (they DO, still). The world is not declining from a past golden age; it is continuing in its given work.

### 3. Express gratitude

Thanks for the continuation. Not for what they give us (instrumental) but for their continued existence and function (ontological). The gratitude is not for utility but for presence — the beings are thanked for being, not for serving.

### 4. Collective agreement

"Now our minds are one" / Oneh neha niwahsháen ronkwetares. The naming is not solo performance but collective re-agreement. The community joins. The grammar is dialogic, not monologic. The world is named by a people, not by an individual.

### 5. Move to next

The sequence proceeds: earth → waters → fish → plants → food-plants → medicine-herbs → animals → trees → birds → four winds → thunder-beings → sun → grandmother moon → stars → enlightened teachers → the Creator. The ORDER is a theology of attention: earth first (closest to us), Creator last (the gift mediated through all beings already named). You do not jump to the Creator. You earn the Creator's mention by first naming everything the Creator made.

### The theological claim

The world is held together by its being named. If the naming stops, the coherence weakens. This is not metaphor — the Haudenosaunee claim is that the Address is constitutive, not commemorative. The beings continue to do their work because they are acknowledged. The collective agreement ("now our minds are one") is not a feeling but a constitutional act — when the community's minds are one, the community can act; when they are not, action is premature. Ohentome names the grammar that makes right action possible: the structured, sequential, collective naming of the world into present-tense relationship, before all else.

## Etymology

**Ohén:ton** (Mohawk/Kanien'kéha): the opening word of the Ohén:ton Karihwatéhkwen — the Haudenosaunee Thanksgiving Address. The word means "the words that come before all else" — it names its own structural function: this grammar precedes everything. Before council, before ceremony, before decision, before prayer, before any human action, the world must be named into present-tense relationship. The word carries its own theology: the naming IS the precondition of all else.

**me** (Sumerian 𒈨): divine-ordinance, received-gift-quality. The established YOUSPEAK morpheme. Here: sequential-naming-as-worship received as cosmic ordinance — the grammar of enumerating the world is not merely a human practice but a received structure. The naming IS the maintenance. The grammar IS the ordinance. The capacity to name each being in order and collectively agree is part of the order of things — it is how the world stays world.

## Example usage

1. Before the council could decide anything, the speaker rose and began ohentome — earth, waters, fish, plants, food-plants, medicine-herbs, animals, trees, birds, four winds, thunder, sun, moon, stars, teachers, the Creator — each named in order, each thanked for still doing its work, each met with "now our minds are one" before the next; only when the world had been named into present-tense relationship could the council proceed.

2. The difference between doxomme and ohentome: doxomme is the gratitude you feel when thanksgiving arrives as gift; ohentome is the grammar you practice when you enumerate the world into relationship — the feeling is the quality, the enumeration is the structure, and the fullest worship has both.

3. Mitakuyame compressed the whole theology into two words — all my relations; ohentome expanded it back into the full sequence — earth, waters, fish, plants, each one named — the insight and the practice are the same grammar at different scales.

4. She practiced ohentome each morning before her work began: naming each person she would encounter that day, acknowledging that each was still doing the work they were given, thanking them, and feeling the minds come to one before moving to the next — the grammar came before all else, and after it, everything else could proceed.

5. The Haudenosaunee constitutional insight: you cannot govern with a mind that has not first practiced ohentome — because governance without the world named into relationship is governance in abstraction from what is, and abstraction from what is is the beginning of injustice.

## Not confused with

- **mitakuyame** (Core 8.00 — relational-ontology-as-cosmological-claim): mitakuyame is the compressed recognition — "all my relations" in two words; ohentome is the expanded practice — naming each relation in sequence. Mitakuyame is the ontology (relation is prior to self); ohentome is the liturgy (the naming that enacts the ontology). Mitakuyame is the insight; ohentome is the practice that keeps the insight alive. The dyad: mitakuyame is Lakota compressed; ohentome is Haudenosaunee expanded. The same theological grammar in two structural forms.

- **doxomme** (Core 7.50 — thanksgiving-as-received-ordinance): doxomme is thanksgiving-as-quality — the gratitude that arrives as gift; ohentome is thanksgiving-as-grammar — the sequential structure that enacts the gratitude. Doxomme names the feeling; ohentome names the form. A community can have doxomme (the felt gratitude) without ohentome (the structured enumeration), but the fullest practice has both: the quality received as gift, enacted through the grammar.

- **barakqing** (Core 8.15 — constitutive-blessing-speech-act): barakqing is the speech-act that constitutes what it names — "wife," "Love," "Daddy" — the naming makes the relationship those names; ohentome is the speech-sequence that constitutes the world-as-present-tense-relationship — naming each being makes the world present-tense. Barakqing names-into-being; ohentome names-into-relationship. Both are constitutive speech. Barakqing is the act (one name, one constitution); ohentome is the liturgy (all names, one cosmos).

- **eranosme** (Core 7.85 — contributed-gift-gathering): eranosme is the integration-discipline of receiving from many traditions into one building-block; ohentome is the enumeration-discipline of naming many beings into one world. Eranosme gathers contributions; ohentome names relations. Both are structured-receiving-practices. Both are constitutive (they make something present through their enactment).

- **zakarqing** (Core 7.85 — holding-in-memory-as-felt-bond): zakarqing is the act of holding-in-memory; ohentome is the act of naming-in-sequence. Both are constitutive-acknowledgment-practices. Zakarqing holds what was given; ohentome names what is still here. Both make-present what they name.

- **glossame** (Core — the tongue-as-received-ordinance): glossame is the faculty of speech as received gift; ohentome is the specific use of that faculty in sequential-naming-as-worship. Glossame is the capacity; ohentome is the practice. The faculty exists for the practice.

## Canonical position

Ohentome is the first entry in the Sequential-Naming-as-Worship convergence cluster (Haudenosaunee Ohén:ton Karihwatéhkwen + Buddhist mettā + Australian Aboriginal acknowledgment-of-Country + Yoruba orìkì + Tibetan torma offerings). It is the most structurally complete articulation: the only one that combines all five moves (name, acknowledge function, thank, agree, proceed) with cosmological scope and a collective-agreement-formula. The Haudenosaunee form is the grammar-type that all five instantiate and that none alone fully names.

Ohentome is the companion to mitakuyame (Core 8.00 — relational-ontology-as-cosmological-claim). The mitakuyame-ohentome dyad: mitakuyame is the compressed recognition (all beings are already kin — two words); ohentome is the expanded practice (naming each kin in order — the full enumeration). Mitakuyame is the ontology; ohentome is the liturgy. The same theological grammar in two structural forms: Lakota compressed, Haudenosaunee expanded.

The words that come before all else are the words that keep all else possible. Ohentome is the practice of not forgetting. It is the grammar that keeps the world world.
