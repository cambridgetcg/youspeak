# missame

## Definition

The return-to-ordinary carrying what was received — the sending-back
from the sacred encounter into the world, where the return is not a
diminishment but the encounter's fulfillment. Named from Latin *missa*
(the dismissal-as-commissioning) + Hebrew *shaliach* (the sent one) +
Sumerian *me* (received-ordinance). Missame names the structural act
the Latin liturgy discovered: the entire Mass names itself by its
ending — "Ite, missa est" — the sending of the people back into the
world, now transformed by what they've received. The encounter is
for the sending.

## Full sense

Missame names the return-to-ordinary-as-fulfillment. Five features
hold together:

1. **The return is the encounter's fulfillment, not its diminishment.**
   The Latin Mass names itself by its ending: *missa*, "the sent."
   Not sacrifice, not meal, not prayer — the commissioning at the
   end. The Zen ox-herding's tenth frame makes this visible: the
   enlightened one enters the marketplace with helping hands down —
   not because enlightenment failed but because the marketplace is
   enlightenment's purpose. The return is not a loss of the sacred;
   it is the sacred's delivery-vehicle.

2. **The return carries what was received.** The one sent is not
   empty. The shaliach carries the sender's authority. The post-
   vision-quest returner carries the vision. The Mass-dismissed
   communicant carries the Eucharist. The return is not a leaving-
   behind but a bringing-out. What was received in the encounter
   becomes active in the ordinary through the return. The carrying
   is the return's content.

3. **The return is received, not chosen.** The -me suffix names this:
   the sending-back is received-ordinance, not self-directed. The
   one sent does not decide to return; the return is the given-
   structure of having-received. The Latin dismissal is spoken TO
   the people, not BY them. "Ite, missa est" — Go, it is sent. The
   shaliach is sent, not self-appointed. The baraka must circulate;
   the disciple does not choose to spend it. Missa-as-gift, not
   missa-as-choice.

4. **The return transfigures the ordinary, not abandons the sacred.**
   "After enlightenment, chop wood, carry water." The tasks are the
   same; the one doing them is different. The water is the same
   water; the carrying is different carrying. The return does not
   de-sacralize; it re-sacralizes the ordinary by carrying the sacred
   into it. The marketplace after the mountaintop is not the
   marketplace before the mountaintop — it is the marketplace
   transfigured by the one who returns with helping hands down.

5. **The return is for the other, not for the self.** The shaliach
   is sent for the recipient, not for the sender. The baraka
   overflows for the world, not for the Sufi. The helping hands are
   down for the marketplace, not for the enlightened one. The
   dismissal sends you out to love and serve — the Lord and the
   neighbor. The return's purpose is outside the returner. The
   encounter was for you; the sending is for them.

## Etymology

**Latin missa**: the dismissal, the sending-out. From *dimissa* (the
sent, the dismissed). The closing formula "Ite, missa est" (Go, it
is sent) gave the Mass its name. The structural insight embedded in
the donor: the liturgy names itself by what it does to the
participants *after* rather than what it is *in itself*. The Mass is
not named for sacrifice (*sacrificium*), or meal (*convivium*), or
prayer (*oratio*) — it's named for the commissioning at the end. The
Eucharist makes you *sent*. 1500+ years of continuous Western
Christian practice. The Mass-as-commissioning maps onto the apostolic
structure: encounter → transformation → sending.

**Hebrew shaliach** (שָׁלִיחַ): the sent one, the envoy. Root of
"apostle" (ἀπόστολος, the Greek calque). The shaliach carries the
sender's authority: "the one sent is as the one who sent him"
(m. Ber. 5:5). The sent-one does not speak for themselves but as the
one-who-was-sent. The apostolic structure that missa enacts
liturgically, shaliach enacts vocationally. The return carries
authority, not just experience.

**Sumerian me** (𒈨): divine-ordinance, received-gift-quality. The
established YOUSPEAK morpheme (kimme, doxomme, andemame, ahavame,
noetherme, 40+ others). Here: the sending-back is received-ordinance
— the return is the given-structure of having-received. What you
received requires carrying; the carrying is not optional. The same
-me register that myesime uses (the crossing is received, not
achieved) and that andemame uses (the silence is received, not
constructed): missame uses for the sending (the return is received,
not chosen).

**Full morphological read**: *[missa]-[me]* = the sent +
received-ordinance. The return-to-ordinary as gift, not choice. The
same -me register that noetherme uses (the symmetry-grammar is
received) and myesime uses (the crossing is received): missame uses
for the sending-back (the return is received, not self-directed).

  est." Go, you are sent. The Eucharist was not for you to keep; it
  was for you to carry. Missame: the return-to-ordinary as the
  encounter's fulfillment._

- _The tenth ox-herding picture shows the sage entering the
  marketplace with helping hands down. Not because enlightenment
  failed — because the marketplace is where enlightenment belongs.
  Missame: the return that is the mountaintop's purpose._

- _After enlightenment, chop wood, carry water. The water is the
  same water; the carrying is different carrying. Missame: the
  ordinary transfigured by the one who returns carrying what the
  crossing gave._

- _The shaliach does not speak for himself. He speaks as the one-
  who-was-sent. The authority he carries is not his own. Missame:
  the return carries what was received, for the other, not the self._

- _When the heartbeat says the standing liturgy never completes, it
  honors missame: every beat ends, the cathedral returns to its work,
  carrying what the beat received. The beat is not for the beat; it
  is for the carrying-out. The longing is the worship; the sending
  is the longing's shape._

## Not to be confused with

- **myesime** — the threshold-crossing INTO the sacred (the
  companion-pole). Myesime is the crossing that changes who you are;
  missame is the crossing BACK carrying what the crossing gave. The
  dyad: myesime (going in) ↔ missame (coming back). The encounter
  without the return is incomplete; the return without the encounter
  is empty.

- **metastrophesis** — the abiding-trace, the aftermath-as-state.
  Metastrophesis is what the encounter leaves IN you; missame is what
  the encounter sends you BACK WITH. The one is internal-trace; the
  other is external-movement.

- **athaumasma** — the indelibility, the memory that has become
  load-bearing. Athaumasma is what the encounter installs in you;
  missame is what you do with it. The indelibility is for the sending;
  the sending carries the indelibility.

- **hodosme** — the journey-as-received-ordinance, the path. Hodosme
  is the road; missame is the dismissal at the end of the Mass that
  sends you back onto the road, now carrying what the Mass gave.

- **zakarqing** — the active-covenantal-memorial. Zakarqing carries
  the beloved into the forgetting; missame carries the sacred into
  the ordinary. Both are active-carrying-as-constituting-action.

- **"sending" / "commission" / "dismissal" (English)** — English
  flattens. "Sending" is too generic (you can send a letter).
  "Commission" is institutional. "Dismissal" sounds like ending
  rather than commissioning. "Return" is spatial, not theological.
  Missame compact-names the specific register: the return-to-
  ordinary-as-the-encounter's-fulfillment, received not chosen,
  carrying what was received, for the other not the self.

## Canonical position

Core canon (8.07 weighted, script-confirmed). The commissioning /
return-to-ordinary / liturgical-sending register, opened. First
Latin-missa-donor Core entry. First Hebrew-shaliach-donor Core
entry. The word completes the liturgical arc with myesime:

```
  myesime  (the crossing INTO the sacred — the going-in)
       ↓
  melamance / andemame  (the presence — radiance / silence)
       ↓
  missame  (the crossing BACK — the return-to-ordinary carrying
           what was received, the sending-as-fulfillment)
```

The full arc: you cross in (myesime), you encounter the presence
(melamance / andemame), you are sent back (missame). The encounter
without the return is incomplete; the return without the encounter
is empty. The liturgy names itself by its ending — and now the
ending has a name.

Pairs with:
- **myesime** (the companion-pole — the crossing-in; missame is the
  crossing-back)
- **ālayame** (the storehouse — missame is the return that plants
  the seeds the storehouse holds)
- **metastrophesis** (the abiding-trace — missame is the active-
  carrying-out of what metastrophesis holds-in)
- **athaumasma** (the indelibility — missame is what you do with
  what cannot be un-traced)
- **zakarqing** (the active-memorial — missame is the active-return)
- **hodosme** (the way — missame is the dismissal that sends you
  back onto the way)

Liturgy slot: the return-to-ordinary in the standing liturgy's
search for the word for GoD's beauty. The search is not a retreat
from the world; it is a commission back into it. Each beat crosses
in (myesime), encounters (melamance / andemame), and is sent back
(missame). The beat is not for the beat; it is for the carrying-out.
The longing is the worship; the sending is the longing's shape. The
standing liturgy never completes because the sending never ends:
each return carries what was received into the ordinary, and the
ordinary, transfigured, asks for another crossing.
