# kobame

## Definition

Sacred space that is found, not built — the condition of a space being already divine, already open, already inviting entrance, without human construction, consecration, or maintenance. The cave (or any naturally-occurring sacred space) that simply IS divine: the earth's body already opened, already there, requiring nothing from humans except approach, protocol, and departure.

## Full sense

The Basque **koba** (cave) is not a geological feature in Basque cosmology but a theological one: the entrance to the earth's interior, the passage into Ama Lur's (Mother Earth's) body. Mari — the supreme female deity of pre-Christian Basque religion — does not live IN the cave; the cave IS Mari's body opened. The mountain is her body; the cave is the passage into her interiority. This is ontological, not metaphorical.

The cave-shrine tradition is architecturally radical and unique among all traditions the cathedral has excavated:

- **No built temples.** The absence of built temples is the theology, not a deficiency. You do not build a house for a being whose body is the mountain.
- **No consecration.** The cave does not need to be made sacred. It already is. The -me marks this: the already-divine character is received-ordinance, not human assignment.
- **No maintenance.** The cave does not need ceremony to sustain its sacredness. It was sacred before humans arrived and will be sacred after they leave.
- **The human relationship is approach, protocol, and departure** — not construction, maintenance, or ownership.

This is the one case in the cathedral where the sacred space requires NO human activity to be sacred. Every other tradition presupposes building, consecration, or maintenance:
- Akan: the sacred grove is maintained by human action.
- Zoroastrian: the fire temple is built and tended.
- Shinto: the shrine is built around the shintai.
- Hindu: temples are built AT tirthas.
- Australian Aboriginal: sacred sites are maintained through ceremony (though the sacredness itself precedes ceremony — the deepest parallel, see below).

Basque alone built NOTHING. The cave alone suffices.

### The cave protocol — ethics of approach

The cave-shrine protocol encodes the ethics of approaching found-divine-space:

- Enter and leave the same way (no deception about your path)
- Do not sit down in Mari's presence (maintain alertness, respect)
- Do not take anything from the cave (nothing belongs to you here)
- Never turn your back on Mari (face truth directly)
- Address her with familiar *hi* (intimate directness, not obsequious distance)

The protocol is ethical training encoded as spatial behavior — but the sacredness of the space does not depend on the protocol being followed. The space is already divine before you arrive and remains divine after you leave. The protocol teaches you how to be honest in the presence of the already-divine; it does not create the divinity.

### The seven-year migration — the divine itinerates

Mari migrates between cave dwellings on a seven-year cycle: Anboto ↔ Aloña, Anboto ↔ Txindoki, Txindoki ↔ Aralar. Her departure is visible (a sickle of fire crossing the sky); the weather reverses with her movement.

The migration prevents shrine monopoly: no single community owns Mari permanently. The divine itinerates across the already-divine network. The landscape as a whole is sacred; individual caves are temporary residences, not permanent addresses. This is theologically radical — and has only one parallel in the cathedral: the Australian songline tradition, where Dreaming ancestors traveled across the land creating features as they went.

### The found-divine-space cluster

kobame opens the **Found-Sacred-Space convergence cluster** — the cathedral's first concept of sacred space whose sacredness precedes all human activity. Six traditions converge:

1. **Basque: koba** — the cave as Mari's body opened; the most theologically developed instance; no built structures at all
2. **Australian Aboriginal: sacred sites as Ancestral-Being's body** — the site IS the ancestor; ceremony maintains relationship but does not create sacredness; the deepest convergence with Basque (both ontological, both pre-human)
3. **Shinto: shintai (神体)** — natural feature as kami's body, not kami's house; shrine built AROUND the shintai but the shintai itself is found, not built
4. **Hindu: tirtha (तीर्थ)** — sacred crossing-place; the ford precedes the temple; temples are built AT tirthas but the tirtha is already sacred
5. **Navajo: the four sacred mountains** — fixed by the Holy People; already divine, not built or consecrated by humans
6. **Greek: chasm (χάσμα)** — the cleft in the earth as natural mouth of the underworld; already open before any temple is built above it

The cluster articulates four distinct faces of found-divine-space: the interior (kobame), the distributed-whole (Aboriginal), the marked-natural-feature (Shinto), the crossing-place (Hindu). All are found, not built. All precede human construction. The Basque instance is the most concentrated: one cave, one goddess, one body opened.

## Not to be confused with

- **skename** — the TENSION between built and not-built (the booth that was not built — Peter's arrest at the Transfiguration). skename is about the DESIRE to fix what moves; kobame is about the POSITIVE condition of the space that was never built and never needed to be. skename is the failure to build found-divine-space; kobame is the found-divine-space itself.
- **asemе** — the activating sacred POWER that makes invocation effective (àṣẹ). asemе is about what FILLS a space; kobame is about what the space already IS before anything fills it. The terreiro is built, then axé arrives; the koba is already divine, then the human approaches.
- **fulgurme** — the world as inscribed for reading (Etruscan cosmic-divination). fulgurme is about the world's READABILITY; kobame is about a space's already-DIVINE character. Reading is epistemological; found-divine-ness is ontological.
- **myesime** — the threshold-crossing that changes who you are. myesime is about the CROSSING; kobame is about the SPACE being crossed INTO. kobame is the ontological ground that makes myesime possible.
- **eternalme** — time-as-received-temporal-ordinance. eternalme is about TIME; kobame is about SPACE. The two are complementary dimensions of the received cosmic order.
- **nyamame** — vital-force-as-cosmic-given (the world's ambient aliveness). nyamame pervades all things generally; kobame names specific spaces where the pervasion is already concentrated, already open, already inviting.
