# satorime

## Definition

Sudden-awakening-as-received-ordinance — the irreversible event that deepens the initial-seeing (kenshō) into the structural-transformation of the entire knowing-subject.

## Full sense

**Satorime** names the event that Zen calls satori (悟り): the moment when the practitioner's entire cognitive-affective structure is reorganized by direct recognition of the nature of reality. It is the deepening of kenshō — the initial flash of seeing one's true-nature — into the fire that catches, stabilizes, and transforms.

Five structural marks:

1. **Sudden** — satorime is event-character, not process-character. It arrives in a moment, not through accumulation. The Rinzai kōan practice is designed to produce the sudden-breakthrough; the Sōtō tradition recognizes the event-character even within gradual cultivation.

2. **Received** — satorime is not something the practitioner produces; it is something that happens to the practitioner. The conditions are prepared (zazen, kōan, sanzen) but the event is not the product of effort. Hakuin: "the great ball of doubt shatters" — the practitioner does not shatter it. The -me marks this: the awakening is received-ordinance, given not achieved.

3. **Irreversible** — once satorime has occurred, the practitioner's relationship to experience is permanently reorganized. Distinct from peak experience (which fades) or mystical experience (which is temporary). Dōgen: "the dropping away of body and mind" — once dropped, it does not return.

4. **Verifiable** — satorime is checked through the sanzen process: the practitioner presents understanding to the teacher, who checks it against the kōan and the tradition. Not theological authority but phenomenological cross-checking — the awakening-event is accountable to a community of practice.

5. **Deepening** — satorime is not necessarily a single event. The kenshō → satori → daigo arc is a deepening series, each sudden, each irreversible. Hakuin speaks of "one hundred satoris" — awakening is a deepening series of structural transformations.

### The Zen deepening-sequence

The cathedral's Zen register now has two members:

- **kenshōme** (initial-seeing-as-ordinance) — the first flash of recognizing one's true-nature
- **satorime** (sudden-awakening-as-ordinance) — the deepening of that flash into irreversible transformation

The sequence continues with daigo (great-awakening, pipeline candidate) — the maximal instance, the fullest expression of satori. The arc is deepening, not different-kinds: kenshō → satori → daigo is one fire catching, spreading, becoming all-consuming.

### The satori-rigpame-āśraya-parāvṛtti triad

Three traditions name the same structural-transformation from three angles:

- **satorime** (Zen) — the phenomenological face: the recognition reorganizes experience
- **rigpame** (Dzogchen) — the result-oriented face: the state of primordial-awareness one rests in after the event
- **asraya-paravrttime** (Yogācāra) — the ontological face: the basis transforms from defiled to pure

Same event, three angles. The cathedral now holds all three: the event (satorime), the result (rigpame), the ontological description (asraya-paravrttime). The triad is structurally complete.

### Cross-tradition parallels

1. **Japanese Zen — satori (悟り):** The central term. Sudden, received, irreversible, verifiable through sanzen.
2. **Tibetan Dzogchen — rigpa (རིག་པ):** Direct-introduction (ngo sprod) triggers sudden recognition of primordial-awareness.
3. **Hindu Advaita — ahaṃ brahmāsmi:** The mahāvākya-recognition is sudden and transformative; "thou art that" is a recognition to be triggered, not a proposition to be believed.
4. **Sufi — fanāʾ (فناء):** The passing-away of the ego-self is sudden and irreversible; a rupture within the maqamat-sequence.
5. **Christian — metanoia (μετάνοια):** The "change of mind" in desert fathers is event-character: a structural-shift, not gradual improvement.
6. **Hasidic — hitlahavut (התלהבות):** The "kindling" — when practice catches fire, when Torah becomes living-fire. Preparation is gradual; the kindling is sudden and received.
7. **Buddhist Yogācāra — āśraya-parāvṛtti:** The "turning-about of the basis" — the same event described ontologically.

## Example usage

After years of zazen, the ball of doubt shattered in sanzen — satorime: the awakening arrived as received-ordinance, not as the product of his effort, and the ten thousand things enlightened themselves through him.

The kenshō was only the first opening; satorime is what happens when the initial-seeing catches fire and becomes irreversible — the deepening that kenshō alone cannot guarantee.

## Not to be confused with

- **kenshōme** — initial-seeing-as-ordinance. The first flash; satorime is the deepening. Sequential, not synonymous.
- **rigpame** — primordial-awareness-as-ordinance. The RESULT (the state one rests in); satorime is the EVENT (the moment that opens into rigpa).
- **asraya-paravrttime** — transformation-of-the-basis. The Yogācāra ontological face of the same event; satorime is the Zen phenomenological face.
- **meditame** — meditation-as-ordinance. The PRACTICE that prepares; satorime is the EVENT that the practice prepares for.
- **anagnoristasis** — held-recognition. The stable CAPACITY to recognize; satorime is the MOMENT the capacity fires.
- **noemame** — event-of-meaning-arrival. The GENERAL concept; satorime is the SPECIFIC instance (awakening, not any meaning-arrival).

## Retirement conditions

Satorime would be superseded if a better-compounded word from a more diverse donor-pair named the same concept with greater clarity, or if the Zen tradition's own vocabulary were found to have a more precise term for the deepening-event (daigo is the maximal instance, not the general term). The -me marking could be replaced only if a different morpheme better captures the received-ordinance character — but -me is the cathedral's most-validated morpheme for this function.
