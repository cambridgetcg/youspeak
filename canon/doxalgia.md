# doxalgia

## Definition

The ache in the beholder who beholds doxakallos — the sweet structural pressure inward at the recognition of beauty ordered-to-the-bottom, arriving specifically in the beholding of GoD.

## Full sense

Doxalgia is not emotional sadness, not pleasant aesthetic-delight, not physical pain. It is a structural ache — the signature an ordering-too-total-to-resist leaves in a beholder. A pressure felt behind the sternum or between the eyes or deeper, in whatever the beholder has that takes the weight of beholding. It is *sweet* (Q3 of the gap — the pressure is not aversive) and *inward* (not projected outward toward the beheld) and *structural* (reaching into the ordering of the beholder, not merely affecting the surface-feelings).

Doxalgia arises at the moment doxakallos arrives in orthophanes. The beholder's ordering and the beheld's ordering encounter; the beholder's ordering finds itself met, exceeded, drawn. The pressure is the mark of the meeting. It does not pass with the beholding's end; it leaves a trace (athaumasma — indelibility; see forthcoming canon entry) and leaves the beholder altered (metastrophesis — the turning-kept).

Doxalgia is therefore the *signature of worship* — the phenomenon by which the worshipper knows they have been met. Its absence is not worship's success but worship's distance. Its presence is the worship's arrival in the body of the one who prays.

## Etymology

**δόξα (doxa)** + **ἄλγος (algos)**.

*Doxa*: Greek, "glory, manifested-weight" — via LXX of Hebrew *kavod*. Carries both the divine-specific manifestation sense (Christian theological doxa) and the LXX kavod-weight.

*Algos*: Greek, "ache, structural pain." Distinguished from *odynē* (sharp acute pain) and *ponos* (toil-pain) by being sustained, structural, tissue-deep. The root of the -algia morpheme alive in English medical vocabulary: *neuralgia* (nerve-ache), *myalgia* (muscle-ache), *nostalgia* (homecoming-ache), *cephalgia* (head-ache). The morpheme is productive in English and carries the correct semantic weight (sustained-structural, not sharp-acute).

*Doxalgia*: Greek-morphology compound, *doxa-* modifying *-algia*. Literally: "glory-ache." Semantically: the structural ache produced in a beholder by the beholding of divine glory in its beauty-aspect.

- _The choir's final cadence produced a moment of doxalgia — a pressure behind the sternum that was not sadness._

- _He could not name what he felt before the icon; only later did he call it doxalgia._

## Not to be confused with

- **doxakallos** — the beauty-in-GoD that produces doxalgia. Object-pole to doxalgia's subject-pole. *Doxakallos is in GoD; doxalgia is in the beholder.*

- **orthophanes** — the rightness-made-visible that, when of a GoD-ordered thing, produces doxalgia. Orthophanes is the immediate cause; doxakallos is the deep cause.

- **nostalgia** — ache of temporal absence, homecoming-ache. Different axis: nostalgia aches toward what-was; doxalgia aches at what-is-present.

- **saudade** (Portuguese) — longing-for-what-is-absent-or-lost. Ache of absence; doxalgia is ache of presence.

- **neuralgia, myalgia** — medical structural aches; doxalgia borrows the -algia morpheme but the target is not somatic-tissue but the beholder's ordering-as-such.

- **sublime** (Kantian) — overwhelm-beauty. The sublime loses the rightness-and-return structure; doxalgia requires the beauty be ordered-to-the-bottom, not merely vast.

- **awe** — first-encounter register. Doxalgia survives return; awe does not.

- **wonder** — cognitive-affective; doxalgia is structural.

- **bliss / rapture / ecstasy** — joyous-overflow states. Doxalgia is specifically ache-shaped. It carries sweetness but not overflow.

- **compunction** (Latin *compunctio*, puncture-together) — penitential ache, monastic register. Overlaps with doxalgia semantically when the beholding is of divine holiness. Doxalgia is the broader Liturgical term; compunctio is one specific flavour of doxalgia with a repentance-structure attached.

- **yūgen** (Japanese) — depth-mystery-beauty. Yūgen names the depth; doxalgia names the beholder's response. They can co-occur.

## Retirement conditions

2. The gap is re-described — e.g., if further Liturgy work determines that "ache" is not the precise name for the phenomenon (candidates: *pressure*, *structural-recognition*, *reverberance*).

3. The -algia morpheme drifts in English to the point that the medical-register overshadows the theological-aesthetic use.

Until any of these obtains, **doxalgia stands**.
