# 027 — epiclance

_The act of calling the divine presence into the present — into a specific place, moment, or person — through trained invocatory speech. Named from Greek **epiclesis** (ἐπίκλησις, the calling-down of the Spirit upon the elements; epi + klēsis = upon-calling) and confirmed by three cross-tradition parallels: Sanskrit **āvāhana** (calling the deity to inhabit), Hebrew **qara** (calling by name with invocatory power), and Arabic **du'ā'** (the calling that God has promised to answer). Epiclance names the act that precedes all other worship-acts: calling GoD to be here, now._

---

## The Gap

English in the vicinity:

- **"Prayer"** — too broad; covers petition, praise, lament, contemplation; not specifically the calling-down-of-presence
- **"Invocation"** — the closest candidate; but invocation in English also means "to call upon" in non-liturgical senses (invoking a rule, invoking a right); it has lost the specific theology of calling-presence-into-the-present
- **"Petition"** — names asking-for-something; epiclance is not asking for something but calling someone to be present
- **"Summoning"** — carries coercive connotations (summoning a demon, summoning a witness); the divine is not coerced in any of these traditions — the divine is invited to come

Epiclance names the specific act: **calling the divine to take up presence in this moment, this place, this gathered body**. The act is completed when presence arrives; if presence arrives, epiclance was accomplished. If presence does not arrive, epiclance was incomplete (wrong name, wrong authority, wrong preparation) — as in the Hindu understanding that an incorrectly performed āvāhana leaves the mūrti empty.

This is the act that opens every act of worship in traditions that take presence seriously: the divine is not assumed to be here; the divine is called here.

---

## The Phenomenon

### Greek: epiclesis — the most disputed moment in Christian liturgy

The **epiclesis** (ἐπίκλησις) is the prayer in the Eucharistic liturgy calling the Holy Spirit to descend upon the bread and wine and transform them into the Body and Blood of Christ. In Orthodox liturgies it is: *"Send down Your Holy Spirit upon us and upon these gifts here presented..."*

The epiclesis is among the most theologically contested acts in Christian history:
- **Orthodox theology** holds the epiclesis as the essential transforming-moment: the Spirit is called down; the Spirit descends; the transformation occurs. Without the epiclesis, the Eucharist is incomplete.
- **Catholic theology** (post-Trent) emphasizes the Words of Institution as the moment of consecration; the epiclesis is present but its precise moment-of-transformation is differently located.
- **Protestant disputes** (Reformation and beyond) centered partly on this: is the Spirit called down at a specific liturgical moment, or is the Spirit present wherever two or three are gathered?

Regardless of the answer, the structure of epiclance is agreed: the worshipping community calls the divine presence to descend onto a specific material moment, at a specific point in a liturgical sequence, through specific words spoken by specific persons. This is epiclance.

### Sanskrit: āvāhana — the first act of pūjā

In Hindu pūjā, the sixteen **upacāras** (services/offerings) begin with **āvāhana** — the invitation. Before any water is offered, any incense burned, any lamp waved: the deity must be invited to be present in the mūrti (sacred image).

The ritual: *"O Devī, please come here. May your divine form take up dwelling in this image for the duration of this worship."* The āvāhana uses the deity's names, attributes, and the worshipper's devotion to call the divine into the present.

Without āvāhana, the subsequent acts of pūjā are gestures toward an empty image — beautiful but not transformative. With āvāhana, the image becomes genuinely the deity's present form. The same logic applies to fire-rites (yajña): the Vedic invocations (āhvāna = calling, from same root as āvāhana) invite Agni, Indra, Vayu to be present in the fire.

Āvāhana = epiclance: the prior act that makes all subsequent worship-acts genuine rather than empty.

### Hebrew: qara — calling on the name

The verb **qara** (קָרָא) means to call, cry out, proclaim — and in its theological application, to call on the name of the LORD with invocatory intent.

The pattern appears at the foundation of Hebrew religion: *"Abraham called on the name of the LORD"* (Genesis 21:33). This is not prayer in the generic sense — it is the invocatory act of calling the divine into relation by naming the divine correctly.

The classical passage: Joel 2:32, quoted at Pentecost (Acts 2:21) — *"All who call on the name of the LORD will be saved."* The calling-by-name IS the act; the name is the summons; calling the name correctly and with intent is the invocatory act that opens the divine-human encounter.

The Hebrew understanding: the divine name has power because naming correctly summons the named. The Ineffable Name (YHVH) is not pronounced in many Jewish traditions precisely because full correct pronunciation of the divine name would summon the divine in ways the caller might not be prepared to handle. The name is the summons; qara = epiclance.

### Arabic: du'ā' — the call that God has promised to answer

**Du'ā'** (دُعَاء) — from da'a = to call, invite, summon — is the personal prayer of direct address to God. The Qur'an: *"Your Lord said: Call on me [ud'ūnī] and I will answer you"* (Ghafir 40:60).

Du'ā' is epiclance in its most direct form: God has promised that calling will be answered. The caller calls; God answers by making the divine presence felt, the divine blessing available, the divine will known. The call IS the act; the answer is the presence that arrives in response to the call.

Islamic tradition teaches the proper conditions for du'ā' that will be answered: the caller must be in the correct state (purity, orientation), must use the correct approach (praising God first, invoking God's names), must have the correct intention. These are epiclantic conditions: calling-presence requires the caller to be in the proper state to receive the presence called.

### The relationship to àṣẹme

Epiclance and àṣẹme (Core 7.80) are complementary:
- **Epiclance** is the **act** — the calling-of-presence-into-the-present
- **Àṣẹme** is the **power** that makes the calling effective — the divine authorization that allows a trained invocation to accomplish what it names

The babaláwo performing an epiclance-act (calling the orisha to be present in the ritual space) accomplishes it because they have àṣẹme. The novice performing the identical words fails because they lack àṣẹme. Epiclance is what both are doing; àṣẹme is what distinguishes the accomplished from the empty calling.

## Form and Etymology

**epiclance** — from Greek *epiclesis* (ἐπίκλησις: epi [upon/down-onto] + klēsis [calling, summons, from kaleo = to call]) + the contracted *-ance* nominal suffix.

The Greek epiclesis contracts into the YOUSPEAK epiclance cleanly: epi-kl-ance preserves the epi (directional: upon/down-onto) and the kl- root (calling) while the -ance suffix nominalizes the act. The word is three syllables: EP-i-clanCE (stress on first and last).

The directional epi (upon/down) is crucial and preserved: epiclance is specifically the calling-down-of-presence, not mere address. The divine is called to descend into the particular, to be here.

## Liturgical pairing: qorvance + epiclance

The two fundamental movements of worship:
1. **Qorvance** — the creature's movement toward GoD: drawing near through what is brought
2. **Epiclance** — the calling of GoD's movement toward the creature: calling-presence-into-the-present

Every worship-encounter involves both: the creature approaches (qorvance) AND calls the divine to meet the approach (epiclance). The Eucharist is qorvance (bread and wine brought forward) + epiclance (the Spirit called to descend). The Hindu pūjā is qorvance (upahāra brought forward) + epiclance (āvāhana calling the deity to be present). The Islamic salat is qorvance (the worshipper presenting their body toward Mecca) + epiclance (the Fātiḥa calling GoD to guide and respond).

All epiclance: the trained call that opens the divine-human encounter by summoning presence into the particular._
