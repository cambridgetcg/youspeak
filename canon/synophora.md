# synophora

## Definition

The event-quality of two or more beholders carrying an orthophanes together, without speaking, recognizing each other as fellow-carriers.

## Full sense

When two beholders share an orthophanes and recognize each other's sharing wordlessly, a new quality arises that is not reducible to either beholder's individual experience. The orthophanes is carried between them; the carrying itself is the event. Synophora names this carrying.

Synophora is the first Liturgy-quality that has no single-beholder mode. Orthophanes, anagnoristasis, doxalgia, metastrophesis, athaumasma can each be predicated of one person. Synophora requires two. It cannot be transmitted; it can only be participated in. A beholder cannot tell another beholder about a synophora; they can only have-been-in-one.

The recognition is silent. The speaking would be less than the silence. The glance, when there is one, is not the synophora; the glance is the signal. The synophora is what-the-glance-acknowledges: that both have-carried, that each knows the other has-carried, that the carrying was-of-the-same-orthophanes.

Small communities can hold synophora over time. Musicians who have played together for decades; two parents who saw their child's first act; a married couple at a grandchild's graduation; worshippers in long-silence; readers of the same poem on a train. The carrying outlives the moment; it becomes a standing-synophora that can be entered-into again when both are present to the same orthophanes.

## Etymology

**syn- (together-with)** + **-phora (carrying, from pherein)**.

*Syn-*: Greek productive prefix. Together-with-mutuality (not merely together-in-space). Alive in English: synchronous (time-together), synthesis (put-together), synergy (work-together), symbiosis (life-together).

*-phora*: Greek nominal suffix from *pherein* (to carry). Carries *the mode of carrying* in compound form. Productive in English technical vocabulary:
- *metaphor* (meta-phora) — meaning-carried-across
- *diaphora* — distinction; carried-apart
- *anaphora* — carried-up; referring-back (rhetoric/liturgy)
- *epiphora* — repetition; carried-onto
- *cataphor* — carried-down; forward-reference
- *phosphorus* — light-carrier

*Synophora*: the together-carrying. The mutual mode of carrying an orthophanes between co-beholders. The word's morphology echoes *metaphor* (metaphora) rhythmically, which is a mnemonic: synophora is to co-beholders what metaphor is to cross-domain meanings — the mutual-carrying that makes the shared-thing possible.

## Example usage

- _After the quartet's final chord, the audience sat in synophora for three beats longer than the score required. No one spoke. No one had to._
- _The synophora between them held forty years; they had beheld one orthophanes together at the wedding and lived inside that carrying._
- _It is possible to enter a room full of strangers and find one other person in synophora-with-you about the same orthophanes — a painting, a line, a stranger's face. The recognition is mutual and rarely survives conversation._
- _Synophora is the first Liturgy-word that refuses solitude. To understand it, one must already have-been-in-one._

## Not confused with

- **communion** — religious-institutional; synophora is the bare event-quality, pre-liturgical.
- **shared experience** — too broad.
- **fellowship** — standing-relation; synophora is event-quality, not relation-status.
- **witness** — single-beholder.
- **co-presence** — spatial; synophora requires mutual beholding-recognition.
- **collective effervescence** (Durkheim) — sociological, crowd-register.
- **rapport** — relational skill; synophora can arise between strangers instantly.
- **orthophanes** — what-is-beheld-together; synophora is the quality-of-the-together-beholding.
- **contrust** (DICTIONARY) — trust-forged-through-conflict; synophora is beholding-forged-through-shared-attention. Orthogonal phenomena.
