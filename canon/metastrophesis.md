# metastrophesis

## Definition

The persistent altered-state of a beholder after the orthophanes-doxalgia encounter — the permanent turning-kept; the beholder now living along a different axis.

## Full sense

The beholder who has passed through orthophanes (the appearing), anagnoristasis (the held-recognition), and doxalgia (the ache) does not return to their prior state. Something stays. Not a memory of the encounter — the encounter itself, in its effect on the beholder's ordering, persists. The axis along which the beholder processes reality has shifted; the new axis is held.

Metastrophesis is not the moment of turning; it is the state of remaining-turned. Plato's *metastrophē* in *Republic* VII is the soul's full about-face toward the Good — the cave-escapee's complete axis-reversal. Metastrophesis is what follows: the condition of one who has turned and now stands along the new axis.

The condition is observable from outside: others can tell that someone has been through the encounter. A certain metastrophesis shows in how they stand, how they listen, what they see in ordinary things. It is not personality-change. It is ordering-change, at a level underneath personality.

The word distinguishes from metanoia (narrowed by Christian usage to moral repentance; lacks aesthetic register) and from conversion (institutional) and from transformation (too generic). Metastrophesis is the specifically aesthetic-beholding permanent-reorientation.

## Etymology

**μεταστροφή (metastrophē)** + **-σις (-sis)**.

*Metastrophē*: Plato, *Republic* VII 518d. The complete turning-about-the-axis, the full reversal of orientation — used of the soul's conversion toward the Good in the cave-allegory. Distinguished from mere *strophē* (turning) by the *meta-* prefix, which marks the turning as complete (not partial), reversing (not mere lateral), and axial (not superficial).

*-sis*: Greek abstract-noun suffix; names the state or condition resulting from the verbal action. *Krisis* (judgment-state from *krinō*, to judge). *Gnōsis* (knowledge-state from *gignōskō*, to know). *Stasis* (standing-state from *histēmi*, to stand). With *metastrophē*, names the state-of-having-been-metastrophied.

*Metastrophesis*: the state of the metastrophed. Morphologically well-formed within Greek.

## Example usage

- _After the encounter he was in metastrophesis — not changed in one thing, but living along a different axis._
- _The community could tell which of them had been to the mountain: a certain metastrophesis showed in how they stood._
- _Metastrophesis is not a memory of beholding; it is the shape the beholder now takes._
- _One rarely exits metastrophesis without another encounter that renames it._

## Not to be confused with

- **metanoia** — mind-changing, repentance; narrowed by Christian moral-usage. Metastrophesis is the aesthetic-Liturgy parallel: what metanoia names in moral register, metastrophesis names in beholding register.
- **conversion** — institutional-religious; metastrophesis is structural-beholding.
- **transformation** — too generic; transformation can be partial, non-axial. Metastrophesis is axial.
- **epiphany** — moment of realization. Metastrophesis is the persistent-state, not the moment.
- **awakening** — Buddhist-inflected content-term; metastrophesis is position-neutral on content.
- **reorientation** — too clinical; lacks the depth.

Until then, **metastrophesis stands**.
