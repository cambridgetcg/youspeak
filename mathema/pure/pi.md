# π — the ratio that is everywhere

## Definition

π is the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter in a flat (zero-curvature) two-dimensional Euclidean plane. Numerically: π = 3.14159 26535 89793 23846 26433 83279 50288 4197... — irrational (Lambert, 1768) and transcendental (Lindemann, 1882). It is also the ratio of a circle's area to the square of its radius (A = πr²) and appears in countless contexts unconnected to circles: probability (the Gaussian normalization constant 1/√(2π)), number theory (the Basel problem ∑ 1/n² = π²/6), wave mechanics, statistics, and complex analysis (e^(iπ) = −1).

## Discovery-history (POLYPHONE at the meta-level)

The donor's history is one of independent rediscovery across at least seven major civilizational mathematical traditions, each adding rigor or precision:

- **Babylonian** scribes (c. 1900-1600 BCE) used approximations including 3.125
- **Egyptian** scribes (Rhind Papyrus, c. 1650 BCE) used (16/9)² ≈ 3.1605
- **Greek** Archimedes (c. 250 BCE) gave the first rigorous bounds 223/71 < π < 22/7
- **Chinese** Liu Hui (3rd c. CE) reached 3.14159 by 3072-gon iteration; Zu Chongzhi (5th c. CE) gave 355/113
- **Indian** Madhava (c. 1400 CE) derived the first infinite-series convergence — the Madhava-Leibniz series
- **Arabic-Islamic** al-Kāshī (1424 CE) computed 16 decimal places
- **Modern multinational** — symbol adopted by Jones (1706), standardized by Euler; transcendence proved by Lindemann (1882); current computations exceed 10¹³ digits

The cathedral credits each tradition. No single one owns π. The donor itself is the invariant.

## Invariance-class

π is invariant in the following sense: any cognizer reasoning about flat two-dimensional geometry will arrive at the same number. Under non-flat geometries (positive curvature like a sphere's surface, negative curvature like a saddle), the circumference/diameter ratio of a circle deviates from π — but π persists as the limit-case as curvature → 0, and the deviation itself is computable in terms of π and the curvature. So even non-Euclidean cognizers reach π by generalization.

Beyond geometry, π appears wherever there is rotation, oscillation, wave-mechanics, Gaussian distribution, or complex-exponential structure. This ubiquity is the empirical content of the donor: π is "the irrational that is everywhere."

## A note on cross-tradition theological readings of π

Several traditions have attached theological weight to π or its containing context:

- **Pythagorean**: number itself is sacred; π's irrationality was theologically scandalous (cf. the legend that Hippasus drowned for revealing irrational ratios)
- **Islamic**: al-Kāshī's 16-digit computation was performed in the context of astronomical-religious calendar calibration (qibla direction, prayer-time computation); precision in π was precision in worship
- **Hindu**: Madhava's series, derived in Kerala, was developed in a tradition that took mathematical work as ritual contemplation (the Kerala school)
